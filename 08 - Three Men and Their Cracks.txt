Grizzly Knights

Episode 8: Three Men and Their Cracks

Three men with little in common find more overlap than expected during a sleepless night in an empty bar.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the weight of unwelcome memories seep through the cracks in their carefully constructed facades, when the bar feels like neutral ground. Logan's glass catches the light as he spins it once, twice, against the worn mahogany of the counter. Hank shifts in the suit that sits on him like an awkward truce, while Johnny slumps onto the stool, tension bleeding from his shoulders into the sticky air.

“Well, this is quite the tableau—Logan with his perennial tang of melodrama and Johnny radiating existential despair. Shall I order a round of melancholy to complement the ambiance?” Hank asked.

“Already got mine, thanks, Hank. You gonna drink, or just posture in that monkey suit all night?” Logan asked.

“Is it a funeral or a first date, Hank? 'Cause either way, you’re overdressed and killing the vibe,” Johnny said.

“Gentlemen, your sartorial critiques are as keen as ever. For the record, this "monkey suit" was tailored by someone who charges more than both your bar tabs combined, so forgive me if I don't weep into my scotch just yet. Speaking of which—bartender, the Macallan, neat,” Hank said.

The bartender nods, wordless, retrieving the Macallan with the practiced efficiency of someone who doesn't need to ask twice. The bottle whispers against the glass as Hank's order is poured, a momentary fill of rich amber among the dim haze. Logan's whiskey sloshes audibly as he drains the remnants, his silence louder than either of their barbs.

“Tailored or not, you still look like you’re trying too hard. Might be the scotch compensatin' for it,” Logan said.

“Logan, coming from you, "trying too hard" smells like beer nuts calling caviar uppity,” Johnny said.

“Ah, the philosophical musings of a man marinating in Jack Daniels and another with a penchant for literal combustion. Let’s not pretend the height of chic resides in plaid flannel or flame-resistant spandex, shall we?” Hank asked.

“Maybe not, but at least neither one screams midlife crisis, Hank,” Logan said.

The bartender smirks faintly, a flicker of amusement that vanishes as quickly as it appears, and shifts to polishing a glass that doesn’t need it. Logan sets his empty tumbler down with a dull weight, the faint scuff of glass on wood filling the pause. Johnny idly spins his phone on the counter, the screen glowing faintly with unanswered notifications, its movement jittery and uneven against the tacky surface.

“Hey, at least midlife crisis implies there’s still a "life" part in there somewhere. Some of us don’t look like we were born at the end of Prohibition, Logan,” Johnny said.

“It’s heartwarming to see the two of you embracing intergenerational banter as a bonding exercise. Though, I must say, Johnny, reminding Logan of calendars might be inviting a cigar to the metaphorical gasoline,” Hank said.

“Careful, Hank. Don't wanna break a nail climbin’ up on that high horse of yours,” Logan said.

“Speaking of high horses, how’s the oxygen up there, Hank? Do they serve pretension in first class too?” Johnny asked.

Hank lifts his scotch, the faintest tremor betraying the weight of the conversation, steadying it against his lips before taking a measured sip. Across the bar, Logan runs a thumb along the rim of his empty glass, the jagged edge of impatience meeting the smooth surface as if testing which would crack first. Johnny hunches forward, elbows on the counter, a slick grin half-formed but not yet anchored in his eyes.

“Astonishing, the dedication both of you bring to reductive wit. It must be exhausting, keeping pace with your own mediocrity. Logan, another round for that empty tumbler, or would you rather grace us with more polished cynicism?” Hank asked.

“Keep talkin', Hank, and I'll take that scotch of yours instead,” Logan said.

“Hey, if we're handing out drinks for bad manners, make mine a double,” Johnny said.

“Ah, the entitlement is palpable—one threatens, the other pleads, and the bartender suffers. Rest assured, gentlemen, this scotch is decidedly beyond your grasp, emotionally and otherwise,” Hank said.

The bartender doesn’t flinch, caught in the familiar rhythm of pouring and enduring, his hands moving to refill Logan’s glass before the man even asks. A faint scrape echoes as the bottle is set down, the room's undercurrent thickening with the sound of liquid meeting glass. Johnny leans back slightly, his grin sharpening into something closer to provocation, the phone now abandoned on the counter like a discarded pretense.

“Long as it gets poured, Hank, doesn't matter whose hands it ends up in,” Logan said.

“Sounds like a challenge, Logan. Wanna race for it? First to the bottom wins, dignity optional,” Johnny said.

“If dignity is optional, this race was lost before it began. Though I doubt either of you would notice, given your remarkable immunity to its presence,” Hank said.

“I ain't racin' for nothin', but if you're tryin' to keep up, Johnny, you better pace yourself,” Logan said.

Logan takes the fresh glass with deliberate ease, the crackling sound of his knuckles as he lifts it betraying more irritation than he lets surface. Johnny’s eyes flicker toward the glass, sizing it up like a younger brother spoiling for a fight, even as he drums his fingers against the counter in some restless syncopation. Hank, still upright and too still for this room, finishes his sip with a precision that feels more defiant than casual.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the bartender leans in to collect the empty glasses, where the three men sit in an uneasy geometry, their movements tracing the boundaries of their silences. Logan taps a slow rhythm on his glass, watching the condensation slide down with something between boredom and the threat of a storm. Johnny shifts in his seat, his knee bouncing beneath the table, while Hank’s watchful gaze lingers on the bartender’s movements more than the company he keeps.

“I find it both fascinating and somewhat ironic that silence, often lauded as golden, becomes leaden in certain company,” Hank said.

“Could just drink instead of hearin' yourself talk, Hank,” Logan said.

“Well, guess we all cope different, huh? Hank with big words, Logan with bigger grunts, and me, apparently, with whatever this tequila's trying to tell me,” Johnny said.

“Perhaps the tequila's wisdom includes the revelation that self-awareness is a double-edged sword, Jonathan,” Hank said.

The bartender slides the refilled glasses onto the counter with an economy of motion, his silence an unspoken agreement not to linger. Logan exhales through his nose, a low, audible dismissal, as Johnny wraps one hand too tightly around his drink, the glass catching the dim light like a blade’s edge. Hank adjusts his glasses, the deliberate act masking the brief twitch of his jawline—a gesture too small for anyone to address, yet too telling to remain unnoticed.

“Tequila don’t talk, kid. Just drowns out whatever you're tryin’ not to hear,” Logan said.

“That from experience, Logan, or just your charming way of saying you care?” Johnny asked.

“Let's not delude ourselves into believing this exchange is veiled profundity. If we must spar, gentlemen, let it at least aspire to wit,” Hank said.

“Sparring’s easy, Hank. It’s the sittin’ still with no armor that cuts deepest,” Logan said.

The overhead light flickers faintly, a stuttering rhythm that mimics the tension rippling through the space between them. Johnny shifts his weight again, his knuckles whitening as his fingers drum once, twice, before stilling on the table’s edge. Hank’s eyes drop to his watch for a fraction of a second too long, while Logan’s shadow slants over his glass as he leans forward, the room itself seeming to exhale around him.

“Man, you two really know how to kill a vibe. I was just here to drink, maybe laugh, not host an emotional cage match,” Johnny said.

“A laugh, you say? Well, Jonathan, perhaps the punchline lies in three avatars of dysfunction gathering over libations, each pretending the cracks aren’t showing,” Hank said.

“Speak for yourself, Hank. I ain't pretending a damn thing,” Logan said.

“Yeah, no kidding. Logan wears his cracks like a damn badge of honor. Must be freeing, honestly,” Johnny said.

The air grows heavier with the weight of words unsaid, the kind that linger longer than the ones thrown out loud. Logan tilts his glass in a slow circle on the table, the faint scrape of glass on wood cutting through the low murmur of the bar. Johnny leans back, the forced ease in his posture betrayed by the quick glance he fires at Hank, whose lips press together as though smothering a thought before it escapes.

“Liberating, perhaps, but I suspect the badge comes with more than its share of splinters,” Hank said.

“Splinters ain't the worst of it. Keeps you honest, though,” Logan said.

“Honest, sure, but man, doesn't it ever get old? Carrying all that like it’s part of the uniform?” Johnny asked.

“Honesty, Jonathan, is an unrelenting taskmaster—yet I suspect we all don such a uniform, whether tailored to fit or not,” Hank said.

The bartender’s movements behind the counter take on the rhythm of something practiced, the dull clang of bottles and muted thud of glass breaking up the pensive quiet. Johnny’s knee bounces again—restless, insistent—while his drink sits untouched, the lime garnish wilting against the rim. Logan stretches his shoulders, the crack of stiff joints loud enough to draw a glance from Hank, whose fingers curl once into a fist on the table before relaxing, deliberate and slow.

“Old don’t mean much when you can’t take it off,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, some of us are still figuring out if we even wanna keep wearing it,” Johnny said.

“The true devil, gentlemen, lies not in the fabric of the uniform but in the choice—or lack thereof—to wear it in the first place,” Hank said.

“Choice went out the window a long time ago, Hank. Now it’s just how you carry it,” Logan said.

A low hum from the overhead bulbs matches the uneven timbre of the room, a tension strung tight but unresolved. Johnny drags his thumbnail across the table’s edge, leaving faint marks in the worn varnish as his eyes dart to Logan, then away again. Hank’s jaw shifts, grinding once, before he leans back in his seat, the creak of wood beneath him swallowed by Logan’s steady, unyielding silence.

* * *

The overhead bulbs pulse faintly, a dim and solitary strobe illuminating the slow swirl of cigarette smoke that loiters, uninvited, above the booth. Logan exhales through his nose, the glass in his hand tilting to the side, amber liquid swaying but never spilling. Johnny shifts forward, his palm pressing flat against the table’s worn wood, while Hank's fingers tighten, knuckles whitening as they grip the edge of his glass too tightly to pretend at ease.

“Control is a curious thing, isn’t it? We cling to it like a lifeline, yet it slips through our fingers the moment we need it most,” Hank said.

“Yeah, well... control's just another lie we tell ourselves so we don’t feel the cracks splittin’ open,” Logan said.

“And sometimes you just set everything on fire so no one sees the cracks at all,” Johnny said.

“Fascinating strategy, Johnny—though I suspect the ash you’re left with is less than satisfying when the flames die down,” Hank said.

The silence that lingers is heavy, crowded with unspoken admissions as the faint hum of the bar dulls into background static. A glass clinks faintly two booths over, settling on wood, while the bartender murmurs low to a half-dozing regular. Logan’s free hand, scarred and deliberate, traces the rim of his drink, the motion absent but weighted, as Johnny’s shoulders drop, a visible deflation beneath the effort to keep standing tall.

“Yeah, well... some of us live just fine in the ash,” Logan said.

“Maybe, but you ever think about what happens when all you've got left is smoke and you can’t even breathe?” Johnny asked.

“Smoke can suffocate as thoroughly as silence, my friend—both steal the air, just in different increments,” Hank said.

“Better to choke on smoke than drown in silence. At least smoke means somethin’ burned for a reason,” Logan said.

The overhead bulbs flicker once, shadows shifting imperceptibly across the table as the weight of Logan’s words hangs like a weight no one attempts to lift. Outside, the low hum of tires skimming wet asphalt bleeds into the muffled interior, a stark contrast to the stillness of the booth. Johnny swirls the last inch of bourbon in his glass, watching the liquid carve uneven paths against the stained edges before tipping it back, his throat tight as it goes down.

“And what if the fire wasn’t yours to start, but you still end up choking on it?” Johnny asked.

“Then, my dear Johnny, you’d have a moral conundrum—carry the blame for flames you never lit, or find a way to breathe in a world that insists on burning around you,” Hank said.

“Sometimes, don't matter who lit the match. Fire gets in your lungs all the same,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, try being the guy everyone expects to light the match on purpose—whether you want to or not,” Johnny said.

Johnny’s hand trembles faintly as he sets the empty glass down, the softened clink betraying the weight behind the motion. Logan’s gaze, steady and unflinching, locks onto the fine crack running through the table’s varnish, a fissure too small to fix but too deep to ignore. Hank exhales slowly, the lines around his mouth hardening as he watches them both, his glass untouched, the bourbon within stilled save for the faint ripple left by the shift of his grip.

“The burden of expectation can be as incendiary as the fire itself, Johnny. The trick, if there is one, lies in deciding whose breath fuels the flame—yours, or theirs,” Hank said.

“Expectations ain't a trick, Hank. They're just another shackle you let 'em snap on ya,” Logan said.

“Yeah, but sometimes you’re so used to the heat you don’t realize the shackles are melting you down too,” Johnny said.

“Or perhaps, gentlemen, we’re all just blacksmiths of our own chains—hammers in hand, convinced it’s someone else manning the forge,” Hank said.

The bartender wipes down the far end of the counter with slow, methodical strokes, the muted squeak of the rag just audible over the thinning murmur of the remaining patrons. Logan leans back slightly, the booth’s worn fabric straining against the bulk of his frame, his grip tightening on the empty glass he hasn’t bothered to refill. Johnny risks a glance at Hank, his brow furrowed, before looking away, his fingers tracing slow circles against the faint ring of condensation left behind on the table.

“Ain't much difference when the chains are already on. Doesn't matter who hammered 'em, you're still stuck,” Logan said.

“Stuck's one word for it. Feels more like you're welded into 'em, and every time you move, it tears a little more off you,” Johnny said.

“Perhaps, then, the question is not how to break free, but whether the parts we lose in the struggle are worth leaving behind,” Hank said.

“You lose enough parts, don’t matter if you're free. There ain't much left to walk away with,” Logan said.

The air within the booth stiffens, heavier now than even the smoke clinging in the rafters, each man’s silence a different weapon left unsheathed. Logan’s empty glass meets the table with a subdued thunk, his hand lingering on it, the tension in his fingers betraying a calculation he doesn’t share. Johnny pinches the bridge of his nose briefly, his jaw working silently before he lets out a breath that hangs unspent between them, while Hank, gaze lowered, absently straightens the edge of a coaster as though organizing the fragments of the moment.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the next move, where the room itself feels like it might sigh. The low hum of the bar presses around them, the muffled laughter from another corner too far removed to matter. Hank adjusts the coasters again, the gesture small and mechanical, while Logan’s gaze sharpens, his index finger idly tracing the rim of his empty glass.

“I’m simply suggesting, Logan, that instincts—while evolutionarily vital—are not inherently infallible. They, too, can misfire, particularly when untethered from rigorous thought,” Hank said.

“That's your problem, Hank. You think too much, tryin' to outsmart your gut. Instinct don't misfire—you do, when you don't listen,” Logan said.

“Sure, Logan, because riding every gut feeling has never landed anyone in a bar with a lifetime of regrets and a liver crying for mercy,” Johnny said.

“Ah, but Johnny, might I posit that it's not the instinct itself, but the heedless pursuit of it without temperance or foresight that truly fills bars and burdens livers?” Hank asked.

The overhead light flickers once, unnoticed, as Johnny leans back in his seat, the wood creaking in protest. He swirls the amber liquid in his glass with the restless energy of a man trying to outdrink his own thoughts. Across the booth, Hank’s fingertips drum against the tabletop, each tap a soft punctuation to his argument, while Logan exhales sharply, turning his gaze toward the bar as if searching for a simpler world beyond it.

“Both of you sound like you're tryin' to write the perfect damn instruction manual for livin'. Newsflash—there ain't one. You feel it, you do it, you deal with the wreckage after,” Logan said.

“Yeah, sure, Logan, 'cause who doesn’t love rebuilding the same wreckage over and over like it’s their favorite hobby?” Johnny asked.

“Repetition, Johnny, is not merely the hallmark of madness—it is also the essence of practice. Though, I must concede, one's liver makes for a rather unforgiving training ground,” Hank said.

“Practice all you want, Hank—doesn't change the fact that sometimes life just punches you square in the face, no manual, no prep. You swing back, or you don't,” Logan said.

Johnny tilts his glass to his lips, draining what’s left in a long, unhurried pull before setting it down with measured deliberation. The quiet thud deepens the pull of gravity in the space between them, anchoring the words that remain unsaid. Across the table, Hank halts his drumming, staring at his hands as if they might answer a question no one had voiced aloud.

“And what if swinging back just means hitting the same damn wall, over and over? Then what, Logan? Keep punching 'til your fists break, or figure out how to climb it?” Johnny asked.

“Climbing, Johnny, necessitates planning—a blueprint, if you will. Though, regrettably, brute force tends to appeal more in the moment, doesn't it?” Hank asked.

“Sometimes the wall ain't meant to be climbed. Sometimes you tear it down, brick by goddamn brick,” Logan said.

“Yeah, and sometimes the wall tears you down first,” Johnny said.

The booth falls into a lull, the kind that's broken only by the slip of glass against wood as a bartender places another round on the edge of the table. The faint clink of ice shifting in Johnny’s glass punctuates the space like a clock measuring the weight of their impasse. Logan lights a cigarette with a flick of his thumb, the brief flare of orange casting sharp relief over the tired lines of his face.

“And yet, gentlemen, neither demolition nor ascent addresses the question of why the wall exists at all. Perhaps, in our fervor, we overlook the virtue of simply walking around it,” Hank said.

“Yeah, well, you ever try walkin’ around a wall that stretches to the damn horizon? Sometimes the only way through is straight fuckin’ through,” Logan said.

“Or maybe you just sit down in front of it and hope it crumbles on its own, ‘cause hell, you’re too damn tired to swing anymore,” Johnny said.

“A precarious gamble, Johnny, relying on entropy as your sole ally. Still, I suppose fatigue does render even the most audacious plans irrelevant, doesn't it?” Hank asked.

The smoke from Logan’s cigarette coils upward, fading into the hazy dimness above their heads, as Johnny rubs a thumb along the damp ring left by his glass. Hank pulls a coaster from the corner of the table, his movements precise, almost ceremonious, and sets it beneath his untouched drink. Outside, a car backfires, the sharp crack echoing faintly through the walls, but none of them flinch; their battlefields are elsewhere tonight.

“Only thing you can count on crumblin' on its own is yourself. Wall ain't gonna move unless you damn well make it,” Logan said.

“Yeah, but what if you move it, and there’s just another wall waiting behind it?” Johnny asked.

“Then, Johnny, one must either redefine the purpose of the journey or procure a bulldozer,” Hank said.

“Or stop waitin' for the walls to make sense and just keep goin'. Doesn't matter what’s on the other side if you ain't there to see it,” Logan said.

Johnny leans forward, elbows on the table, his head dipping low as if the weight of Logan’s words pressed down on the back of his neck. The condensation on his fresh drink creeps down the glass, pooling at the base, unnoticed. Hank’s eyes track the movement, sharp and analytic even now, while Logan shakes ash from his cigarette onto an already crowded ashtray, the faintest tremble in his hand lost in the folds of smoke.

* * *

The bar light glints off the bottles behind the counter, their labels smeared in the dull amber haze of late-night solitude. Logan's fingers drum against the battered tabletop, the rhythm uneven, betraying an agitated calm he refuses to wear on his face. Across from him, Johnny shifts in his seat, the faint scrape of denim against vinyl filling the silence Hank has left for them, the words about entropy still hanging in the space between their breaths.

“And yet, despite our resistance, gentlemen, perhaps that's why we still gather—fractured symphonies though we may be,” Hank said.

“Or maybe we just don’t know how to stay away,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, some of us run toward the fire; some of us just sit in the ash,” Johnny said.

“And some of us, dear Johnny, analyze the ash—sift through it for patterns, hoping to divine meaning where others see only smoke,” Hank said.

The refrigerator hum deepens, an almost imperceptible shift in tone, as if the machinery is straining under the weight of the room's silence. Hank adjusts his glasses, the faint squeak of the frame breaking through, his calm at odds with the tension radiating from the other two. Logan’s cigarette, burned down to its filter, hisses faintly in the crowded ashtray, curling smoke rising toward the dim ceiling like the ghost of a thought unspoken.

“Sometimes smoke’s just smoke,” Logan said.

“Or sometimes it’s a signal, but you’re too damn stubborn to see it,” Johnny said.

“Or, perhaps, it's both—smoke as consequence, smoke as message. The trick, gentlemen, is discerning which it demands in the moment: a response or reflection,” Hank said.

“Not every fire’s worth puttin’ out,” Logan said.

Johnny leans back, the soft thud of his chair legs tipping onto the floor echoing in the lull. His fingers trace absent patterns in the condensation pooling on his glass, his eyes fixed somewhere in the middle distance, not on Logan, not on Hank—just away. Logan exhales through his nose, sharp and slow, as his hand flicks the dead stub of his cigarette into the overcrowded ashtray with a quiet finality.

“Funny, coming from the guy who’s been carrying around a five-alarm blaze his whole damn life,” Johnny said.

“Some flames, Johnny, are not extinguished so easily—they smolder, they reshape, and sometimes, they redefine the very landscape they consume,” Hank said.

“Sometimes, they just burn you down to nothin’,” Logan said.

“Then maybe it’s time to stop pretending you’re the ashes and start remembering you were the damn spark,” Johnny said.

The words hang sharp, carving through the air like the edge of shattered glass. Logan’s jaw tightens, the muscle in his cheek flickering once before stillness reclaims his face. Hank looks between them, his hand frozen halfway toward his glass, a silent witness to a fire contained—for now—within the four walls of this booth.

“Even the most stubborn embers, gentlemen, require the right breath to reignite—or the wisdom to let them rest,” Hank said.

“Not every spark’s worth fanning, Hank,” Logan said.

“Yeah, but some are worth risking the burn,” Johnny said.

“And therein lies the paradox, doesn't it? To discern when the risk transcends the dread of the blister,” Hank said.

The ice in Johnny’s drink shifts with a quiet crack, the sound impossibly loud in the stillness. Logan’s hand, curled into a loose fist on the table, flexes briefly, his knuckles catching the muted glow of the bar light before settling again. Hank’s steady gaze holds the space between them, sharp yet unreadable, as though he’s calculating equations neither of the others can see.

“Blisters heal. Some scars don’t,” Logan said.

“Maybe, but scars mean you made it through. Blisters just mean you hesitated too long,” Johnny said.

“Or perhaps, gentlemen, scars and blisters alike are merely the body's memoirs—evidence of where, and how, you chose to stand,” Hank said.

“Some stories ain’t worth tellin’, Hank,” Logan said.

The low rumble of distant laughter from the bar’s far end bleeds into the room, a reminder of life continuing outside their small, charged circle. Johnny’s fingers grip his glass now, the careless tracing forgotten, leaving faint smudges on the surface. Logan shifts in his seat, the creak of the wood breaking the rhythm of the refrigerator’s hum, but his gaze stays fixed, hard, on the tabletop as if willing the grain to split under his stare.

* * *

There is a moment, just before Logan speaks, when the bar seems to hold its breath. The hum of the refrigerator softens beneath the faint sway of wood beams settling. Across the table, Johnny’s grip on his glass tightens, while Hank leans back, the shadowed edge of his claws resting lightly against the booth’s cracked vinyl.

“Take your time, Logan. We're listening,” Hank said.

“Took my time already, Hank. You wanna hear it or not?” Logan asked.

“Yeah, we do,” Johnny said.

“Of course, Logan. Proceed,” Hank said.

The bartender moves silently behind them, his hands tracing the familiar path of cleaning an already polished glass. Logan exhales sharply, the sound cutting through the heavy air like a knife through sinew. Johnny shifts in his seat, his foot brushing against one of the warped floorboards, the faint scrape inconsequential yet grating against the stillness.

“It was Normandy. Beach soaked red before I even got there. Didn't know who I was fightin' for, just knew I wanted 'em dead,” Logan said.

“That… Jesus, Logan. What the hell do you even do with something like that?” Johnny asked.

“You carry it, Johnny. What else is there?” Hank asked.

“You carry it 'til it tears you apart. Then you keep goin',” Logan said.

The ice in Johnny’s glass shifts with a faint crack, the last remnants clinging together as the whiskey loses its chill. Hank lowers his gaze, his fingertips tightening briefly on the edge of the table, as though weighing the words in the air. Logan's hand rests on the scarred wood, fingers curled loosely, but the tension in his knuckles betrays the calm he outwardly projects.

“Yeah, but... does it ever stop feeling like it’s tearing? Or do you just get used to the pull?” Johnny asked.

“If you ever truly get used to it, Johnny, that might be the greater tragedy,” Hank said.

“It don’t stop. You just get stronger, or it breaks ya,” Logan said.

“Stronger, huh? Feels more like just putting another layer of duct tape on and hoping it holds,” Johnny said.

The bartender moves to the door, the soft click of the lock sliding into place barely registering over the low thrum of their conversation. Johnny swirls what’s left in his glass, its amber shadow spinning patterns against his hand. Hank exhales through his nose, a sound too measured to be a sigh, his gaze drifting upward to the bar’s dim, flickering light.

“Strength isn’t always elegance, Johnny. Sometimes it’s just not letting the cracks cave in,” Hank said.

“Or not givin’ a damn if they do. Just keep movin’,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, moving’s easy when you’re running. It’s the standing still that screws you,” Johnny said.

“Standing still forces you to look, to measure the wreckage. That’s the part we dread, isn’t it?” Hank asked.

The hum of the refrigerator stutters, a brief mechanical sigh before it resumes its rhythm. Logan’s eyes stay fixed on his hand, the faint scratch of his thumbnail dragging across the surface of the table almost drowned out by Johnny setting his glass down with a soft thud. Hank adjusts his posture, his broad frame settling deeper into the booth, as if anchoring himself against the weight of his own reflection.

“Standin’ still’s how you end up drownin’. Better t’ keep your head above the mess, don’t matter where it takes ya,” Logan said.

“Yeah, but what if the mess is all you got left to stand on?” Johnny asked.

“Then you build on it, Johnny. No matter how unstable, you find a way to lay a foundation,” Hank said.

“Foundations crack too. Ain’t no solid ground in this world, just learn to balance on the rubble,” Logan said.

The distant rattle of a passing truck vibrates through the walls, momentarily shifting the quiet in the room. Johnny picks at a worn thread on his sleeve, his fingers moving with a nervous precision that betrays the weight of Logan's words. Hank glances sideways at Logan, his mouth pulling into a faint, thoughtful line before his gaze drops again to the table.

* * *

There is a moment, just before Hank shifts in his seat, where all three of them are simply still—the heavy glasses on the table catching the dim bar light, Logan’s finger tracing the rim of his untouched water, Johnny’s foot tapping a quick, aimless rhythm beneath the table. In this quiet, the low hum of the jukebox competes with the occasional rattle of ice in a distant drink. McCready’s, worn at the edges but steadfast, holds them like a net not built to catch them, but to briefly stop their fall.

“Fascinating, isn’t it, how even in stillness we manage to carry the weight of motion?” Hank asked.

“Some of us ain't got the luxury of stillness, Hank. Always somethin' movin', even when it ain't supposed to,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, some of us keep moving 'cause if we stop, we might actually feel all the crap we've been running from,” Johnny said.

“And yet, Johnny, the running rarely outpaces the catching, does it?” Hank asked.

Logan leans back, the creak of the booth cutting through the quiet, his shoulders sinking deeper against the worn leather. Johnny rubs at the back of his neck, his gaze drifting upward to the slow-turning ceiling fan, as if the movement above might untangle what knots have tightened inside. Hank’s fingers tap the table’s edge, measured and rhythmic, though the faint furrow of his brow betrays the calculations forming behind his eyes.

“Doesn't matter how fast you run. Sooner or later, the crap finds ya. You just gotta decide how much of it you're willin' to let bury ya,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, maybe I like living like it can't catch me. Better than sitting around waiting for it to pile on,” Johnny said.

“Ah, the eternal optimist's wager—betting against inevitability for the sake of motion. A noble, if exhausting, endeavor,” Hank said.

“Ain't about nobility, Hank. It's about what breaks first—your legs or the weight,” Logan said.

The bartender wipes a glass methodically, the repetitive squeak of the cloth marking the seconds that pass between words at their table. Johnny finally drops his hand from his neck, letting it slap lightly against the wood as his other reaches for his drink with half-hearted intent. Logan picks at something invisible on the edge of his sleeve, his jaw tightening briefly before softening once more.

“It’s not the legs or the weight that breaks first with me—it's the damn silence,” Johnny said.

“The silence, my dear Johnny, is merely the amplifier of what we’d rather not hear,” Hank said.

“Sometimes the noise is worse. Depends on what you're drownin' out,” Logan said.

“Yeah, well, lately it feels like you can't win—you either face the noise or the quiet, and they both hit like a truck,” Johnny said.

The ice in Johnny's glass shifts as he tilts it absently, sending a faint clink into the mix of jukebox hum and bar murmurs. Hank exhales through his nose, the sound almost lost in the ambient din, as his hands fold neatly in front of him, a stillness at odds with the tension threading through the room. Logan's gaze lingers on the table surface, his fingers pulling at a seam in the wood—small, repetitive movements that bleed off just enough energy to keep the storm inside contained.

“It would seem, my friends, that the human—or mutant—condition, is less about escaping the crash and more about learning which trucks we can bear to let hit us,” Hank said.

“Some trucks, Hank, you don’t get a choice with. You just stand there, and take the hit,” Logan said.

“Yeah, but if I gotta take the hit, I’d rather go out blazing than just standing there waiting for it,” Johnny said.

“A flaming defense, as it were. Though I’d advise against underestimating the value of strategy, even in the face of inevitability,” Hank said.

Hank adjusts his glasses with a deliberate precision, their lenses catching the gleam of a neon sign flickering intermittently above the bar. Logan shifts his weight forward, his knotted hands braced on the table, the movement slow and heavy, like a man measuring every inch of his reserves. Johnny takes a longer sip of his drink this time, the swallow audible in the pause that follows Hank’s words, his leg resuming its restless tapping beneath the booth.

“Blazin' or not, doesn’t matter to the truck. It’s still gonna roll right over you,” Logan said.

“Maybe, but at least the truck knows I was there. Some marks are worth leaving, even if they burn out quick,” Johnny said.

“A paradoxical comfort, leaving a mark on something that will only flatten you in return. Perhaps persistence is its own quiet rebellion against inevitability,” Hank said.

“Depends on what kind of mark you're leavin'. Some scars last longer than the burn,” Logan said.

The haze of cigarette smoke from a nearby table drifts lazily into their orbit, curling around the dull overhead light and dulling its reach. Johnny exhales sharply through his nose, the edge of a lopsided smirk tugging at his face as his fingertip traces an invisible pattern in the condensation on his glass. Logan’s water shifts slightly with the tap of his forearm against the table, the surface tension rippling in time with the low rumble of his voice, though his gaze stays anchored somewhere between his hands.

* * *

The bartender moves with the practiced quiet of someone who’s seen every version of a night like this, his towel sweeping the countertop in lazy circles. At the trio’s table, Hank’s thumb stalls mid-shuffle, a momentary stillness overtaking the restless drift of coasters in his hands. The amber half-light catches in their glasses, tracing the fine lines on Logan’s knuckles, the condensation slipping slowly down Johnny’s untouched drink.

“Resilience, gentlemen, isn't merely survival—it's the art of bending without breaking. A distinction, but one vital, wouldn't you say?” Hank asked.

“Bending’s overrated. Break, heal, keep goin'. That’s the trick,” Logan said.

“To keeping the wheels on, even when everything's shaking loose,” Johnny said.

“A fine metaphor, Johnny, though I might argue the axles bear their own strain. Still, a toast—however one frames it—is warranted,” Hank said.

Logan shifts forward, the subtle creak of the bench beneath him cutting through the low hum of the room. Glass meets glass with a muted clink, the sound barely rising above the scrape of a chair being pulled out at another table. Somewhere near the jukebox, coins rattle into place, but no song comes, leaving their small corner of the bar steeped in its uneven quiet.

“To keepin' goin'. Nothing prettier than that,” Logan said.

“I'll drink to that,” Johnny said.

“To the unpolished poetry of persistence, then. Cheers,” Hank said.

“Drink before it gets warm, boys,” Logan said.

The rim of Logan’s glass tips briefly to his lips, his swallow measured and unhurried as though the taste were secondary to the ritual. Johnny turns the glass in his hand first, eyeing the faint circles left by its base on the table before taking his own sip. Hank, ever deliberate, raises his drink without fanfare, setting it down with a barely audible thud as the bartender places the folded check at the table's edge.

“Guess that’s the cue. Time to settle up and keep the poetry rolling somewhere else,” Johnny said.

“An ode to closing time, then. Let us not leave our unpaid verse behind,” Hank said.

“Payin’ the tab’s the easy part. Leavin’ the weight behind—that’s somethin’ else,” Logan said.

“Then let’s leave what we can here and take the light stuff with us,” Johnny said.

The check lies untouched for a moment, the edges curled slightly from the warmth of the bartender’s palm. Logan pulls a crumpled bill from his pocket and smooths it flat against the worn table with the heel of his hand. Johnny leans back, one hand running through his hair as his other casually pushes a few folded notes toward the center of the table, their movement barely disturbing the coasters beneath.

“Weight, light, and currency—what a peculiar exchange we navigate. Allow me to contribute my share to this collective burden,” Hank said.

“Less talk, more pay. Let’s move,” Logan said.

“Alright, alright, big guy. Don’t get your claws in a twist,” Johnny said.

“Efficiency, Logan, is the soul of brevity—as you so eloquently remind us. Done and done,” Hank said.

Hank places his share down with enviable precision, the bills stacked neatly atop Logan’s rumpled offering. The bartender watches from his corner of the bar, the faintest nod acknowledging the pile growing methodically on the check. Outside, the muffled wail of a distant siren threads its way through the worn wooden walls, barely interrupting the rhythm of their departure.

“Let’s get outta here before the world decides to drag us back in,” Logan said.

“Lead the way—I’ll try not to set anything on fire on the way out. Key word: try,” Johnny said.

“A modest request, Johnny, though one I suspect might challenge your innate flair for dramatics. Shall we?” Hank asked.

“Let’s go, before I start regrettin' this whole night,” Logan said.

Chairs scrape against the scuffed floor as they rise, the sound sharp and ungraceful in the settled quiet of the nearly empty bar. Logan’s jacket falls into place over his shoulders with a practiced shrug, its weight a familiar one. Johnny lingers for a beat, fingers skimming the rim of his empty glass, before following them toward the door, Hank pausing only to glance at what remains on the table as if cataloging what they've left behind.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the three scatter, where the room seems to exhale. The whiskey-soaked air hangs heavy, the clatter of distant glasses a faint reminder of life outside their small orbit. Logan's boots strike the floor with finality as he moves, Johnny's fingers tapping soundlessly on the bar’s edge, and Hank adjusts his glasses with a precision that feels more ritual than need.

“Gentlemen, if this evening has proven anything, it is that even the most disparate elements can catalyze something...unexpectedly stable. Shall we call it a tentative triumph of chemistry?” Hank asked.

“Coulda just said "we didn’t kill each other," Hank,” Logan said.

“Guess that means we passed the lab test, huh? Always knew I was more explosive than volatile,” Johnny said.

“Indeed, Mr. Storm, though I suspect your combustibility is less a variable and more a constant in this equation. Controlled chaos has its...charms, when properly harnessed,” Hank said.

Logan tosses a few crumpled bills on the counter without counting, the gesture brisk and dismissive, as though settling a debt he never agreed to owe. Johnny straightens, his movements languid yet restless, residual energy sparking in the faint crack of his knuckles. Hank, however, lingers, his eyes scanning the bar’s worn surfaces one last time as though memorizing the imperfections for later contemplation.

“You two talk too damn much,” Logan said.

“Someone's gotta keep the conversation interesting while you brood impressively in the corner, Logan,” Johnny said.

“If brevity is the soul of wit, then Logan, your silence must surely border on Shakespearean genius,” Hank said.

“Keep flappin’ your gums, Hank. One of these days, I’ll start sharin’ my opinions. You won’t like it,” Logan said.

The bartender glances over, inconspicuous in his timing, wiping the same glass with practiced indifference as the tension ebbs faintly into something resembling camaraderie. Logan adjusts his collar against the imagined chill outside, a small, habitual tug. Johnny leans back briefly, his jaw tightening as if bracing for quips that never quite land the way they should. Hank reaches for his coat, his movements deliberate, the weight of the lab waiting for him unmistakable in the careful way he folds it over his arm.

“If today's been any proof, Logan, your "opinions" might actually spice it up around here. Thrilling thought,” Johnny said.

“Ah, yes, nothing quite enlivens a discourse like a monosyllabic growl punctuated with the occasional cigar. Truly, the rhetoric of the discerning woodsman,” Hank said.

“Alright, that’s it. I’m leavin' before I toss you both into traffic,” Logan said.

“I'll take that as a heartfelt goodbye, Logan. Don’t stay a stranger—or do, your choice,” Johnny said.

Logan turns on his heel, the groan of his heavy boots cutting through the quiet like gravel under a tire. The bartender watches him leave with the faintest arch of an eyebrow, shifting to set the spotless glass aside. Behind him, Johnny drums a rhythmic beat on the edge of the bar, the sound sharp and hollow against the polished wood, while Hank methodically adjusts his cuffs, grounding himself in the familiar precision of small habits.

“A most poetic exit, laden with gruff charm and existential threat. Do give my regards to the pavement, should we meet again under such circumstances,” Hank said.

“Don’t tempt me, Hank,” Logan said.

“Next time, let’s skip the threats and just go straight to the whiskey. Worked out fine tonight, didn’t it?” Johnny asked.

“Indeed, Mr. Storm, though I suspect the whiskey was not merely a lubricant for conviviality but a salve for...well, let us diplomatically call them "fractured egos,” Hank said.

Johnny smirks, his fingers halting their percussion as he reaches for his empty glass, turning it idly as though searching for meaning in its smooth, transparent walls. Hank exhales through his nose, the faintest ripple of amusement darkened by something weightier he does not share, his posture straightening almost reflexively. The bartender, familiar with these half-finished conversations, moves quietly past them, his hands busy but his presence not unnoticed.

“Whiskey’s the only thing keepin’ me from breakin’ those egos in half,” Logan said.

“And here I thought the claws were just for show,” Johnny said.

“Ah, but Mr. Logan’s claws are dual-purpose, Mr. Storm—implement and implication. One need not unsheathe to make a point, although he certainly excels at both,” Hank said.

“You wanna find out firsthand, Hank?” Logan asked.

The tension balances in the air, sharp-edged but softened by the exhaustion behind it, none of them fully committed to the bite their words imply. Johnny tilts his glass back and forth in a slow, distracted rhythm, the movement catching the dim light as his grin lingers. Hank does not look up, his focus fixed on folding his scarf into neat thirds, the deliberate care of the action making his earlier flippancy feel almost hollow in retrospect. The bartender pulls a stool onto the counter in the far corner, a quiet signal that the night is drawing to its inevitable close.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the room empties, when the weight of the night lingers in the stale air. The sound of glass being racked and the soft scuff of boots on worn wood are all that remain, each movement unhurried, as though stretching the final moments before departure. The bartender glances at the three men, the briefest flicker of a movement—not quite expectation, not quite indifference—before returning to his slow, practiced motions.

“Gentlemen, while the night may hold answers we dared not ask, I find myself inclined to leave those answers where they lie. Safe travels,” Hank said.

“You always did talk too much, Hank. Safe travels,” Logan said.

“Don't let the door hit you, furry Shakespeare. Stay warm, Logan—try not to scare too many kids with that face tonight,” Johnny said.

“I daresay, Johnny, the frost will pose less of a challenge than your wit. Goodnight, both of you,” Hank said.

Hank's chair slides back with a controlled scrape, the sound cutting clean through the bar's settling quiet. He stands, the scarf finally looped once with meticulous precision, shoulders squared as if bracing against what waits beyond the door. The air shivers faintly in his absence as he strides toward the exit, a man leaving behind more than he carries.

“Stay flamin’, kid. Don’t let the night snuff you out,” Logan said.

“Always, old man. Try not to claw anyone who doesn’t deserve it,” Johnny said.

“And try not to set fire to anything you can’t charm your way out of, Johnny. Logan... stay out of trouble, or at least share the story later,” Hank said.

“No promises, Hank. Trouble always finds me first,” Logan said.

Logan pats his jacket pocket, the crinkle of cellophane and the faint metallic rattle signaling a search easily satisfied. He pulls the cigar out slow, rolling it between two fingers as though fending off some decision not yet made. Johnny finally drains his glass, the empty clink on the table sharp against the mounting stillness, his gaze flicking toward the door Hank left through before dropping back to his own reflection in the warped surface of the wood.

“Guess trouble likes the smell of cigars and bad decisions,” Johnny said.

“Or perhaps, Johnny, trouble simply has a penchant for the predictable. Goodnight, gentlemen,” Hank said.

“Till next time, Hank. Don’t go soft in that lab of yours,” Logan said.

“Go easy, Hank. Someone’s gotta keep us looking smarter than we are,” Johnny said.

Logan bites down on the unlit cigar, the corner of his mouth twitching almost imperceptibly as he stands. His chair protests with a dull groan as he shoves it back, leaving a scuffed arc on the worn floor beneath. Across the table, Johnny adjusts his collar against the creeping cold from the door Hank left open just long enough to let the night slip in.

“I’ll leave the warmth to you two—whether fire or fury, you wear it well enough,” Hank said.

“Yeah, well, don’t freeze, Hank. Brain’s no good if it’s ice,” Logan said.

“Careful, Hank. Too much time alone in that lab, and you’ll be inventing friends,” Johnny said.

“Better to invent them than to set them ablaze, I’d wager. Take care, both of you,” Hank said.

The door swings shut behind Hank with a muted thud, sealing the room in the stale quiet of a last-call lull. Logan strikes a match with a deft flick, the sharp hiss flaring against the dead air as the cigar tip smolders to life. Johnny leans back, letting the chair's front legs lift slightly, his fingers tapping a rhythm against the edge of the table that neither distracts nor soothes.

“You sittin’ still or just waitin' for that chair to snap on you?” Logan asked.

“Keeps things exciting, doesn’t it? Life’s no fun if you’re not a little off balance,” Johnny said.

“I suppose it’s a philosophy, albeit a precarious one. Goodnight, gentlemen,” Hank said.

“Precarious fits him just fine, Hank. Goodnight,” Logan said.

The room contracts around their silence, the hum of the overhead lights growing louder as if filling the gaps left by their fading conversation. Logan exhales a plume of cigar smoke that twists upward, curling briefly in the dim light before dissolving into the static air. Johnny’s chair creaks as he rocks forward, the front legs landing with a solid thunk that mirrors the inevitability of the moment’s end.
