Grizzly Knights

Episode 1: Before Anyone Noticed

Wednesday afternoon at the Cheesecake Factory. No mission. No reason. Nobody knows they're friends. Two people who found each other outside the binary, splitting fries and not explaining themselves to anyone.

* * *

The table was cluttered with half-squeezed lime wedges and the condensation rings of two margarita glasses. Felicia's nails, sharp and glossed in black, tapped aimlessly against her glass as she leaned back into the booth, one leg crossed over the other. Wade’s straw made a wet, rasping noise as he slurped the last dregs of his mango abomination, unbothered by the sideways glance she shot his way.

“You slurp like you're auditioning for the adult film awards—what is that, mango syrup and regret?” Felicia asked.

“Takes one to know one, kitty-cat. And yeah, it’s mango syrup and regret—it pairs nicely with my crippling self-awareness. Want a sip? Or are you too classy for frozen sins?” Wade asked.

“I’ll stick with tequila that doesn’t come with a brain freeze, thanks. But I admire your commitment to ruining both your dignity and your taste buds in one go,” Felicia said.

“Dignity’s overrated, and my taste buds took off years ago—probably with my sense of shame. But hey, at least I’m not pretending tequila fixes bad decisions. It's just fuel for better ones. Speaking of which, Peter shirtless—talk to me. Two thumbs up, or Triple X rating?” Wade asked.

Felicia raised an eyebrow and reached for the salt rim of her glass, dragging a fingertip through the crystals before licking it clean. Wade leaned forward, elbows on the table, his mask pulled just high enough to show the kind of grin that dared you to take the bait. Outside the window, the afternoon sun caught on the smudge of fingerprints, streaking the view of a half-full parking lot.

“Two thumbs up, and then some—especially when he’s oblivious about it, which is most of the time. But Triple X? Please, Wade. That boy’s still PG-13 until someone else takes the wheel,” Felicia said.

“PG-13? Sweetheart, with me in the passenger seat, that boy's diving headfirst into the unrated director’s cut. And don’t act like you wouldn’t pay to watch that trainwreck,” Wade said.

“Oh, I’d pay—but only so I could have front-row seats to the inevitable moment Spider-Boy realizes exactly whose hands are on the wheel and loses control of his oh-so-pure hero complex,” Felicia said.

“And that, my dear Felicia, is when I lean in, whisper something unspeakable in his ear, and watch his mask’s lenses fog up faster than a teenager finding their first Playboy under the bed,” Wade said.

Felicia let out a slow, indulgent exhale through her nose, shoulders shaking just enough to send a subtle ripple through the chain resting against her collarbone. Wade punctuated his performance with a lazy swirl of the straw in his empty glass, the mango sludge at the bottom briefly parting in a spiral. The server approached with a practiced, cautious nod, carrying a basket of breadsticks that landed in the tight space between their drinks.

“Wade, you’re a menace—and I hope you know you just made our server reconsider their career choices. Pass me a breadstick before you corrupt those too,” Felicia said.

“I’m not corrupting the breadsticks, Felicia—they’re already filthy. Look at them, all glistening and naked, begging for it. Now, tell me, do you want the long, thick one or the girthy stub?” Wade asked.

“Surprise me, Wade. I trust your... refined expertise in breadstick anatomy,” Felicia said.

“Ah, excellent choice. I’ll go with the one that screams “overachiever with daddy issues” and pairs well with your tequila.” Wade said.

Wade grabbed a breadstick with a dramatic flourish, holding it up like an exhibit before setting it on Felicia’s napkin with a mock bow. She rolled her eyes, snapping it in half with a decisive crack that echoed faintly beneath the hum of the restaurant’s midday crowd. The faint scent of butter and garlic mingled with the sharp tang of lime lingering on the table.

“You really do have a gift for projecting your neuroses on innocent carbs, Wade. Should we start naming them too, or is this where we draw the line?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, we’re naming them, alright. Meet Sir Breadington of Carbshire, noble knight of the table—destined to be devoured by a femme fatale with excellently questionable taste in companions,” Wade said.

Felicia smirked as she dipped one half of the dismantled breadstick into the shallow pool of warm marinara sauce, her movements slow and deliberate. Wade, undeterred, balanced his own breadstick vertically against his finger, letting it wobble like some precarious tower before biting the end off with an exaggerated snap. The server hovered a few tables away, clearly debating whether it was worth coming back yet.

* * *

The server delivered the plates with the perfunctory speed of someone managing the Wednesday afternoon lull, sliding the avocado egg rolls and the fried mac and cheese balls onto the table first, followed by the chicken parm with its side of fries. Felicia took a slow sip of her margarita as Wade immediately tore into one of the mac and cheese balls, its molten center stringing out onto his fork like a dare. The air smelled heavy with oil and garlic.

“Did you even taste that, Wade, or are you just trying to prove a point to the food?” Felicia asked.

“I tasted it, Felicia. Tasted like victory and third-degree burns. You want one, or are you just here to criticize my intimate relationship with cheese?” Wade asked.

“I’m here to criticize, obviously. But you know what? Pass one over—I might as well see what all the molten drama is about,” Felicia said.

“Careful, darling. It's like biting into the sun if the sun was made entirely out of regret and lactose. Enjoy responsibly—or recklessly, your call,” Wade said.

Felicia speared a mac and cheese ball with her fork, the crispy shell cracking open to reveal a steaming, golden center. She paused for a second, letting it cool just enough before taking a cautious bite. The stretch of cheese lingered between her lips and the fork, snapped, then disappeared as she chewed thoughtfully. Wade leaned back, watching her like a man waiting for a verdict he already knew.

“Alright, I’ll admit it—regret and lactose have their charms. But don’t let it go to your head, Wilson; I’m not ready to serenade a cheese ball just yet,” Felicia said.

“No serenades? Damn, and I was just about to pull out the tiny violin. Next time, we’ll get the singing mozzarella sticks, really step up the romance,” Wade said.

“If this is your idea of romance, Wade, I think I understand why you’re still single. Stick to cheese and regret—it suits you,” Felicia said.

“Suits me? Please, regret is my custom-tailored tux, and cheese is the boutonniere. But hey—don't knock it 'til you've eaten dessert with me. Cheesecake, party of two?” Wade asked.

Felicia arched an eyebrow but reached for an avocado egg roll, dipping it into the cilantro-lime sauce without looking at Wade. She bit into it cleanly, her eyes scanning the room as she chewed, casually ignoring his theatrics. The background hum of silverware and soft conversations softened the sharpness of the exchange, leaving just the clink of her fork against the plate as punctuation.

“Dessert with you? Tempting, but I think I’d need more tequila—and maybe a signed death waiver,” Felicia said.

“Tequila, I can do. Death waiver? Sweetheart, I am the death waiver. Sign here with your avocado-smeared finger, and let's live dangerously,” Wade said.

“Danger’s already my middle name, Wade, but thanks for the offer. I’ll stick to tequila and plausible deniability for now,” Felicia said.

“Plausible deniability? That’s cute. Because nothing screams low profile like margaritas with the guy in the red onesie who talks to himself,” Wade said.

Felicia smirked, sliding her margarita glass closer and tilting it to catch the condensation with her fingertips. Wade popped a fry into his mouth without breaking eye contact, chewing lazily as though her amusement was its own reward. The ice in her glass shifted as she took a long sip, the faint rattle blending into the cluttered orchestra of the restaurant.

“Well, Wade, subtlety was never exactly your color. Guess I’ve got a thing for lost causes—and questionable life choices,” Felicia said.

“Lost causes? Baby, I’m the patron saint of those. Stick with me, and we’ll redefine questionable—starting with whatever’s left on this table,” Wade said.

Wade gestured broadly to the spread between them, snatching one of the last fries and dunking it recklessly in Felicia's cilantro-lime sauce. She responded by flicking a crumb from her avocado egg roll in his direction, landing it square on the edge of his plate. The corner of her mouth curled again, a silent declaration that if this was a competition, she hadn't lost yet.

* * *

The plate rim showed the slick residue of marinara, a drooping parsley garnish abandoned at its edge. Felicia swirled the last half-inch of her vodka soda absently, the ice clicking against the glass. Wade leaned back, drumming two fingers on the tabletop, the rhythm syncopated, just shy of irritating.

“You keep that up, Wilson, and I’ll have to assume it’s Morse code for "annoy her until she stabs me with a breadstick,” Felicia said.

“Finally, someone who gets me. Though I'd prefer the breadstick to the face—less clean-up, more carbs,” Wade said.

“Noted. I’ll aim for your good side—oh, wait, do you even have one?” Felicia asked.

“Depends on your angle. The left side’s got personality, but the right? That’s where the magic happens,” Wade said.

The waitress passed by without stopping, a glance at Wade’s empty glass and then at the bar, where the frantic shake of a tin meant nobody was coming soon. Felicia shifted, propping her chin on her knuckles, studying him with the kind of stillness that makes people talk too much. The straw in her drink tilted ominously, forgotten.

“Magic, huh? I’d ask for a demonstration, but I’m fresh out of rabbits and second chances,” Felicia said.

“Good thing I specialize in pulling disasters out of thin air. You want spectacle or just the usual emotional carnage?” Wade asked.

“Surprise me, Wade. Though I’m betting your "usual" comes with way more explosions than I’m dressed for,” Felicia said.

“Please, Felicia, you’re always dressed for explosions. You just call them "accessories,” Wade said.

The corner of Felicia’s mouth twitched, and she reached for her glass, the ice rattling as she swirled it one more time before draining the last sip. Wade shifted forward, elbows on the table now, leaning into her space—not enough to crowd her, just enough to underline the grin playing at his lips. Somewhere across the room, a waiter dropped a fork, the sharp clang breaking briefly through the low din of chatter and clinking silverware.

“You know, Wade, for someone who insists on being chaos incarnate, you sure do have a talent for organized sabotage. It’s almost... charming. Almost,” Felicia said.

“Charming? Careful, Felicia—flattery like that and I might start thinking you’re softening up. Or plotting something, which, let’s face it, is just as hot,” Wade said.

“Softening up? Not a chance. Plotting, though? Always. Keeps things... interesting,” Felicia said.

“Dangerous and interesting—my two favorite things. Just make sure I’m not the one tied to the train tracks when your next act drops. Or do. I like a challenge,” Wade said.

Felicia tapped her nails against the side of her empty glass, the rhythm slow, deliberate. Wade’s grin widened, teeth catching the muted light from the overhead fixtures, his fingers idly peeling the corner of a napkin. The faint smell of something fried lingered in the air, mixing with the sharper tang of spilled tequila from the nearby bar.

“Oh, don’t worry, Wade. If I tie you to the tracks, I’ll make sure the train’s running a little late. I’m not completely heartless,” Felicia said.

“A late train? How considerate. Gives me just enough time to monologue my way out of it—or, you know, write you into the script as the one pulling me off the tracks last second. Plot twist, you do care,” Wade said.

Felicia leaned back against the booth, one hand smoothing the edge of the table as if brushing away invisible crumbs. Wade’s napkin tearing slowed, his focus shifting to the glint in her eye that didn’t match her casual posture. The hum of a blender from the bar sliced through the room, drowning out the distant chime of someone’s laughter.

* * *

Felicia cupped her hand around the tip of the cigarette as the lighter sparked, the flicker catching the soft sheen of her nail polish. She took a sharp drag, shoulders lifting with the rhythm, exhaled out of the corner of her mouth without looking at him. Wade rocked back on his heels, thumbs hooked in his belt loops, grin stuck somewhere between charm and teeth.

“You keep staring at me like that, Wade, and I’m gonna start charging,” Felicia said.

“Oh, please do—finally, a bill I'd happily get behind… or under, your choice,” Wade said.

“Careful, sweetheart, I charge double for flattery that transparent,” Felicia said.

“Worth every penny—besides, I’m already emotionally bankrupt, so what’s a little financial ruin between friends?” Wade asked.

The cigarette dangled from Felicia's fingers as she tilted her head, letting smoke curl in lazy streams toward the fading afternoon light. Wade shifted closer, a half step inside her orbit, the sour-sweet tang of lingering whiskey and cologne moving with him. The sidewalk stayed busy, footsteps passing without interruption, but neither seemed to notice.

“Bankrupt? Sweetheart, you couldn’t handle the interest rate I’d slap on your kind of chaos,” Felicia said.

“Try me—I've already got a platinum card in bad decisions, and I’m dying to see if you max me out,” Wade said.

“Oh, Wade, honey, I don’t max people out—I leave them wondering what else they could’ve had,” Felicia said.

“That's cute—leave 'em wanting more, huh? Don’t worry, I’m all about the sequel anyway,” Wade said.

Felicia stubbed the cigarette against the metal trash can, the faint hiss lost under the hum of passing cars. She flicked the butt in, her wrist snapping lightly, and finally turned to face him fully. Wade’s grin widened, the kind of smile that dared her to move first.

“Sequel? Sweetheart, you should be more concerned about surviving the first act,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I always make it past the first act—hell, I’ve got rewrites and deleted scenes waiting in the wings. You wanna direct, or should we just… improvise?” Wade asked.

“Improvise, obviously—scripts are so boring, and I’ve never been one for sticking to the rules,” Felicia said.

“Good, 'cause I’ve never seen a rule I didn’t wanna break over my knee—preferably while making uncomfortable eye contact with the cameraman,” Wade said.

Felicia let out a sharp laugh, quick and unrestrained, before biting down on her lower lip to stifle the rest. She adjusted the strap of her bag on her shoulder, the sharp metallic clink of its chain cutting through the ambient chatter. Wade leaned in slightly, like a tide drawn toward the shore, his eyes fixed fast on hers.

“Careful, Wade—look at me like that too long, and you might just drown,” Felicia said.

“Drown? Sweetheart, I’m the guy who cannonballs into the deep end just to see how much the lifeguard can take,” Wade said.

Felicia tilted her head, letting a slow, deliberate smirk spread as she shifted her weight onto one hip. The breeze moved a loose strand of hair across her face, and she flicked it aside with a quick sweep of her fingers. Wade’s hands fidgeted briefly at his sides, restless energy coiled tight, before settling back into his belt loops.

* * *

Felicia set the glasses down on the low iron table between them, the sharp clink absorbed by the open air. She twisted the cap off the Don Julio with a practiced hand, the faint crack of the seal breaking nearly lost under the hum of distant traffic and the occasional lash of wind against the building. Wade leaned against the rooftop ledge, thumbs hooked lightly in his pockets, watching her hands instead of the skyline.

“You gonna stare all night, Wade, or do you want a drink to go with that view?” Felicia asked.

“Depends. Does the drink come with a side of "mysterious rooftop invitations mean I'm special" sauce, or is it just chips and guac for the soul tonight?” Wade asked.

“If you're waiting for me to coo about how special you are, Wade, you might want to pace yourself with the tequila. But... maybe tonight's menu has a little surprise pairing,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I do love a good surprise pairing—like chimichangas with existential dread. Pour me one, Kitty, let's see what you've got cooking,” Wade said.

Felicia poured two fingers into each glass, the liquid catching the last of the sun like molten amber. She slid one across the table toward him, her nails dragging briefly over the metal surface. Wade straightened up just enough to reach for it, the faintest squeak of his glove against the glass as he took it.

“To surprises, then. And whatever trouble they bring,” Felicia said.

“To surprises—and the glorious chaos that makes them worth it. Cheers to bad decisions with great company,” Wade said.

“I’ll drink to that—though I reserve the right to redefine “bad decision” on a whim. Cheers, Wade.” Felicia said.

“Cheers, Felicia—though I gotta say, if redefining bad decisions was an Olympic sport, I'd have a gold medal and a concussion from the podium dive,” Wade said.

Their glasses met with a quiet, deliberate clink, sharp against the open evening air. Felicia leaned back in her chair, the faint curve of her smile catching at the edge of her glass before she took a sip. Wade stayed standing, rocking once on the balls of his feet, tequila untouched as he turned to glance across the skyline.

“Careful, Wade. If you start brooding at the skyline, you're dangerously close to stepping on someone else's shtick,” Felicia said.

“Brooding? Nah, I don't do brooding, babe. I do dramatic gazes with a side of "shit, did I leave the stove on?” Wade asked.

“Well, if your stove’s anything like you, it’s probably burning something down by now,” Felicia said.

“Only thing I'll burn down tonight is my dignity, with a shot of tequila and maybe a poor life choice, present company excluded... or not—dealer's choice,” Wade said.

Felicia set her glass down with a soft tap, the sound swallowed by distance and wind. Her eyes lingered on Wade, sharp in that sunset haze, before she tilted her head just enough to catch the breeze in her hair. He finally lifted his glass, swirling the pale amber liquid once, the faintest twitch of a grin settling at the corner of his mouth.

“Careful, Wade, you keep talking like that and I might start to think you’re angling for a real gamble tonight. Hope you’re a good player,” Felicia said.

“Gamble? Sweetheart, life dealt me a deck full of jokers and I still bet the house. Let’s see who folds first,” Wade said.

The sun dipped lower, turning the rooftop into a patchwork of gold and shadow. Felicia leaned forward slightly, elbow on the arm of her chair, fingers idly tracing circles around the rim of her glass. Wade downed his tequila in one smooth motion, the empty glass catching the light before he set it back on the table with a precise, unhurried gesture.

* * *

The bottle sat between them, its label peeling slightly from the damp of their hands. Wade leaned back, palms against the rough rooftop edge, the tilt of his head exposing the raw, uneven skin of his neck to the sky's final light. Felicia adjusted her position, crossing one leg over the other, her boot catching the faint gleam of a streetlamp flickering on below.

“You know, if you fall off, I’m not hauling your ass back up—those boots are vintage,” Felicia said.

“That’s why I’m aiming for the garbage truck, sweetheart. Soft landing, and the smell matches my aesthetic,” Wade said.

“Just make sure you stick the landing—bad form is such a turn-off,” Felicia said.

“Guess I’ll have to nail the triple flip dismount, then—nothing less for my audience of one, right?” Wade asked.

Wade shifted his weight forward, his boots scraping faintly against the brick lip of the roof. Felicia turned the bottle in her hands, watching the amber liquid slosh and settle. The breeze tugged a strand of her hair across her mouth, and she didn’t bother moving it.

“Careful, Wade. You're dangerously close to being impressive, and I’m not sure I can handle the whiplash,” Felicia said.

“Don’t worry, kitten, I’ll dial it back to mildly endearing—wouldn’t want to set the bar too high for myself,” Wade said.

“Mildly endearing? Oh, sweetheart, that ship sailed the moment you climbed up here uninvited with tequila,” Felicia said.

“Hey, tequila's like a universal key—it gets me into places I’m not technically supposed to be and makes people glad I showed up,” Wade said.

The streetlights below buzzed faintly, their glow softening the sharp edges of shadows that stretched long across the rooftop. Wade absently tapped his fingertips against the brick, the rhythm uneven but deliberate. Felicia tilted the bottle again, studying the way the light fractured through the glass onto the gravel at her feet.

“Flattery and tequila, huh? Bold strategy, Wilson. Lucky for you, I’m a sucker for good timing,” Felicia said.

“Timing’s my specialty—well, that and regenerating. But let’s be honest, it’s the tequila that’s working overtime here,” Wade said.

“Don’t sell yourself short, darling. The tequila’s good, but the company’s not half bad either. For now, anyway,” Felicia said.

“High praise coming from you, Felicia. I'll try not to ruin it by opening my mouth… oh wait, too late,” Wade said.

Wade smirked, the scars on his face deepening in the low light as he reached for the bottle between them. Felicia shifted her weight, leaning back on one arm, the worn leather of her jacket creaking softly. The air between them was cool now, carrying the faint metallic tang of the city below.

“Don’t worry, I’ve got a high tolerance—for tequila, bad jokes, and questionable choices. You’re ticking all the boxes tonight,” Felicia said.

“What can I say? I’m an overachiever—except when it comes to self-preservation and tasteful innuendos,” Wade said.

Felicia arched an eyebrow, the corner of her mouth curling into a smirk as she lifted her glass for another slow sip. Wade spun the bottle cap in his fingers, the faint metallic scrape breaking the quiet. Somewhere below, a car horn barked, sharp and incongruent against the rooftop calm.
