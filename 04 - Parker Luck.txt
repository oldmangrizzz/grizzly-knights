Grizzly Knights

Episode 4: Parker Luck

Peter, MJ, and Felicia in the same orbit for one afternoon. MJ sees everything. Felicia sees everything MJ sees. Peter is the only one who does not notice what is happening.

* * *

The door chimes as Peter steps in, a gust of late autumn air folding behind him. His hoodie is slightly damp at the shoulders, and his eyes scan the room too fast before locking on MJ at the corner table. Her coffee is half-gone, fingers curled lightly around the mug; her expression keeps steady, but her foot jiggles under the table.

“Hey, uh... sorry for the dramatic entrance. Subway wasn't feeling very... swingy today. Mind if I—?” Peter asked.

“You’re fifteen minutes late, Peter. Sit down before you try to charm your way out of it,” Mary said.

“Right, of course, yeah, sitting—charm's off, promise. Late's my new cologne, apparently,” Peter said.

“Smells a lot like trouble and bad decisions. You sure it’s not just Eau de Parker?” Mary asked.

Peter pulls out the chair across from her, the scrape of metal legs against the floor cutting through the low hum of conversation. His knuckle brushes the edge of the table as he settles, raw and swollen, and MJ glances at it for half a second longer than she needs to. The coffee between them steams faintly, but his side of the table has no cup yet, no barrier.

“Yeah, well, Parker Industries™—we’re real pioneers in accidental fragrances. Thinking about bottling 'Regret' next, maybe with a hint of 'Ran Out of Time,” Peter said.

“Just make sure you don’t splash on too much 'Could’ve Called First.' It’s a little overpowering,” Mary said.

“Fair. Totally fair. I’ll, uh, tone it down next time—go for a lighter spray of 'Meant to Be on Time, but Crime Happened,” Peter said.

“Ah, the classic. Limited edition, right? Because it's always out of stock when you actually need it,” Mary said.

MJ leans back slightly, crossing her arms, her nails tapping an uneven rhythm against her sleeve. Peter shifts in his seat, one leg bouncing under the table, the motion setting the edge of it quivering almost imperceptibly. The barista calls out a name wrong behind them, followed by someone sighing as they go to claim their drink.

“Yeah, supply chain issues. Villains keep hijacking the delivery trucks,” Peter said.

“Maybe you should stick to hand-delivering it yourself—you’ve got the whole 'web express' thing down, right?” Mary asked.

“Sure, but the customer complaints are killer. "Package arrived with a side of property damage" isn't the best Yelp review,” Peter said.

“Well, maybe if the delivery guy wasn’t showing up late, bruised, and half-broken, the reviews wouldn’t be so harsh,” Mary said.

Peter's grin flickers unevenly as he drops his gaze, thumb rubbing at the edge of his split knuckle. MJ's nails stop tapping, her hand drifting back to the mug, fingers tightening on the handle. Outside, a horn blares short and sharp, the sound muffled but threading through the moment anyway.

“Guess I’m not winning Employee of the Month anytime soon, huh? Adds to the whole underdog charm, though, right?” Peter asked.

“Underdog charm only gets you so far before people start wondering if you’re just bad at time management... or prioritizing the wrong things,” Mary said.

The hum of the espresso machine kicks up again, a hiss of steam filling the quiet that trails her words. Peter shifts, his shoulders collapsing inward slightly as he rests an elbow on the table and runs a hand through his still-out-of-place hair. MJ doesn't move this time, her gaze steady and unreadable, the tiny cracks in the glaze of her coffee mug catching the light.

* * *

The door swings open, bringing a sharp gust of late October air that stirs napkins on the counter and raises goosebumps along Peter's forearms. Felicia steps in, her coat just starting to slip off her shoulder, and pauses, her eyes catching on the familiar shapes at the corner table. The edge of a smirk tugs at her lips as she walks forward, her heels clicking against the worn tile.

“Oh, fantastic. Just when I thought I had a shot at a quiet morning without cardiac arrest,” Peter said.

“Play nice, Tiger—your heart could use the workout,” Mary said.

“Is this a bad time, or just my favorite kind of awkward?” Felicia asked.

“That depends—are you here to steal the Wi-Fi or the emotional stability? Because I’m fresh out of both,” Peter said.

Felicia pulls out the chair across from them, the scrape of metal legs on tile loud enough to turn a couple of heads. She sits with deliberate grace, crossing one leg over the other, her coat slipping completely off her shoulder now, resting in the crook of her arm. MJ leans back in her seat, her fingers trailing along the rim of her coffee cup, the faintest flicker of amusement playing at the corner of her mouth.

“Careful, ‘Licia, sounds like somebody’s running a deficit—and I’d hate to see you try to rob Peter to pay Peter,” Mary said.

“Oh, I don't need to rob him—he does a fine job of leaving himself wide open,” Felicia said.

“Wide open? Oh, sure, why not—it's not like I have a reputation for impeccable boundaries or anything,” Peter said.

“Impeccable boundaries? Tiger, the last time you tried that, it lasted about as long as a latte in this place,” Mary said.

A barista sets a fresh plate of croissants on the counter, the warm smell of butter and yeast briefly cutting through the sharper notes of espresso in the air. Felicia rests her elbow on the table, her chin against her knuckles, watching Peter’s reaction like it’s the most entertaining thing in the room. Peter shifts in his seat, one knee bouncing under the table, the toe of his sneaker scuffing the floor on the downbeat.

“Careful, love, or you’ll vibrate through the floor—and then where would your impeccable boundaries be?” Felicia asked.

“Probably in the basement, along with whatever’s left of my dignity,” Peter said.

“Hey, at least the basement’s got good company—it’s where we keep the spare sense of humor too,” Mary said.

“Oh, don’t worry, MJ—his humor’s intact. It’s just buried under all that charming self-deprecation,” Felicia said.

The corner of Felicia's mouth curves upward as she adjusts the sleeve of her coat, her silver bracelet catching the light for a split second. MJ huffs a quiet laugh through her nose, brushing a strand of hair back and keeping her focus on the now slightly smudged rim of her mug. Peter exhales sharply, dropping his head into his hand, the heel of his palm rubbing at one tired eye.

“Great, glad to know my misery doubles as entertainment. Should we start selling tickets, or is this strictly a free show?” Peter asked.

“Depends—are you planning a two-act tragedy, or can we squeeze in a musical number?” Mary asked.

Felicia leans back in her chair, hands draped lightly over the armrests like she’s settling in for the main event. MJ bites back a grin, finally lifting her coffee for a small sip, the steam curling upward in lazy ribbons. Peter mutters something under his breath, his fingers tugging at the cuffs of his hoodie as his knee resumes its restless bounce.

* * *

The chair legs scrape faintly against the floor as Felicia shifts her weight, crossing one leg over the other and letting her heel dangle. MJ sets her cup down with a soft clink, fingertips brushing the ceramic rim for a beat too long. Peter's knee is bouncing faster now, the fabric of his jeans creaking with each jittery movement.

“So... anyone else feeling this incredibly fun and not-at-all-tense energy, or is it just me?” Peter asked.

“Oh, it's just you, tiger. We're all perfectly comfortable over here,” Mary said.

“Speak for yourself, red. I'm thriving right now,” Felicia said.

“Great, so everyone's thriving except the guy who feels like a bug under a microscope. Perfect,” Peter said.

Felicia smirks, reaching out to pluck a sugar packet from the holder and flipping it idly between her fingers. MJ leans back in her chair, arms crossed, a faint arch to one brow as her gaze flicks between them. Peter exhales sharply through his nose, his hand scrubbing up the back of his neck like he's trying to wipe off a stain.

“Relax, Pete. Nobody’s pinning you down with a magnifying glass... yet,” Mary said.

“Yet being the keyword. Don’t squirm too much, bug—might distract us from our coffee,” Felicia said.

“Oh, sure, wouldn't want to throw off the coffee experience. Heaven forbid the spider ruins anyone's espresso zen,” Peter said.

“Well, considering the spider’s the main attraction, maybe try enjoying the spotlight for once. It’s a rare crowd that drinks their coffee while rooting for you, tiger,” Mary said.

Felicia tips her chair back on two legs, the sugar packet sliding lazily over her knuckles before she lets it drop onto the table. MJ uncrosses her arms to adjust the cuff of her sweater, her movements steady, deliberate. Peter's fingers drum on the edge of the table, a quiet staccato against the worn wood, his eyes flickering to the door like he's mentally mapping escape routes.

“Careful, Peter—if you bolt, you’ll miss the rare chance to see two women actually agreeing on something. Almost poetic, isn’t it?” Felicia asked.

“Yeah, poetic in that, "watch the spider squirm" kind of way. Super uplifting. Thanks for the support, team,” Peter said.

“Oh, don’t be so dramatic. You’re not squirming—you’re... enduring. Very heroically, I might add,” Mary said.

“Enduring heroically is such the vibe, isn’t it? Like watching Atlas shrug, but cuter,” Felicia said.

The corner of MJ's mouth twitches, her fingers tracing the rim of her coffee cup in a slow circle. Felicia leans forward, planting the chair legs back on the floor with a muted thud, her chin resting on one hand as her eyes glitter with sharp amusement. Peter shifts in his seat, his shoulders hunching slightly as one hand disappears under the table to tap against his thigh.

“Great, I'm glad my catastrophic life choices are a source of entertainment. Really warms the heart, guys. Truly,” Peter said.

“Come on, tiger. If we didn’t laugh, we’d probably cry. Consider it a public service,” Mary said.

Felicia chuckles, low and throaty, as she leans back again, her arms draping over the back of her chair in a posture too casual to be accidental. MJ lifts her coffee, taking another measured sip, her eyes never leaving Peter's face. Peter exhales through pursed lips, the sound just barely audible over the ambient hum of coffee grinders and murmured conversations around them.

* * *

The three of them moved as a loose knot, sneakers scuffing uneven pavement. The sun bounced in sharp angles off car windows, but Peter kept his head turned toward MJ, his hands gesturing too big in the space between them. Felicia walked with her hands in the pockets of her leather jacket, her hips rolling in a lazy rhythm that didn’t match the pace of his words.

“So, uh, did you know the guy who invented the fire hydrant never actually got credit 'cause nobody could find the patent? ... Okay, tough crowd,” Peter said.

“Sounds like you and him have something in common, tiger,” Mary said.

“Careful, MJ, we don't want to bruise his ego too badly. It's already working overtime keeping his neck upright with all that chatter,” Felicia said.

“Hey, I can multitask, okay? Walking, talking, and apparently getting roasted—I’m like a human Swiss Army knife,” Peter said.

MJ smirked and tipped her sunglasses down, watching Peter without turning her head. Felicia’s lip curled just enough to show teeth before she glanced past him to MJ, one sharp eyebrow lifting. Peter’s arms windmilled as he dodged a piece of broken sidewalk, still oblivious.

“Just don’t trip on your charm while you’re at it, Swiss Army,” Mary said.

“Oh, I think he sharpened that blade on a brick wall years ago, MJ. It’s more of a spork now,” Felicia said.

“Hey, sporks are versatile! You ever try eating soup with a knife? Didn’t think so,” Peter said.

“Sweetheart, the day you compare yourself to cutlery and win, I’ll personally engrave the spork,” Mary said.

Felicia laughed, quick and low, the sound snapping like a spark before it faded. MJ adjusted her sunglasses back into place, the corner of her mouth pulling tight to stifle a grin. Peter shrugged, his hands shoved deep into his hoodie pockets now, his pace hitching for half a step before he caught up.

“Careful, Spidey, or you’ll end up with “World’s Sharpest Spork” on your headstone.” Felicia said.

“Yeah, well, at least it’d be unique. “Died as he lived: part spoon, part fork, all disaster,” Peter said.

“And somehow still manages to leave a mess no matter what you use him for,” Mary said.

“You know, boys like him are proof chaos really does have a fan club,” Felicia said.

A delivery van honked as it crawled past, its rear wheels splashing through a shallow puddle by the curb. MJ sidestepped the spray without breaking her stride, her arm brushing Peter’s as she moved closer. Felicia stayed planted to the further edge, her smile sharp and lingering as she glanced down at her reflection in the water.

“Okay, but chaos fan club... does that make me the president, or just the unwitting mascot? Asking for a friend,” Peter said.

“Pretty sure you’re the logo, tiger—big, bold, and impossible to miss,” Mary said.

Peter laughed, the sound a little too quick, the kind that tried to smooth jagged lines. MJ's fingers absently brushed the strap of her bag where it cut across her chest, her nails tapping in time with her steps. Felicia’s eyes flicked to MJ again, catching her lingering movement, an almost-smirk tugging at her lips before she looked back to Peter.

* * *

Peter adjusted his glasses, the corner of the metal frame glinting in the overcast light. MJ angled her body slightly, crossing one leg over the other, the leather of her boot pressing into the fabric of her jeans. Felicia leaned back, draping her arms along the bench, her silver hair catching the wind and brushing the side of Peter's shoulder.

“So, uh... anyone else feel like we’re in the setup for a really weird rom-com, or is that just me?” Peter asked.

“Sounds more like the start of a true crime podcast, but sure, let’s call it quirky,” Mary said.

“Quirky? Darling, you're underselling it. This has "critically acclaimed but doomed" written all over it,” Felicia said.

“Doomed? Great, now I'm imagining the Rotten Tomatoes score for my life—critics love it, audience thinks it's a hot mess,” Peter said.

MJ smirked, her fingers tightening slightly around the strap of her bag as she looked past Felicia at a dog chasing its tail near the fence. Felicia’s nails, painted some sharp metallic shade, tapped lightly against the bench’s worn wood, the rhythm more deliberate than idle. Peter shifted forward just enough for his elbows to rest on his knees, lacing his fingers together with a faint creak of leather gloves stuffed into his jacket pocket.

“Oh, don’t worry, Tiger. The audience is definitely rooting for the underdog... even if he trips over his own shoes half the time,” Mary said.

“The underdog is charming, sure, but eventually, people tune in for the chaos. Tripping over shoes? That's just foreplay,” Felicia said.

“Foreplay? Wow, remind me never to take you shoe shopping, Felicia—it sounds like things might get... complicated,” Peter said.

“Only if you don't know how to keep up—though, let’s be honest, have you ever bought shoes that didn’t end up scuffed or webbed up within a week?” Mary asked.

Peter scratched the back of his neck, the faint red mark from his mask’s seam still visible beneath his hairline. Felicia tilted her head, a slow grin spreading as her gaze traced the scuff marks on his sneakers. MJ leaned back into the bench, the edge of her teeth catching her bottom lip as she watched him flounder for a response.

“Scuffed shoes tell a story, Red. The real tragedy is when the storyteller doesn’t realize how good the story is,” Felicia said.

“I mean, it’s not exactly "Once upon a time." More like, "He tripped, fell into a dumpster, and somehow saved the day anyway,” Peter said.

“Sounds like a bestseller to me—action, comedy, and just the right amount of secondhand embarrassment,” Mary said.

“Don't forget the romance—every good story needs at least one heartbreaker, preferably in black,” Felicia said.

Felicia shifted, her knee brushing against Peter’s as she angled herself toward him, her smile sharp enough to cut. Peter glanced down at the contact, his fingers curling tighter between his knees before letting go. MJ straightened, her eyes lingering on the space where their legs touched, fingers tapping once against her bag before going still.

“I think we can all agree, the real romance here is between me and gravity—longtime partner, never lets me down. Except, you know, constantly,” Peter said.

“Well, at least it's a consistent relationship. Not everyone can say that,” Mary said.

A breeze pushed through the park, shuffling loose leaves across the path in uneven drifts. Peter rubbed the heel of his palm against his thigh like he was trying to work out some phantom itch. Felicia’s gaze flicked up to MJ’s face, sharp and measuring, before her smile curved wider. MJ held it, steady as stone, before finally looking away.

* * *

Felicia adjusted the cuff of her leather jacket, slow like she had all the time in the world, then pushed herself up off the bench. The zipper glinted as she shifted, catching a quick flash of sunlight. MJ’s fingers curled tighter around the paper coffee cup, knuckles whitening against the damp sleeve.

“Wait—uh, Felicia, you're just gonna—you're leaving now?” Peter asked.

“Let her go, Tiger. She’s got better things to do than babysit your guilt trip,” Mary said.

“Oh, don’t worry, Red. I leave him in such capable hands,” Felicia said.

“...Okay, what the hell just happened?” Peter asked.

Felicia stepped back onto the path, her boots crunching over a thin scatter of gravel. She looked at MJ, her expression sharp but not biting, eyes narrowing just a shade before softening into something unreadable. MJ didn’t flinch, her jaw tightening as a breath escaped through her nose, slow and measured.

“Nothing you can’t handle, Pete. If you’d just take your head out of your ass for five seconds,” Mary said.

“Sweetheart, he’d need a crowbar and a map for that,” Felicia said.

“Wow, love the team spirit here. Really feeling supported. Thanks, guys,” Peter said.

“Oh, don’t be so sensitive, Tiger. You’re the one who invited the circus; we’re just working the crowd,” Mary said.

Felicia’s mouth quirked, an almost-smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. She adjusted the strap of her bag over her shoulder, fingers brushing it like a habit, more automatic than deliberate. MJ stayed seated, her gaze trailing Felicia for a beat before flicking back to Peter, the corners of her lips pressed tight against whatever she wasn’t saying.

“Careful, Red, or he might think we’re actually enjoying the show,” Felicia said.

“Am I the only one who feels like I missed the rehearsal for whatever this was?” Peter asked.

“Maybe, or maybe you just weren’t paying attention—happens a lot, doesn’t it?” Mary asked.

“Don’t be too hard on him, MJ. Multitasking’s never been his strong suit,” Felicia said.

Felicia turned on her heel, the motion clean and deliberate, and started down the path without another glance back. The sound of her boots faded quickly, swallowed by the occasional bark from the dog run and the distant hum of traffic. Peter shifted on the bench, one hand dragging through his hair, while MJ tilted her coffee cup, staring into whatever dregs were left.

“Right, so... are we gonna talk about that, or should I just keep guessing at which landmine I stepped on this time?” Peter asked.

“You’ve got two options, Tiger: you can keep guessing, or you can sit with the fact that sometimes the blast radius isn’t about you,” Mary said.

The foam clinging to the edge of MJ’s cup finally gave way, dripping into the puddle at the bottom as she set it down on the bench beside her. Peter’s leg started bouncing, the heel of his sneaker tapping out a staccato rhythm against the concrete. Across the path, a golden retriever barked twice before darting after a tennis ball, tail whipping like a flag.

* * *

Their shoes scuffed against the pavement, one step a half-beat behind the other. A faint breeze pressed the sounds of the city into the quiet between them—car horns, distant laughter, the sharp snap of a plastic bag caught in the wind. Peter adjusted the strap of his sagging backpack, eyes on the cracks in the sidewalk.

“So, uh... Are we just gonna pretend the last two hours were totally normal? Or do I get to make a joke about the no-foam rule breaking your coffee heart?” Peter asked.

“You get one joke. Make it count, tiger,” Mary said.

“Only one? You're cutting me off mid-bit, Watson. This is comedy cruelty,” Peter said.

“Hey, I’ve got a reputation to protect. If I let you spiral into a five-minute stand-up routine, the F train might make it out alive, but I won’t,” Mary said.

MJ tugged her jacket tighter against the breeze, the zipper catching for a moment before giving way. Peter kicked a pebble off the curb, watching it skip into the gutter and disappear. Their pace evened out, shoulders brushing lightly as the shadow of a building stretched long across their path.

“Alright, alright, no five-minute routines. I'll save my tight ten for the subway crowd—captive audience and all that,” Peter said.

“Just make sure they don't start passing around a hat for you, Pete. My ego can only handle so much secondhand embarrassment,” Mary said.

“You kidding me? If anyone's passing around a hat, it's 'cause my material’s so good they want an encore,” Peter said.

“Sure, because nothing says "comedy gold" like a guy in a spider suit riffing on delayed subways. Total Netflix special material,” Mary said.

Peter smiled, the corner of his mouth twitching up as he glanced at her, and then back at the sidewalk. MJ ran a hand through her hair, tucking a strand behind her ear before letting her arm swing loose again. The afternoon sunlight dappled through the trees, catching the red in her hair for a second before fading.

“Hey, don’t knock the spider suit—visual gags are half the act. Plus, I hear spandex really sells in the comedy world,” Peter said.

“Only if you’re aiming for the niche "awkward superhero chic" demographic. Bold strategy, Parker,” Mary said.

“What can I say? Gotta corner the market before Deadpool snatches up all the spandex-based punchlines,” Peter said.

“Deadpool's not your competition, Pete. He's your cautionary tale,” Mary said.

The crosswalk signal blinked to life ahead of them, the red hand switching to a white silhouette mid-step. A delivery guy on an electric bike zipped past, the hum of the motor fading quickly behind. Peter slowed just slightly, his shoe scuffing the edge of the curb as they stepped off together.

“Ouch. Reminder taken. I'll aim for charming disaster, not full-blown chaos muppet,” Peter said.

“Charming disaster suits you—you’ve got it down to an art form. Just don’t let the muppet part take over, okay?” Mary asked.

The subway entrance loomed a block ahead, railings glinting under the sun where the paint had worn thin. MJ shifted her bag higher on her shoulder, her movement quick and instinctive. Peter pushed his hands into his pockets, the fabric bunching awkwardly around his fingers as they walked.
