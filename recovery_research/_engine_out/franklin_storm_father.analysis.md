# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP — FRANKLIN STORM (SR.), M.D.

Subject designation: Franklin Storm, father of Susan and Jonathan Storm. Civilian, non-powered. Recurring tertiary character across the Fantastic Four canon, 1966–present.

---

## 1. HISTORY — THE BEHAVIORAL RECORD

Franklin Storm is introduced retroactively in *Fantastic Four* #32 (Lee/Kirby, Nov. 1964) — "Death of a Hero!" — already in the third act of his life. The reader meets him not as a father in domestic context but as a fugitive: a once-prominent surgeon who has fallen catastrophically from grace, served prison time for manslaughter, and resurfaced linked to the alien Skrull conspiracy targeting his children. Almost every behavioral datum we possess about him is filtered through this terminal arc and the flashbacks/recollections it generates.

**Pre-fall era (reconstructed from dialogue and later flashbacks, FF #32, Annual #5, FF Vol. 3 era retrospectives, and the Byrne-era FF #245 family memory beats).** Franklin was a respected New York surgeon. His wife Mary Storm was killed in an automobile accident in which Franklin was the driver; Mary required emergency surgery, Franklin operated himself in extremis (or could not), and she died on the table. This is the inflection point of the entire life. Following her death, Franklin's documented pattern is: withdrawal from medicine, gambling debts, alcohol use (consistently coded in 1960s comics language as "drink"), and eventual entanglement with loan sharks. In an altercation with one such creditor, the man died — by Franklin's hand. Franklin pleaded guilty (or was convicted of manslaughter; the canon hedges), surrendered his medical license, and went to prison. Crucially, he did NOT contest the charge and refused to involve his children in his defense. Sue and Johnny were told a sanitized story; Sue, the elder, retained more of the truth.

**Behavioral signature of that interval:** voluntary self-erasure. He did not write. He did not appeal. He let his children believe he had effectively died with their mother. Sue Storm raised Johnny largely alone. This is a man who, having failed once catastrophically as protector, withdrew rather than risk failing again — an avoidant-shame solution rather than a reparative one.

**Reappearance arc (FF #32).** Released from prison, Franklin is destitute, ill (the issue establishes a terminal condition — variably rendered as a brain tumor or radiation-related illness in later retellings), and immediately preyed upon by the Skrulls, who surgically alter him to impersonate the "Super-Skrull" antagonist. The behavior to note: when reunited with Sue, his first instinct is concealment and self-deprecation — he tries to keep her from learning who he is, and when forced to interact, he frames himself as unworthy. The arc culminates in him sacrificing himself to save Reed by absorbing an energy blast meant for his son-in-law-to-be. The death is staged by Lee/Kirby as redemptive substitution: he dies in the place of the man who will protect his daughter. Read clinically, this is suicide-by-rescue — the final expression of a decades-long conviction that his only remaining utility is to be subtracted from his children's lives at maximum benefit to them.

**Posthumous appearances and retcons.** Franklin recurs in flashback (FF #271, the Byrne run; various Sue Storm-centric issues; Waid/Wieringo and Hickman-era family memory sequences) and is briefly reintroduced as alive in the Skrull-related "Nathaniel/Franklin confusion" plots before being re-killed or re-clarified. The pattern across writers is remarkably consistent: he is always remembered with painful affection by Sue and complicated mixed feeling by Johnny (Johnny was younger; his memories are sparser and more idealized, hence more brittle). Reed Richards, notably, treats Franklin's memory with respect bordering on reverence — Reed names his and Sue's firstborn son "Franklin" (FF Annual #6, 1968). That naming decision is itself a piece of evidence: Reed, who knew Franklin only briefly and largely in his degraded state, nonetheless recognized something in the man worth memorializing — most plausibly the moral clarity of the final sacrifice and the surgeon's residual competence Franklin briefly displayed under crisis.

**Evolution across eras.** Silver Age writers used him as a melodramatic pivot (fallen man / redemptive death). Bronze and Modern Age writers (Byrne, Waid, Hickman, Slott) reframed him retrospectively as a study in inherited trauma — explaining traits in Sue (over-responsibility, parentification, conflict-avoidance in family matters) and Johnny (impulsivity, recklessness, difficulty with sustained intimacy) as legible downstream consequences of Franklin's collapse. The character did not evolve so much as the *use* of him evolved: he became the etiological key to his children's psychology.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Substance use.** Heavy alcohol use post-bereavement, sustained over a period of years, sufficient to compromise professional judgment and contribute to financial collapse. No on-panel evidence of detox, treatment, or AA-equivalent. By the time of reappearance, prison would have enforced sobriety, but the prior pattern indicates an alcohol use disorder — likely moderate-to-severe in the grief years.

**Surgical/physical alterations.** Underwent forced/coerced Skrull cosmetic and somatic alteration (FF #32) to impersonate an enemy — non-consensual bodily modification with obvious psychological sequelae (depersonalization risk, body-integrity disturbance) had he survived.

**Terminal illness.** Canon variously cites an inoperable intracranial mass or radiation-induced terminal condition diagnosed during or shortly after incarceration. Functional implication: at the time of his sacrificial death, Franklin knew he was dying. This materially alters the read of the sacrifice — it is not pure altruism but a *use* of foreknowledge, an attempt to convert remaining lifespan into moral capital for his daughter.

**Traumatic injury history.** Survivor of the motor vehicle accident that killed his wife — likely some physical injury, certainly significant psychological injury. No evidence of treatment for PTSD-spectrum symptoms; the era and his profession would have militated against seeking it.

**Occupational exposure.** Decades as a surgeon — chronic stress, sleep disruption, normalized proximity to mortality. This is relevant: surgeons have above-baseline rates of substance use and suicide; Franklin's trajectory after his wife's death is statistically consonant with a known high-risk demographic, not idiosyncratic.

**Carceral medicine.** Multi-year incarceration with terminal diagnosis emerging in or around release. Quality of care presumptively poor. Likely uncontrolled pain and untreated depression at point of reappearance.

**Psychological impact synthesis:** A surgeon's identity is built on the fantasy of agentic control over mortality. Losing his wife on (or near) his own table demolished that organizing fantasy. Every subsequent behavior — drinking, gambling, the killing, the refusal to defend himself, the abandonment of his children, the sacrificial death — can be read as iterations of a single somatic-existential injury: *I was supposed to be the one who saves people, and I could not save her.*

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Pre-bereavement: presumably secure-organized, given a functional marriage and two children who, despite everything, retained capacity for love (Sue's grief is uncomplicated and warm in flashback — children of cold or abusive fathers do not grieve that cleanly). Post-bereavement: collapsed into avoidant-dismissive style with strong shame-driven withdrawal. He does not seek his children. He does not write from prison. The avoidance is not indifference — it is the avoidant defense in its purest clinical form: *contact would require me to face what I have become, so I will sever contact and call it protection.*

**Defenses (in approximate hierarchy).**
- *Reaction formation and undoing* — the sacrificial death is textbook undoing: a single grand act intended to retroactively rebalance a ledger that years of small repair could not.
- *Reversal/turning against the self* — refusing to defend himself at trial; accepting the maximum moral weight rather than mitigation.
- *Isolation of affect* — when finally face-to-face with Sue, his speech is controlled, almost surgical; he discusses his own situation in the third person register of clinical description.
- *Identification with the aggressor* — allowing the Skrulls to refashion him literally into the form of an enemy: an external instantiation of his internal self-concept (*I am the thing that hurts my family*).
- *Altruistic surrender* (A. Freud's term) — vicarious living-through-others as substitute for self-care; visible in his framing of his death as "for them."

**Affect regulation.** Poor under sustained stress, brittle under acute stress, but capable of striking composure in terminal/decisive moments. The pattern — diffuse dysregulation when no clear action exists, sudden crystalline calm when a single decisive act presents itself — is characteristic of depressed individuals with surgical training: they manage acute crisis better than chronic ambiguity, because their professional formation trained for the former and not the latter.

**Cognitive style.** Intelligent, analytical, ruminative. The kind of mind that re-litigates a single decision (the surgery, the accident, the killing) endlessly. High capacity for moral reasoning weaponized against the self — he is a fair judge to others and a hanging judge to himself.

**Self-concept.** Catastrophically collapsed. The pre-fall self ("competent surgeon, good husband, capable father") has been replaced by a single integrated bad-object self-image ("the man who kills the people he is supposed to save"). This self-concept is stable, ego-syntonic in its negativity, and resistant to disconfirmation — Sue's continued love is metabolized not as evidence of his worth but as further evidence of *her* goodness and his unworthiness of it.

**Compensatory mechanisms.**
- *Positive:* Residual surgical competence and moral seriousness; capacity for protective action when the situation is unambiguous; truth-telling under pressure (he does eventually disclose to Sue).
- *Negative:* Self-punishment as a lifestyle organizing principle; preference for grand redemptive gestures over the slower, harder work of presence; use of his own dying as a final transaction.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Differential diagnosis derived from the behavioral record:

**Primary: Major Depressive Disorder, recurrent, severe, with melancholic features, in the context of complicated/prolonged grief.** Evidence: anhedonia inferable from abandonment of career and children; pervasive guilt with delusional intensity around wife's death; psychomotor changes implied by descriptions of his degraded state; functional collapse not attributable to substance use alone (substance use is consequence, not cause); affective constriction in late-life presentation. Onset clearly tied to bereavement event; chronicity sustained across years without remission.

**Comorbid: Alcohol Use Disorder, moderate-to-severe, in forced remission (incarceration).** Self-medication phenotype — drinking begins after the index trauma, escalates with debt and shame, contributes to but does not cause the violent incident. No evidence of premorbid use disorder.

**Comorbid: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, chronic.** Index events: the accident, the operative failure, and arguably the manslaughter itself. Avoidance cluster is dominant (he avoids his children, his profession, his city, his name). Intrusive symptoms inferable from rumination. Negative alterations in cognition and mood are pervasive. Hyperarousal less documented but consistent with the irritability that produced the fatal altercation.

**Suicidality.** Passive suicidal ideation across the decade of his disappearance is the most parsimonious read of his behavior. The final death is best characterized as **suicide-by-rescue** — a culturally sanctioned, morally redemptive form of self-destruction available to a man with terminal diagnosis, criminal record, and a child in immediate danger. It satisfies clinical criteria for suicidal action (intent, means, lethality, foreknowledge) while permitting the actor to die inside a narrative he can endorse.

**What he is NOT.**
- *Not Antisocial Personality Disorder.* The manslaughter is situational, not patterned; he shows remorse to the point of self-destruction; he accepts punishment rather than evading it; no evidence of pre-trauma conduct problems or callousness.
- *Not Narcissistic Personality Disorder.* The grand sacrificial gesture could superficially resemble narcissistic theatre, but the affective substrate is shame, not grandiosity. He hides, he does not perform. NPD does not refuse to defend itself at trial.
- *Not Bipolar.* No documented manic or hypomanic episodes; the energy of the final act is crisis-mobilization, not mood-elevation.
- *Not primary Substance Use Disorder.* The substance use is etiologically downstream of the depression and grief.
- *Not a redemption arc, clinically.* The narrative frame says redemption; the clinical frame says completed suicide rationalized as rescue. Both can be true; the analytic file should hold both.

## 5. PERSONALITY — SYNTHESIS

**Core drive.** To restore a moral order he believes he personally broke. Every action after Mary's death is a transaction in a private ledger that cannot, in his accounting, ever balance — but which he never stops trying to balance.

**Worldview / operational code.** *Competence is moral obligation. Failure of competence is moral failure. Moral failure must be paid for, in full, by the failed party, with no discount sought and no help requested.* This is the ethos of a particular kind of mid-century American professional man — surgeon, soldier, priest — for whom role-failure is identity-annihilating. He cannot separate the function he failed at from the self that failed.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm and capable when the role is clear (husband, father, surgeon); withdrawn and self-erasing when the role is contaminated by his sense of unworthiness. He does not burden others with his suffering — which is itself a form of burdening, because it leaves his children to construct him from absence. With Sue specifically, his pattern is *protective concealment*: he tells her less than she can handle, in the name of sparing her, which is the same error his profession trained him in (the doctor who decides what the family can bear to hear).

**Stress response and escalation.** Under chronic, ambiguous stress: deterioration, withdrawal, substance use, rumination. Under acute, clarified stress with a single right action available: sudden return of competence, calm, decisiveness — and a tendency to seize the most self-costly correct option as if it were the only one. The escalation curve is unusual: he does not get more dangerous to others as he decompensates, he gets more dangerous to himself.

**Pressure points.**
- Any framing of him as a *good* father or *good* man — he cannot tolerate it and will move to falsify it.
- His children's safety — overrides every other consideration, including survival, dignity, and truth-telling.
- Reminders of his wife — destabilizing; the unhealed wound.
- Offers of help or restoration — refused, often violently, because accepting them would require revising the self-concept on which his entire post-trauma identity is built.

**The legible inheritance.** Sue inherits his over-responsibility, his protective concealment, his tendency to absorb cost silently. Johnny inherits the unmetabolized grief in its acted-out form: recklessness as a way of daring mortality to take him too. Reed, by marriage, inherits the role Franklin abdicated and the name Franklin could not bear to keep clean — and honors it by giving it to his son. The character's narrative function across sixty years is to be the etiological ghost in the Storm-Richards family system: a man whose single collapse organizes three generations of behavior, and whose final act is mistaken — by himself and by the genre — for redemption when it is more precisely a closure.
