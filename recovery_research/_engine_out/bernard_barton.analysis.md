# UATU ANALYTICAL DOSSIER: BERNARD BARTON, alias "TRICK SHOT"

*Pre-synthesis intelligence workup. All inferences derived from cited behavioral record.*

---

## 1. HISTORY

Bernard "Buck" Barton enters the published record obliquely — he is established first through the trauma he caused, not through his own deeds. The carnival-era backstory introduced in *Solo Avengers* #2-3 (1987, Gruenwald) and elaborated across Mark Gruenwald's *Hawkeye* limited series and the subsequent Hawkeye solo runs (Fabian Nicieza, Chuck Dixon, Jim McCann, Matt Fraction's tangential references) establishes the operative timeline:

**Carnival period (pre-Avengers Hawkeye):** Barton appears as the headliner archery act at the Carson Carnival of Travelling Wonders, working alongside the Swordsman (Jacques Duquesne). He takes on Clint Barton — a runaway child fleeing an abusive, alcoholic father (Harold Barton) and an institutional childhood after his parents' fatal car accident — as an apprentice. The arrangement is formally pedagogical and emotionally exploitative. Trick Shot trains Clint to professional, then superhuman, marksmanship over years. The Swordsman is Clint's other paternal figure; Trick Shot is the more talented, more disciplined, less overtly criminal of the two — initially.

**The betrayal event (*Solo Avengers* #2, the canonical origin beat):** When the Swordsman is caught embezzling from the carnival till and flees, Clint refuses to follow him into criminality. Trick Shot, *who has been quietly partnering with the Swordsman in the theft and in other crimes*, beats Clint severely and leaves him for dead (some retellings: shoots him through the side with a barbed broadhead) when Clint will not join their crew. This is the formative rupture. Trick Shot disappears from Clint's life for years.

**Reappearance as antagonist:** Trick Shot returns in the Gruenwald *Hawkeye* limited series (1983) and again in *Solo Avengers* arcs, repeatedly attempting to either reclaim Clint as protégé, kill him in a final test, or — critically — manipulate Clint into killing *him*. The "dying mentor wants assisted suicide via combat" beat recurs (terminal cancer storyline), in which Trick Shot, diagnosed terminal, hunts Clint to force a duel he intends to lose. Clint refuses to kill him. This is the most psychologically revealing single arc in Barton's file.

**Late-period appearances** (post-Heroic Age, scattered cameos through the 2000s-2010s) keep him alive intermittently, sometimes as reluctant ally, more often as a ghost of Clint's past surfacing during identity crises. He has trained at least one other "Trick Shot" successor in some continuities, suggesting a pattern of taking apprentices.

**Pattern across eras:** Every writer who handles Barton converges on the same shape: a master craftsman who cannot stop instrumentalizing the people closest to him, who experiences love only through the language of pedagogy and combat, and who returns to Clint in cycles — never able to leave, never able to apologize.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Physiology:** Ordinary human male, late-middle-aged to elderly across appearances. No metahuman augmentation. Peak-trained ocular acuity, fine-motor control, and proprioception sustained well into a chronological age where such faculties typically degrade — implies obsessive maintenance regimen and likely chronic overuse injuries (shoulder, rotator cuff, draw-hand tendinopathy are occupational certainties for a lifetime archer, though never depicted explicitly).

**Terminal illness arc:** Canonically diagnosed with terminal cancer (Gruenwald-era and follow-ups). Type unspecified but presented as untreatable and progressing. He conceals the diagnosis from Clint and weaponizes the timeline — choosing combat-suicide-by-protégé over palliative decline. The psychological reading of this choice belongs to §3, but medically it confirms (a) he sought no aggressive treatment, (b) he had access to the diagnosis early enough to plan an elaborate confrontation, and (c) he experienced the prospect of physical diminishment as more intolerable than death.

**Substance use:** No on-panel pattern of alcoholism or drug use, which is notable given the carnival milieu and given Clint's father's alcoholism (Clint's wariness around drink suggests Trick Shot did *not* model it). The discipline of the archer's craft appears to have been protective against the substance pathology that destroyed the elder Barton family.

**Injury history:** Combat wounds across appearances, none disabling. The relevant somatic fact is the *absence* of self-care collapse — Barton's body is the one stable instrument in his life, and he has treated it as such.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment architecture:** Dismissive-avoidant with strong narcissistic overlay. The evidence: Barton forms intense, asymmetric bonds (Swordsman, Clint, successor apprentices) but consistently structures them so that intimacy is mediated through *transmission of skill* and *evaluation of performance*. He cannot tolerate horizontal relationship; he requires hierarchy. When Clint attains autonomous moral identity (refusing the theft), Barton's response is not negotiation or grief — it is annihilating violence. This is the avoidant attachment pattern under threat: when the other becomes a separate subject rather than an extension, they must be erased.

**Core defenses (in observable hierarchy):**

- **Sublimation** — primary, and what makes him functional. Every affective state is routed into the bow. Rage, love, grief, pride: all become marksmanship. This is why he is great. This is also why he cannot speak.
- **Identification with the aggressor / pedagogical reenactment** — he was almost certainly trained brutally himself (the carnival/itinerant-performer ecosystem of his generation, the cruelty of his teaching style, the assumption that beating-as-instruction is normal). He passes the wound forward as method.
- **Projective identification** — he experiences his own disowned tenderness *in* Clint, then attacks it. Clint's mercy in the terminal-cancer arc is precisely the quality Barton cannot bear in himself.
- **Reaction formation around grief** — the assisted-suicide gambit is a reaction-formed mourning. Unable to ask Clint to be present at his death, he stages a duel that *forces* Clint's presence. Love disguised as combat is the only love he has grammar for.
- **Splitting** under stress, with idealization-devaluation cycles directed at Clint specifically.

**Affect regulation:** Constricted. Range narrowed to pride, contempt, and a controlled performative menace. Tenderness leaks only through pedagogical choices — what he chose to teach, the years he invested, the fact that he *returns*. He cannot regulate shame; shame becomes rage that becomes aggression toward the witness of his shame (Clint).

**Cognitive style:** Highly focused, narrow-aperture, perfectionist. Excellent procedural and somatic intelligence; poor mentalizing of others' interiority. Thinks in tests, trials, and demonstrations. Likely concrete-operational in moral reasoning despite tactical sophistication — outcomes are pass/fail, students are succeeded/failed, lives are won/lost.

**Compensatory mechanisms (positive):** The craft itself. Discipline, the protective regimen against the substance collapse he likely watched destroy peers. Genuine aesthetic investment in marksmanship as an end in itself.

**Compensatory mechanisms (negative):** The pedagogical relationship as the sole permitted vehicle for intimacy, which guarantees that every loved object eventually becomes either a successor (and disappears) or a failure (and must be destroyed). The grandiosity of "the world's greatest archer" identity, which cannot survive being surpassed — and which he has, in fact, been surpassed by, by his own student. That fact organizes much of his later behavior.

**Self-concept:** Defined entirely by skill ranking and by relational position to apprentices. Outside the bow and outside the teacher-role, he has no stable interior. This is why terminal diagnosis produces suicidal planning rather than reflection: there is no self to reflect *with*.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Working differential, derived from the behavioral record rather than asserted:

**Primary: Narcissistic Personality Disorder, with prominent antisocial features.** Evidence: grandiose self-concept tied to unique skill, exploitation of subordinates (Clint as both apprentice and unwitting accomplice plan), inability to tolerate the emergence of the apprentice as moral peer, rage at narcissistic injury (the refusal-to-join scene escalates to attempted murder — a textbook narcissistic rage response, not instrumental violence), lifelong pattern of using others to regulate self-esteem. The antisocial features (carnival theft, attempted murder of a minor, comfort with sustained criminality) are real but appear *secondary* to and *organized by* the narcissistic structure — he is not a primary psychopath. He has a conscience; it is buried under contempt and only audible at end-of-life.

**Rule out: Antisocial Personality Disorder, primary.** Against this diagnosis: he experiences something like remorse (the assisted-suicide arc is, read clinically, an attempt to make recompense via a structurally legible act); he sustains long-term attachments even if pathologically; he does not exhibit the shallow affect, parasitic lifestyle drift, or stimulus-seeking impulsivity of true ASPD. He is too disciplined. The criminality is opportunistic and narcissistically motivated, not constitutional.

**Rule out: Sadistic personality traits (DSM-III appendix; ICD trait domain).** Some present — pleasure in dominance, cruelty rationalized as pedagogy — but not freestanding. Cruelty is a means to the narcissistic end, not the end itself.

**Comorbid: Likely complex trauma history (own childhood).** Inferred, not depicted. The intergenerational transmission of brutal pedagogy plus the affective constriction plus the inability to articulate any need that is not framed as a contest all point upstream to a developmental environment that punished vulnerability. This is inference, but it is the parsimonious etiology.

**Comorbid (late-period): Adjustment response to terminal diagnosis with prominent suicidal ideation, organized through pre-existing character structure.** He does not develop a depressive episode in the conventional sense; instead, the diagnosis is metabolized through his existing narcissistic-pedagogical grammar — death must be *masterful*, *staged*, and *administered by the worthy successor*. This is character pathology shaping mortality response, not a new Axis-I picture.

**NOT present, despite surface suggestion:** Psychopathy (Hare-criterion sense — too much capacity for sustained investment); paranoid PD (he is not suspicious, he is contemptuous); sadistic homicidal ideation (the violence is reactive and instrumental, never recreational).

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drive:** To remain the unsurpassed master — and, beneath that and contradicting it, to be definitively surpassed by the one student who matters, so that his life's work has a legible terminus. These two drives war with each other and account for nearly all of his observable behavior toward Clint.

**Worldview / operational code:** The world is a series of contests adjudicated by skill. Skill is the only honest currency; everything else (affection, loyalty, mercy) is sentimentality that the disciplined practitioner has trained out. Excellence justifies cruelty because cruelty produces excellence — this is what was done to him, this is what he does, this is what is *true*. The only meaningful relationship is teacher-to-student, and the only honest endings are graduation, failure, or death.

**Interpersonal pattern:** Magnetic to younger, talented, fatherless men. Initially generous (with time, with technique, with access). Tightens into control as the bond deepens. Tests escalate. When the student demonstrates autonomous moral identity, Barton experiences this as betrayal and responds with annihilating force. If the student survives, Barton spends the rest of his life orbiting them — unable to apologize, unable to leave, returning under pretexts of combat, of pedagogy, of judgment.

**Stress / escalation curve:** Baseline — controlled, contemptuous, performative. Mild stress — sharpening of contempt, increased pedagogical scrutiny. Moderate stress (narcissistic challenge) — testing behavior, contests of skill, public framing. Severe stress (narcissistic injury, especially from Clint) — narcissistic rage, lethal violence, attempts to destroy the source of the injury. Terminal stress (mortality, irrelevance) — elaborate staged confrontations designed to convert his death into a pedagogical event he controls.

**Pressure points (operational):**
- Any suggestion that he has been surpassed, especially by Clint. He has been. He knows it. He cannot bear having it spoken.
- The word "father" or any direct naming of the paternal role he occupied. He has no defenses against the unmediated affective claim.
- Witnessed tenderness — being seen offering or receiving care collapses his self-image and produces either flight or aggression.
- Clint's mercy. Specifically Clint's refusal to kill him. This is the one stimulus for which he has prepared no response, because it violates the grammar of contest in which he lives.

**Synthesis read for the profiler:** Barton is not a villain in the structural sense — he is a man whose only available language for love is the transmission of a craft, and whose only available language for failure is violence. He produced, in Clint Barton, the apprentice who exceeded him morally as well as technically, and he has spent the rest of his life unable to metabolize that fact. Every return appearance is a man trying, in the only grammar he possesses, to say something he cannot say. The terminal-cancer arc is the closest he ever comes to saying it. Clint, refusing to kill him, refuses also to translate it for him. They part with the sentence unfinished. That unfinished sentence is the whole of the file.
