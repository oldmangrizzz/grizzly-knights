# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP — SUBJECT: ROGERS, STEVEN GRANT

Alias: Captain America. Stage 1 of 2 (Analysis). All conclusions derived from cited behavioral record.

---

## 1. HISTORY — THE LONGITUDINAL BEHAVIORAL RECORD

**Pre-Project Rebirth (Lower East Side, 1920s–1940).** The earliest canonical material (*Captain America Comics* #1, 1941; retconned and elaborated in *Tales of Suspense* #63, *Captain America* vol. 1 #176, #225, #247, #255, and Brubaker's *Captain America* vol. 5 flashbacks) establishes the formative substrate: only child of Sarah Rogers (Irish-immigrant nurse, widowed; tuberculosis fatal when Steve is in late adolescence) and Joseph Rogers (alcoholic veteran of WWI, died young; later writers — particularly Waid and Brubaker — emphasize domestic violence Steve witnessed and could not stop as a small, sick boy). Subject is repeatedly depicted as asthmatic, frail, anemic, undersized, frequently beaten in alley fights he refuses to walk away from. The recurrent panel — Steve picking himself up from a beating saying some variant of "I can do this all day" / "I'll just get up again" (*Captain America: Man Out of Time* #1; *Captain America* vol. 5 #50 flashback) — is not a quip; it is the encoding of his earliest survival strategy: moral stubbornness substituting for physical capacity.

**Project Rebirth and the Erskine Imprinting (1940–41).** *Tales of Suspense* #63 and Stern/Byrne's *Captain America* #255 are central. Erskine's selection criterion is explicitly moral, not physical, and Erskine dies in front of him within hours of the procedure. Steve's first independent act as the enhanced soldier is to chase and kill the assassin — an immediate enactment of the pattern that will define him: enhancement is meaningless except as the instrument of a promise to a dead mentor. Erskine functions as a transferential father (the good one, replacing Joseph) whose death Steve experiences as a charge he must personally redeem.

**The War Years and Bucky (1941–45).** Across the Simon/Kirby originals, the Stern–Byrne run, and Brubaker's retroactive reconstruction (*Captain America* vol. 5 #1–14, "Winter Soldier"), Steve forms his first true peer-attachment to James Buchanan Barnes. Brubaker reframes Bucky from sidekick to covert-ops asset — meaning Steve was the public face of a war in which his closest friend did the wet work Steve was kept clean of. Steve's belief in Bucky's death on the Zemo drone (*Avengers* #4 retcon) becomes the load-bearing trauma of his postwar life: a death he witnessed, could not prevent, and for which he held himself responsible because he had brought a boy into the war.

**The Ice (1945–1964 narrative time).** *Avengers* #4 (1964): recovered frozen. The defrosting sequence and its aftermath — elaborated in *Captain America* #112, the Englehart run, the Gruenwald run, Waid's *Man Out of Time*, and Brubaker — is the second load-bearing trauma. Steve does not merely lose Bucky; he loses the entire cohort, the war's meaning, the social world, the technology, the idiom, the streets he knew. Englehart's #176 and the Nomad arc (#180) show the first time Steve consciously interrogates whether the identity "Captain America" is survivable.

**Avengers Era and Identity Crises (1960s–80s).** A repeating cycle becomes visible across runs: Steve binds to the Avengers as surrogate family; encounters a betrayal by the government or symbolic America (Nixon-analog suicide, #175; Commission removal, Gruenwald #332); abandons the identity (Nomad, The Captain); returns to it because the abandonment proves more painful than the contradiction. The pattern is diagnostically important: he can suspend the *uniform* but cannot suspend the *function* — i.e., the identity is not costume, it is compulsory caretaking.

**Gruenwald Long Run (#307–443).** The richest behavioral corpus. The Streets of Poison arc (#372–378): forcibly addicted to a designer stimulant, experiences uncharacteristic rage, refuses ever to use even legal stimulants again — evidence of how thin his tolerance is for any threat to executive control. The Capwolf, Bloodstone, and Superia arcs are less significant than the John Walker arc (#332–350), in which the government's replacement Captain America commits the atrocities Steve fears latent in the iconography itself. Steve takes Walker back, trains him, forgives him: pattern of repairing rather than discarding broken successors.

**Post-Heroes Reborn, Waid, and Rieber (late 1990s–early 2000s).** Waid's *Man Out of Time* and "American Nightmare" make grief explicit; Rieber's post-9/11 run shows Steve struggling visibly with patriotism as marketing versus patriotism as ethical practice.

**Brubaker (2005–2012).** Diagnostic peak of the canon. Bucky returns as the Winter Soldier (#1–14): Steve's response is not relief but a deepening of guilt — he failed Bucky not by losing him but by leaving him alive in enemy hands for sixty years. Civil War (Millar, 2006–07): Steve leads the anti-Registration insurgency, is captured, surrenders publicly to stop civilian casualties, and is assassinated on the courthouse steps (*Captain America* #25). Resurrection (Brubaker, *Reborn*) reveals he has been displaced through his own timeline, forced to re-witness every formative loss — a literalization of complicated grief as plot mechanism.

**Modern Era (Remender, Spencer, Coates, Aaron).** Remender's *Dimension Z*: stranded for years subjective time raising an adopted son (Ian); returns; the son is largely written out — another orphaning. Spencer's *Secret Empire*: a cosmic-cube-altered Steve is revealed as a lifelong Hydra sleeper. Whether one treats this as canon or aberration, the *real* Steve's response on restoration is characteristic: grief and accountability, not denial, for things he did not do. Coates: confronts the gap between symbol and country. Aaron's *Avengers* and the recent runs: increasingly written as the unit's elder, the one others grieve to.

The longitudinal pattern across 60+ years and dozens of writers is remarkably stable: a man whose formative wound (smallness, helplessness, witnessed harm to loved ones) was treated by a single biological intervention and an unbroken behavioral one — never stop getting up.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Pre-serum somatic record.** Documented asthma, scarlet fever, rheumatic fever sequelae implied, chronic sinusitis, partial deafness in one ear (Brubaker), anemia, scoliosis, hypertension on enlistment exam, malnutrition consistent with Depression-era urban poverty. Multiple unhealed orbital and nasal fractures from street fights. Significance: his body was, for ~20 years, an unreliable instrument and a source of shame and constraint. The psychology of someone who has lived inside a failing body does not vanish when the body is replaced; it sediments as a permanent ethical orientation toward the vulnerable.

**Project Rebirth (Erskine serum + Vita-Rays).** Effects across canon: peak human (Stern era) to low-superhuman (modern); enhanced healing; near-immunity to infection, intoxication, and fatigue toxins; cellular near-stasis (basis for ice survival); ~95% efficient metabolism. Critical clinical implication: he cannot get drunk (*Captain America Annual* #4 and repeated gags) — a coping channel available to every other veteran in the canon is foreclosed to him. He cannot pharmacologically blunt anything.

**Cryogenic preservation, 1945–present.** Suspended animation in North Atlantic ice. No conventional medical sequelae but the canonical equivalent of a profound dissociative dislocation: he goes to sleep in one world and wakes in another. Treat as a discrete medical event with neuropsychiatric, not somatic, downstream cost.

**Major traumatic injuries.** Repeated concussions, gunshots, stabbings, energy-weapon burns, broken bones — all healed without sequelae. The relevant clinical fact is *invulnerability-adjacent self-perception*: he has decades of evidence that his body will tolerate what kills others, which licenses self-sacrificial behavior that would, in a non-enhanced subject, read as suicidality.

**Substance exposures.** Streets of Poison (Gruenwald): non-consensual exposure to "Ice"; serum eventually purges it; subject thereafter refuses caffeine, alcohol equivalents, and any pharmacological mood agent — a phobic-grade avoidance traceable to the experience of *not recognizing himself* under intoxication. Super-Soldier Serum itself is a chronic somatic exposure; its periodic destabilization (*Captain America* vol. 5 "Reborn"; Spencer "de-serumed" arc, where Steve aged into a frail nonagenarian) tends to be borne with eerie equanimity — he reverts to his pre-1941 body and is, behaviorally, recognizably the same person. Strong evidence that the serum amplified but did not constitute him.

**Aging.** Effectively retarded. The medical reality of outliving his cohort — Peggy's dementia and death (*Captain America* vol. 5 #14, and reiterated elsewhere) — is a chronic somatic-existential exposure: he visits the woman he loved in a care facility where she does not always know him.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Reconstructed from the record: early attachment with Sarah Rogers reads as secure-but-overburdened — she was loving, ill, and overworked; he learned love as caretaking-under-strain. Joseph Rogers reads as a frightening/unavailable figure (alcohol, violence) who modeled the negative template Steve organizes his adult identity against. Resulting adult pattern: *earned secure* attachment with a strong caregiver tilt — he forms deep, loyal bonds (Bucky, Peggy, Sam Wilson, Sharon Carter, Natasha, Tony pre-Civil War) and tolerates enormous interpersonal cost to maintain them, but those bonds disproportionately position him as the giver, the rescuer, the one who absorbs the other's danger. Evidence: refusal to abandon Bucky/Winter Soldier despite tactical cost; taking responsibility for Walker; the *manner* of his Civil War surrender (he stops fighting the moment civilians become collateral — relational ethics overriding strategic logic).

**Defenses (mature-spectrum, with specific costs).** Predominantly mature defenses: sublimation (the work of being Captain America metabolizes grief and rage into protective action); altruism (gives away what he cannot keep for himself); humor (dry, self-deprecating, especially across Waid and Brubaker); suppression (deliberately defers feeling until an operationally appropriate moment — which, in his case, rarely arrives). The neurotic-spectrum defense that recurs is *reaction formation* against helplessness: any situation in which he is constrained from acting produces disproportionate distress (Civil War; Registration; his behavior when held captive). The immature defense visible only under extreme load is *isolation of affect* — he reports losses in a flat, briefing-room register (his account of Bucky's death in *Avengers* #56, his account of Peggy's death in vol. 5 #14). This is not absence of feeling; it is feeling so encysted that it can only be addressed in oblique form.

**Affect regulation.** Exceptionally high baseline control. Anger is the affect he is most cautious with — repeatedly across writers he flags it as the affect that, unchecked, would make him his father or make him Walker. Grief is chronic, low-grade, ego-syntonic; he does not experience it as pathology because it never leaves and he has built his life around it. The Streets of Poison arc is the canonical demonstration of what he is regulating: stripped of inhibition, he is brutal. The control is real, effortful, and load-bearing.

**Cognitive style.** Concrete, principled, and forward-projecting; not rigid (he updates — see his evolution on race, on women in combat, on Sam Wilson as successor, on his own role in institutional violence across Coates) but slow-and-deep rather than fast-and-flexible. He prefers to be the last to speak in strategy sessions (Hickman *Avengers*). He reasons morally before he reasons tactically, which is sometimes a strategic vulnerability (Zemo and the Red Skull both exploit it) and structurally a strategic asset (it makes his commitments legible and thus coalitional).

**Self-concept.** Two-layered. Surface: a soldier doing a job. Underlying: the sick kid from Brooklyn who got lucky and owes the debt forever. He does not believe he is Captain America in the sense the public believes it; he believes he is the custodian of a promise made to Erskine and a debt owed to everyone whose war he survived. This is the operative engine of survivor guilt and the reason the identity cannot be retired voluntarily — to lay it down would be to default on the dead.

**Compensatory mechanisms.** Positive: extraordinary work capacity, mentorship (Bucky, Rick Jones, Sam, Walker, Ian, Kate Bishop in some configurations, Miles), institutional loyalty calibrated to ethics not authority. Negative: chronic over-functioning; emotional opacity to intimates; difficulty receiving care; tendency to interpret personal needs as luxuries to be deferred indefinitely; romantic life characterized by partners (Peggy, Sharon, Bernie, Rachel, Diamondback) who either match his risk profile or are kept at distance by it.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Diagnoses are derived; etiology and behavioral exemplars given.

**Complicated/Prolonged Grief Disorder** (ICD-11 6B42; DSM-5-TR Prolonged Grief Disorder). Strongly supported. Per Pop-Jordanova (2021), complicated grief is a superimposed prolongation of mourning intensified by sudden/traumatic loss and avoidance. Steve's losses meet every aggravator: sudden (Bucky's apparent death; the ice; Erskine), traumatic (combat, assassination of mentor, his own assassination and resurrection), cumulative (entire cohort), and structurally avoidant (he metabolizes grief through duty rather than processing it). Behavioral evidence: decades of unresolved Bucky-grief that organizes his identity (Avengers #56 onward); ritualized visits to Peggy; recurring nightmares (Waid, Brubaker); the equanimity-bordering-on-relief with which he meets his own deaths.

**Posttraumatic Stress Disorder, chronic, with delayed and recurrent expression.** Supported. Criteria evidence across canon: intrusive recollection (Bucky's fall — depicted in nightmares from Stern onward); avoidance of grief-cue contexts (won't discuss the war casually; off-register when forced to); negative alterations in cognition (survivor guilt; persistent belief he should have died instead — explicit in Brubaker #14); hyperarousal expressed as vigilance rather than reactivity (sleeps lightly, scans rooms, never fully off-duty). Atypical presentation: hyperarousal is harnessed not floridly symptomatic, because the work absorbs it. The diagnosis is real but adaptive-presenting.

**Adjustment disorder, chronic, with mixed features (the "Man Out of Time" syndrome).** A pervasive sub-syndromal acculturation distress visible from Avengers #4 through Waid's *Man Out of Time* and into modern runs. He has adapted *behaviorally* but the existential dislocation is permanent and untreated.

**Major Depressive Disorder, recurrent, in partial remission.** Supported episodically (post-Bucky, post-Peggy, post-Civil War, post-Secret Empire). Not chronic — interepisode functioning is high — but the episodes are real and the self-report (when extracted) is consistent with depressive cognition narrowed to self-blame rather than anhedonia.

**Differential — what he is NOT.**

- *Not OCPD,* despite the regimented life: his rigor is value-driven and he tolerates ambiguity in others without contempt. Rule-following is moral, not characterological.
- *Not narcissistic.* The iconography invites the misread; the behavior refutes it. He defers, he yields the spotlight (Sam Wilson succession), he forgives Walker, he can be argued out of positions by subordinates. No grandiosity, no exploitation, persistent over-empathy.
- *Not antisocial / not the militarized-aggression profile* even though combat-trained from age 22 with no peer; Streets of Poison demonstrates what removing his inhibition looks like and how much he fears it.
- *Not depressive personality:* baseline mood is engaged, often warm; the depression is reactive.
- *Not dissociative disorder* despite extreme dislocations; identity is continuous across ice, Dimension Z, Reborn, de-serumed, and the Hydra-Steve restoration — he reliably returns to the same self.
- *Not suicidal,* though his risk-taking superficially resembles it. The distinction: he takes mortal risks *in pursuit of others' survival*; he has never sought death for its own sake and has multiple on-panel choices for life when offered (the most explicit being his refusal to stay dead in *Reborn* once a path back exists).

## 5. PERSONALITY — SYNTHESIS

**Core drives (in order of operational priority).**
1. Protect the vulnerable — the originating drive, predating the serum, traceable to the sick boy who could not protect his mother from his father or himself from the alley.
2. Honor the dead — the debt-structure that makes the identity non-negotiable.
3. Bear witness to the principle — when institutions fail, he embodies the principle in his own person (Nomad, The Captain, Civil War insurgency) so that the principle survives the institution.
4. Belong to a unit — the surrogate-family need, downstream of early loss; satisfied by Howlers, Avengers, Invaders, the modern teams.

**Worldview / operational code.** Moral realism without cynicism. He believes goods and harms are real, that institutions are instruments and not ends, that loyalty is owed to persons and principles and only conditionally to organizations, and that the only durable response to power is constraint by conscience — *his own first*. He is genuinely democratic in temperament (he takes orders, he yields command, he submits to lawful process) right up to the point at which lawful process becomes the instrument of harm, at which point he becomes implacable.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm, attentive, slightly formal; reads others well; gives more than he asks; tolerates being misunderstood; struggles to receive care and to articulate need. Forms few intimate bonds and never severs them voluntarily — every major break in his life (Tony, Sharon at various points, the government) is initiated by the other party. With subordinates and successors: patient, generous, capable of stepping aside. With adversaries: respectful where possible (Namor, Magneto in some configurations), unsparing where necessary (Red Skull, Zemo), forgiving where there is repair (Walker, Bucky-as-Winter-Soldier).

**Stress response and escalation curve.** Baseline: calm, observant, dry-humored. Early stress: increased control, decreased disclosure, more work. Mid stress: terse, tactically narrow, emotionally inaccessible to intimates. High stress: morally absolutist, physically forward, willing to absorb consequence himself rather than delegate it. Decompensation (rare): the Streets of Poison register — unmodulated aggression — or, alternatively, the Reborn/post-loss register — temporary depressive withdrawal. He does not break loudly; he goes quiet and then he goes alone.

**Pressure points (for the synthesis stage to weight).**
- Threats to non-combatants in his vicinity — he will trade himself instantly and predictably.
- Betrayal by institutions he has lent his name to — produces moral injury, not rage; the response is withdrawal of legitimacy, then opposition.
- Being prevented from acting while others are harmed — his single least-tolerable condition; the engine of most of his confrontations with authority.
- Reminders of Bucky, Peggy, Erskine, Sarah — produce the flat, encysted affect that signals the unmetabolized grief beneath the function.
- Loss of self-control (intoxication, mind-control, Hydra-Steve) — phobic-grade, because his entire adaptation rests on inhibitory mastery.
- Asking him to retire the identity — equivalent to asking him to default on the dead; he cannot, and the cost of demanding it is the loss of him as an ally.

**The synthesizing read.** Rogers is a high-functioning, mature-defended, chronically grieving moral exemplar whose pathology is invisible because it has been seamlessly converted into vocation. The serum did not make him; it made him *legible*. What is actually load-bearing is the ethical posture of the sick boy in the alley, sustained for eighty subjective years across two centuries of calendar time, fed by an inexhaustible supply of losses he has never had the safety, the substance, or the permission to mourn.
