# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: AARON DAVIS / THE PROWLER

*Classification: Pre-Profile Intelligence Workup — Independent Assessment*

---

## 1. HISTORY

Aaron Davis is a creation of the Ultimate Marvel imprint (Bendis/Pichelli, *Ultimate Comics: Spider-Man* #1, 2011), retained and recontextualized for the prime Marvel Universe via *Spider-Man* (2016) and the cultural breakout via *Into the Spider-Verse*. The behavioral record is comparatively short but unusually dense, because nearly every appearance is a pressure-test of a single fault line: the man's loyalty to his nephew versus his criminal appetite.

**Origin pattern — the Davis brothers.** Aaron is consistently introduced as the elder, "sharper," more transgressive brother to Jefferson Davis. In Ultimate continuity, the brothers' father was abusive; both boys came up in the same household but split. Jefferson chose the straight path (in Ultimate, a recovering criminal turned legitimate; in 616/Spider-Verse, a cop or security officer). Aaron chose theft, escalation, and the costumed identity of Prowler — stealing the suit/tech from the original Hobie Brown lineage in some tellings, building his own kit in others. This is the foundational behavioral datum: **given identical inputs, Aaron consistently chose the higher-risk, higher-reward, lower-accountability path.** Not impulsively — strategically. He is not a brawler; he is a cat burglar, infiltrator, fence, and tech-thief.

**The Osborn job (Ultimate Comics: Spider-Man #1-5).** Aaron breaks into Oscorp to steal proprietary genetic research. The Oz-derived spider that bites Miles Morales is on Aaron's person — Miles is bitten in Aaron's apartment, during a visit. This is the inciting event of Miles's existence as Spider-Man, and Aaron is its unwitting vector. The behavioral tell here is small but important: Aaron lets a child into a space contaminated with stolen bio-research. Not malice — the carelessness of someone who has compartmentalized "uncle" away from "operator" so thoroughly he doesn't audit overlap.

**The Scorpion arc / blackmail of Miles.** Once Aaron realizes Miles has powers, his behavior is diagnostic. He does not protect; he does not warn; he does not ask. He **leverages**. He coerces Miles into helping him fight the Scorpion for control of New York's criminal market, threatening to expose Miles to his father if he refuses. This is the cleanest behavioral evidence in the canon: in a forced choice between the welfare of a beloved child and a tactical advantage, Aaron took the advantage and rationalized it as mentorship ("I'm teaching you the game"). He dies in that arc when his own Prowler tech misfires — a death he caused by escalating against his own nephew.

**616 resurrection and "reformation" (Spider-Man 2016–, Bendis).** When Aaron reappears in mainline continuity, the writing arc is ostensibly redemptive: he claims to be done with crime, wants a real relationship with Miles, occasionally helps. The behavioral record is more equivocal. He repeatedly relapses — taking jobs, running schemes, getting entangled with the Tinkerer, with Hammerhead, with Iron Spider tech theft. The pattern is **cyclical**: contrition, sincere overture, opportunity presents, rationalization ("one last score," "this one is different," "I'm doing it for Miles"), relapse, damage control, contrition. Classic addictive-criminal cycle, not a character genuinely in remission.

**Spider-Verse / Homecoming portrayals.** The MCU *Homecoming* cameo and the *Spider-Verse* film treatment crystallize the same dyad: Aaron *can* perform avuncular warmth with complete authenticity (the "what's up with the ladies" scene is not a performance — he is genuinely tender), and Aaron *will* later attempt to murder his nephew under a mask because the contract is the contract. The films dramatize what the comics establish: **the two selves are not in conflict inside him; they are sealed off from each other until they collide externally.**

**Evolution across eras.** Early Bendis Aaron: pure antagonist, opportunistic predator wearing the mask of family. Later Bendis Aaron: softened, struggling, more tragic — a man who knows what he is and cannot stop being it. Spider-Verse Aaron: tragic-hero finish, dies refusing to kill Miles, the *one* moment where the loyalty axis wins outright. The arc across the canon is from sociopathic opportunist to **akratic** figure — a man whose values and behavior have a permanent gap he cannot close.

## 2. MEDICAL

No documented chronic illness, no canonical substance dependency in the pharmacological sense, no disabling injury that persists. Aaron is presented as physically prime: lean, agile, high cardiovascular and gymnastic conditioning consistent with parkour-style infiltration work.

**Augmentation profile.** The Prowler identity is entirely **exo-augmented**, not metahuman. His capability stack: pneumatic grapnel gauntlets, magnetic climbing claws, optical/EM cloaking in later iterations, vibration daggers, often a visor with thermal and counter-surveillance optics. This matters psychologically: every threat he poses is **removable**. He is a man who built his danger; he was not given it. This shapes self-concept (see §3).

**Injuries of record.** Ultimate-continuity death by his own malfunctioning gauntlet during the Scorpion fight — self-inflicted via equipment he over-trusted. 616 incarnation has accumulated standard criminal-life trauma: gunshot wounds, beatings, concussive blows from costumed encounters, none disabling. No traumatic brain injury arc has been written, but the cumulative load is non-trivial and consistent with mid-career operators who present with subtle executive-function decline and irritability.

**Childhood somatic history.** Inferred only: an abusive paternal household (canonically referenced for both Davis brothers) implies childhood exposure to physical violence, hypervigilance conditioning, and likely elevated baseline cortisol architecture — the somatic substrate of the adult's preternatural calm under threat and his comfort with violence as a transactional medium.

**Substance use.** Not depicted as a user. The "addiction" in Aaron is behavioral — the score, the risk, the thrill — not chemical. Worth flagging that behavioral-process addictions (gambling, theft, risk) produce similar reward-circuit conditioning and relapse profiles to substance dependencies, and Aaron's cyclical pattern in 616 fits that template precisely.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Disorganized leaning toward dismissive-avoidant, with one preserved secure-ish anchor (Miles, and to a lesser degree Jefferson). The abusive father generates the classic split: the child learns that the caregiver is both source of safety and source of harm, and the adaptive response is to **decouple intimacy from dependency**. Aaron's adult relational style reflects this exactly — he can be warm, present, even tender in discrete encounters, but he does not organize his life around anyone. He visits. He drops in. He disappears. Even his love for Miles is **episodic** rather than structural; it does not constrain his choices, it only colors them.

**Defenses (in rough order of prominence).**
- **Compartmentalization** (primary). The "Uncle Aaron" self and the "Prowler" self are walled. This is not dissociation in the clinical sense; he has full continuity of memory and identity. It is a voluntary, well-defended psychological architecture that allows him to mean every word he says to Miles and also coerce Miles the next week without experiencing the contradiction as unbearable.
- **Rationalization.** "I'm teaching him." "One last job." "I'm doing it for family." Every relapse is pre-justified.
- **Minimization.** Of harm done, of risk imposed on others, of his brother's legitimate grievances.
- **Identification with the aggressor.** The abusive father is not escaped but partially internalized — Aaron's coercive use of Miles structurally mirrors paternal coercion, even as he would consciously reject the comparison.
- **Reaction formation in later arcs** — the "trying to be good" 616 Aaron is, in part, a defense against the shame of who he has been; it functions as much to manage his own self-image as to actually change behavior.

**Affect regulation.** Excellent under acute stress; poor under chronic boredom. He is calm in burglaries, fights, negotiations, standoffs. He decompensates when life becomes domestic, low-stimulus, accountable. The criminal life is, among other things, an **affect-regulation strategy** — it keeps his nervous system in the arousal band it was conditioned for in childhood.

**Cognitive style.** Tactically excellent, strategically poor. He plans heists with real sophistication. He cannot plan a life. Time horizon collapses past about ninety days. High pattern recognition for threat and opportunity; low pattern recognition for his own recurring self-sabotage.

**Self-concept.** This is the load-bearing wound. Aaron's self-narrative is "the smart brother, the one who saw the game for what it was, the one who refused to be a sucker." This identity *requires* him to keep operating; if he goes straight, he becomes Jefferson — and becoming Jefferson means admitting Jefferson was right, which means the entire life-shape was a mistake. **The criminal identity is load-bearing for his ego; he cannot dismantle it without ego collapse.** This is why every reform attempt fails: he is not trying to change behavior, he is trying to keep the identity *and* perform the behavior change, which is structurally impossible.

**Compensatory mechanisms.**
- *Positive:* the mentorship of Miles, when sincere, is a genuine attempt at generativity — passing on competence to the next generation, an Eriksonian late-stage move he is reaching for prematurely and inconsistently.
- *Negative:* the costume itself. The Prowler identity is a compensatory grandiose self — the masked competent operator who is feared, respected, and *seen*, which the unmasked Aaron, the failed brother, the absent uncle, is not.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Working differential, derived strictly from behavioral evidence.

**Primary: Antisocial Personality Disorder, moderate severity, with preserved selective attachment.** Evidence: longstanding pattern of unlawful behavior, deceitfulness, impulsivity in life-planning (not in tactics), disregard for others' safety (Miles in the apartment; Miles coerced into Scorpion fight), consistent irresponsibility regarding family obligations, lack of remorse that survives moments of expressed remorse (the relapses are the tell — verbalized regret without behavioral correction is the diagnostic signature). The crucial nuance: he is **not** a Hare-checklist psychopath. He has genuine affective bonds (Miles, Jefferson), capacity for guilt, capacity for self-sacrifice (Spider-Verse death scene). This rules out primary psychopathy and places him in the **secondary/sociopathic** band — antisocial traits shaped by developmental adversity rather than constitutional affective flatness.

**Secondary: Behavioral-process addiction (criminal-thrill subtype).** The cyclical relapse pattern in 616 is textbook addiction architecture: cue-triggered craving (a job comes in), rationalization, use, post-use shame, vow of abstinence, repeat. Treating Aaron as "a bad guy who keeps choosing wrong" misses the mechanism; he is using crime the way a gambler uses the table.

**Tertiary / contributory: Complex post-traumatic features from childhood abuse.** Not full PTSD — no documented flashbacks, intrusion, or hyperarousal in the clinical sense — but the developmental trauma footprint is visible in the attachment style, the affect-regulation profile, and the identification with the aggressor.

**Differential — what he is NOT:**
- *Not Narcissistic PD.* He does not require admiration, does not present grandiosely outside the mask, tolerates being underestimated, even cultivates it.
- *Not Borderline PD.* Identity is stable (over-stable, in fact); affect is regulated, not labile; no abandonment terror — he is the one who leaves.
- *Not primary psychopathy.* The Miles bond is real and behaviorally costly; he dies for it in the Spider-Verse version. A psychopath does not pay that price.
- *Not Dissociative Identity.* The two selves are voluntarily walled, not split; continuity of memory and agency is intact.
- *Not Substance Use Disorder* in the chemical sense.

**Etiology.** Abusive paternal household producing (a) insecure attachment, (b) early normalization of violence as currency, (c) identity-forming sibling differentiation in which Aaron took the "tough/smart" role and Jefferson took the "good" role — a split that became ossified and self-reinforcing across decades. The criminal identity is overdetermined: it serves trauma-mastery, identity-coherence, affect-regulation, and economic function simultaneously, which is why it is so resistant to extinction.

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drives, in order:** (1) preservation of the self-concept as "the one who sees the game" — non-negotiable, ego-load-bearing; (2) the dopaminergic loop of the score — the planning, the entry, the take; (3) the bond with Miles, and through Miles a partial restoration of the bond with Jefferson — real, but ranked below the first two and therefore consistently sacrificed to them; (4) operational autonomy — he will work with crews, fences, and even bosses, but he resents being owned, and the resentment eventually surfaces as betrayal.

**Worldview / operational code.** The world is a rigged game. The straight life is for people who haven't figured that out, or who have figured it out and lack the nerve to act on it. Loyalty is real but local — family, a small handful of names, nothing larger. Institutions are marks. Cops are weather. Heroes are interesting variables, occasionally useful, ultimately obstacles. Violence is a tool, not an appetite; he prefers stealth and would rather not hurt anyone, but he will, cleanly, if the job requires it. **He is not cruel. He is transactional.** That distinction matters: cruelty would make him predictable in one way; transactionality makes him predictable in another, and the second is more dangerous to people who think they are inside his circle.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Disarming. Warm on contact, easy laugh, reads a room fast, makes the person across from him feel uniquely seen. Builds rapport with terrifying efficiency. Then — and this is the pattern — at the decision point where rapport competes with advantage, advantage wins, and he handles the rapport-cost as a manageable expense rather than a moral injury. With Miles specifically, this pattern is dampened but not eliminated; Miles gets more grace than anyone else and still gets used.

**Stress / escalation profile.** Low baseline reactivity. Does not bluster, does not escalate verbally, does not telegraph. Under pressure he gets *quieter* and more precise, which is the operationally dangerous direction. The single reliable trigger for visible dysregulation is **shame in front of Miles or Jefferson** — being seen, by the people whose opinion still matters to him, as what he actually is. He cannot tolerate this and will either flee the encounter or weaponize it (going harder into the criminal posture as a defense against the exposure).

**Pressure points.**
- The Jefferson comparison. Any framing that positions him as the failed brother to a successful brother bypasses his defenses.
- Miles's safety, *specifically when caused by his own action.* He can rationalize endangering Miles abstractly; confronted with concrete harm he caused, the rationalization breaks and produces either genuine sacrificial behavior (Spider-Verse) or catastrophic shame-flight.
- Being handled by a larger criminal organization. He reads it as ownership, and ownership is the one thing his identity cannot metabolize. Bosses who think they have him have a finite window before he moves against them.
- Boredom. A pacified Aaron is a relapsing Aaron. Remove the stimulus and the addiction architecture lights up.

**Synthesis.** Aaron Davis is a high-functioning, developmentally-shaped antisocial operator with a preserved capacity for love that he routes through, rather than against, his pathology. He is not a villain who happens to have a nephew; he is a man whose nephew is the one piece of his life his identity-defense cannot fully metabolize, and the entire dramatic engine of the character is the friction between an unyielding self-concept and a love that should, but does not, override it. The tragedy is not that he is bad. The tragedy is that he is *almost* able to be otherwise, repeatedly, and the architecture of who he made himself into will not let him cross the gap.
