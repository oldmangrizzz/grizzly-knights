# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: ELEANOR BISHOP

**Subject designation:** Eleanor Bishop, civilian; mother to Kate Bishop (Hawkeye II); wife of Derek Bishop; sister-in-law to Susan Bishop.

---

## 1. HISTORY

Eleanor Bishop is a relatively late-canon addition to the Marvel record, but the published material that exists is dense and deliberately revisionist — each appearance retroactively rewrites what we thought we knew about her. The analytical record must therefore be read in two registers: the *apparent* Eleanor (as Kate remembered her) and the *operative* Eleanor (as gradually revealed).

**Era One — The Absent Mother (Matt Fraction / David Aja *Hawkeye* run, 2012–2015; references back through Heinberg's *Young Avengers*, 2005–2006).** In Kate Bishop's earliest published characterization, Eleanor is a grief-shaped absence. She died — apparently — when Kate was a child, and Kate's entire self-construction is built on that loss: the wealth-and-trauma cocktail of an emotionally remote, possibly criminal father, the home invasion/sexual assault Kate suffered as a young teen, and a mother who was simply not there to intervene. Eleanor is invoked in flashback and dialogue as warm, idealized, lost. Kate's archery, her drive, her insistence on independence from her father's money — all of it is in the negative space Eleanor left.

**Era Two — The Returning Mother (Kelly Thompson *Hawkeye* / *West Coast Avengers* / *Hawkeye: Kate Bishop*, 2016–2022).** Eleanor reappears alive. The reveal is staged across Thompson's runs: Kate is hired, courted, manipulated. Eleanor explains her absence with a narrative of having faked her death to escape Derek (framed as protective), then claims she has been managing the family's affairs from the shadows. She offers Kate reconciliation, resources, structure. Kate — starved for exactly this — accepts in stages, then increasingly recoils as Eleanor's "management" becomes coercive: Eleanor tries to control Kate's casework, her finances, her romantic life, and her vigilante identity.

**Era Three — The Madame Masque Entanglement / The Reveal.** Across Thompson's arcs, Eleanor is shown to have made a deal with Madame Masque (Whitney Frost), trading something — leverage, money, access — for protection or for the means of her "death." She is in debt to organized criminal-mystical actors, and Kate is, in part, the collateral. The pivotal beat is Eleanor being revealed not as a victim of Derek but as a co-architect of the family's corruption, willing to use Kate as currency in adult bargains Kate never consented to. Eleanor's apologies in this period are characteristically structured: explanation, justification, request for trust, deflection of agency onto Derek or Masque.

**Era Four — Estrangement and Conditional Reentry (*Hawkeye: Kate Bishop* miniseries; supporting beats in *Marvel's Voices*, event tie-ins).** Kate establishes hard distance. Eleanor's later appearances are characterized by attempts at controlled re-approach — gift-giving, indirect surveillance through family channels, performative concern broadcast through Susan or other intermediaries. Kate's stance hardens into an adult, ambivalent estrangement: she will not pretend Eleanor is a stranger, but she refuses the role of redeemed daughter.

**Evolution of pattern.** Across these eras the through-line is *control mediated by sentiment*. Early-Eleanor's idealization is a function of her absence (Kate could fill the gap with anything). Returning-Eleanor immediately reaches for the levers a wealthy parent has — money, lawyers, leverage on Kate's friends and lovers — while speaking the language of maternal warmth. The Masque arc demonstrates that this is not a recent corruption; the willingness to trade family for position predates Kate's adulthood. The pattern does not escalate so much as *reveal*: each story peels a layer.

## 2. MEDICAL

The record is thin on somatic detail, which is itself diagnostically relevant — Eleanor is a non-powered, non-augmented human who has navigated a world of enhanced threats by proxy and capital.

- **Faked death.** The mechanism is never fully detailed but implicates either pharmacological staging, accomplice in a medical/coroner role, or both. Whatever the method, Eleanor was willing to undergo bodily risk and prolonged identity suppression to execute the disappearance. This suggests baseline physical hardiness and high tolerance for somatic discomfort in service of a plan.
- **No documented enhancements, no combat injuries.** Eleanor does not fight. She does not bleed in the record. Her body is, operationally, a *non-instrument*; her instruments are other bodies (Kate's, Madame Masque's enforcers', lawyers').
- **Substance use.** Not depicted at clinical levels. Social alcohol consistent with her class register. No evidence of dependence.
- **Age cohort.** Mid-to-late life; presents as well-maintained, conventionally controlled appearance — grooming and dress as managed surface, consistent with someone whose self-presentation *is* her primary professional skill.
- **Psychological impact of the somatic record.** The faked-death period is the most consequential medical fact: years of living under suppressed identity, no children, no spousal contact, presumably under assumed name and possibly under Masque-adjacent protection. This is a sustained dissociative-by-circumstance state — not a clinical dissociative disorder, but a prolonged enactment of *erasure-of-self-for-survival* that almost certainly hardened her capacity for compartmentalization and reduced her affective access to the costs her absence imposed on Kate. You cannot live as your own ghost for years and return with intact maternal instinct; the muscle atrophies.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Eleanor's behavior toward Kate is the diagnostic centerpiece. She returns expecting access; she experiences Kate's autonomy as injury; she reaches for control reflexively and codes it as love. This is the textbook signature of **insecure-preoccupied attachment expressed through a parental role** — attachment hunger that cannot tolerate the separateness of the attached object. Her own attachment history is not on the page, but her adult template (marriage to a man she ultimately needed to escape; alliance with a figure as predatory as Madame Masque) suggests an originating pattern of attaching to powerful, dangerous figures and negotiating safety from inside their orbit rather than outside it.

**Defenses.** The dominant defensive architecture is **rationalization stacked on splitting**. Eleanor consistently presents her decisions as the only available choices ("I did it to protect you," "Derek left me no option," "Masque would have killed us both"). The splitting is between an *innocent-victim Eleanor* and a *forced-hand Eleanor*, with no integrated agent in between. Secondary defenses include **reaction formation** (performances of maternal warmth precisely when she is most instrumentalizing Kate) and **projective identification** (locating her own controlling impulses in Derek and then claiming to fight them).

**Affect regulation.** High surface control; low tolerance for being contradicted by Kate specifically. Eleanor does not rage outwardly in the record — she escalates by *tightening*: more lawyers, more conditions, more surveillance, colder warmth. This is regulation through environmental control rather than internal soothing.

**Cognitive style.** Strategic, long-horizon, transactional. She thinks in terms of arrangements, leverage, ledgers. She is not impulsive. The faked-death plan, the Masque bargain, the staged reentry into Kate's life — all are multi-year operations. Her cognition is closer to a fund manager's than a criminal's: she does not enjoy the manipulation, she optimizes it.

**Self-concept.** The working self-image appears to be *the competent woman who held the family together while others (Derek, Kate) acted out*. This self-image requires constant external validation and collapses when Kate names the actual structure ("you used me"). Eleanor's responses to such naming are characteristically *injured* rather than *defensive*, which is itself the tell — she experiences accurate description of her behavior as an attack on her identity.

**Compensatory mechanisms.** *Positive:* genuine competence, the capacity to plan, the ability to survive Derek and Masque without becoming either. *Negative:* the conversion of every relationship into a managed account; the inability to let Kate be a person rather than a project; the use of maternal language as a control vocabulary.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Working diagnostically from behavior, not from label:

**Primary impression: Narcissistic Personality features, vulnerable subtype, with prominent obsessional/controlling traits.** The evidentiary basis: (a) consistent prioritization of her own narrative and survival over Kate's developmental needs, including the foundational decision to fake her death without provision for the child; (b) inability to tolerate Kate's separate selfhood without experiencing it as rejection; (c) characteristic injured-party affect when accurately described; (d) covert rather than grandiose presentation — Eleanor does not boast, she martyrs. The vulnerable/covert narcissistic presentation specifically tracks better than grandiose NPD because Eleanor's currency is *being seen as having sacrificed*, not as having triumphed.

**Co-occurring features consistent with traits from Cluster B more broadly, particularly antisocial-spectrum instrumentality** — the Masque bargain involving Kate as collateral is not within normal-range parental decision-making, even under duress. It indicates a willingness to convert a child into an asset that exceeds the explanatory power of NPD alone. However, full Antisocial Personality Disorder is *not* supported: Eleanor shows no thrill-seeking, no pervasive disregard for law (she works *through* legal and financial structures), and apparent capacity for selective attachment.

**Differential — what Eleanor is NOT:**
- *Not Borderline.* Affect is too regulated, identity too stable, relationships too strategically maintained. No self-harm record, no abandonment-panic behavior; she is the abandoner.
- *Not Histrionic.* She does not seek attention; she seeks control. Her emotional displays are targeted, not diffuse.
- *Not a true sociopath/full ASPD.* She is capable of selective genuine investment (the marriage was real before it wasn't; the maternal feeling toward Kate is real even where it is also instrumentalizing). Full ASPD would not produce the *quality* of her injured affect when Kate withdraws.
- *Not Munchausen-by-proxy / Factitious.* She does not generate crises in Kate to nurse them; she generates structures and offers them as gifts.

**Etiology (inferential).** Likely originating in a family-of-origin where love was conditional on usefulness and where female agency was negotiated through alliance with powerful men. The marriage to Derek reads as a continuation of that template; the Masque deal as a desperate lateral move when the original alliance failed. Kate, born into this, was always going to be both genuinely loved and structurally available as currency, because Eleanor's emotional vocabulary did not contain a third option.

**Trauma layer.** The years living as her own ghost are independently traumatizing and produced (or deepened) a dissociative-flavored capacity to act against Kate's interests without full affective registration of doing so. This is *not* DID; it is the ordinary numbing of someone who has practiced not-feeling for too long.

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drives.** Security through control of the surrounding field; preservation of a self-image as the competent sufferer who did what had to be done; reabsorption of Kate into a managed orbit, coded as love but functioning as proof of Eleanor's centrality.

**Operational code.** *Everything is an arrangement.* Relationships are positions in a portfolio; threats are counterparties to negotiate with or remove; affection is a legitimate currency and so is fear. The world divides into people who can be managed and people who must be escaped, and the work of life is to keep moving everyone into the first category.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm-presenting, attentive in detail, generous in resource, *cold in the architecture underneath*. Eleanor remembers what you like. Eleanor also remembers what you owe. With Kate specifically: alternation between gift-giving/closeness-bids and pressure/conditional withdrawal, calibrated to keep Kate oriented toward her without quite letting Kate name what is happening. With peers and adversaries: transactional civility, durable grudges, preference for indirect action through retained professionals.

**Stress response and escalation ladder.** Eleanor does not decompensate loudly. Under pressure she (1) increases performance of warmth, (2) increases material offers, (3) deploys intermediaries (lawyers, family, allies), (4) tightens information control, (5) accepts strategic losses to preserve the long position. She will cut a limb to keep the body. She does not rage; she ledgers.

**Pressure points.**
- *Being accurately named.* When Kate articulates the structure of Eleanor's behavior in plain language, Eleanor's defenses strain visibly — the injured-party affect is the audible crack.
- *Loss of the maternal frame.* Eleanor needs to be Kate's mother in a recognizable sense; total estrangement that refuses even the title would be more destabilizing than open conflict.
- *Exposure of the Masque bargain in its full shape.* The reframing of herself as victim depends on the bargain being legible as duress; rigorous accounting of what she traded and when would collapse the self-narrative.
- *Being out-strategized by Kate.* Eleanor's self-image requires being the adult in the room; an arrangement in which Kate manages *her* would be intolerable.

**Synthesis for downstream profile work.** Eleanor Bishop is best understood not as a villain in the costumed sense but as a particular kind of damaged competent — a woman whose survival strategies were forged in a marriage and a milieu that rewarded the conversion of intimacy into leverage, and who returned to her daughter's life genuinely loving her and genuinely unable to stop instrumentalizing her, because she has no other vocabulary for closeness. The danger she poses to Kate is not violence; it is the slow re-absorption of Kate's autonomy into Eleanor's management. The pathos is that Eleanor experiences this absorption as repair.
