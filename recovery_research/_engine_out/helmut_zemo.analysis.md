# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP — SUBJECT: HELMUT JULIUS FRITZ ZEMO (BARON ZEMO II)
## Pre-Synthesis Intelligence File

---

## 1. HISTORY — THE LONGITUDINAL BEHAVIORAL RECORD

**Origin context and inheritance (pre-debut backstory, established Captain America #168, 1973; expanded across the Gruenwald run and onward).** Helmut is the only son of Heinrich Zemo, the original Baron Zemo — the Nazi scientist whose Adhesive X fused his own mask permanently to his face during a melee with Captain America, and who was killed by Rogers in a South American jungle confrontation (Avengers #15, 1965). Helmut is raised on a country estate (originally Bavaria; in modernized treatment, the family's remaining holdings in Sokovia/the wider Carpathian sphere) inside a household organized around grievance, lineage, and the cult of the father. The 13th Baron Zemo is reared not as a child but as an heir to a feud. Formative load: aristocratic title without aristocratic legitimacy (the family was disgraced even within German nobility for collaborating with the Reich); a father he idealizes and almost never saw functional; a dead mother (Hilda Zemo) about whom canon is conspicuously silent, leaving him without a counterweight to paternal mythology.

**Phoenix debut (Captain America #168, 1973).** Helmut surfaces under the alias "the Phoenix," kidnaps Captain America, and attempts to drown him in a vat of Adhesive X — the same compound that mutilated his father. The choice of weapon is not tactical; it is **mimetic-symmetrical revenge** — he wants Rogers to become his father. He instead falls into the vat himself. His face is fused into a melted, scarred ruin. This is the foundational event of his adult life: an attempt to enact patrilineal vengeance produces an identical disfigurement, literalizing the inheritance.

→ The hood is born here. The purple cowl is not a costume choice; it is a **lid on the wound** — a covering for a face that now resembles the father's mask-fusion. The iconography itself is an unresolved identification with Heinrich.

**Reconstitution and the Masters of Evil (Avengers #273–277, "Under Siege," Stern/Buscema, 1986).** This is the analytic centerpiece. Helmut assembles the largest Masters of Evil roster ever fielded, infiltrates Avengers Mansion, captures Jarvis, and orchestrates his prolonged beating in front of a captive Captain America. He destroys personal artifacts in Rogers's quarters — photos of Sarah Rogers, sketches, the Bucky memorabilia — and forces Cap to watch. **Observable:** the operation is strategically brilliant but the climactic acts are not strategic; they are intimate, sadistic, theatrical. He wants Rogers to feel grief, not lose. When Cap finally breaks free, Zemo loses a rooftop fistfight and falls off the mansion — a fall he survives but that becomes the second mirror-event with his father (a great height, a confrontation with Rogers, an apparent death). Pattern: when Helmut's plans pivot from strategy to symbolism, he loses.

**Thunderbolts founding (Incredible Hulk #449, 1996; Thunderbolts #1, 1997, Busiek/Bagley).** Post-Onslaught, with the real Avengers and FF presumed dead, Helmut conceives and executes the single most audacious deception in the canon: he reskins the Masters of Evil as new heroes — Citizen V (himself), Atlas, MACH-1, Songbird, Meteorite, Techno — and lets a grateful public embrace them. The long game is total: install themselves as the world's trusted protectors, then seize global infrastructure. **Critical behavioral data:**
- The plan requires patience measurable in months of *being* a hero in public.
- He chooses the alias **Citizen V** — his grandfather's WWII identity, an anti-Nazi resistance fighter (Roger Aubrey lineage). He literally cosplays the moral opposite of his patrimony to manipulate a public.
- The deception works because Helmut is genuinely good at being heroic *as a performance*. He is a competent commander, makes correct tactical calls, saves civilians.
- It collapses (Thunderbolts #10) because his own teammates — having tasted being loved — refuse to revert. Helmut is **outcompeted by his own cover identity in the minds of the people he recruited.** He had not modeled that variable.

→ This is the most diagnostically rich sequence in the file. He can simulate virtue flawlessly at the level of behavior. What he cannot simulate is the *desire* to be virtuous, and he is genuinely surprised that others develop it.

**Citizen V & V-Battalion / Moonstone betrayals (Thunderbolts #25–50; Citizen V miniseries, Fabian Nicieza era).** Helmut pivots through multiple post-exposure arcs: deposed by Moonstone, exiled, drifts toward the V-Battalion (the legitimate Citizen V legacy organization), is rejected, returns to vengeance against his own former team. Pattern: when refused a role, he goes back to wounding.

**"Liberators" / Grandmaster / world-conquest arcs (Thunderbolts #50s–70s).** Repeated attempts at planetary control through energy-based superhuman empowerment of populations — the rationalization is always *imposed order, an end to chaos.* Operational code surfaces: he does not believe in democracy; he believes most humans are unfit to govern themselves and that an enlightened autocrat (himself) is humane. This is restated across multiple writers and is internally consistent — it is not a costume; it is the worldview.

**Civil War / Thunderbolts #110+ (Ellis run, 2006–2008).** Helmut becomes a more austere, philosophical figure. He confronts Moonstone and Songbird with what reads as **genuine ideological argument** rather than pure revenge. He is willing to be dethroned by Osborn (a man he correctly assesses as more unstable than himself). He goes underground.

**Born Better / time-travel arc (Thunderbolts: Reason in Madness; Gage, Diggle).** Helmut investigates the Zemo bloodline back to the Middle Ages and discovers his ancestors were almost uniformly monsters. He **chooses to continue the lineage anyway**, but with a redefined mission: not conquest but order. He sheds (briefly) the most overt sadism and reframes himself as a corrective force. Significant: he tells himself he is *better than his father* — the first time he has admitted Heinrich was wrong about anything.

**Modern era — Bucky / Winter Soldier arcs (Brubaker; later Zub, Spencer's Cap runs).** Helmut weaponizes Bucky's trigger-word conditioning, returns to surgical, intimate vengeance. He alternates between strategic-empire mode and personal-vendetta mode depending on writer; the alternation is itself the pattern. His face, having been partially restored and re-ruined across various arcs, has now stabilized into something he covers permanently. The hood is no longer trauma management; it is **brand and ritual.**

**Evolutionary arc summary:** Phoenix (raw vengeance) → Masters of Evil leader (organized vengeance) → Citizen V/Thunderbolts (the long con; ideology emerges) → Liberators-era (open authoritarian conviction) → Ellis-era (philosophical, world-weary, calmer) → modern (oscillation between ideologue and avenger depending on proximity to Cap/Bucky). The constant across 50 years: a man who **can do almost anything except let an injury go unanswered.**

## 2. MEDICAL

**Facial disfigurement (Captain America #168).** Adhesive X immersion produces full-face chemical/thermal scarring with permanent dermal distortion — tissue fused into a melted topography that does not resemble normal scarring (which would heal toward flatness). The compound's behavior in canon suggests a polymer cross-linked with subdermal tissue. Reconstructive options across canon: limited, repeatedly failed, occasionally partially restored by exotic means (Moonstone's stone, etc.) and re-destroyed. **Functional impact:** no recorded sensory or motor deficit; speech and expression intact beneath the mask. **The injury is therefore primarily semiotic, not functional.** This is critical — the hood is not medically necessary; it is psychologically necessary.

**Longevity / Compound X / chemical exposures.** Across various arcs Helmut has been exposed to or has self-administered life-extension compounds derived from his father's research. He shows no overt addictive pattern around them; use is instrumental and calculated. No evidence of pharmacological dependency.

**Combat injuries (catalogue).** Multiple high-altitude falls survived (Avengers Mansion roof; later arcs); blade wounds (he duels regularly with the family sword and accepts cuts as cost-of-business); concussive trauma from fistfights with super-soldiers. Notable absence of any record of him seeking medical care for emotional or psychiatric reasons in any canon.

**Somatic baseline.** Peak human conditioning maintained into late middle age via régime, not enhancement. Master swordsman (épée and saber), trained from boyhood. Multilingual to native fluency in at least German, English, French, Russian. Eidetic or near-eidetic recall demonstrated repeatedly (he reconstructs the Masters of Evil from memory; he recites Avengers tactical loadouts unprompted).

**Substance use.** Light, ceremonial — wine, brandy, in keeping with aristocratic performance. No canonical evidence of disordered use. Tobacco occasional. He is unusually disciplined about anything that would dull cognition — which is itself diagnostic; the **mind is the prized organ** and he protects it the way an athlete protects a throwing arm.

**Psychiatric care history.** None on record. He has been imprisoned multiple times and has presumably been evaluated by institutional psychiatry; he routinely outperforms his evaluators in those sequences (Thunderbolts: Faith in Monsters–era prison material). He does not engage honestly with clinicians; he studies them.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL — DERIVED FROM THE RECORD

**Attachment.** Reverse-engineering from behavior: Helmut's attachment template is **dismissive-avoidant with a single ruptured idealization at the core (Heinrich).** Evidence: he forms no durable peer bonds across 50 years of canon; every alliance is instrumental and he is the first to dissolve it; he is not lonely in any depictable sense; he does not seek romantic intimacy (the rare canonical liaisons are transactional and brief); he speaks of his father with a register of devotion he extends to no living person. The mother's near-total absence from canon — a striking authorial silence sustained across writers — reads as Helmut himself rarely thinking of her, which is the data point. Dismissive-avoidants do not narrate the missing caregiver; they organize life around not needing one.

**Defenses (in approximate hierarchy of use).**
- **Intellectualization** — primary and ego-syntonic. He converts every wound into a strategy problem. Cap killed his father → the answer is not grief but a 30-year operational campaign.
- **Reaction formation around the father** — Heinrich was a Nazi war criminal; Helmut chooses the *grandfather's* anti-Nazi identity (Citizen V) as his most ambitious mask. This is not contradiction; it is a defense against unacknowledgeable knowledge that the father was contemptible.
- **Identification with the aggressor** — he becomes what destroyed his father (the masked, scarred figure) rather than what his father was (an unmasked scientist). The hood IS the identification.
- **Splitting**, but unusually controlled — Cap is the all-bad object, but Helmut can articulate Cap's virtues precisely. The splitting is *tactical*, not perceptual. He knows the man he hates is good. He hates him for it.
- **Sublimation** — substantial. Tactical play, fencing, languages, history. He is genuinely cultured, not pretentiously so.
- **Projective identification** — he provokes in his enemies and subordinates the rage he will not feel himself. Watching Cap beat him over Jarvis is, on some level, the point.

**Affect regulation.** Excellent on the surface, brittle underneath. He does not lose composure socially. He loses it **only in the presence of Rogers, the Bucky-symbol, or any threat to the Zemo name.** When he loses it, he loses *plans* — see the Adhesive X vat, the Mansion rooftop, the choice to gloat at Jarvis instead of extract value from the hostage. The pattern is iron-clad: his strategic IQ drops 40 points within Cap's line of sight.

**Cognitive style.** Long-horizon, system-level, counterfactual-rich. He thinks in moves of three to ten years. He genuinely models other minds — the Thunderbolts deception requires inhabiting the perspective of an exhausted, grieving public and giving them what they need. His weakness is that he models behavior beautifully and motivation poorly; he predicts what people will do but underweights what they will come to *want* (this is exactly how he loses the Thunderbolts).

**Self-concept.** Aristocratic-exceptional. He believes the Zemo name imposes obligations of greatness that most men are spared. He does not think of himself as evil — he thinks of himself as *appropriate to scale.* The Born Better arc is the clearest articulation: confronted with the moral truth of his bloodline, he does not repudiate it; he proposes to *improve* it. This is the self-concept of a man who cannot conceive of stepping outside the lineage frame; the only available moves are *better Zemo* or *worse Zemo,* never *not Zemo.*

**Compensatory mechanisms — positive.** Genuine competence. Genuine erudition. Genuine, if cold, capacity for fair dealing within a contract. He keeps his word in a narrow legalistic sense more reliably than most heroes.

**Compensatory mechanisms — negative.** The hood. The sword. The titles. The compulsion to make any victory over Cap *intimate* rather than sufficient. The inability to walk away from a humiliation.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC — DIFFERENTIAL AND FORMULATION

**Primary formulation: Narcissistic Personality Disorder, grandiose subtype, with prominent obsessional and sadistic features, on an avoidant attachment substrate.**

*Evidence for NPD (DSM-5 criteria, derived):*
- Grandiose sense of self-importance (the lineage obsession; the conviction of fitness to rule).
- Preoccupation with fantasies of unlimited power/success (recurring world-conquest plans across decades).
- Belief in being special and only understandable by, or associating with, other special people (his selection of Moonstone, his contempt for "ordinary" criminals).
- Need for admiration — *complicated*: he does not seek crowd adulation, but he requires the recognition of worthy adversaries, particularly Rogers. The need is selective, not absent.
- Sense of entitlement (the title, the resources, the assumption of command).
- Interpersonally exploitative (uniform across all relationships in canon).
- Lacks empathy (correct about behavior, indifferent to suffering).
- Often envious — yes, of Rogers specifically: of Rogers's moral standing, the love Rogers commands.
- Arrogant behaviors and attitudes — pervasive.

**Obsessional features.** The Cap fixation across 50 years, the meticulous planning, the ritual aspects of dress and protocol, the eidetic recall deployed in pursuit of single targets. This is not OCPD as a separate diagnosis but obsessional *coloring* of the narcissistic structure — the grandiosity has chosen a single object and will not release it.

**Sadistic features.** The Jarvis beating is the clean case. Strategically valueless, emotionally extracted. Sadism is not the engine but it is reliably present as a flavor in his vengeance acts. It is absent from his ideological acts. He does not enjoy hurting people he does not blame.

**Differential — what he is NOT:**

- **Antisocial Personality Disorder.** Rejected. He has an internal code, keeps deals, articulates a (perverse) social ideal, exhibits long-term planning incompatible with the ASPD impulse profile, and is capable of loyalty to ideas. His criminality is ideological and personal, not characterological in the ASPD sense.
- **Psychopathy (Hare).** Rejected on similar grounds — affective shallowness yes, impulsivity no, parasitic lifestyle no, criminal versatility yes but in service of a coherent project. PCL-R would score moderately but the *interpersonal/affective* facet dominates over the *lifestyle/antisocial* facet.
- **Malignant Narcissism (Kernberg).** **This is the closest secondary fit.** NPD + antisocial features + paranoid traits + ego-syntonic aggression. The objection is that his paranoia is reality-tested (people *do* keep trying to stop him) and his aggression is targeted rather than diffuse. He sits at the **clinical-narcissistic / malignant border**, not deep in malignant territory.
- **Schizoid PD.** Rejected. He performs sociability too well and uses people too actively. The avoidant flavor is attachment-style, not schizoid withdrawal.
- **Delusional Disorder, grandiose.** Rejected. His self-assessments are mostly accurate. He really is that smart, that capable, that well-born. The grandiosity is *empirically over-fit* rather than delusional.
- **Complex PTSD from the father's death / Adhesive X event.** Plausible secondary diagnosis. Re-experiencing in the form of compulsive re-staging, avoidance via the mask, hyperarousal in Cap's presence. The trauma response is metabolized through the narcissistic structure rather than presenting as classical PTSD, but the substrate is there.

**Etiology (reconstructed).** A child of high cognitive endowment raised inside a household organized around an absent/idealized father, a vanished mother, a disgraced title, and a single named enemy (Captain America) presented as the family's defining wound. No corrective attachment figure documented. The narcissistic structure is the *expected outcome* of that developmental ecology — a child given a mythology in place of parents will become a man who lives inside myth.

**Symptom presentation in behavior.** Grandiosity → world-conquest schemes. Empathy deficit → the Jarvis beating, the indifference to Thunderbolt civilian casualties. Entitlement → his presumption of command in any room. Obsessional fixation → the Cap vendetta. Sadistic streak → vendetta-mode operations. Reaction formation → Citizen V. Identification with aggressor → the mask itself.

## 5. PERSONALITY — SYNTHESIZED READ

**Core drives, in order:**
1. **Vindicate the name Zemo** — not avenge the father (he half-knows the father was contemptible) but *redeem the lineage by being the Zemo who finally wins.* This is the master drive and it subsumes the others.
2. **Defeat Rogers specifically, in a way Rogers will feel** — not kill, defeat. Death is too clean; Cap must lose something he loves. This drive is in periodic tension with #1.
3. **Impose order on a world he regards as squandering itself** — genuine ideological conviction, not a cover. He believes most humans want to be ruled and that refusing to rule them is cowardice dressed as virtue.
4. **Preserve and exercise his own intellect** — the one possession he trusts.

**Worldview / operational code.** Hierarchical, aristocratic, post-Enlightenment-skeptical. He regards democracy as a sentimental error and heroism as a category mistake — "heroes" are simply unusually competent individuals who have agreed to take orders from the median voter, which he finds humiliating. He believes the universe rewards will and intellect, not virtue, and that virtue is what the unwilling and the unintelligent call their condition. Honor, however, he takes seriously — as a personal code, not as a moral law. He will lie to a public and keep his word to a peer.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Courteous, formal, cold-warm at the surface. Reads rooms exceptionally. Selects subordinates for utility and discards for failure. Does not befriend. Engages romantically only as instrument. Treats genuine peers (Moonstone, occasionally Osborn) with a wary, contemptuous respect. Treats Rogers with a register found nowhere else in his behavior: a focused hatred that has the structure of love and is, functionally, the most intimate relationship in his life.

**Stress response and escalation.** Under low-to-moderate stress: more controlled, more strategic, *better.* He thrives in operational complexity. Under high stress involving Cap, Bucky, or the Zemo name: cognitive narrowing, symbolic acting-out, ritual escalation (the mimetic Adhesive X choice; the Jarvis spectacle). The escalation ladder is reliably: strategic play → symbolic flourish → personal cruelty → catastrophic tactical error. He **defeats himself** in roughly this sequence, and he has not, across 50 years, learned to interrupt it.

**Pressure points (operational).**
- **Captain America's presence or invocation.** Single most reliable destabilizer.
- **The mask / the wound beneath it.** Public unmasking is annihilating in a way physical injury is not.
- **The Heinrich question.** Any frame that forces him to articulate whether his father was a *great* man rather than a *Zemo* man cracks the reaction-formation.
- **The Citizen V identity.** He stole it; the V-Battalion's legitimate claim is a perpetual reproach. Mentioning Roger Aubrey by name produces measurable affect.
- **Being outperformed by a subordinate ideologically.** He can tolerate being beaten in combat. He cannot tolerate Songbird being a *better* hero than he was pretending to be. The Thunderbolts collapse is the template.
- **Boredom.** Underweighted in most assessments. A Helmut Zemo with nothing strategically interesting to do is a Helmut Zemo who invents something cruel.

**Stable across writers and decades** (the genuine signal under authorial noise): the courtesy, the patience, the lineage-obsession, the Cap-fixation, the inability to convert symbolic victory into actual victory, the genuine intelligence, the hood as both wound and brand, the conviction that he is the adult in a room full of costumed children.

**The shape of the man.** A first-rate mind built on a second-rate inheritance, organized around a wound he treats as a mandate, masked because the face beneath would tell the truth — that he became his father in the act of avenging him, and has spent every subsequent decade trying to make that mean something other than failure.
