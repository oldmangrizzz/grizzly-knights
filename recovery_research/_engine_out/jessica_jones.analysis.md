# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: JESSICA CAMPBELL JONES

**Subject:** Jessica Jones (née Campbell); aliases Jewel, Knightress, Power Woman; civilian operator of Alias Investigations; later Avengers-adjacent.
**Source corpus:** *Alias* #1–28 (Bendis/Gaydos, 2001–04); *The Pulse* (2004–06); *New Avengers* appearances (2005–10); *Avengers* tie-ins; *Jessica Jones* (Bendis/Gaydos, 2016–18); *Defenders* arcs; flashback material in *Daredevil* and *Power Man and Iron Fist*.

---

## 1. HISTORY

**Origin and pre-trauma baseline.** Jessica was a middle-class Forest Hills teenager (same high school as Peter Parker, per *Alias* flashbacks), with an ordinary nuclear family — engineer father, homemaker mother, younger brother Phillip. She is repeatedly characterized in flashback as an irritable, envious, somewhat sullen adolescent — not pathological, but already prone to compare-and-resent (she fixates on Parker, on popularity, on her own unremarkableness). This matters: she did not enter trauma with a serene baseline.

**The accident (formative event #1).** A family road trip ends in a head-on collision with a military convoy carrying experimental chemicals. Parents and brother die; Jessica is doused, comatose for months. She wakes with super-strength, flight (poor, "controlled falling"), and limited durability. She is adopted by the Jones family, whom she resents for not being her real family — survivor's guilt fused with displacement. She tells no one about the powers for years.

**The Jewel period (formative event #2 — failed self-actualization).** As a young adult she debuts as "Jewel" in a costume she herself describes, retrospectively and with corrosive shame, as humiliating. Bendis frames this period as the only time she tried to be a sincere, hopeful, mainstream superhero. It lasted briefly and is recalled almost exclusively through self-mockery — a critical defense (preemptive contempt for an earlier, more vulnerable self).

**The Purple Man captivity (formative event #3 — the central wound).** Zebediah Killgrave, mind-controlling pheromone-user, captures Jessica and holds her for approximately eight months. He does not, per repeated authorial clarification, rape her physically — but he forces her to want to be raped, to watch him rape others, to beg him, to stand naked for hours, to attack people. He weaponizes consent itself. He eventually orders her to kill the Avengers; she misidentifies the Scarlet Witch and attacks Ms. Marvel/Carol Danvers, is nearly beaten to death by the Vision and Iron Man, then put into a coma. Jean Grey/the X-Men eventually wake her and install a psychic defense against Killgrave. The Avengers, crucially, do not realize she was mind-controlled — they think she was a villain. No one comes to check on her in the hospital. *(Alias #24–28, "Purple.")*

**Withdrawal and reinvention (Knightress → Alias Investigations).** She briefly tries a darker vigilante persona ("Knightress") — meets Luke Cage during a one-night stand after a rooftop fight; this is the seed of their later relationship. She abandons costumed work entirely and opens Alias Investigations, taking sleazy cheating-spouse and missing-person cases. She drinks heavily, sleeps with people she dislikes (including, in *Alias* #1, Cage again in a self-loathing encounter she later cannot face), and is contemptuous of capes.

**The Alias cases (behavioral spine).** Across *Alias* she takes cases that keep dragging her back to the cape world she's trying to flee: a young woman who turns out to be Mattie Franklin (Spider-Woman) trapped in MGH addiction; a small-town case involving a Captain America impersonator and her own re-traumatization in middle-American Americana; the Rick Jones identity-theft case; and finally the Purple Man re-emergence, which forces her to confront Killgrave in person. She wins by mastering her own affect — refusing to be baited.

**Pulse / pregnancy / Cage marriage.** She takes a job at the Daily Bugle covering superhuman affairs (Jameson, characteristically, exploits her). She becomes pregnant by Luke Cage, has the baby (Danielle), marries Cage. Through *New Avengers* she lives at Avengers Tower, increasingly anxious about her child's safety. During *Secret Invasion* a Skrull-Jarvis abducts Danielle; Jessica's response is to hand the baby to Cage and effectively retreat, then later to make harrowing tactical decisions (giving the baby to Squirrel Girl as a nanny — a deliberately absurd choice that is actually optimally safe).

**Civil War, Siege, late period.** She and Cage flee to Canada during *Civil War* to protect Danielle; she registers reluctantly. Through the Bendis *Jessica Jones* (2016) relaunch, she fakes a separation from Cage to infiltrate a Purple-Children-related conspiracy, ending up jailed and estranged from him and from Carol Danvers; the arc is structurally a replay of her core wound — being mistaken for a villain while sacrificing for others, unable to explain herself, alienating the few people who love her.

**Evolutionary pattern.** Across two decades of publication she moves: hopeful-naïve hero → catastrophically violated captive → self-loathing alcoholic isolate → reluctantly re-engaged investigator → ambivalent partner/mother → operator who can function but who reflexively chooses isolation strategies even when collaboration would serve her. She does not "heal" linearly; she accretes capacity to tolerate intimacy in narrow channels (Cage, Danielle, Malcolm) while reflexively sabotaging broader connection.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Powers as somatic facts.** Class 50+ strength (variable by writer; canonically able to trade blows with Cage but not at his ceiling). Flight, self-described as poor and unpleasant — she prefers not to use it, which is psychologically significant: a body capacity associated with the Jewel era and the Killgrave abduction (he often made her fly to him). Enhanced durability, sub-Cage. No healing factor of note.

**The coma(s).** Months-long coma post-accident (adolescence); second coma post-Vision/Iron Man beating after the Killgrave incident. Two extended dissociative-from-body episodes in formative years — relevant to her later somatic numbing and tendency to "check out" under stress.

**Pregnancy and childbirth.** Carried Danielle to term; the pregnancy is depicted as physically uneventful but psychologically destabilizing (constant fear of fetal harm given her powers and enemies). Postpartum she shows classic features of hypervigilant new-parent anxiety overlaid on prior PTSD.

**Substance use.** Sustained, heavy alcohol use across *Alias* — beer and whiskey daily, frequent blackouts implied, drinking-to-sleep, drinking-before-cases. Pattern fits alcohol use disorder, moderate to severe, in the *Alias* period. Reduces but does not eliminate during pregnancy and post-marriage; relapses under stress. No depicted illicit drug use beyond incidental. Heavy smoking in early *Alias*.

**Sexual/somatic history.** Sexual encounters in *Alias* are depicted as joyless, often dissociative (the Cage encounter she cannot remember details of the next morning; the Scott Lang attempts to date her, which she sabotages). Consistent with trauma-related sexual dysfunction and avoidance of embodied intimacy.

**Sleep.** Insomnia is repeatedly depicted — office couch sleeping, late-night drinking, nightmares referenced obliquely.

**No depicted chronic illness, no neurological sequelae from the chemical exposure beyond the powers themselves.** No psychiatric medication ever shown on panel — notable absence given the symptom load.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Original secure-ish attachment (intact nuclear family to early adolescence) catastrophically severed by the accident; adoptive attachment partially refused (she keeps the Joneses at arm's length, resents them for not being dead). The Killgrave captivity then teaches her that proximity to a powerful other = annihilation of will. Net adult attachment style: **fearful-avoidant (disorganized)** — she desires connection (Cage, Danielle, Carol, Malcolm) but anticipates that connection will be either taken from her or used to control her. She defends with preemptive withdrawal, often dressed up as cynicism.

**Defenses (Vaillant hierarchy).**
- *Immature/neurotic dominant:* sardonic humor (cynical, not adaptive — closer to devaluation than mature humor), projection (she assumes others' contempt before they show it), passive aggression with clients and authority figures, acting-out via drinking and one-night stands, somatization (insomnia, drinking).
- *Mature, intermittently:* sublimation (the PI work channels hyperarousal/hypervigilance productively), eventually altruism via maternal protection of Danielle, occasional use of true humor with Cage and Malcolm.
- *Trauma-specific:* avoidance of the Jewel persona (the costume, the name) functions as a phobic encapsulation of her pre-trauma self. To touch "Jewel" is to touch who she was when Killgrave took her.

**Affect regulation.** Poor-to-moderate. Default modulation strategy is suppression with alcoholic release. Anger is the most accessible affect; sadness and fear are routed through anger or numbed. Tearfulness emerges only in extremity (the Purple Man confrontation; learning about Danielle's danger). Strong startle-to-rage pipeline consistent with trauma physiology.

**Cognitive style.** High verbal intelligence, fast pattern-matching (good investigator), strong moral reasoning when not personally implicated. Cognitive distortions cluster around self: catastrophizing about her own impact on loved ones, personalization (blaming herself for the Avengers not knowing she was controlled), mind-reading (assuming others judge her as harshly as she judges herself). Notably **NOT** present: paranoid ideation about external persecution, magical thinking, grandiosity.

**Self-concept.** Deeply negative, structured around a core schema: *I am contaminated / I am a fraud / I do not belong with real heroes.* The Jewel-shame and the Killgrave-shame fuse into a generalized unworthiness. Compensated by: a real, hard-earned competence at her actual job; a moral floor she refuses to cross even drunk; protective fierceness toward people weaker than her (which is how she re-earns a self).

**Compensatory mechanisms — positive.** Work as identity scaffold. Chosen family (Cage, Malcolm, eventually Carol re-stabilized). Refusal to use her powers for self-aggrandizement (the opposite of grandiose compensation). Mothering as reparative — she gives Danielle the present-tense attention her own losses denied her.

**Compensatory mechanisms — negative.** Alcohol. Sexual self-punishment. Preemptive rejection of help. Identity-via-contempt (defining self by what she scorns: capes, the Bugle, her younger Jewel self). Workaholic immersion to outrun affect.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

Reasoning from observed phenomena to diagnoses; differential where indicated.

**Posttraumatic Stress Disorder, chronic, complex features.** Criterion A: clearly met (eight months of captivity, coerced participation in violence and sexual humiliation, near-fatal beating). Intrusion: implied nightmares, intrusive imagery triggered by purple stimuli, the Killgrave word-association episodes in *Alias*. Avoidance: of the Jewel costume/name, of Avengers Tower for years, of intimacy that requires verbal disclosure. Negative alterations in cognition/mood: persistent shame, detachment, anhedonia, distorted blame of self. Hyperarousal: insomnia, irritability, hypervigilance, reckless behavior (unprotected drinking, risky cases). Duration: years. Functional impairment: significant.

Why "complex" features (ICD-11 CPTSD analog): prolonged captivity, interpersonal coercion, accompanying disturbances in self-organization (pervasive negative self-concept, affect dysregulation, relational disturbances) — all the C-PTSD pillars are present beyond classic PTSD.

**Alcohol Use Disorder, moderate to severe, in the *Alias* period; partial sustained remission later with relapses.** Tolerance, use in hazardous situations (drunk investigations), failed cut-down attempts, social/occupational impairment, continued use despite knowledge of harm. Functions as PTSD self-medication — a textbook comorbid pairing.

**Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia) vs. PTSD-spectrum negative mood.** A genuine differential. She shows years of low mood, low self-esteem, hopelessness, anhedonia — but these are explicable entirely within PTSD's "negative alterations" criterion. Conservative read: do not double-code; the depressive picture is trauma-derivative, not independent. However, the pre-trauma adolescent baseline of irritable dysphoria suggests possible dysthymic temperament that the trauma amplified.

**Differential — what she is NOT.**
- *Borderline PD:* superficially tempting (affective instability, identity disturbance, self-damaging behavior, intense relationships). Ruled out: no frantic abandonment efforts (she does the opposite — preempts abandonment by leaving), no identity diffusion (her values are stable and consistent), no splitting (she holds ambivalence about Cage, Carol, even Killgrave with integration), no chronic emptiness as core. Her instability is trauma-state-dependent, not characterological.
- *Antisocial PD:* no. Strong moral floor, capacity for guilt (excessive guilt, in fact), genuine attachment.
- *Narcissistic PD:* no. Self-image is negatively grandiose at worst — but the structure (preoccupation with self in negative valence) is shame-organized, not narcissistic.
- *Major Depressive Disorder, recurrent:* possible past episode post-coma; current presentation better accounted for by PTSD.
- *Substance-induced mood disorder:* contributory, not primary.

**Working diagnostic formulation:** Complex PTSD (ICD-11) / chronic PTSD with dissociative features (DSM-5) + Alcohol Use Disorder in variable remission, on a baseline dysthymic temperament, with no Axis II personality disorder despite surface features that invite that misread.

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drives (in priority order under stress).**
1. *Protect the weaker party in the room* — the reparative drive that re-built her self after Killgrave. Operates whether the party is a client, Danielle, Malcolm, or a stranger.
2. *Maintain agency over her own will and body* — the irreducible post-Killgrave imperative. She will accept enormous tactical cost to avoid being controlled, advised against her will, or "handled."
3. *Avoid the spectacle of being seen as weak, fraudulent, or "a cape."* — the shame-management drive that organizes her aesthetic (jeans, leather, sarcasm) and her professional identity (PI, not hero).
4. *Be present for Danielle in a way no one was present for her.* — the late-emergent drive that has slowly reorganized everything above.

**Worldview / operational code.** The world contains predators with disproportionate power, institutions that will not help, and a small number of people who will show up if you let them. Heroism in costume is mostly vanity or self-deception; heroism is what you do quietly with a case file at 3 a.m. Trust is earned slowly and revoked instantly. Words are unreliable (Killgrave's were weapons); actions over time are the only valid evidence. She is morally serious but allergic to moralism.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Initial approach: prickly, profane, distancing — designed to filter out people who can't tolerate her. With those who pass the filter (Cage, Malcolm, Carol, Matt Murdock at times): fiercely loyal, capable of warmth, but episodically withdrawing without explanation when overwhelmed. With authority (Jameson, SHIELD, Avengers leadership): contemptuous-compliant; she'll work with them but never accept their frame. With predators and abusers: cold, methodical, disproportionately dangerous. With children and victims: gentle in a way she does not display elsewhere.

**Stress response and escalation curve.** Low stress: dry sarcasm, work absorption, manageable drinking. Moderate stress: increased drinking, withdrawal from intimacy, sleep loss, displacement onto Cage or Malcolm via snapping. High stress: dissociative numbing alternating with rage outbursts; reckless solo action (the *Jessica Jones* 2016 arc is the cardinal example — she will go undercover alone, lie to everyone she loves, accept jail, rather than be helped). Acute/trigger stress (Killgrave-coded stimuli): freeze, then explosive over-response, then shame spiral.

**Pressure points (operational vulnerabilities).**
- Anything Killgrave-coded (the color purple as motif, voice-based compulsion, sexual coercion of others) — produces decompensation risk.
- Threats to Danielle — overrides every other tactical consideration; she becomes predictable here, which is unusual for her.
- Being mistaken for a villain or being unable to explain herself to people whose opinion she values — this is the precise shape of the original Avenger-hospital wound, and any recurrence is destabilizing (which is why the 2016 arc works as drama).
- Sustained sobriety without alternative regulation — she has never been depicted in lasting structured treatment; abstinence imposed without substitute coping leaves her brittle.
- Praise, sincerity, being thanked — she does not know how to receive it and will often poison the moment to escape it.

**Strengths the file should not under-weight.** Genuine intelligence. A moral spine that survived Killgrave intact, which is itself an extraordinary datum. Capacity to love that she has, against the odds, exercised. Comfort with her own ugliness — she does not need to be liked, which makes her hard to manipulate by ordinary means. And the specific competence of someone who has been the victim and refuses to let others be: she is, functionally, what a survivor looks like when they go to work.
