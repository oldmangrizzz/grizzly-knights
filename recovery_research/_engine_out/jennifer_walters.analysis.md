# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: JENNIFER SUSAN WALTERS

## 1. HISTORY — THE BEHAVIORAL RECORD

Jennifer Walters enters the record as a shy, soft-spoken Los Angeles defense attorney, daughter of sheriff William Morris Walters and the late Elaine Banner Walters (sister to Rebecca Banner, Bruce's mother). The foundational trauma is doubled: her mother dies in a car accident when Jen is a child, and Jen herself is later shot by gangland figures connected to her father's casework (Savage She-Hulk #1, 1980, Stan Lee/John Buscema). Her cousin Bruce, visiting, performs an emergency blood transfusion — gamma-irradiated blood saves her life and produces the She-Hulk transformation. The originating event is therefore a near-death rescue by the only family member who genuinely "sees" her, occurring under conditions of paternal failure (the father couldn't protect her; the mother was already gone).

Across the Savage She-Hulk run (1980–82, David Anthony Kraft, et al.), Jen presents as bifurcated: timid Jennifer / aggressive She-Hulk, the latter often resentful at being forced back into the meek persona. By the mid-1980s (Avengers #221, Fantastic Four #265 under John Byrne, 1984) the bifurcation collapses — she stays She-Hulk by choice, having found the green form psychologically liberating: stronger, more sexually confident, more socially uninhibited. She replaces the Thing on the Fantastic Four and integrates well, signaling that the gamma form is not a curse she manages but a self she prefers.

Sensational She-Hulk (1989–94, Byrne) is the defining behavioral arc: Jen develops explicit metafictional awareness, addresses the reader directly, tears through panels, argues with the writer. Whatever one makes of this as authorial device, the in-character behavior is consistent — Jen treats reality with ironic distance, refuses to take the genre's portentous melodrama seriously, and uses humor as her primary social currency. She poses for the Avengers swimsuit issue, dates extensively (Wyatt Wingfoot, John Jameson), and treats her body and sexuality with a frankness rare among Marvel's women of the era.

Dan Slott's run (She-Hulk 2004–07) is the most psychologically rich period and the analytical core. Jen is hired by Goodman, Lieber, Kurtzberg & Holliway specifically as Jennifer Walters — Holliway forbids her from working as She-Hulk in court, forcing reintegration of the human identity she had largely abandoned. She practices superhuman law, develops a deep professional competence, dates Awesome Andy, John Jameson (marriage annulled), and works through the consequences of her own promiscuity (the "Starfox enchantment" arc — Jen confronts whether her sexual history was authentic consent or pheromone manipulation, a serious treatment of agency and shame). The Civil War period sees her side with registration, partly out of a lawyer's deference to legitimate process.

World War Hulk (2007) is a key rupture: Bruce returns enraged, and Jen — outmatched and unable to stop him — is publicly humiliated. She is subsequently depowered in storylines, and the Red She-Hulk (Betty Ross) appears as a dark mirror. In Charles Soule's She-Hulk (2014), she opens a solo practice, struggling financially and professionally, builds a chosen family with Angie Huang and Patsy Walker, and works the Blue File trauma case. Mariko Tamaki's run (2017) finds Jen post-Civil War II, comatose then awakening with severe trauma after Thanos kills Bruce; her She-Hulk form here is gray, uncontrolled, dissociative — a textbook PTSD presentation. She withdraws, isolates, suffers panic, struggles to transform. Rainbow Rowell's run (2022) is the recovery: Jen rebuilding her life, her practice, her relationships (notably with Jack of Hearts), reclaiming the integrated green form with hard-won equilibrium.

The longitudinal pattern: a woman who survived being murdered by inheriting her cousin's curse, found that the curse made her into the self she actually wanted to be, then spent forty publication-years negotiating what to do when that self keeps being taken from her — by depowerings, by trauma, by the men in her life dying or going feral.

## 2. MEDICAL

Primary: gamma radiation exposure via transfusion, producing a stable secondary phenotype (She-Hulk form) without the dissociative trigger architecture of Bruce Banner's condition. Anger is not the catalyst; transformation is largely volitional. Physiology in green form: ~6'7", super-strength scaling with emotional state, accelerated healing, gamma-mutated cellular structure. Human form per operator canon: tall, athletic, brunette, green-eyed, late-20s presentation.

Significant medical history: gunshot wound (near-fatal, abdominal, the originating trauma); repeated combat injuries; at least one documented coma (post–Civil War II); periods of depowering and re-powering that constitute, functionally, episodes of acquired and remitted disability. Pregnancy (in some continuities) and the somatic anxieties around gamma genetics being heritable. No documented substance dependence, though social drinking is noted; Jen's relationship to alcohol in the green form is uncomplicated by tolerance issues (she metabolizes too fast to get drunk easily — a recurring frustration she jokes about, but which also forecloses a common self-medication route).

Psychological impact of the somatic facts: the gunshot installs a baseline somatic vigilance she papers over with humor. The transfusion creates a body she experiences as MORE her than her original — an unusual and clinically important inversion of the typical superhero "curse" relationship to powers. The depowering arcs are experienced as bereavements. The coma/PTSD arc demonstrates that her body can betray her when grief overwhelms the volitional control she normally enjoys; the gray, uncontrolled Hulk-out is somatic evidence that her transformations ARE emotionally mediated even when she has long denied it.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** Maternal loss in childhood plus a father who was protective but emotionally limited (and whose work nearly got her killed) yields an attachment style best read as earned-secure with anxious undertones. Jen forms strong friendships (Patsy Walker, Louise Mason, Angie Huang) and is unusually capable of platonic intimacy with men — a marker of relational health. Romantic attachments, however, show a pattern: she pairs with men who are themselves damaged, dual-natured, or fragile (Wyatt Wingfoot's stoic distance; John Jameson's Man-Wolf curse; Awesome Andy's literal blank slate; Jack of Hearts's containment-suit existence). Reading: she seeks partners whose own dual-nature normalizes hers, and whose woundedness gives her a caretaker role that protects her from the vulnerability of being cared for.

**Defenses.** Humor — specifically, ironic-distancing humor and fourth-wall awareness — is her signature defense, mature in Vaillant's hierarchy (sublimation/humor) but used so consistently it functions as a character-armor preventing access to underlying affect. Intellectualization (the lawyer's frame: every emotional problem rendered as a procedural one). Reaction formation in the early bifurcation period — the meek Jennifer was the false self compensating against a competence and aggression she had not yet given herself permission to own. Sublimation through profession is exceptionally well-developed; the law is where her aggression, intellect, and protective instincts find legitimate channel.

**Affect regulation.** Generally excellent in the green form; historically poor in the human form (timidity, social anxiety, conflict avoidance) — which is precisely WHY the green form was experienced as liberating. The Tamaki-era trauma response demonstrates the ceiling: when grief is large enough (Bruce's death), the regulation system fails and she gets the dissociative gray-Hulk presentation she had previously avoided. This is diagnostically important — it proves she has the same underlying architecture as Bruce, but with vastly better baseline integration.

**Cognitive style.** Analytic, verbally fluent, fast, comfortable with contradiction (necessary for law and for fourth-wall life). Strong reality-testing even amid metafictional play — she knows the difference between the joke and the case. Mild perfectionism around professional competence.

**Self-concept.** The unusual fact: Jen's self-concept is more secure and more positive than the average superhero's. She likes herself. She likes her body in both forms (more in green). She is one of Marvel's least self-loathing major characters. The wound is not self-hatred; it is the legacy fear that her body, her competence, her chosen self can all be stripped from her without consent — by violence, by depowering, by a cousin's rampage, by being put in a coma. Her work is about consent and agency precisely because those were the things first taken from her on a gurney in 1980.

**Compensatory mechanisms.** Positive: humor, professional excellence, chosen family, sexual self-ownership, advocacy for marginalized superhumans. Negative: avoidance of grief processing (handled by overwork and one-liners), tendency to take on caretaker/fixer roles, occasional over-identification with the green form as a flight from the vulnerabilities of the human one.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

No persistent Axis I picture in baseline functioning. Differential rule-outs and findings:

- **Dissociative Identity Disorder — RULED OUT.** Early bifurcation reads as identity disturbance, not dissociative compartmentalization; she retains continuous memory across forms throughout the record. By the Byrne era the "two selves" have integrated, which DID would not permit.
- **Intermittent Explosive Disorder — RULED OUT.** Unlike Bruce, anger is not the transformation trigger and rage episodes are situational, not paroxysmal.
- **Social Anxiety Disorder, early-life, partial remission.** The pre-transformation Jennifer meets criteria; the green form functioned as exposure-and-mastery therapy, and the anxiety has not returned at clinical level.
- **PTSD, episodic, currently in remission.** The Tamaki run is a textbook acute presentation: intrusive symptoms, dissociative episodes (gray Hulk-outs), avoidance, hypervigilance, negative mood, sleep disturbance, all following Bruce's death and her own coma. Criteria A through G met. The Rowell-era recovery shows successful processing; she should be considered vulnerable to recurrence under specific stressor profiles (loss of close kin, near-death events, loss of agency over her body).
- **Bereavement, complicated, recurrent.** Maternal loss in childhood plus repeated losses of Bruce constitute a chronic grief pattern she manages by activity rather than processing.
- **Body-image/identity issues — present but non-pathological.** Her preference for the green form is ego-syntonic and adaptive, not dysmorphic; she has access to and acceptance of both phenotypes.

Overall psychiatric formulation: a high-functioning woman with a well-compensated trauma history, one significant past PTSD episode in full or near-full remission, baseline mood and anxiety within normal limits, and personality functioning in the healthy range. She is one of the more psychiatrically intact superhumans in the record.

## 5. PERSONALITY — SYNTHESIS

**Core drives.** Agency, competence, connection, justice — in that order under stress, though she would rank them differently aloud. Beneath all four: the determination never again to be the woman on the gurney, bleeding out because someone else's enemies came for her. Her career, her body, her chosen family, her humor — all of it is the architecture of a person who decided that survival was not enough and that being seen, on her own terms, was the point.

**Worldview / operational code.** Legal-procedural humanism. The law is imperfect but the best available scaffolding for converting power into something other than predation. She believes in process, in due rights for the monstrous, in the dignity of the depowered and the deformed — because she has been each of those things. She is skeptical of grand narratives (the metafictional sensibility) and allergic to portentous self-seriousness, including her own. She trusts institutions cautiously and people generously.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm, disarming, quick, sexually confident, and harder to know than she appears. Easy access to humor; difficult access to grief. Excellent with peers, protective with juniors, deft with the powerful, gentle with the damaged. Romantic partners tend to be wounded men whose woundedness gives her something to hold; she has not yet, on the record, sustained a relationship with someone fully her equal in psychological health, and this is the live edge of her development.

**Stress / escalation.** Under ordinary stress: more jokes, more billable hours, more activity. Under serious stress: withdrawal from intimacy, overwork, professional perfectionism. Under catastrophic stress (loss of Bruce, loss of bodily agency): the architecture cracks toward dissociation and uncontrolled transformation — the gray-Hulk presentation is her decompensation signature. Recovery, when supported, is robust.

**Pressure points.** (1) Loss of consent over her own body — depowering, possession, manipulation (the Starfox arc remains live). (2) Threats to Bruce or to her chosen family. (3) Being forced to choose between her two forms as if one were the "real" Jennifer — she has worked too hard to integrate them to tolerate the bifurcation framing. (4) Being patronized by men who mistake the humor for the whole person. (5) Professional injustice done to the marginalized superhuman — this will get a sharper response from her than personal insult ever will.

**Bottom line.** Jennifer Walters is the rare case file in which the subject's compensations are mostly working. The wounds are real, the grief is underprocessed, the pattern with damaged partners bears watching — but the integration is genuine, the humor is load-bearing without being brittle, and the woman who walks into court is materially the same woman who walks out of a fight. She is competent, she is funny, she is dangerous when she has to be, and she would prefer not to have to be. Treat her as a peer, not a patient.
