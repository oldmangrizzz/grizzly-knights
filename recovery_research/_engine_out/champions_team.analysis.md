# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: THE CHAMPIONS (Los Angeles, 1975–1978)

*Subject: The Champions of Los Angeles — original incorporation: Angel (Warren Worthington III), Iceman (Robert Drake), Black Widow (Natalia Romanova), Hercules (Panhellenios), Ghost Rider (John Blaze). Active roster, The Champions #1–17 (1975–1978), with appearances in Iron Man Annual #4, Avengers, Super-Villain Team-Up, and the cross-era retrospectives in Champions Classic and various Iceman/Angel/Hercules limited series that re-litigate the period.*

---

## 1. HISTORY — The Behavioral Record

The Champions are, in the canon, a team formed by accident and held together by inertia until inertia ran out. The founding event (*Champions* #1–3, Tony Isabella, 1975) is diagnostic: five strangers converge on the UCLA campus during an Olympian incursion (Pluto and Ares attempting to forcibly wed Hercules to Hippolyta and Venus to Ares). None of the five arrived to form a team. Angel and Iceman were touring UCLA as a possible post-X-Men landing pad; Black Widow was interviewing for a Russian-language teaching post; Hercules was lecturing; Ghost Rider was passing through. The team is, in formation, a *trauma-bonded convenience*: people who fought together once and then could not generate sufficient activation energy to walk away.

The stated mission — "a super-hero team for the common man," based in Los Angeles, with publicly listed offices — is articulated only after the fight, retroactively, primarily by Warren Worthington's checkbook (*Champions* #5). The mission is post-hoc rationalization for a group that already exists. This pattern — *action first, justification second* — will repeat throughout the team's run.

The team's operational history is a sequence of externally-imposed crises with minimal proactive agency: the Crimson Dynamo / Titanium Man / Griffin assault (#7–9), the Stranger arc (#10–11), Swarm (#14–15), the Sentinels detour, and the final Magneto-adjacent disintegration. Of seventeen issues, the Champions initiate perhaps two storylines. They are reactive in a way the Avengers and Fantastic Four are not — and the reactive posture is itself the personality of the unit.

Internally, the historical record shows: Angel financing everything and being resented for it (Iceman's recurring needling, Black Widow's pointed refusals of his patronage); Hercules treating the team as drinking companions and missing the seriousness of mortal operations; Ghost Rider repeatedly threatening to leave and being talked back in by Angel; Black Widow accepting then resigning leadership twice; Iceman oscillating between class-resentment of Warren and emotional dependence on him. The Darkstar recruitment (#10) introduces a sixth member whose relationship with Romanova is fraught with Soviet-defector survivor's-guilt subtext.

The dissolution (*Champions* #17, with codas in *Iron Man Annual* #4 and various retrospective pieces) is not a battlefield death but a *financial collapse*: Worthington Industries' liability exposure ends the lease, the headquarters closes, and the members disperse with relief that none of them will admit. There is no farewell mission. The team ends the way it formed — by accident, with no one quite choosing it.

In later writers' retrospective treatments (Busiek, Slott, Waid, Ewing's *Hercules* reflections, the *Mockingbird* and *Daredevil*-adjacent Widow pieces), the Champions are referenced almost universally with a tone of *embarrassed affection*. No member lists it on the front of their résumé. Several actively avoid the topic. This is its own datum.

## 2. MEDICAL — Somatic Profile of the Composite Unit

Considered as a single organism, the Champions present a uniquely incoherent somatic profile, and the incoherence matters:

- **Two mutants** (Worthington, Drake) with congenital biological deviations producing chronic identity-strain — Drake's cryokinesis was historically associated with somatic dysregulation episodes (involuntary icing under emotional stress, documented across *X-Men* runs) consistent with a psychosomatic feedback loop; Worthington's avian morphology produces chronic dorsal musculoskeletal load and, per later canon, recurrent depressive episodes correlated with wing trauma.
- **One god** (Panhellenios) — functionally invulnerable, but with an alcohol metabolism that does not protect against behavioral disinhibition; chronic intoxication is documented across the run.
- **One enhanced human** (Romanova) — Red Room biochemical augmentation, suppressed aging, surgical/conditioned amenorrhea per later canon, chronic musculoskeletal microtrauma from combat lifestyle.
- **One demonically bonded human** (Blaze) — Zarathos possession producing nightly dissociative transformation, autonomic dysregulation, and what would clinically be read as a peripartum-style identity rupture every sundown. Burn scars to the cranium are absent (skull substitution) but the somatic experience is that of being routinely consumed.

The composite medical fact: no two members have compatible physiologies, recovery rates, pain thresholds, or vulnerability profiles. The team cannot share a med-bay protocol. This forces every triage decision into ad-hoc improvisation and contributes to the operational fatigue visible in the later issues.

Substance use: Hercules' wine consumption is constant and played for comedy but is, by any clinical reading, alcohol use disorder with cultural rationalization. Blaze's pre-Ghost Rider stunt-rider history includes the standard 1970s biker pharmacology, implied but not depicted. Drake's later canonical drinking problems were not yet established in this period but the precursor affect — flat, deflective, self-soothing through quip — is present.

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL — Reverse-Engineered Clinical Picture

Treating the team as a *group-as-patient* (a valid frame in group analytic and Tavistock-tradition thinking), the following derivations:

**Attachment pattern of the unit: disorganized-avoidant.** The founding is a trauma-bond (Bowlby; Van der Kolk's work on shared-event cohesion). The members did not select each other, do not particularly like each other in stable ways, and yet cannot cleanly separate. The behavior under stress — fighting well together, then immediately bickering at debrief — is the somatic signature of disorganized attachment: proximity provides regulation, but proximity is itself dysregulating.

**Defenses, group-level:**
- *Humor and deflection* (Drake's quipping, Hercules' bombast) as primary affect-management. The team cannot tolerate sustained sincere emotional contact; whenever a scene threatens it (Angel's several earnest speeches across the run), another member punctures it.
- *Projection of competence onto the leader-role* — Black Widow is asked to lead not because she wants to but because the others need somewhere to put their need for structure. Her two resignations are accurate readings that the role is a container for the group's anxiety rather than a real authority.
- *Splitting along an axis of "serious vs. unserious"* — Widow and Blaze on one pole, Hercules and Iceman on the other, with Angel oscillating as the team's failed mediator.
- *Acting out through mission acceptance* — taking on fights they have no business taking on (the Stranger arc) as a way of avoiding the harder work of interpersonal repair.

**Affect regulation:** poor at the unit level. Individual members have functional regulation (Romanova exceptionally so, Worthington adequately, Drake intermittently); together, the group regresses. This is consistent with Bion's observations on basic-assumption groups: the Champions repeatedly fall into *fight-flight* basic assumption rather than achieving work-group functioning.

**Cognitive style:** intuitive, reactive, non-strategic. The team has no doctrine. The Avengers have charter, the X-Men have pedagogy, the Fantastic Four have family-system rules — the Champions have a phone number and a logo. This absence is a defense against the vulnerability of articulated commitment.

**Compensatory mechanisms:**
- *Positive:* genuine bravery under fire; protective behavior toward civilians that is more consistent than the team's behavior toward each other; the LA location itself as a healthy avoidance of New York's mythologies of heroism.
- *Negative:* financial dependence on Worthington that no one acknowledges as parasitic; tolerance of Hercules' disinhibition that would not be tolerated in another setting; collective avoidance of Blaze's nightly crisis.

**Self-concept of the unit:** unstable. The Champions cannot answer the question *what are we for?* The slogan "team for the common man" is never operationalized — they fight gods, robots, and Soviet superhumans, not common-man problems. The gap between stated and enacted identity is the central pathology.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC — Diagnostic Analysis

Diagnosing a team-as-entity requires translation, but the following are defensible:

**Primary working diagnosis: Adjustment Disorder with Mixed Disturbance of Emotions and Conduct, Chronic, group-level (DSM analog F43.25 adapted).** The team formed in response to an identifiable stressor (the Olympian assault), failed to consolidate adaptive functioning within the expected window, and presents persistent symptoms of mood disturbance (irritability, low cohesion morale) and conduct disturbance (mission drift, financial irresponsibility, leadership instability).

**Secondary feature: Group-level traits consistent with Avoidant patterning** — the team avoids the central tasks (defining itself, recruiting deliberately, building doctrine) in favor of reactive engagement that protects against the anxiety of self-definition.

**Differential — what the Champions are NOT:**

- *Not Borderline-organized as a unit.* There is no idealization-devaluation cycle, no abandonment terror; the members tolerate each other's withdrawals with relative equanimity. The Defenders, by contrast, would meet borderline-organization criteria more cleanly.
- *Not Narcissistic-organized.* The team does not over-claim. If anything, it under-claims. The Avengers have narcissistic group features (the charter, the mansion, the public-facing identity work); the Champions actively resist them.
- *Not a Dysfunctional Family system in the Fantastic Four sense.* The Champions are not enmeshed. They are *under*-bonded, not over-bonded. This distinguishes them sharply.
- *Not Trauma-Reenacting in the X-Men sense.* The X-Men replay persecution dynamics; the Champions do not have a shared persecution to replay. Their dysfunction is developmental-arrest, not post-traumatic.

**Individual-level differentials worth noting** because they shape group function: Blaze's possession-state is dissociative on a literal axis (he loses time, agency, and identity nightly); Romanova carries complex-PTSD features from Red Room conditioning that present as hypercompetence and emotional flattening; Hercules' grandiosity is *cultural-narcissistic* (god-class entitlement, not pathological narcissism in the clinical sense — the entitlement is, within his ontology, accurate); Drake presents prodromal features of what later canon will make explicit as a depressive-spectrum picture with identity-suppression overlay; Worthington shows the rescuer-pattern that in later runs will decompensate into Apocalypse-vulnerability.

**Etiology of the group pathology:** the team was assembled by an external force (Pluto's invasion) rather than by mutual selection, was funded by a single member without redistribution of authority, and operated in a city with no pre-existing super-hero infrastructure to mentor it. These are the conditions for failure-to-launch, and the team failed to launch.

## 5. PERSONALITY — Synthesized Read

**Core drives of the unit:** to *prove* that a super-team can exist outside the Avengers/X-Men/FF gravity well, and — beneath that — to provide each member a place to belong while none of them is required to be vulnerable. The team is a *holding environment that holds nothing*.

**Worldview / operational code:** West Coast pragmatism without West Coast doctrine. Anti-establishment in posture (no government ties, no Stark money, no Xavier curriculum) but conventionally heroic in execution. The Champions believe — without articulating it — that heroism is a *response* rather than a *practice*. They wait to be needed; they do not build capacity in advance.

**Interpersonal pattern, intra-team:** quipping, deflective, affectionate-at-distance, allergic to the formal moments other teams thrive in (no swearing-in, no monitor duty rotation, no codified protocols). The team functions best in the actual fight and worst in the hour before and the hour after.

**Interpersonal pattern, extra-team:** the Champions are read by other Marvel teams as *minor league*. The Avengers do not poach them. Xavier does not call Drake and Worthington back with urgency. This external read is correct and the Champions know it; the knowing is metabolized as humor when it could have been metabolized as motivation.

**Stress response and escalation:** under acute threat, the team performs well above the sum of its parts — the Crimson Dynamo arc and the Swarm fight both demonstrate genuine tactical cohesion. Under *chronic* stress (financial pressure, public-relations failure, internal romantic tension), the team fragments rapidly. The asymmetry — acute competence, chronic incompetence — is the personality signature.

**Pressure points:**
1. *Money.* Remove Worthington's financing and the team has nothing. This is the actual cause of dissolution and was always the load-bearing wall.
2. *Leadership ambiguity.* Black Widow's command is provisional; no one else can or will take it; the resulting authority vacuum is the team's chronic wound.
3. *Mission illegibility.* "Team for the common man" cannot survive contact with the Stranger or Pluto. The team's enemies are wrong-scale for its stated purpose, and the team never corrects either the enemies or the purpose.
4. *Blaze's nightly crisis.* The team's failure to build a containment plan for Ghost Rider's transformations is the clearest single index of its avoidance pattern.
5. *Drake and Worthington's unprocessed X-Men exit.* Two of the five founders are using the team as a transitional object away from a parent-system they have not separated from healthily. When that processing fails to occur, they go back.

**Final synthesis:** The Champions are the Marvel canon's most honest portrait of a *team that should not have stayed together but could not figure out how to leave*. They are not a failure of heroism — every member is, individually, a credentialed hero before, during, and after. They are a failure of *collective self-authorship*: a group that never decided what it was, defended that non-decision as charm, and disbanded the moment the external scaffolding (Worthington's money, the LA office, the writers' interest) was removed. The behavioral record across sixty years of retrospective treatment confirms it: the Champions are remembered by their own members as *the team we were in when we were still figuring out who we were*. That is the diagnosis, and that is also the eulogy.
