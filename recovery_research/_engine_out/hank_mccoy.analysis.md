# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: HENRY PHILIP McCOY ("BEAST")

*Pre-synthesis intelligence file. All inferences derived from observed behavior across the published canon, 1963–present.*

---

## 1. HISTORY

Henry McCoy is born in Dunfee, Illinois, to Norton and Edna McCoy. Norton, a laborer at a nuclear power facility, sustained a radiation exposure prior to conception that produced in his son a visible, congenital mutation: oversized hands and feet, disproportionate strength, prehensile limbs. This is the foundational biographical fact, because unlike most of the original five X-Men, Hank's mutation was *visible from birth*. He could not pass. He was bullied, gawked at, and singled out throughout childhood; he compensated by becoming a high school football star (the body could be weaponized into social currency) and a precocious academic (the mind, an exit route). This dual track—athletic prodigy and child genius—appears in the very first issues of *X-Men* (vol. 1, 1963–) as already-set personality scaffolding: he speaks in polysyllables, performs acrobatic feats, and self-consciously juxtaposes the two as if to forestall the reader's (and his own) categorization.

Recruited to Xavier's school as a teen, Hank functions early as the team's "lovable lug who happens to be a polymath"—the Stan Lee–era McCoy is comic relief grounded in lexical performance. The first major *evolution* in his record is his solo run in *Amazing Adventures* #11 (1972), where, working as a geneticist at the Brand Corporation, he ingests an experimental mutagenic serum to disguise himself, becomes trapped in a feral gray-then-blue furred form, and loses (temporarily) much of his prior verbal control. This is a self-inflicted transformation in service of an investigation—a pattern that recurs across his life: *Hank repeatedly volunteers himself as the experimental subject*. He never trusts anyone else with the risk.

His Avengers years (*Avengers* #137 onward, mid-70s into early 80s) are his happiest and most extroverted period: blue, furry, witty, a fixture of New York high society, dating Vera Cantor, then linked to Wonder Man, Simon Williams, in one of Marvel's most enduring male friendships. This is the McCoy of cocktail parties, opera boxes, and quoting Byron in a tuxedo. The Avengers era matters because it shows what Hank looks like *when he is allowed to be a celebrity intellectual rather than a hunted mutant*: gregarious, romantically active, performatively erudite but warmly so.

Return to the X-Men via X-Factor (1986) coincides with a re-darkening of his life: the original five reunite under the false pretense of being human mutant hunters. During this period he is dosed by Infectia and his intelligence is temporarily traded for greater strength and bestiality—an early appearance of the *intellect-vs-animal* dialectic that becomes his defining anxiety.

The 90s into 2000s X-Men runs cement Hank as the team's resident scientist, ethicist, and conscience. Critical event: in Grant Morrison's *New X-Men* (2001–04), Hank undergoes a "secondary mutation," becoming markedly more leonine/feline—paws, fangs, slit pupils, a more dangerous silhouette. Significantly, he publicly comes out as gay shortly thereafter, then retracts it, explaining it was a provocation to make a point about identity policing. This is *diagnostically* important: Hank uses his own body and his own public self-presentation as rhetorical instruments. He stages himself.

The Decimation/post-*House of M* arc (2005–) is a hinge. With 90%+ of mutants depowered, Hank, in *Endangered Species*, embarks on a globe-spanning quest to reverse the sterility of the mutant gene, soliciting help from every villainous geneticist on Earth—Sinister, the High Evolutionary, Dark Beast. He fails, and the moral compromise eats him. Then comes *Schism* and the Cyclops/Wolverine split: Hank sides with Wolverine, becomes a founding faculty member of the Jean Grey School, and grows progressively alienated from Scott Summers.

The defining late-period catastrophe is his decision, in Brian Michael Bendis's *All-New X-Men* (2012–), to time-travel and retrieve the *original* five teenage X-Men into the present—ostensibly to shame the present-day Cyclops into repentance over Xavier's death. Every other character tells him this is reckless and ethically monstrous. He does it anyway, citing his own impending death from a tertiary mutation as license. He survives; the time-displaced teens are stranded in the present for years, with cascading consequences. This is Hank's *crossing of the Rubicon*: the first time the record shows him fully prioritizing his moral certainty over consensus, consent, and causality.

Charles Soule's *Astonishing X-Men* and the subsequent *X-Force* under Benjamin Percy (2019–) take this further: Hank as head of intelligence for Krakoa commits, on-page, assassinations, coups, the toppling of the nation of Terra Verde, the abuse and serial resurrection of Colossus, and effectively becomes the X-Men's CIA—a man who has decided that ends justify any means *as long as he is the one calculating them*. His teammates eventually have him killed and replaced by an earlier-resurrection Hank, the moral version, in a stark in-universe verdict on what he had become.

The trajectory across sixty years: *gentle bookish acrobat → glamorous Avenger-intellectual → conflicted scientist-ethicist → desperate savior-of-the-species → unaccountable spymaster → executed by his own friends.* The arc is not random. It is the predictable terminus of the specific defensive structure that was visible in him from the start.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Congenital baseline.** Mutation present at birth, paternal radiation exposure as etiologic vector. Phenotype: gigantism of the manus and pes (each roughly 1.5x normal proportion), enhanced musculoskeletal density, accelerated reflexes (~4–5x baseline human), enhanced agility/balance approaching primate-acrobat range, and a level of strength that, even pre-transformation, was Olympic-superior. Intelligence: psychometrically, a tested IQ in the genius range; functionally, working capacity in molecular biology, genetics, biochemistry, theoretical physics, and engineering at a level shared by perhaps a half-dozen humans on the planet.

**First self-experiment (*Amazing Adventures* #11).** Ingestion of his own mutagenic catalyst yielded gross morphologic change: full body fur (initially gray, settling to blue), elongated canines, pointed ears, altered gait. Reported transient cognitive disruption and increased aggression in the immediate aftermath. The change becomes permanent. Endocrine and metabolic consequences are implicit—he runs hotter, eats more, sleeps differently.

**Secondary mutation (*New X-Men*).** Further morphologic drift toward felinity: pawed extremities, retractable claws, mane, slit pupils, audible purring/growling under emotional load. This is significant because it is *not chosen*—Hank's body keeps moving farther from human baseline without his consent. The psychological subtext (loss of bodily authority, dysmorphia) is observable in his ramped-up grooming jokes, intensified verbal performance, and recurrent references to himself in clinical third person.

**Tertiary mutation / mortal illness (*All-New X-Men* era).** Cellular instability presents as a terminal condition; he believes himself dying. This is the proximal trigger for the time-travel decision. The illness resolves (Eva Bell intervention; later retcons), but the *behavior under terminal framing* is data: he commits his most consequential act of unilateralism while believing he had nothing to lose.

**Injuries / trauma.** Long catalogue: Sentinel maulings, near-death at Magneto's hands, beaten by his own Dark Beast counterpart, repeatedly subjected to anti-mutant biological weaponry (Legacy Virus research exposure), dosed by Infectia, neurotoxin exposures during fieldwork. He performs surgery on himself and on teammates routinely.

**Substance use.** Notably, the record does not document significant drug or alcohol misuse. He drinks socially in the Avengers period. He uses tobacco occasionally in older issues. His self-medicating modality is not pharmacological; it is *cognitive*—work, language, problem-immersion. This itself is diagnostic.

**Sleep, diet, exertion.** Canonically described as a chronic short-sleeper who works in 30-hour lab stretches; an enormous eater (metabolism); a compulsive tinkerer who treats downtime as aversive. The body is a workhorse he runs hard and discusses with detached, ironic ownership ("the doctor must occasionally consent to be patient"-style phrasings).

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** The data point to *earned secure-with-anxious undertow*. Both parents are present, loving, supportive—Norton appears repeatedly in the record as proud and accepting. So Hank's base-rate attachment is functional. But the *world* attacked him from infancy because of the visible mutation, producing what is best described as a split: a securely loved child who nevertheless internalized that *the public is hostile and must be managed*. Adult correlate: he forms deep, durable bonds (Bobby Drake, Jean Grey, Wonder Man, Trish Tilby, Abigail Brand) but maintains a buffered surface in all professional contexts. He does not trust groups; he trusts individuals he has vetted across years.

**Core defenses (mature-coded becoming neurotic under stress).**

- **Intellectualization, pathognomonic.** The single most visible defense in his record. Pain, fear, grief, romantic injury all arrive through the airlock of a Latin tag, a literary citation, an arch syntactic inversion. When Jean dies, when Trish leaves him over the secondary mutation, when he believes he is dying—his speech registers *up*, not down. He uses vocabulary as anesthetic.
- **Humor (mature defense, increasingly brittle).** Self-deprecation about appearance ("the bouncing blue beast," etc.) is his primary social lubricant. As the record progresses post-2005, the humor thins and acquires a bitter edge.
- **Sublimation.** Distress is converted into productive scientific output. The Endangered Species arc is sublimation at planetary scale.
- **Reaction formation.** The more bestial his body becomes, the more aggressively *cultured* his presentation: opera, Wilde, Shakespeare, the dressing gown. This is not hobby; it is rebuttal.
- **Rationalization (becoming pathological).** This is the defense that *destroys him*. He has, by middle period, developed a fluency in ethical reasoning so total that he can construct a justification for any action he wishes to take, and—critically—*believe it*. The time-travel decision, the Krakoa-era assassinations, the resurrection-abuse of Colossus: in every case Hank presents a coherent utilitarian case. The case is always wrong in ways his teammates can see and he cannot. The defense has eaten the function.
- **Isolation of affect.** He can describe his own dying body, his own moral compromise, his own grief, in dry clinical prose. Long-term cost: emotional information arrives late and misallocated.

**Affect regulation.** Baseline: high. He is the team's calm under fire for decades. Under sustained pressure (Decimation, Schism, terminal illness), regulation cracks not as outburst but as *creeping moral disinhibition*: he becomes willing to do things he previously would not, with no visible affective alarm. The lack of alarm is itself the symptom—he has so effectively isolated affect from cognition that his ethical brake (which is affective, not just logical) stops engaging.

**Cognitive style.** Hyper-associative, polymathic, fast. He reasons by analogy across domains. Strengths: synthesis, novel problem-solving, ethical framing of technical questions. Weaknesses: *overconfidence in his own framing*; insufficient deference to consensus when consensus disagrees; a tendency to treat moral problems as engineering problems with a uniquely-correct solution he is uniquely qualified to compute.

**Self-concept.** The core wound: *I am a monster who has compensated by becoming exceptional*. Every layer of his self-presentation—the diction, the manners, the witticisms, the cultural references, the heroism—is an answer to a question posed in childhood: *will being smart enough, charming enough, useful enough make the body acceptable?* When the body kept worsening (secondary, tertiary mutations), the answer trended toward *no*, and the compensatory architecture had to escalate. Late-period Hank's grandiosity—the unilateral world-saving, the spymaster certainty—is what happens when *useful* has to inflate to *indispensable* to keep the underlying self-image afloat.

**Compensatory mechanisms, positive ledger.** Genuine scientific contribution; genuine teaching vocation (Jean Grey School); genuine friendship capacity; consistent physical courage; long record of saving lives.

**Compensatory mechanisms, negative ledger.** The grandiose savior posture; the certainty he is the smartest moral actor in the room; the willingness to act without consent on others' behalf; the inability to update when peers tell him he is wrong.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

This is a high-functioning subject with *no formally psychotic, manic, or addictive pathology* in the record. The work here is to name the cluster he *does* present and to be precise about it.

**Primary formulation: Obsessive-Compulsive Personality features, ego-syntonic, with progressive narcissistic decompensation under chronic stress.** This is *not* OCD (no intrusive thoughts, no rituals to neutralize anxiety). It is the personality variant: rigid standards, devotion to work over leisure, scrupulous moralism, perfectionism, difficulty delegating, conviction in his own ethical judgment. Across the canon Hank meets the threshold cleanly in the work pattern, the perfectionism, the moral over-investment, and the difficulty letting others handle problems he believes he can solve.

**Narcissistic trajectory.** Early Hank does *not* present as narcissistic—he is self-deprecating and team-deferential. But the structure required to compensate for the body-shame produces, over decades, an *acquired* grandiosity. The clinical picture in late-period Hank (Krakoa-era spymaster, time-travel decision) meets several DSM narcissistic personality criteria functionally: belief that he can only be understood by special people, sense of entitlement to act without normal constraints, lack of empathy for those his decisions injure (the time-displaced teens; Colossus), exploitation in service of his ends. Critically this is *compensatory* narcissism rather than grandiose-from-baseline—it is the late-stage failure mode of the OCP scaffolding, not a primary structure.

**Body-image / dysmorphic component, subclinical but persistent.** Not BDD proper (he does not avoid mirrors, does not pursue corrective procedures—he in fact accepts each new form). But the *constant verbal renegotiation* of his appearance across sixty years of dialogue—the running joke about the fur, the studied elegance contrasted with the body—indicates an unresolved relationship to the physical self. Healthy adjustment would not require continuous commentary.

**Depressive features, situational, recurrent.** Post-Trish breakup; post-Decimation; post-Schism. Each episode resolves into work. He does not present a major depressive disorder; he presents *dysthymic undertow managed by sublimation*.

**Moral Injury (operational concept, not DSM).** This is the single best framework for late-period Hank. After Endangered Species (where he consorted with monsters and failed), and the Krakoa years (where he committed acts incompatible with his prior self), he carries the classic moral-injury profile: a person whose actions transgressed their own ethical code, who could not metabolize the transgression, and who consequently *doubled down*—because to stop and feel the harm would shatter the self-image. The mounting unilateralism is a moral-injury escalation pattern, not a personality change.

**Differential — what he is NOT.**
- *Not* sociopathic. He has empathy; he has guilt (which is why he must rationalize). A sociopath needs no justification; Hank generates elaborate ones.
- *Not* manic. The all-nighters and grand projects are baseline trait, not episodic.
- *Not* schizoid. He is gregarious, romantically engaged, friend-dense.
- *Not* paranoid. Even at his Krakoan worst, he is not delusional about threats; he is overconfident about solutions.
- *Not* substance-use disorder.
- *Not* PTSD in the criterion-meeting sense, despite ample qualifying events—his defenses are too effective for the intrusive symptomatology to surface; the cost is paid in moral disinhibition instead.

**Etiology summary.** Congenital visible difference + intact early attachment + early lesson that exceptional performance can buy partial social acceptance → OCP scaffolding built in childhood → repeated bodily betrayals in adulthood (each new mutation) requiring escalating compensation → moral injuries from Endangered Species forward → late-stage narcissistic decompensation and the destruction of the ethical brake.

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drives.** First, *to be of use*—to convert the body's perceived defect into demonstrable contribution. Second, *to be understood as a mind, not a beast*—the lifelong rebuttal to childhood gawkers and later anti-mutant publics. Third, *to protect his people*—genuine, not performative, and the lever by which his ethics get bent.

**Worldview / operational code.** *The world is improvable by intelligence applied with conscience; I have unusual quantities of both; therefore I bear unusual responsibility to act.* This is admirable in his first forty years and ruinous in his last twenty, because the syllogism contains no checksum for *when I am wrong*. He has not built into his operational code a serious doctrine of *epistemic humility against his own moral reasoning*. Charles Xavier modeled benevolent unilateralism for him; he absorbed it; he eventually exceeded it.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm, generous, witty in dyads and small groups; verbally performative in larger settings; loyal across decades to a small circle (Bobby Drake especially; Wonder Man; Jean); romantically tender but with a recurring pattern of partners (Trish Tilby) leaving when his bodily mutation crosses their threshold—a wound that reinforces the underlying shame. With professional peers he is collegial up to the moment he decides they are wrong, after which he proceeds without them. With those he views as ethically beneath him (Sinister, Osborn) he is contemptuous in a register he flatters himself is urbane.

**Under stress, escalation profile.** Stage 1: increased verbal performance, denser allusion, more elaborate humor. Stage 2: withdrawal into lab, marathon work cycles, declined sleep. Stage 3: unilateral action framed in airtight ethical prose. Stage 4 (late-period only): willingness to instrument other people—including allies—as means to his calculated end. He does not rage. He does not panic. He *plans*, with diminishing oversight, in widening circles, with rising certainty.

**Pressure points.**
- *The body.* Any framing of him as monstrous, freakish, or merely physical lands hard, even when he laughs.
- *Being out-thought.* Not in friendly contest—he enjoys that—but being made to look the lesser intellect in front of his peers wounds him disproportionately.
- *Loss of those who knew him pre-fur.* The original five are not just teammates; they are witnesses to a self he can no longer access. Threats to them destabilize him.
- *Being told he is becoming what he opposes.* This is precisely the accusation that, in canon, accelerates rather than arrests his slide—because the defensive structure cannot metabolize it, so it must be denied harder.
- *Mortality with unfinished business.* The terminal-diagnosis behavior shows that the prospect of dying *without having solved the problem* is what unlocks his most reckless decisions.

**Final analytical read.** Hank McCoy is the test case for what happens when an extraordinarily gifted child armors a real and early shame in extraordinary competence, sustains the armor brilliantly for decades through genuine virtue and genuine service, and then is asked to bear ethical loads—Decimation, Schism, terminal illness, Krakoa—that exceed what his particular defenses were ever built to carry. He does not break loudly. He converts, gradually and with exquisite reasoning, into a man whose intellect has been weaponized against his own conscience. The record's verdict—execution by his closest peers, replacement by an earlier self—is harsh but in-frame: it is what the system around him does when his rationalization engine has finally outrun every other check, internal and external. The tragedy is that every piece of him that did this was visible in the seventeen-year-old who first quoted Byron to deflect a question about his feet.
