# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP — SUBJECT: MORALES, MILES GONZALO

## Alias: Spider-Man (II) | Status: Adolescent–Young Adult, Active

---

### 1. HISTORY

The subject's behavioral record spans roughly fourteen years of published material (Ultimate Comics: Spider-Man #1, 2011, onward through the Bendis Ultimate run, the post-Secret Wars 616 integration, the Saladin Ahmed solo run, the current Cody Ziglar run, and crossover events including Spider-Verse, Champions, Civil War II, and Outlawed). What emerges is a remarkably consistent psychological subject across writers, with the variance largely a matter of focal depth rather than character contradiction.

**Formative substrate (pre-bite).** Brooklyn-raised, only child of Jefferson Davis (Black, former criminal turned upright family man with concealed history) and Rio Morales (Puerto Rican, working hospital staff). His paternal uncle, Aaron Davis, is the Prowler — a career thief whose presence in Miles's life is forbidden by his father. The subject is therefore raised inside a household whose foundational rule is **the suppression of family truth**: his father's past is hidden from him; his uncle's existence is hidden from the household; later, his father's S.H.I.E.L.D. work is also concealed. The subject is, from the start, embedded in a system where love and lying coexist. This is decisive for everything that follows.

He is academically gifted — admitted to Brooklyn Visions Academy by lottery — and his parents have placed enormous developmental capital on this single bet. The lottery scene (Ultimate Comics Spider-Man #1) is the foundational image: Miles weeps not from joy but from the *weight* of having been chosen. Even pre-bite, the subject is a child who experiences fortune as obligation.

**The bite and immediate aftermath (Ultimate Spider-Man #1–4, 2011).** The bite occurs in Aaron's apartment — i.e., Miles's powers literally originate in a violation of his father's rule. Causation and guilt are fused in the origin. His first response to discovering his powers is *concealment and refusal* — he wants to be normal, explicitly rejects Peter Parker's offer to discuss them. This refusal is broken only by Peter's death (USM #160), which Miles witnesses from the periphery and arrives too late to prevent. The subject's superhero career thus begins with **a failure to save a man whose role he then takes** — a survivor-guilt template that will recur structurally.

**Ultimate-era arcs.** Across the Bendis run, several events deepen the pattern: (a) the death of his mother Rio during the Venom assault (USM #200 era), for which Miles privately holds himself responsible and which causes him to quit being Spider-Man for over a year; (b) the revelation that his father had been a S.H.I.E.L.D. asset infiltrating Hydra — another layer of paternal concealment; (c) the discovery that Aaron is the Prowler and the ensuing confrontation in which Aaron dies (USM #200). The Ultimate-era Miles loses his mother, his uncle, and his mentor in serial fashion before age fifteen.

**Post-Secret Wars / 616 integration.** Rio is restored (a cosmic reset Miles is aware of in a complicated, unresolved way — *Spider-Men II* surfaces some of this). Behaviorally, the subject does not luxuriate in the restoration; he treats it as a gift he must justify. The Champions arc (Mark Waid, 2016–onward) is critical: Miles co-founds the team in explicit rejection of adult superhero violence after Civil War II, in which he is prophesied to kill Captain America and is briefly traumatized by Ulysses's vision. His response to being *told he will become a killer* is to reorient his entire ethical practice around prevention, de-escalation, and youth-coalition building. This is a defining ethical-formation event.

**Saladin Ahmed run (2018–2022).** Introduces Billie (baby sister), deepens the family-centered emotional core, and introduces the **clone arc** (Ultimatum / Selim) — a literal externalization of Miles's anxiety about his own identity and whether he is the "real" or "legitimate" Spider-Man. The arc with Ultimatum (a Miles from another reality who blames "our" Miles for displacement) is a near-perfect dramatization of imposter dread.

**Spider-Verse and identity contests.** Across multiple Spider-Verse events Miles is repeatedly framed by external forces (Morlun, the Inheritors, later the Spider-Society in adaptations) as **anomalous, additional, or illegitimate** — and reacts each time by asserting his right to exist through action rather than argument. The pattern is consistent.

**Ziglar run / current.** College-aged Miles working through differentiation from Peter, romantic instability (Barbara Rodriguez, Starling), and continued ambivalent contact with Aaron (resurrected) — the subject is now navigating *young adult* identity tasks while still carrying adolescent guilt structures.

**Evolution.** The subject hardens around competence but does *not* harden around affect. Where Peter Parker's arc tends toward irony and weary endurance, Miles's arc has trended toward earnestness deepened by experience — the humor remains nervous-adolescent rather than defensive-adult. He has become more capable and more willing to lead (Champions), but his core conflict — the gap between the private boy and the public symbol — has not resolved; it has only become more sophisticated.

### 2. MEDICAL

**Baseline.** Adolescent male, Afro-Latino, no pre-bite chronic conditions of record. Normal developmental trajectory.

**Inciting event.** Bite by a genetically altered Oz-formula spider (Ultimate continuity) / equivalent in 616. Resulting alterations:

- Proportional strength, reflex, and durability augmentation consistent with the standard Spider template.
- Wall-adhesion.
- Two abilities **unique to the subject**: **bio-electric "venom blast"** (a discharged neuromuscular shock, capable of being scaled from stun to structural damage) and **camouflage** (chameleon-effect blending with surroundings, including clothing).
- A "spider-sense" analog — somewhat less reliably depicted than Peter's; some writers downplay it, which itself is psychologically relevant (see below).

**Psychological significance of the powerset.** The two unique abilities are, not coincidentally, **a hiding power and a power that hurts on contact**. The subject's body has been issued an invisibility option and a defensive-electrical option. Both are introvert-coded. The venom blast in particular is one Miles initially struggles to control and discharges under emotional dysregulation — i.e., his body literally electrocutes things when he is overwhelmed. This is a somatic externalization of affect-regulation difficulty and should be read clinically as such.

**Injury record.** Standard for the role: repeated concussive trauma, lacerations, broken ribs, at least one episode of being shot (Ultimate era), multiple near-death experiences against Inheritors, Rabbles, the Assessor, etc. No documented chronic sequelae, but cumulative subclinical TBI burden is implicit and not addressed in-universe.

**Significant somatic-emotional events.**
- Witnessing parent death (Rio, Ultimate) — acute trauma exposure, Criterion A event.
- Witnessing/causing uncle death (Aaron) — perpetrator-victim trauma, particularly toxic etiology.
- Witnessing mentor death (Peter) — survivor exposure.
- Repeated capture, restraint, and depowering scenarios.

**Substances.** Negative. The subject is conspicuously straight-edge across the canon — no alcohol use depicted even in college-era stories, no recreational drugs. This is consistent with high parental superego internalization and an over-controlled coping style (see Psychological).

**Sleep.** Chronically insufficient. Patrol + school + family obligations create a documented sleep deficit referenced across multiple runs. Relevant to affect dysregulation and the venom-blast control issue.

### 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment.** The cleanest read in the corpus. The subject presents a **secure attachment base with anxious overlay**. The base is secure because Jefferson and Rio, despite their concealments, are present, warm, consistent, and explicitly loving — Miles can return to them and does, repeatedly, including for emotional regulation. The anxious overlay derives from (a) the perceived fragility of that base (he has watched his mother die once and cannot un-know this even after restoration), (b) the foundational experience that loved ones lie to him *for his protection*, which teaches him both that protective lying is love AND that he can never be sure what he is being protected from. The result is a young man who **loves easily but braces for loss**, and who replicates the protective-lying pattern in his own relationships (Ganke, his parents, romantic partners) without recognizing it as a reenactment.

**Defenses (in approximate order of prominence).**
1. **Sublimation** — the dominant healthy defense. Anxiety, grief, and rage are channeled into the patrol role, into protecting strangers, into building the Champions. This is mature and stabilizing but **load-bearing**; when sublimation is interrupted (the year he quits after Rio's death), the subject decompensates into withdrawal and depressive features.
2. **Compartmentalization** — extensive. School-Miles, family-Miles, Ganke-Miles, Spider-Miles, and (in some arcs) Peter's-protégé-Miles are kept in separate operational drawers. This is *learned from his father's example* and is functional until the drawers leak (which they do, repeatedly, and which causes the most painful scenes in the canon).
3. **Identification with the lost object** — taking up the Spider-Man name after Peter's death is the textbook example. This is partially adaptive (gives grief a vocation) and partially unresolved (he has spent his entire career trying to determine whether he is being Peter or being himself, and the clone/Ultimatum arc shows he has not finished this work).
4. **Reaction formation** against aggression — having been told he could become a killer (Civil War II), having an uncle who is a violent criminal, having a father with a violent past, the subject **over-corrects toward gentleness**. His refusal to use lethal force is not casual — it is structural and ideologically held, and reads as a defended position, not a neutral one.
5. **Intellectualization** — moderate; he is bright and articulate about his situation, can name his feelings, but the naming sometimes substitutes for the feeling.

**Affect regulation.** Underdeveloped for his cognitive level. The gap between his intellectual self-awareness and his physiological regulation is wide. The venom-blast control problem is the somatic tell: high arousal exceeds his containment capacity and discharges. With age this improves but does not resolve.

**Cognitive style.** Verbal, narrative, relationship-oriented. Strong theory of mind. Tends to construct ethical problems as problems-about-people rather than problems-about-principles, which is a strength in mediation and a weakness in strategic settings. Tends toward second-guessing during action and ruminative review afterward.

**Self-concept.** The core wound is **imposter dread structured around legitimacy** — Is he really Spider-Man? Did he earn it or inherit it? Is he the "right" Black Spider-Man or just the available one? Should he exist alongside Peter or only after him? The Ultimatum arc and the clone arc are external dramatizations of this internal question. He defends against this dread by **doing the job well**, which works until someone (an Inheritor, a Spider-Society, a clone) makes the legitimacy question explicit, at which point he is briefly destabilized before recovering through action.

**Compensatory mechanisms — positive.** Coalition-building (Champions), mentorship of younger heroes, a robust friend-confidant relationship with Ganke (one of the healthiest non-romantic bonds in the entire Spider-corpus), and a continued return to family as ground. These are real and protective.

**Compensatory mechanisms — negative.** Over-functioning, chronic sleep deprivation, the protective-lying reenactment, and a tendency to absorb responsibility for outcomes not actually under his control (Peter's death, Rio's death, civilian casualties he could not have reached). The internal accounting ledger always runs in the red by his own bookkeeping.

### 4. PSYCHIATRIC

A careful diagnostic read, derived from behavior, not assumed from genre.

**Meets criteria, by evidence:**

- **Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, chronic, with delayed expression in places.** Criterion A satisfied repeatedly (witnessing parental, mentor, and uncle deaths; near-death experiences). Criterion B intrusion symptoms shown in the post-Rio withdrawal arc and in nightmare/flashback panels across multiple runs. Criterion C avoidance shown in his year of quitting. Criterion D negative cognitions ("it should have been me," self-blame) recurrent. Criterion E hyperarousal supported by the venom-blast control problem and sleep deficit. Diagnosis is well-earned.

- **Persistent Complex Bereavement / Prolonged Grief features**, specifically regarding Peter, Aaron, and (counterfactually) Rio. The grief for Rio is *complicated by her restoration* — there is no clinical map for grieving someone who has come back, and the subject's behavior around her in post–Secret Wars stories suggests an unprocessed residue.

**Subthreshold but present:**

- **Generalized anxiety features** — chronic worry across multiple domains (family, school, identity, romantic, mission), but generally well-channeled and not, on the evidence, meeting full GAD criteria.
- **Depressive episode, single, moderate, in partial remission** — the post-Rio quitting period. Recurrence risk is real but no clear second episode in the record.

**Imposter syndrome** — not a DSM diagnosis but the clinical construct fits precisely and is arguably the subject's central organizing psychological feature. The trigger conditions (succeeding a famous predecessor, being publicly contested as the "real" version, being singled out as exceptional within his family) are textbook.

**Differential — what he is NOT.**

- **Not depressive personality / dysthymic.** Baseline affect is warm, humorous, engaged. The depressive episode was reactive and discrete.
- **Not narcissistic.** The legitimacy anxiety is the opposite of narcissistic structure; he questions his entitlement to the role rather than asserting it.
- **Not antisocial / conduct.** Despite an uncle who is and a father who was, the subject's prosocial orientation is genuine, internalized, and consistent under pressure. The Civil War II prophecy is dramatic precisely because it is so out-of-character.
- **Not avoidant personality.** He withdraws episodically under acute trauma, not characterologically. He forms and sustains close bonds easily.
- **Not OCD** — no compulsive or ritualized features beyond patrol routines, which are role-appropriate.
- **Not a substance use disorder** — actively negative.

**Etiology summary.** Constitutional warmth and intelligence + secure-but-anxious attachment + family culture of protective concealment + early exposure to multiple Criterion A traumas + premature role acquisition (took on a public adult identity at 13) + chronic role-overload + unresolved legitimacy narrative = the picture above. The wound is **earned, specific, and treatable**; he is not characterologically disordered, he is a traumatized adolescent with intact ego strength carrying more than his developmental stage is built to carry.

### 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drives.** (1) To deserve what he has been given — the academy seat, the powers, the name, the mother restored. (2) To protect the family bond from the kinds of ruptures that have already happened once. (3) To be *himself* inside a role defined by someone else. These three drives are in tension: the first pushes toward self-erasure into duty, the second toward concealment, the third toward differentiation. The tension is the engine of the character.

**Worldview / operational code.** Fundamentally communitarian. The subject does not believe in lone heroism — he believes in coalition, in younger people, in neighborhoods, in showing up for specific named individuals. Ethical reasoning is **relational rather than principled**: he reasons from "what does this person in front of me need" outward to rules, not from rules inward to cases. He is suspicious of institutional authority (S.H.I.E.L.D., adult Avengers post–Civil War II) without being anti-institutional in temperament; he wants better institutions, not no institutions. He believes lying to protect people is sometimes correct *and* corrosive, and he has not resolved this and probably will not.

**Interpersonal pattern.** Warm, self-deprecating, quick to apologize, slow to confront. He defers to people he respects (Peter, his father, Ms. Marvel as a peer) and protects people he loves through both presence and concealment. He is unusually capable of friendship across difference. Romantically he is earnest, anxious, and prone to letting the Spider-Man secret hollow out the relationship before he addresses it directly. He does not weaponize his intelligence. He apologizes more than he should and asks for help less than he should.

**Stress / escalation pattern.** Under low-to-moderate stress: humor, increased verbosity, sublimation into the mission, sleep deficit. Under high stress: compartments leak, the venom-blast destabilizes, second-guessing intensifies, he becomes more accommodating outwardly while the internal ledger grows more punishing. Under acute trauma: withdrawal, quitting behavior, depressive collapse — followed, given enough time and the right relational support, by reintegration and return. Decompensation is not characterological; recovery is reliable when support is available. **He does not become cruel under pressure.** He becomes either heroic or absent.

**Pressure points.**
1. Threats to family — especially to Rio (now twice-vulnerable in his mind) and Billie. The subject can be moved to act against his own ethical code only here, and even here he has held.
2. Legitimacy challenges — being told he is not the "real" Spider-Man, is an anomaly, is replaceable, is a clone, is a mistake. He recovers from these but they cost him.
3. The Aaron axis — any reactivation of the uncle relationship reopens the original wound (the bite, the father's rule, the death, the resurrection). Aaron is the unhealed limb.
4. Being asked to kill, or being told he will. The Civil War II vision is a permanent splinter.
5. Public exposure of the identity to his parents (a recurring threat, periodically realized and reset). The protective-lying structure cannot survive a fully transparent household, and he knows it.

**Resilience factors.** Genuine and substantial: intact family bonds, durable friendship with Ganke, peer network through the Champions, working relationship with Peter that has matured past pure mentorship, internalized ethical clarity, cognitive flexibility, capacity for help-seeking when finally cornered into it, and a baseline temperament that returns to warmth. The subject's prognosis, in any clinical sense, is good. He is a young man carrying an enormous load with the equipment to carry it, provided the load does not increase faster than the equipment matures.
