# UATU ANALYTICAL WORKUP: RICHARD MILHOUSE JONES

## 1. HISTORY

Richard "Rick" Jones enters the published record as the proximate cause of the Hulk's creation (*Incredible Hulk* #1, 1962): a teenage orphan on a dare, driving onto a gamma bomb test site to prove his nerve to peers. Bruce Banner sees him, runs onto the range to push him into a trench, and absorbs the gamma blast. Rick survives untouched. This single act — a dare-impulse causing the irradiation of another man — is the cornerstone trauma and the engine of every subsequent behavior pattern for sixty years of publication.

His immediate post-origin behavior is telling: he does not flee. He attaches himself to Banner as caretaker, secret-keeper, and emotional anchor, sleeping outside Banner's lab, fielding General Ross, calming the Hulk with verbal soothing and (eventually) lullabies (*Hulk* #3–6, early Lee/Kirby/Ditko run). The pattern — *I caused harm to a powerful figure, therefore I must merge my life with his and absorb the consequences* — is set within the first arc.

When Hulk leaves with the Avengers, Rick smoothly transfers attachment. He becomes Captain America's de facto sidekick during the Lee era (*Avengers* #4 onward, mid-1960s), explicitly framed by Cap as a Bucky-substitute. Rick himself initially welcomes this, then chafes when Cap refuses to formally name him Bucky — the first recorded instance of his pattern of seeking adoptive father-figures and being wounded by their reservations. He briefly wears a Bucky costume (*Cap* #110-ish, Steranko era) and is nearly killed, reinforcing Cap's reluctance.

The Kree-Skrull War (*Avengers* #89–97, 1971–72, Thomas) is the second formative arc. Bonded to Captain Marvel (Mar-Vell) by Nega-Bands — they swap places between universes by clapping the bands — Rick becomes literally fused to another powerful man whose life he must share. Under existential pressure he manifests "the power of the Destiny Force," briefly conjuring Golden Age heroes from imagination to end the war. Significant: his power manifests only when he is the vessel/sidekick for someone else, never as primary actor.

Mar-Vell's death from cancer (*Death of Captain Marvel*, 1982, Starlin) is Rick's second catastrophic loss-of-a-symbiote. He is present at the deathbed. The pattern of *attaching to dying or doomed great men* is now confirmed: Banner (cursed), Cap (frozen out of time, perpetually grieving Bucky), Mar-Vell (terminal). Rick selects partners whose tragedy mirrors and validates his own guilt.

The Peter David *Hulk* run (1987–98) is the deepest interior portrait. Rick marries Marlo Chandler — his first non-symbiotic, lateral adult attachment, and notably it is she who pursues him. He writes a tell-all book about the Hulk (*Hulk* #-1, retrospective), which earns him money but estranges Banner; classic guilt-driven self-sabotage of the very bond he most needs. He becomes briefly Hulk-ified himself during "Heroes Reborn"–adjacent arcs and after Tony Stark / Abomination-related incidents — repeatedly *becoming the thing he caused*, a near-perfect symbolic enactment of survivor's guilt.

Later eras: bonded again to a second Captain Marvel (Genis-Vell) via Nega-Bands (Peter David's *Captain Marvel*, late '90s–2000s); the symbiote pattern is so entrenched it now reads as compulsion. He becomes "A-Bomb" (blue Abomination analog) under the Red Hulk arc (*Hulk* vol. 2, Loeb, 2008+) — voluntarily taking on a monstrous body to fight alongside Banner. Loma Shade / Abomination retcons aside, the through-line: Rick keeps choosing transformation into a monstrous body to stand beside Banner as equal rather than as the unscathed onlooker. *Survivor's guilt resolved through self-monsterization.*

Across all eras and writers (Lee, Thomas, Mantlo, Byrne, David, Jones, Loeb, Pak, Ewing), the constants are: chronic proximity to gamma/cosmic catastrophe; serial sidekick-bonding; recurrent kidnapping/hostage status; ingratiating verbal style; and an almost evangelical loyalty disproportionate to what he receives.

## 2. MEDICAL

**Gamma exposure (presumed sub-lethal):** Origin moment placed Rick at ground zero of a gamma-bomb detonation, shielded by trench and Banner's body. Later transformations (Hulk-form during David run; A-Bomb conversion via Leader/Intelligencia tech) strongly suggest latent gamma sensitization — the writers retroactively confirm his cells were primed. Repeated bodily transformations follow.

**Bodily transformations:** (a) temporary gamma mutation into a Hulk-analog; (b) A-Bomb form — full somatic conversion to armored blue behemoth, partially reversible; (c) decades of Nega-Band swaps with Mar-Vell and Genis-Vell — repeated displacement of his physical body into the Negative Zone, replaced by another's. The cumulative somatic experience: *my body is not reliably mine; it is a switching station for other men's existences.*

**Injuries:** Multiple concussions and near-deaths during Bucky-substitute period; severe wounds in Kree-Skrull War; cardiac/neural strain reported during Destiny Force manifestation; radiation-related illness implied in late David run.

**Substance use:** Not a defining feature — notably *absent* given his trauma load and 1960s/70s teen-runaway profile. He does not anesthetize chemically. This matters diagnostically: his anesthesia is *attachment itself.*

**Adolescence/development:** Orphaned young (parents deceased; raised by an uncle who died, leaving him fully alone shortly before the origin event). No completed secondary education at origin. Itinerant, guitar-playing, drifter aesthetic — period-coded but reflecting genuine rootlessness.

**Psychological impact of somatic history:** The repeated experience of his body being a vessel, swap-partner, or monster-shell produces a stable identity dissociation from the body — he treats his physical form as instrumental, expendable, and properly subordinate to whatever mission a stronger figure requires. The lack of permanent disability despite proximity to catastrophes that maim or kill others reinforces survivor's guilt at a cellular level: *why am I still walking around?*

## 3. PSYCHOLOGICAL

**Attachment style:** Anxious-preoccupied with strong caregiving (compulsive-caregiving) overlay. Orphaned early, no secure base; the gamma incident gave him a trauma-bonded surrogate parent (Banner) whom he could simultaneously need and atone toward. He approaches every subsequent significant relationship in this template: idealize the powerful other, fuse with them, serve them, fear abandonment, tolerate enormous mistreatment (the Hulk has nearly killed him repeatedly; he returns). Marriage to Marlo represents his only sustained lateral attachment and is achieved largely because she does the pursuing, neutralizing his anxious approach.

**Core wound:** Causal guilt. He believes — accurately, in literal terms — that his impulsivity created the Hulk. Every subsequent behavior is reparation. This is not a delusion; it is true. Which makes it harder to treat than ordinary survivor's guilt, because reality keeps confirming it.

**Defenses (in rough hierarchy from most to least mature):**
- *Altruism / compulsive caregiving* — the dominant adaptive defense. He genuinely helps and is genuinely useful. This is the healthy face of his pathology.
- *Identification with the aggressor / identification with the wounded* — he becomes Hulk-like, becomes A-Bomb, takes Bucky's mantle, accepts the Nega-Band; merging with the figure he wronged or the figure who replaces the one he wronged.
- *Reaction formation* — the rage and resentment one would expect at being chronically endangered, kidnapped, and underestimated rarely surfaces; replaced by cheerful loyalty and self-deprecating humor.
- *Undoing* — perpetually trying to re-do the original moment correctly (throwing himself between Banner and threats, taking the hit).
- *Acting out (mild)* — the tell-all book; brief flirtations with celebrity; periodic outbursts at being sidelined.

**Affect regulation:** Outwardly stable, even buoyant. Verbal stream-of-quip is a regulation tool — he talks through fear, talks Hulk down, talks himself into action. Under genuine isolation (rare in his life; he engineers against it) he decompensates into depressive rumination (visible in late David run and post-Mar-Vell-death issues).

**Cognitive style:** Quick, street-smart, pattern-matching rather than analytic. Not formally educated; functionally intelligent. Highly emotionally attuned — he reads Banner's mood shifts faster than trained scientists. This emotional acuity is a trauma adaptation (hypervigilance to caregiver state, classic in children of unstable households and orphans).

**Self-concept:** Bifurcated. *Surface*: regular guy, lucky kid, sidekick, "just Rick." *Substrate*: I am the man who caused the Hulk; I owe a debt I cannot pay; I am only valuable as an adjunct to greatness. He resists individuation actively — being his own protagonist would mean facing that the debt is unpayable.

**Compensatory mechanisms (positive):** genuine courage; demonstrated ability to act under extreme threat; loyalty that has at points saved the Avengers, Banner, and the Kree-Skrull from worse outcomes; the Destiny Force manifestation suggests his guilt-driven attachment can, under maximum pressure, output something like creative grace.

**Compensatory mechanisms (negative):** chronic self-endangerment; inability to refuse the next bond; tolerance of mistreatment that enables others' pathology (he stabilizes the Hulk *and* enables Banner's avoidance of his condition); occasional grandiosity spikes (book, brief celebrity) that read as compensatory bursts followed by guilt-deflation.

## 4. PSYCHIATRIC

The presentation does not cleanly fit one DSM category; it is a trauma-spectrum picture with personality features. Reasoning shown:

**Primary working formulation: Complex PTSD (ICD-11) with prolonged survivor's guilt.** Evidence: index trauma (gamma blast) with ongoing reminders; affective constriction around anger; persistent negative self-concept ("I caused this"); interpersonal disturbance characterized by fusion with surrogate figures; chronic threat exposure (kidnappings, near-deaths) without escape. The C-PTSD frame fits better than simple PTSD because the trauma is *ongoing and identity-defining*, not a discrete event being recovered from.

**Comorbid: Persistent survivor's guilt with "moral injury" features.** Distinguished from depressive guilt because it is reality-anchored (his action did cause harm) and because it drives toward action (reparation) rather than withdrawal. Closer to combat-veteran moral injury literature than to MDD-guilt.

**Personality features (subclinical, not full disorder):**
- *Dependent traits*: difficulty initiating activity without a primary attachment figure; tolerates exploitation; transfers attachment rapidly when one figure is lost. Falls short of full Dependent PD because he demonstrates independent agency under crisis and sustains the Marlo marriage.
- *Self-defeating / masochistic features* (DSM-III-R appendix category, still clinically useful): chooses relationships and situations leading to disappointment/harm; rejects opportunities for pleasure; undermines own successes (tell-all → estrangement). Does not meet threshold for personality disorder; better understood as guilt-driven.

**Differential — what he is NOT:**
- *Not Borderline PD*: stable identity (however guilt-organized), stable long-term relationships, no chronic emptiness, no characteristic splitting or rage. The fusion behavior superficially resembles BPD but lacks the abandonment-rage signature.
- *Not Histrionic*: the celebrity moments are compensatory bursts, not a stable attention-seeking style.
- *Not Major Depressive Disorder (recurrent)*: euthymic baseline, situational depressive episodes only around loss (Mar-Vell death).
- *Not Substance Use Disorder*: notably abstinent given risk factors.
- *Not Antisocial*: hyper-prosocial; conscience is in fact hypertrophied, the opposite end of the spectrum.
- *Not a delusional disorder despite the cosmic-scale guilt*: the guilt is factually grounded.

**Risk profile:** Low suicidality (his guilt drives toward service, not self-destruction directly — though self-endangerment serves an equivalent function and should be noted). Moderate-to-high risk for repeated traumatic exposure by virtue of life choices. Low risk of harm to others. High risk of being exploited by figures who recognize and use his loyalty pattern.

## 5. PERSONALITY

**Core drive:** Atonement through proximity. Not heroism for its own sake — heroism *beside* and *for* a specific damaged great man. The drive is to be the indispensable adjunct, because being indispensable cancels the original sin of being the unharmed bystander.

**Operational code:** (a) When in doubt, attach to the most wounded powerful figure in the room. (b) Make yourself useful in ways the great figure cannot do for himself — translation, emotional management, mundane logistics, willingness to be hit. (c) Take the dare, then take the consequences. (d) Never be the protagonist; the protagonist position is reserved for those who deserve it. (e) Loyalty is paid in full regardless of what is received back.

**Worldview:** Fundamentally hopeful and pro-social, despite the trauma load — he believes in the possibility of redemption (his own) and in the essential goodness of his chosen figures (even the Hulk). He does not see the universe as hostile; he sees himself as a debt-holder within a basically decent one.

**Interpersonal pattern:** Magnetically loyal; reads emotional weather expertly; defuses tension through humor and physical presence; tolerates contempt and danger from primary attachment without retaliation; warm but slightly performative with peripheral figures; struggles with peer/lateral relationships because he has no template for them (Marlo is the exception that proves the rule). Tends to attract figures with savior wounds because he fits their need to be needed-by-someone-loyal.

**Stress / escalation pattern:** Under low stress: chatty, deflecting, useful. Under moderate stress: doubles down on caregiving, takes physical risks. Under high stress (primary attachment endangered or lost): capable of extraordinary action — Destiny Force manifestation, voluntary monsterization, suicide-runs toward the threat. Under sustained isolation (rare): depressive collapse, rumination on original guilt, regression to orphan-self. He does not rage outward; the energy goes into the body or into self-sacrifice.

**Pressure points / leverage:**
- The Banner/Hulk attachment is the master key — threats to or from Banner override every other consideration.
- Implication that he is *not* needed, that he is a hanger-on, a tourist to heroism — cuts deeper than physical threat.
- Memory of the gamma test site; any reminder of the original moment destabilizes affect.
- Loss of a surrogate-father figure: each prior loss (Mar-Vell, others) reopens the wound; another such loss would risk genuine breakdown.
- Conversely, *being told the debt is paid* by the wronged party — by Banner, sincerely — is the unspoken thing he has organized his life around and never quite received, and the prospect of it could be used to move him in any direction.

**Strengths the file must respect:** Real courage, repeatedly demonstrated. Real loyalty, not performed. Real emotional intelligence — he is one of the few human beings whom the Hulk consistently listens to, which is not a small datum. Whatever the pathology of the origin, the man built from it is, by any honest accounting, a good one.
