Grizzly Knights

Episode 7: Late Night, Long Shadows

In the stillness of an after-hours bar, Logan, Jean, and Clint grapple with ghosts from their pasts and the weight of connection.

* * *

There is a moment, just before Clint drops the duffel onto the scuffed linoleum, where the whole room acknowledges his entrance without looking. The jukebox hums quietly beneath the rattle of the ice in Logan’s glass. An old fan ticks overhead, spatters of light from the neon beer signs catching on the faint sheen of sweat that Clint hasn’t bothered to wipe away.

“Rough night, Barton?” Logan asked.

“Want to make it rougher, or are we sharing this time?” Jean asked.

“Depends. Sharing the booze, the story, or the punching bag?” Clint asked.

“Whichever one gets you to shut up and sit down,” Logan said.

Clint slides into the booth, the vinyl seat hissing under his weight as the duffel settles at his feet with a dull thud. Jean leans back, the faintest trace of a smirk curling at the edge of her lips as a wisp of her auburn hair catches the dim red glow. Across the table, Logan tilts his glass, watching the amber swirl like it might answer a question he hasn’t asked aloud.

“If we're picking, I'll take the story first. You owe us that much just for the entrance,” Jean said.

“Thought the duffel said it all—bad guys, bad timing, bad landing,” Clint said.

“Hmph. Sounds like Tuesday,” Logan said.

“So what made this one worth dragging it in here?” Jean asked.

Clint exhales sharply, reaching for the nearest glass and tipping it toward his lips, not bothering to ask if it’s his. The old wood of the table creaks faintly as Jean leans forward, her elbows resting just shy of the forgotten ring marks left by decades of drinks. Logan doesn’t shift, doesn’t blink—he’s still watching the whiskey, though now it’s clear his attention is elsewhere.

“Dog needed saving. Again. What can I say, he’s got a talent for trouble,” Clint said.

“Guess he learned from the best,” Logan said.

“Or he just knows you’ll always show up, no matter how bad the odds. Not a terrible trait to pick up,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, somebody’s gotta. Figure if I screw up enough, maybe he won’t grow up to be me,” Clint said.

The jukebox clicks, the next song slower, heavier, as though it senses the weight that has settled over the booth. Clint spins the empty glass in his hand, the base rasping against the table’s surface in tired circles. Jean’s eyes linger on him longer than necessary, her expression unreadable, while Logan finally looks up, the tilt of his head sharp enough to cut through the uneasy silence.

“Takes one to break the cycle. You sure you're not just stallin'?” Logan asked.

“And if he is? Sometimes stalling means you're still figuring out which way to move without making it worse,” Jean said.

“Or it just means you’re hoping the problem solves itself before you have to,” Clint said.

“Hope’s got a hell of a track record, don’t it?” Logan asked.

A distant laugh from the bar punctuates the quiet between them, too loud and too oblivious to the storm brewing at their table. Clint flicks the edge of the glass with his thumb, the faint chime of crystal meeting calloused skin drowned out as Logan pours himself another drink. Jean keeps her hands still now, though the tension in her shoulders betrays the calm she’s trying to wear.

“Hope doesn’t solve problems. It’s just what gets you to stand up and try again,” Jean said.

“Yeah, and trying again is just another shot at screwing it up worse,” Clint said.

“Could be. Or it’s the shot that gets it right. Only way to find out’s doin' it,” Logan said.

“Or deciding what you’re willing to risk losing if you don’t. That’s the part we never say out loud, isn’t it?” Jean asked.

The hum of the jukebox settles into the steady rhythm of a bassline, low and brooding, filling the cracks in their silence. The duffel at Clint’s feet leans awkwardly, its half-open zipper revealing a smear of dried blood on canvas. Logan’s finger traces the rim of his glass, methodical and slow, while Jean’s gaze drops, her eyes fixed on some unspoken weight in the wood grain of the table.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the bartender returns, where the quiet feels territorial. Jean shifts her weight against the cracked leather of the bench, her nails pressing into the varnish as if something might splinter under her touch. Logan’s gaze flicks to the bloody duffel again—just a glance, sharp and uninterrogating—before it lands back on Clint, who isn’t moving much except to breathe.

“You gonna tell me whose blood that is, or we just gonna enjoy the ambiance?” Logan asked.

“Not sure "enjoy" fits the mood, Logan. But I’d like to know too,” Jean said.

“Not mine, if that’s what you’re worried about. Well... mostly not mine,” Clint said.

“Mostly. That’s a damn fine answer for a man who looks like he lost a round with a woodchipper,” Logan said.

The bartender slides the beer across the table, the glass catching the dim light in fractured lines along the foam. Clint’s fingers close around it, slow and deliberate, but the tension in his shoulders doesn’t release. Jean’s eyes linger on the motion before drifting upward, her expression unreadable, caught between apprehension and something harder to name.

“If it’s not yours, then whose is it? That’s not the kind of thing you leave half-answered, Clint,” Jean said.

“The kind where more details just make it worse, Red. Trust me on this one,” Clint said.

“Seen worse. Heard worse. So spill, or I can sniff it out myself—your call,” Logan said.

“It’s not just his call if we’re sitting this close to the mess,” Jean said.

The jukebox hums low, a blues riff creeping under the weight of their stalled conversation. Clint tilts the beer to his lips, the glass clinking faintly as he sets it down harder than he means to. Logan leans back, the creak of the bench underscoring the unspoken standoff, while Jean’s gaze sharpens, slicing into the quiet like a scalpel hovering just above the skin.

“It wasn’t supposed to turn into a mess, alright? Broken arrow situation, bad intel, and now I’ve got a bag full of regret and a lap full of questions I don’t really wanna answer,” Clint said.

“Guess you better start with the ones that matter, 'cause I ain't much for sitting here playin' therapist,” Logan said.

“Clint, we’re not asking for therapy. We’re asking for clarity. Who’s hurt, and is it over?” Jean asked.

“Who’s hurt? Too many people, Jean. Is it over? Not even close,” Clint said.

The answer lands heavy, absorbing the room like spilled liquor on old wood. Jean exhales through her nose, a measured release that barely moves the air, her fingertips tracing a shallow arc against the tabletop. Logan’s jaw tightens, the smallest flicker of his teeth as he grinds down whatever was on the tip of his tongue. Clint’s eyes stay locked on the half-empty glass, as if draining it might spare him from the weight of everything unsaid.

“Then finish your drink and figure out your next move, 'cause waitin' around’s a good way to get more people hurt,” Logan said.

“Or worse, to make the kind of mistake you can’t walk back from,” Jean said.

“Mistakes are kinda my brand, Jean. Walking back from 'em? That’s the part I’ve been winging for years,” Clint said.

“Sounds like you’re due for an upgrade, then. Winging it’s only gonna get you so far before it gets you dead,” Logan said.

The jukebox skips on a note, the hesitation cutting through the air like an unsteady heartbeat before the next track picks up, slower than the last. Clint’s hand slips from the glass to his temple, rubbing circles into the skin as if trying to erase a thought he can’t outrun. Logan shifts forward, elbows resting heavy on the table, the faint scrape of his jacket breaking the quiet like the scrape of a match against rough stone. Jean watches them both, her stillness a weight all its own.

“Clint, winging it’s not enough when the cost isn't just yours. What’s the plan?” Jean asked.

“Plan? Right now, it’s survive the night and figure out who to apologize to first. Beyond that... still working on it,” Clint said.

“Apologies don’t do much for the dead, Barton. Start working on the part that keeps 'em alive,” Logan said.

“Or find someone who can hold the line with you, before you end up carrying the weight alone again,” Jean said.

The overhead bulb sputters faintly, casting uneven shadows that stretch and contract like they’re pacing. Clint glances sideways, toward the duffel on the floor, the weight of its presence pressing against the conversation without needing a word. Logan drums his fingers once against the table—sharp, deliberate, a sound that cuts but doesn’t linger—while Jean, still unmoving, locks her focus on Clint like she’s daring him not to look away first.

* * *

There is a moment, just before Jean sits, when the tavern feels like it holds its breath. Logan’s weight shifts incrementally, the scrape of denim soft against the worn leather booth. Clint’s gaze flickers between the two of them, his beer untouched, the amber liquid trembling faintly as his hand rests too firmly on the table.

“Jean, you want somethin’, or you just here to stare holes in me?” Logan asked.

“Maybe both. You’ve been quieter than usual—thought I’d check if the sky was falling or if it’s just you,” Jean said.

“Guess that makes me the lucky bastard caught in the fallout zone then, huh?” Clint asked.

“Coulda picked a better spot, Barton. Ain’t exactly known for blue skies around me,” Logan said.

The overhead bulb sputters again, casting fleeting shadows over Jean’s face as she leans back, her posture deceptively casual against the booth’s padding. Logan’s fingers find the edge of his glass, spinning it a fraction without lifting it, the amber liquid moving just enough to scatter the light. Clint exhales hard through his nose, his fingers drumming twice on the table before falling still.

“Seems like nobody here’s aiming for sunshine,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, might as well toast to the storm, right?” Clint asked.

“Drinkin’ to storms don’t stop ‘em from wreckin’ you, but hell, pass the bottle,” Logan said.

“Depends on the storm. Some you drink through, some you just have to burn your way out of,” Jean said.

The room seems to shrink around the weight of her words, the pause thick with something unspoken and unyielding. Logan’s jaw tightens, the faint grind of teeth audible under the low murmur of the tavern’s other patrons. Clint shifts in his seat, his hand finally wrapping around his beer, but he doesn’t raise it, the fingers firm and unmoving like the drink might anchor him.

“Guess it’s a pick-your-poison kinda night, then,” Clint said.

“Always is. Just don’t fool yourself into thinkin' there’s a safe one,” Logan said.

“Safety's relative. Sometimes it’s just the choice that hurts less,” Jean said.

“And sometimes it’s the one that blows up in your face, but hey, keeps it interesting,” Clint said.

The air between them feels like static now, buzzing faintly with the friction of bruised truths and half-meant bravado. Logan tips his glass back, the liquid burning its slow path as he swallows, his gaze fixed somewhere far beyond the empty space of the table. Jean’s hand, resting on her knee, curls slightly—her nails press dull crescents into denim without a sound.

“Interestin' ain't always the same as worth it,” Logan said.

“No, but we all keep playing anyway,” Jean said.

“Guess we’re just a bunch of lousy gamblers, huh? House always wins, but we still throw down,” Clint said.

“Maybe. Or maybe we’re just too damn stubborn to fold,” Logan said.

The din of the tavern fades into a muted hum, swallowed by the weight of their exchange. Clint finally brings the beer to his lips, the glass clinking faintly against his teeth as his eyes linger on Logan, gauging the edges of that stubbornness. Jean shifts closer to the table, her other hand brushing the edge of Logan’s sleeve, a fleeting contact that lands heavier than the motion suggests.

“Stubborn’s one word for it. Relentless, reckless—take your pick,” Jean said.

“Reckless? I’m sitting right here, y’know,” Clint said.

“She ain’t wrong, Barton. You’re a walkin’ advertisement for bad ideas,” Logan said.

“Takes one to know one, Logan,” Jean said.

The faint smirk tugging at Logan’s mouth doesn’t reach his eyes, his gaze still anchored to some middle distance that the others can only guess at. Clint sets his beer down with a solid thunk, the moisture from the glass spreading in a slow circle on the wood. Jean’s posture softens just enough to betray the exhaustion she’s been so carefully masking, her shoulders edging down as though the weight of the booth itself is pressing against her.

* * *

The overhead lights hum softly, their flickering casting uneven shadows over the scratched surface of the table. Clint turns his bottle in slow, idle rotations, the beer inside sloshing faintly as condensation slicks his fingertips. Jean’s stare lingers on the clear liquid in her glass, the wedge of lime untouched, while Logan’s hand tightens around his tumbler with the sort of still menace reserved for storms building on the horizon.

“Guess we all got our ghosts. Some louder than others,” Logan said.

“Or just... closer,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, mine are loud, close, and drunker than I am,” Clint said.

“Sounds like you need another round, Barton. Turn down the volume a bit,” Logan said.

The bartender leans against the counter, polishing a glass with the kind of lethargy that only a near-empty room permits. Somewhere near the door, a patron coughs, his chair legs scraping against the weathered floor as he shifts. The booth holds its silence longer than it might've in a brighter hour, each occupant tethered to the gravity of their own unspoken truths.

“Sometimes turning it down just makes you hear it clearer,” Jean said.

“Then I’ll stick to drowning it out. Seems like the more reliable option,” Clint said.

“Drowning don't fix much. Just leaves you soggy and pissed off,” Logan said.

“Better soggy than burned. Trust me,” Jean said.

The ice in Jean’s glass settles with a faint crackle as she sets it down, the sound barely audible beneath the low drone of the jukebox playing to no one in particular. Logan leans back, his weight pulling the leather seat with a groan, but his eyes remain fixed on the scuffed tabletop as if the grain might hold an answer it can't give. Clint exhales through his nose, not quite a laugh, not quite a sigh, the bottle at his lips long enough to suggest he wants the silence, but not the thoughts that fill it.

“Soggy, pissed off, and still standing beats dry and six feet under. Pretty sure that’s the math,” Clint said.

“Standing don’t mean you’re living, Barton,” Logan said.

“And dying doesn’t always mean you’re gone,” Jean said.

“Guess we’re all just somewhere in the in-between, huh? Cheers to that mess,” Clint said.

Clint raises his bottle slightly, the dull clink as it meets the edge of the table a sound only they could hear in this near-abandoned bar. Jean doesn’t move to toast, her fingers resting lightly against the rim of her glass, still and deliberate. Logan’s tumbler stays on the table, untouched, the amber liquid inside catching the low light and holding it, heavy and unmoving.

“Not much of a toast, Barton. But I’ve heard worse,” Logan said.

“The in-between’s where all the weight is anyway,” Jean said.

“Yeah, feels like home—messy, loud, and impossible to unpack,” Clint said.

“Home’s just a word for a place that lets you stay. Rest don’t matter much,” Logan said.

A low rumble of laughter from the far end of the bar cuts through the room’s quiet, sharp and short-lived, like someone remembering they shouldn’t be happy here. The faint scrape of Logan’s thumb against the rim of his glass is the only response in the booth for a long moment, his gaze dragging to Clint before it drops again. Jean shifts slightly, her knee brushing the edge of the table, an almost imperceptible motion that only seems to deepen the collective stillness.

“Sometimes staying’s the hardest part,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, leaving's no walk in the park either. Trust me,” Clint said.

“Leaving’s easy. It’s what you leave behind that does the damage,” Logan said.

“Or what you take with you,” Jean said.

The jukebox skips, a brief hiccup in the song before the notes realign and carry on like nothing happened. Clint sets his bottle down harder than he means to, the sound breaking against the booth’s silence like a crack along the surface of ice. Jean’s hand curls tighter around her glass, her thumbnail pressing faint crescents into the condensation slick on the sides.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the bar’s low murmur gives way to sharp voices, when the quiet feels almost delicate, something precariously balanced. The argument, when it comes, is nothing—a burst of syllables too slurred to make out, chairs scraping as bodies shift sharply in the wrong rhythm. Logan’s eyes cut toward the sound, his shoulders coil hard as a wound spring, and the shallow ripple of the jukebox’s tune can’t drown the tension that settles over the booth.

“Enough racket already. Keep it down,” Logan said.

“It’s all right, Logan. Let it pass. Not every noise is a fight waiting to happen,” Jean said.

“You ever notice how the quiet before the noise is the worst part? Like you’re bracing for a hit that might not even come,” Clint said.

“Yeah, Clint. 'Cause it usually does,” Logan said.

The bartender's rag moves in slow, indifferent circles over the counter, his eyes flicking toward the men at the heart of the scuffle but not committing to intervention. One of them stands, a little too fast, the uneasy roll of his center of gravity betraying how much whiskey is dragging on his equilibrium. Chairs scrape again, louder this time, and Logan’s fingers flex against the edge of the table, knuckles whitening, as though the noise has reached inside his skull and jarred something loose.

“Logan, focus. They're drunk, not dangerous. Save the claws for the real hits,” Jean said.

“She’s right, Logan. Last thing this joint needs is you playing bouncer. Let the bartender earn his tip for once,” Clint said.

“I ain't lookin' to play anything. Just don't like leavin' my back open,” Logan said.

“You’re not. We’ve got it, Logan. You can let it down, just a little,” Jean said.

The booth falls still, though the tension lingers like smoke that refuses to clear. Clint’s gaze settles on Logan’s hand, the way it hovers just shy of a fist, faint grooves from years of scars catching the low light. Across the room, the bartender leans his weight forward with deliberate slowness, speaking in a low growl to the staggering patron, guiding him like a stubborn dog toward the door.

“See? Handled. No blood, no claws, no headlines tomorrow about the Rustwood Massacre,” Clint said.

“Funny, Barton. I’ll try to keep my name outta the papers tonight,” Logan said.

“We could all use a quiet night for once. Though I’ve stopped betting on those,” Jean said.

“Quiet's overrated anyway. Makes you start thinking too much,” Clint said.

A laugh drifts through the tavern, low and raw from a table across the room, punctuating Clint’s words like a dare. The jukebox stutters again, the song faltering as though it, too, is exhausted by the night’s uneven rhythm. Jean runs her finger along the rim of her glass, the delicate circle drawing faint whispers of sound, the kind that only people already deep in their own heads bother to notice.

“Thinking too much’s never done me any favors. Noise ain’t much better, though,” Logan said.

“Depends on the kind of noise. Some of it’s just distraction. Some of it’s what keeps you from breaking apart,” Jean said.

“Yeah, but if the noise is coming from inside your own head, what’s the difference?” Clint asked.

“Difference is, the inside noise don’t give you a break. Just keeps thrummin’, no matter what you do,” Logan said.

The whiskey bottle between them glints under the dim light, smudged fingerprints refracting the glow in uneven patterns. Clint rolls his empty beer cap across the table, the soft scrape of metal over wood nearly lost under Logan’s words. Jean’s gaze slips toward the streaked window beside them, where the outside dark presses heavy and solid, offering no reflection, only the sense of something looming.

“The inside noise... it shifts. Sometimes it’s a roar, sometimes it whispers, but it’s always there, isn’t it? It's learning to live with it that cuts the deepest,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, living with it and liking it ain't the same thing, is it?” Clint asked.

“Never is. You just keep movin’, same as always,” Logan said.

“Moving doesn't mean you're not dragging it with you. Sometimes it gets heavier the further you go,” Jean said.

The booth holds them in its narrowing gravity, the kind of stillness that draws deeper breath and heavier silence. Clint’s thumb works against the rim of his glass, the faint rhythm a counterpoint to everything unsaid. Logan’s shoulders ease, just barely, but the line of his jaw remains set, as though his body can’t decide whether to listen or to push the weight of the moment away.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the bartender wipes the last of the bar dry, when the ambient hum of the room shifts—chairs scraping faintly as bodies adjust, glasses nudged as though delaying their inevitable emptiness. Logan’s hand tightens briefly around the neck of his bottle, a grip heavy without purpose, before he releases it with the slow grace of someone who’s done this a thousand times. Jean’s eyes follow the motion, not unkind but unrelenting, her fingertips still resting near the edge of her untouched drink.

“Guess we all waitin' for somethin', huh? Even the damn glasses,” Logan said.

“Or maybe just letting go is harder than it looks,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, at least the glasses don’t have to wake up and regret it,” Clint said.

“Speak for yourself, Barton. My glasses always regret it,” Logan said.

The bartender tosses the rag over his shoulder with the resigned air of someone who’s marked the ritual a thousand nights over. Logan shifts in his seat, the leather of the booth creaking faintly under his weight, his knuckles ghosting over the rim of his bottle like he’s weighing whether to finish the last swallow. Clint leans back, spine loose in the way only exhaustion and bourbon can pull off, his fingers tapping out an absent rhythm on the table’s worn surface.

“It’s not the glasses, it’s the cracks. They just hold until they don’t,” Jean said.

“Well damn, Jean, way to make me rethink every cup of coffee I've ever had,” Clint said.

“Guess that’s why I stick to bottles. Harder to see the cracks til they bite ya,” Logan said.

“Or until they leak enough to drown you first,” Jean said.

The overhead light flickers, its dying glow casting shadows that stretch and distort across the pitted wood of the table. Jean’s hand shifts, her nails tracing a light, aimless path around the rim of her glass, her expression anchored somewhere between challenge and reflection. Logan exhales through his nose, low and uneven, as though weighing the sharpness of her last words, his gaze fixed on a point just shy of meeting hers.

“Well, that’s cheerful. Anybody want me to call a cab before we all swim home in existential dread?” Clint asked.

“I’ll walk. Dread keeps better on foot,” Logan said.

“Sometimes it follows you either way,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, at least it’s got shitty shoes. Mine’s always tripping over itself,” Clint said.

Logan’s lips press into the faintest edge of a smirk, more a concession to Barton’s deflection than true amusement, as his fingers tighten briefly around the neck of the bottle. Jean tilts her head, her gaze dropping for a moment to the condensation pooling near Clint’s untouched glass, her silence carrying the weight of whatever answer she’s holding back. Clint shifts forward, his elbows settling onto the table, the sharp angles of his posture softened by the steady blur of the night’s drinks.

“Shoes or not, it catches up. Always,” Logan said.

“Only if you stop long enough to let it,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, stopping’s never really been my strong suit,” Clint said.

“Running ain’t much better. You just get tired and it’s still there,” Logan said.

The room seems to shrink around them, the thinning crowd and hollow quiet leaving only the scrape of glass against wood and the muted hum of distant traffic. Jean sits back, spine straight but shoulders loose, her untouched drink now just a forgotten prop between her and Logan’s words. Clint’s fingers still, resting flat on the table now, his gaze flicking between them like he’s deciding whether to push or let the moment fall into its own gravity.

“Maybe it’s not about running or stopping. Maybe it’s just about facing it before it decides to face you,” Jean said.

“Yeah, but what if it’s got a punch like Logan? That’s a fight I’m not signing up for sober,” Clint said.

“You couldn’t take me drunk either, Barton. Don’t kid yourself,” Logan said.

“We all have our fights, Logan, and not all of them need fists,” Jean said.

Logan’s jaw tightens, the muscle flickering beneath his skin as he lowers his gaze to the last inch of whiskey in his bottle. Clint leans back again, a sharp exhale breaking the stillness, his expression caught somewhere between a grin and a grimace that doesn’t quite settle. The light catches on Jean’s glass as she finally reaches for it, her fingers wrapping around the base with deliberate care, though she doesn’t bring it closer.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the door creaks shut behind them, when the warmth from the bar’s heavy air tries to cling. It doesn’t last. The night greets them with the kind of cold that bites first and lingers later, cutting through the haze of whiskey and smoke.

“Colder than a Yukon morgue out here,” Logan said.

“Could be worse. At least the whiskey's still warming on the way down,” Jean said.

“Could be way worse. Could be sober,” Clint said.

“Ain't that the damn truth,” Logan said.

Logan takes the cigarette from Clint with a grunt that could pass for thanks, the glow from the lighter briefly illuminating the roughened lines of his knuckles. Jean tugs her coat tighter, her movements measured and deliberate, as if bracing against more than just the cold. Clint exhales his first drag into the dark, the smoke unraveling upwards in lazy, curling tendrils, only to vanish into the night.

“Speak for yourself. Some of us still remember what sober feels like,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, some of us are trying real hard not to,” Clint said.

“Sober ain’t all it’s cracked up to be, Red,” Logan said.

“Depends on what you're trying to outrun, I guess,” Jean said.

Clint shifts his weight, his boot scuffing against the gritty pavement, the sound sharp in the stillness. Jean’s glance lingers on him, not sharp but not soft either, her hands sliding into her coat pockets, hiding the tension in her fingers. Logan exhales smoke through his nostrils like a stove piping out steam, his gaze fixed somewhere distant, somewhere none of them would name.

“Hell, if we're keeping score, I think I'm on lap thirty-seven of that race. Trying not to win,” Clint said.

“No medals for this race, anyhow,” Logan said.

“Unless you count scars as trophies,” Jean said.

“Guess that makes us all decorated, then,” Clint said.

The faint scrape of Logan’s thumbnail against the cigarette filters the silence, an unconscious rhythm ticking down the moments between truths they won’t speak. Jean tilts her face up toward the dark sky, her breath fogging in the chill, dissolving just as quickly as it forms. Clint flicks ash from the end of his smoke with a flick of his wrist, the ember flaring briefly before dimming to a dull crimson glow.

“Some trophies ain't worth keepin',” Logan said.

“But we carry them anyway,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, some of us are just bad at letting go. Call it a hobby,” Clint said.

“Hobbies're supposed to make life easier, not heavier,” Logan said.

Clint smirks faintly, the kind that doesn’t reach his eyes, as he takes another pull from the cigarette, the glow briefly cutting through the shadow on his face. Jean watches him from the corner of her eye, her posture too still, her hands unmoving in her pockets. Logan shifts his stance, the gravel crunching beneath his boots, heavy as the words between them.

“Maybe we just pick the weight we know how to carry,” Jean said.

“Or the weight that knows how to stick,” Clint said.

“Heavy’s heavy, no matter what you call it,” Logan said.

“Some weights remind you you’re still standing. Even if it’s just out of spite,” Jean said.

The wind cuts through the lot, sharp and sudden, dragging leaves across the asphalt in a restless scrape that matches none of their movements. Clint lifts his cigarette to his lips again, his hand steady, but his shoulders betray the slight sag of familiar exhaustion. Jean’s coat shifts as she leans back against the nearest car, the cold metal grounding her as much as the silence between them.

* * *

The orange-red tips of their cigarettes flare in the darkness, brief and vivid against the black sky. Smoke curls upward, dissipating before it meets the stars Jean is still fixed on—sharp and unblinking as they hang in the cold void above. The truck’s rusting frame groans faintly under the weight of Logan’s shoulder, an indifferent protest unnoticed by the man himself.

“Could’ve picked a warmer spot to brood, Red,” Logan said.

“Could’ve picked a warmer decade to smoke, Logan,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, could’ve picked a better habit, but here we are,” Clint said.

“Ain’t the habit that’s killin’ us, Barton,” Logan said.

The smoke hangs between them for a moment longer, neither thick enough nor willing enough to obscure the space they share. Jean's eyes flick down from the heavens, the faintest trace of breath visible in the chill as she exhales. Clint rolls the cigarette between his fingers absently, ash crumbling to the asphalt below, unnoticed.

“No, it’s not,” Jean said.

“Yeah, funny how that works,” Clint said.

“Real laugh riot,” Logan said.

“Sometimes it is, just to keep from screaming,” Jean said.

Logan shifts his weight against the truck, the metallic groan sharper now, cutting through the low hum of the night. Clint’s jaw tightens as he stubs his cigarette out on the truck’s dented fender, the charred end smearing faintly against the grime. Jean’s arms cross over her chest, her gaze now fixed somewhere between them and the asphalt, her stance steady but no less sharp-edged in the quiet.

“Yeah, screaming’s overrated anyway,” Clint said.

“Depends who’s listenin’,” Logan said.

“Depends what you're trying not to say,” Jean said.

“Or who you’re trying not to say it to,” Clint said.

The wind cuts through again, sharper this time, tugging at Logan’s flannel and ruffling the hem of Jean’s coat. Clint folds his hands into his jacket pockets, his fingers curling inward like the thought he doesn’t finish. Above them, the stars remain indifferent, their cold light no warmer for the lives unraveling beneath their gaze.

“World ain’t waitin’ on our confessions,” Logan said.

“Doesn’t mean we’re not carrying them anyway,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, some of us travel light. Or try to, at least,” Clint said.

“Tryin' don’t mean much when the weight sticks to your bones,” Logan said.

Logan flicks the last embers of his cigarette to the ground, grinding them out beneath his boot with deliberate, weary force. Jean tilts her head just slightly, an almost imperceptible motion, her hair catching the wind as though it might carry her somewhere else. Clint leans back against the truck now, his weight pressing into it like he’s testing whether the steel will hold something he knows he can’t.

“Some weights you don’t get to set down,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, doesn’t mean you stop moving,” Clint said.

“Even when draggin' half your past behind ya,” Logan said.

“Or all of it, when it refuses to stay where you left it,” Jean said.

The headlights from a distant car sweep briefly across the lot, carving long, shifting shadows out of their bodies before disappearing into the dark. Logan's gaze follows it for half a second before falling back to the cracked asphalt underfoot. Clint exhales, not smoke this time, just air heavy enough to feel like it’s sagging in the cold.

* * *

There is a moment, just before words settle like ash on the edges of silence, where the air hangs uneven between them. Jean shifts her weight, boots scuffing against gravel, the sound faint but too sharp in the stillness. Logan rubs his thumb along the scarred leather grip of his flask, as if searching for an answer it has never given him.

“Some memories don’t fade. They just... hang on ya, like a shadow you keep steppin’ in,” Logan said.

“Yeah... and sometimes it feels like they’re not yours anymore, just... playing on a loop for someone else to watch,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well... sometimes I wonder if I’m the shadow,” Clint said.

“Ain't you, Barton. Shadows don't get up and try... even when it hurts like hell,” Logan said.

The headlights return, fleeting and oblivious, casting limbs and ghosts into sharp relief before sweeping past. Gravel grits beneath Clint’s heel as he shifts closer, arms slack at his sides, fingers itching for something they won’t grab. Jean’s breath clouds the air between them, brief, shapeless, deflating faster than anyone moves to speak.

“Or maybe we’re all just stuck under the same damn shadow, pretending it’s not ours,” Jean said.

“Sure feels like that sometimes. Problem is, even when you know it’s there, it still pulls you under,” Clint said.

“Yeah... but you keep fightin’, even when it don’t make sense. Shadows don’t win, unless you let ‘em,” Logan said.

“Except sometimes fighting feels like feeding it. Like every swing just makes it bigger, heavier,” Jean said.

A wind cuts through the lot, low and indifferent, tumbling a crumpled beer can until it stops against the back tire of a truck. Logan looks up briefly at the sound, the edge on his face softening, but only just. Jean wraps her arms across her chest, her gaze downward, her hair catching faintly in the breeze before settling back into place, untouched.

“Maybe. But not swinging? That’s how it takes everything,” Clint said.

“Swings hurt less than sittin’ still, bub. Least you feel somethin',” Logan said.

“Yeah, sometimes it’s the only way to remind yourself you’re still there... still here,” Jean said.

“Being here’s the hardest part, though, isn’t it? Plenty of days, I’d rather not be,” Clint said.

A passing jet growls faintly overhead, the sound distant but persistent, dissolving slowly into the quiet like a held breath finally let go. Clint’s eyes track the empty dark where it vanished, his shoulders tightening under the weight of unsaid things. Logan shifts his stance, one boot grinding deeper into the gravel, the metallic tang of ever-present smoke still clinging to him.

“Yeah, well... bein’ here’s all we got. Rest’s just ghosts,” Logan said.

“Sometimes ghosts don’t just haunt—they weigh you down, wrap around you like chains,” Jean said.

“Yeah, and you’re the idiot dragging them along, thinking maybe this time they’ll let go first,” Clint said.

“They don’t let go. You gotta cut ‘em loose yourself... or drown with 'em,” Logan said.

The buzzing neon sign above the tavern flickers unevenly, casting faint, jagged light across the cracked asphalt. Jean glances toward it, her fingers tightening briefly around the sleeve of her jacket before falling slack again. Clint tips his head back, eyes tracing the stars invisible beyond the haze, the quiet stretching thinner with every second.

“Cutting them loose is easy to say... harder when they’re the only things that still feel real,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, real doesn't always mean worth keeping, does it?” Clint asked.

“No... but holdin’ on’s easier than lettin’ go. Even when it cuts,” Logan said.

“Sometimes the cut feels like proof you’re still alive... even if it’s bleeding you out,” Jean said.

A car pulls out from the tavern's side lot, tires crunching over loose gravel before rolling onto the main road, its taillights fading into the distance. The absence it leaves is heavier than its presence, the stillness returning as though it had never been disturbed. Logan’s fingers tap once on the flask at his hip, the motion sharp and rhythmic, breaking the fragile quiet without shattering it.

* * *

The gravel lay undisturbed, save for the scuff of boots shifting weight between exhaustion and unspoken truce. Smoke coiled thin and slow in the cold air, a faint burn biting the morning still. Logan’s hand lingered at his flask as Clint balanced his cigarette between two fingers, the ember pulsing dim against the dark.

“Therapy, huh. You try that, Barton?” Logan asked.

“Not everyone’s built to sit still long enough for it to take,” Jean said.

“Yeah, well, sitting still’s never been my strong suit,” Clint said.

“Ain’t mine either. Flask’s faster,” Logan said.

The corner of Logan’s mouth twitched upward, a smirk ghosting across his face before vanishing into the lines carved deep by time and wear. Clint dropped his cigarette, grinding it into the dirt under the heel of his boot, the motion deliberate but unhurried. Jean exhaled, a sharp breath visible in the cold, though her arms stayed crossed tight, her weight leaning ever so slightly away from the two men.

“Quicker doesn’t mean cleaner,” Jean said.

“Yeah, but it gets the job done, doesn’t it?” Clint asked.

“Gets it done 'til it don’t. Then you’re pickin’ up pieces you didn’t know you broke,” Logan said.

“And hoping the sharp edges don’t cut too deep,” Jean said.

The silence held, heavy and raw, as the distant hum of tires on asphalt carried faintly through the air, an ungraspable sound fading before it fully arrived. A breeze stirred loose gravel around their boots, scattering it in half-circles, little movements that went unnoticed. The glow of the tavern's sign flickered unevenly, casting their shadows long and jagged against the lot's cracked surface.

“Yeah, well, I’ve never been great at spotting the edges 'til I’m already bleeding,” Clint said.

“Guess that’s why we keep walkin’, huh? Better the scars than standin’ still,” Logan said.

“If you keep moving long enough, you start confusing survival with living,” Jean said.

“Survival’s easier to measure. Living’s just hoping you don’t screw it up too bad,” Clint said.

Jean turned her head slightly, her gaze fixed on some distant, invisible point beyond the parking lot, the faintest tension sharpening her profile against the dim glow. Logan shifted his weight, the faint crunch beneath his boots breaking the stillness as his hand flexed once against the flask, then stilled. Clint rubbed the back of his neck, the motion abrupt but somehow weary, as if trying to dislodge something that lingered heavier than the cold.

“Survival don’t leave much room for hopin’. It’s just gettin’ to the next damn day,” Logan said.

“But what’s the point of the next day if it’s just another loop around the same pain?” Jean asked.

“Guess that’s the trick, huh? Figuring out if the next day’s worth it before you even get there,” Clint said.

“Ain’t about figurin’ it out. You just get there, see what sticks,” Logan said.

The last wisps of smoke from Logan’s forgotten cigarillo curled near his feet, twisting briefly before vanishing into the damp chill. Jean uncrossed her arms, brushing a thumb against her palm as though grounding herself to the reality of the sharp air and crumbled asphalt. Clint’s eyes dropped to the ground, tracing the faint scuff marks in the dirt like he could map some pattern in the wear, even if he knew better.

“Sometimes just getting there is the hardest part,” Jean said.

“Yeah, but somehow we keep showing up, don’t we? Stupid, stubborn, or just don’t know how to quit,” Clint said.

“Maybe all three. Sure as hell ain’t wisdom,” Logan said.

“Wisdom’s overrated. It doesn’t make the load any lighter,” Jean said.

The glow from the tavern’s sign guttered again, a faint buzz accompanying each flicker as it momentarily stabbed brighter into the darkness. Logan adjusted his stance, the habitual motion tight and restrained, his boots grinding down fresh lines in the gravel with a rhythmic inevitability. Jean tilted her head slightly, her breath clouding the air in soft bursts that dissipated just as they began to take shape.
