Grizzly Knights

Episode 5: The Argument That Doesn't End

Frank and Matt in the back booth of a Hell's Kitchen dive neither of them should be at. The argument they have been having for years is the only conversation either of them knows how to have with the other one. Neither of them is trying to win tonight. They just keep showing up for it.

* * *

The jukebox flickers faint neon on its busted face, casting fractured light against the torn leather of the booth. Frank leans back, the collar of his jacket gone soft at the edges, while his hand stays loose around the mouth of his bottle. Matt’s fingers rest on the rim of the unopened Schlitz, tension running quiet down his arm.

“You should’ve finished it, Red,” Frank said.

“And let the devil in? That's your answer to everything, Frank,” Matt said.

“If it gets the job done, yeah,” Frank said.

“The job? You think this is just a job? It's a life, Frank. One I don’t get to take without reason,” Matt said.

Frank tilts the bottle to his lips, the swallow deliberate, his eyes steady on Matt over the glass. Matt shifts, the wooden bench creaking under his weight as his jaw tightens, head tilting fractionally toward the low hum of the bar. The unopened Schlitz stays untouched.

“Your "reason" left him breathing. Now he's out there, Red—waiting for someone else to pay the price,” Frank said.

“And you think killing him settles the debt? It doesn’t. It just makes you the next one to owe,” Matt said.

“I don’t owe shit, Red. I clear the ledger,” Frank said.

“You don’t clear it, Frank. You just write it in blood instead of ink,” Matt said.

Frank sets the empty bottle down with a dull thud, his hand staying on it like it anchors something. Matt exhales sharply through his nose, his fingers curling and uncurling just once against the tabletop. The low buzz of conversation from the bar drifts over, muted but constant, filling the space between their words.

“Blood gets results. Ink just leaves loose ends,” Frank said.

“Results without restraint? That’s not justice, Frank. That’s just more chaos,” Matt said.

“Justice doesn’t bring 'em back, Red. Results at least make sure it doesn’t happen again,” Frank said.

“You don’t get to play God, Frank. Making sure it doesn’t happen again doesn’t give you license to decide who lives or dies,” Matt said.

Frank's jaw flexes, a brief grind of teeth, his grip tightening on the neck of the empty bottle. Matt stays still, the weight in his posture deliberate, but his head turns slightly toward the indistinct sound of glass clinking at the bar. The Schlitz in front of him remains unopened, its cold sweat pooling a faint ring on the table.

“I don’t play God, Red. I’m just the one who makes sure the devil pays his due,” Frank said.

“And you don't stop to ask what that makes you, do you, Frank?” Matt asked.

Frank lets go of the bottle, the glass rattling faintly against the sticky wood of the table. His stare doesn’t shift, heavy and unflinching, while Matt presses his fingertips into the edge of the Schlitz. The jukebox sputters in its corner, the neon light stuttering once before dimming further.

* * *

Frank tips the bottle back into his glass, the whiskey moving slow, deliberate. There's grit under his nails, dark crescents that don’t suit the clean lines of the pour. Matt's fingers flex once against the edge of the table, knuckles whitening as the booth traps the creak of old wood.

“You gonna get to the point, or we sittin’ here all night?” Frank asked.

“The point is a kid gets to make it out of this with a shot at a decent life—but only if we don't bury the guy who can still fix it,” Matt said.

“You sure about that? 'Cause I’ve seen too many of these guys, Matt. They don’t fix nothin’,” Frank said.

“I'm sure about the kid, Frank. And that's enough for me to try,” Matt said.

Frank scrapes his thumb across the rim of his glass, smudging a streak through the condensation. Matt's jaw shifts like he's chewing on the silence, his sunglasses catching the low flicker of the dying neon. Somewhere behind the bar, Josie lights a cigarette, the faint crackle almost lost under the stumble of someone missing their stool.

“Try all you want, but when he burns that chance, don’t come lookin’ for me to clean it up,” Frank said.

“I’m not asking you to clean it up. I’m asking you to give him the chance not to burn it,” Matt said.

“And when he does? ‘Cause you know what happens then, Matt,” Frank said.

“Then I deal with it. My way. Not yours,” Matt said.

Frank leans back, the booth creaking like it might give under his weight, his eyes fixed steady on Matt. The whiskey rolls slow in his glass as he tilts it, untouched. Matt’s head dips slightly, the corners of his mouth tight, and his fingers finally lift from the table, only to hover like they don’t know where to settle.

“Your way leaves bodies behind too. You just don’t like to admit it,” Frank said.

“I admit it, Frank. Every day. But it’s still not the same as yours,” Matt said.

“Doesn’t matter if it’s the same. Dead is dead. Lines you draw don’t change that,” Frank said.

“The lines are the only thing that stop us from becoming them. You know that, even if you won’t say it,” Matt said.

Frank turns the glass in his hand, the bottom grinding faintly against the table’s sticky surface. Matt’s head tilts just enough to suggest he’s listening harder, the lenses of his sunglasses obscuring whatever’s pulled tight in his eyes. Over by the busted jukebox, the hum cuts out, leaving only the low buzz of Josie’s TV above the bar.

“The lines are for you, not them. Don’t mean a goddamn thing to the dead,” Frank said.

“Maybe not. But they mean something to the living,” Matt said.

Frank sets the glass down, the thunk dull against the wood, a faint ripple breaking the surface of the whiskey inside. Matt shifts his weight, his knee brushing the edge of the booth, tension coiling in the slight movement. The neon from the sign near the window catches in the sweat on Frank’s forearm, casting faint, wavering streaks of red.

* * *

Frank rolls the glass between his fingers, the whiskey inside sloshing, catching the dim amber refracted from the bar lights. Matt taps the booth’s edge with two fingers, deliberate, his head tilted slightly toward the sound of the glass. A faint hum of poorly tuned jukebox leaks through the room, worn, unsteady.

“You got something to say, Murdock, or you just gonna keep pretending you can’t smell the shit on my hands?” Frank asked.

“If I smelled everything on your hands, Frank, we'd be here all night,” Matt said.

“Wouldn't be the first long night I’ve had because of 'em,” Frank said.

“And it won't be the last, will it?” Matt asked.

Frank sets the glass down hard, the whiskey lurching up the sides but not spilling. Matt doesn’t flinch, just shifts his weight again, the leather of the booth creaking under the movement. The jukebox skips, a brief stutter, then trudges on.

“Not unless someone stops me, and we both know that ain't gonna be you,” Frank said.

“I’m not here to stop you, Frank. I’m here to stop you from drowning in it,” Matt said.

“I don't drown, Murdock. I sink, I walk, I keep going,” Frank said.

“Yeah, and you drag the weight with you every step,” Matt said.

Frank leans back, the booth groaning under his weight, his arms stretching across the top edge like he’s staking a claim. The whiskey glass sits untouched in front of him now, the faint smudge of his thumbprint catching the light. Matt tilts his head downward, his fingers tracing the scarred wood of the table, steady and quiet.

“Better to carry it than let it bury me,” Frank said.

“Until it gets too heavy to move, and then what?” Matt asked.

“Then I stop moving. Simple as that,” Frank said.

“It's never that simple, Frank. You know that,” Matt said.

Frank’s jaw tightens, a muscle jumping beneath the scruff on his face, but he doesn’t look at Matt. The hum of the neon sign flickers, buzzing faintly louder for a beat before settling again. The whiskey stays untouched, a ring of condensation slowly bleeding into the table around it.

“Doesn’t have to be complicated either,” Frank said.

“Life's complicated, Frank. Whether you want it to be or not,” Matt said.

Frank exhales through his nose, sharp, almost a laugh but carrying none of the weight. He drums his fingers once on the back of the booth, the dull thuds absorbed by the worn vinyl. Matt shifts his hand slightly, resting his palm flat on the table now, grounding himself.

* * *

The alley is slick with rain that’s dried unevenly, leaving dark patches on the cracked asphalt. Frank tilts his head just enough to blow smoke away from Matt, but not enough to stop daring him into the silence. The cherry of the Marlboro flares, biting red in the dim floodlight spilling out from the kitchen door.

“You wanna keep breathing down my neck or you got something to say, Red?” Frank asked.

“You ever think about the difference, Frank? Between punishment and justice?” Matt asked.

“Every goddamn day. Difference is, I don’t just think about it—I do something,” Frank said.

“And you think what you do fixes anything? Really fixes it? Or just leaves more blood in the street?” Matt asked.

Frank flicks ash to the ground, the grey scatter mixing into the damp grime like it belongs there. Matt’s jaw tightens, a faint twitch running along the edge of his temple, but his hands stay loose at his sides. The distance between them feels narrower than it is.

“It stops 'em, Red. Dead men don’t hurt anyone else,” Frank said.

“And who decides which men die, Frank? You? You think that makes you any better than them?” Matt asked.

“I ain’t better. Never said I was. But I’ll damn well make sure I’m worse for them,” Frank said.

“That’s the part you don’t see, Frank. You don’t stop being worse. You carry it, and it carries you. Forever,” Matt said.

Frank drags hard on the cigarette, the paper crackling faintly as his breath pulls it to the filter. The sharp exhale comes with the faintest tremor, smoke curling quick and uneven before dissolving into the cold night air. Matt's head tilts slightly, just enough to catch the edge of Frank’s shadow shrinking under the floodlight.

“You think I don’t know that, Red? I carry every goddamn pound of it, and I’ll keep carrying it ‘til it buries me,” Frank said.

“And what then, Frank? You think that weight’s gonna balance the scales, or just drag you under?” Matt asked.

“Doesn’t matter. As long as it drags me and them down together, I’ll call it even,” Frank said.

“That’s not even, Frank. That’s just more bodies in the ground,” Matt said.

The cigarette burns low, a thin line of ash threatening to collapse onto his knuckles. Frank lets it hang there, ignoring it, the edge of his lip curling just slightly in something close to a sneer. Matt straightens his shoulders, turning his face fully into the dim light, sharp lines carved deeper by the shadow of the doorway behind him.

“Yeah, well, sometimes the ground’s the only place they belong,” Frank said.

“And who made you the one to decide that?” Matt asked.

Frank drops the cigarette, the faint sizzle of ember snuffed out by the wet asphalt. He grinds it under his boot, the motion slow and deliberate, the sound gritty, like teeth on stone. Matt doesn’t flinch, but his fists clench now, bloodless knuckles brushing the fabric at his sides.

* * *

The city hums low around them, a distant churn of garbage trucks and splashing tires. Matt’s cane taps the sidewalk in steady rhythm, sharp against the wet concrete, while Frank’s boots crunch on scattered grit. The smell of hot tar and stale piss rises from a subway grate as their strides fall into an uneasy parallel.

“You got a reason to keep following me, Red, or you just like the sound of your own shoes?” Frank asked.

“You know damn well why I’m here, Frank,” Matt said.

“Then stop dancing around it and say it,” Frank said.

“You're gonna kill someone tonight, and I’m not letting that happen,” Matt said.

Frank blows out a sharp breath, more laugh than exhale, the kind that cuts. He adjusts the collar of his jacket, shoulders rolling against the tight leather, his pace unbroken. Matt’s head tilts slightly, as if listening closer to something Frank hasn’t said yet, the cane’s rhythm skipping once before falling steady again.

“You ain’t lettin’ anything, Red. Stay outta my way,” Frank said.

“Not how this works, Frank. You know I won’t,” Matt said.

“Guess we’re gonna have a problem then,” Frank said.

“Seems like we always do,” Matt said.

Frank’s jaw tightens, a muscle flickering sharp under two-day stubble. His hands curl into fists, not raised, just there, heavy and ready at his sides as they keep moving. Ahead, the halo of a busted streetlight gutters weakly, stretching their shadows long and uneven across the slick pavement.

“Yeah, well, maybe this time you should make your peace with it,” Frank said.

“I’ve got plenty to make peace with, Frank, but not this. Not you,” Matt said.

“I ain’t lookin’ for your forgiveness, and I sure as hell don’t need it,” Frank said.

“Doesn't mean I’m not going to fight to stop you,” Matt said.

Frank stops abruptly, boots skidding on damp concrete, and turns just enough to square his body toward Matt without closing the gap. The air between them feels dense, cut through only by the faint metal-on-stone sound of Matt’s cane as it comes to a halt. A siren wails faintly in the distance, too far off to matter.

“Fight all you want, Red, but you know how this ends,” Frank said.

“Not if I can change it, Frank,” Matt said.

Frank’s lip curls, just barely, the flicker of something between amusement and disgust. His shoulders drop, loose now, but the tension crackles in the set of his stance. Matt shifts his weight forward, not aggressive, but deliberate, his head angled toward the source of Frank’s breath.

* * *

The streetlights cut long shadows across the cracked sidewalk. Frank shifts his weight, gravel crunching under his boot. Matt’s fingers twitch once at his side, a quick adjustment in the way his cane angles toward the curb.

“You keep walking your way, Murdock. I'll keep fixing what you can't,” Frank said.

“And how many ghosts do you think it takes to fix anything, Frank?” Matt asked.

“As many as it fucking takes,” Frank said.

“That's what scares me,” Matt said.

Frank exhales sharply through his nose, a sound more like a snarl than a breath, and his jaw hardens under the streetlight’s glare. Matt tilts his head slightly, listening, his grip tightening just enough on the cane’s handle to discolor his knuckles. A car passes behind them, tires hissing against wet asphalt, but neither man moves.

“You should be scared, Murdock. 'Cause you're the one who leaves 'em alive,” Frank said.

“And you're the one who makes sure they never get the chance to change,” Matt said.

“Change doesn’t bring back the dead,” Frank said.

“Neither does killing, Frank. But it sure as hell makes more of them,” Matt said.

The wind picks up, swirling grit from the gutter around their boots. Frank wipes at his mouth with the back of his hand, slow and deliberate, before letting it drop to his side. Matt stands rooted, head slightly angled toward Frank, his chest rising and falling in tight, measured breaths.

“Then you keep counting on hope, and I’ll keep counting bodies,” Frank said.

“Hope's the only thing that keeps us from becoming the bodies, Frank,” Matt said.

“Hope didn't save 'em. I did,” Frank said.

“By deciding who gets to live and who dies? That’s not saving anyone, Frank—it’s playing God,” Matt said.

Frank steps in closer, the brimstone heat of him radiating out into the winter air, his fists flexing tight at his sides. Matt doesn’t flinch, his stance solid, but the tension ripples across his jaw like a wire pulled too taut. The distant hum of a siren carries low through the city, fading before it ever gets close.

“God’s the one who let it happen, Murdock. I’m just cleaning up the mess,” Frank said.

“You think this mess ends because you clean up a piece of it? It just bleeds till there’s nothing left,” Matt said.

Frank’s mouth pulls into a hard, thin line, teeth pressed tight enough to creak. The wind shifts again, sharp and cold, ruffling the edges of Matt’s collar. For a long second, neither of them moves, locked in the weight of the other’s shadow.
