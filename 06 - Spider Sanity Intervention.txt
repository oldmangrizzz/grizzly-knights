Grizzly Knights

Episode 6: Spider Sanity Intervention

Felicia and Wade hatch a chaotic plan to help Peter Parker relax, roping in Mary Jane and Johnny along the way.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the cheesecake-laden chaos overwhelms their small corner of an already gaudily lit dining hall, where Wade slouches so deeply into the booth it’s a wonder he hasn’t merged with the vinyl. Felicia's laughter cuts through the clatter of silverware and the syrupy top-40 soundtrack, her fingertips dusted with breadcrumbs as she gestures toward the crude ransom note that Wade scribbles with the concentration of a madman at a child’s placemat. The table blooms with the debris of indulgence—martini stems frosted with the residue of melted ice, greasy fingerprints smudging the edges of untouched sliders, a warzone of appetizers that speaks volumes louder than words ever could.

“I’m just saying, if we convince MJ to text him something cryptic, like "Your stress owes me a favor," we could watch his head implode in real-time,” Felicia said.

“Oh, that’s evil. Deliciously evil. Like deep-fried cheesecake wrapped in bacon - not on the menu, but by god, should be,” Wade said.

“You two really think dragging me into this is going to help Peter stress less? Adorable. Truly,” Mary said.

“Oh, come on, MJ, it’s not like he doesn’t already stress about you 24/7. We’re just... refocusing it. Call it creative stress management,” Johnny said.

MJ leans back, arms folded, her half-empty mimosa precariously close to the edge of the table, vibrating slightly each time Wade gesticulates too wildly. Felicia is already reaching for the untouched stack of napkins, using one to dab at a smear of spilled sauce that has reached the corner of Wade’s swirling ransom-doodle chaos. Johnny, ever the effortless counterbalance to the room's absurdity, absently spins a salt shaker like he’s testing the laws of physics—while purposefully ignoring the judgmental glance from a middle-aged server passing by.

“Oh, MJ, darling, "helping Peter stress less" isn't the goal. Watching him squirm just a little? That's the true artistry here,” Felicia said.

“Artistry? Felicia, this is practically performance art. Call MoMA—Spider-Man stress relief as an exhibit! Or at least a TikTok series,” Wade said.

“If you think I’m stepping into your little performance piece, think again. Peter doesn’t squirm gracefully—he implodes, and guess who ends up with the rubble?” Mary asked.

“Yeah, but isn’t cleaning up Peter’s rubble basically a shared hobby at this point? You’re like... the team MVP for that,” Johnny said.

The corner booth tilts precariously on the line between playful antagonism and genuine exasperation, with Mary Jane’s nails now drumming a deliberate, steady rhythm on the table’s lacquered edge. The air carries the mingling notes of overripe mimosas and fried appetizers, punctuated by the sharp scratch of Wade’s pen as it tears through yet another napkin beneath the weight of his enthusiasm. Felicia smirks behind the rim of her martini glass, the olive skewer lazily rolling between her fingers, while Johnny leans into his careless charm, the salt shaker abandoned and spinning slowly to a halt.

“MJ, let’s not pretend the rubble isn’t half the fun. Besides, he’s basically a walking insurance claim; we’re just the damage assessors,” Felicia said.

“And I’m the quirky contractor who shows up, shirtless, late, and with a flask, to rebuild the place with questionable methods and absolutely no permits,” Wade said.

“Right, and I’m the one standing in the driveway, wondering why I put up with any of you,” Mary said.

“Because deep down, MJ, you know we’re the chaos you secretly enjoy. Admit it, we make your life... less boring,” Johnny said.

MJ exhales sharply, her gaze flicking toward the rim of her mimosa glass as though weighing its proximity to a full-scale tip over as part of her response. Wade leans forward, scribbling a new flourish on the disintegrating napkin, his intensity bordering on absurdity as flecks of ink fly loose. Felicia leans closer to MJ, the amusement always just beneath her eyelids now fully shimmering, while Johnny reclines smugly, his foot tapping out a syncopated beat beneath the table.

“Face it, MJ, you stick around because you know nothing’s more entertaining than watching a train wreck when you’re not the one driving,” Felicia said.

“Oh, she’s not just watching the train wreck, Felicia—she’s the sophisticated commuter on the platform with a latte, filming it for Instagram with a perfectly hashtagged “#WTFWednesday,” Wade said.

The table erupts—not with the chaos of true conflict, but the sharp, biting laughter that leaves no one untouched. MJ shakes her head, the upturned corner of her mouth betraying amusement she pretends not to feel, as her mimosa finally trembles to stillness. Wade’s napkin art grows denser, ink spreading like spider veins, while Felicia settles against the booth with the satisfaction of someone who knows she’s made her point. Johnny, ever the instigator, casts a sidelong glance at the server hovering nearby, raising a hand to signal for a fresh round—because the chaos is far from over.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the next drink lands, when the air at the booth grows heavier—not with malice, not yet, but with the simmering tension of plans waiting to take shape. Felicia's fingers dance idly across her phone screen, her smirk sharp enough to cut the sunlight filtering through the window. Wade, leaning too far forward, abandons his penwork and starts shredding a sugar packet, the granules spilling unnoticed onto the sticky, laminated menu.

“So, Wade, when's the last time you meddled in Peter's life and it didn't blow up spectacularly?” Felicia asked.

“Define "blow up spectacularly." Are we talking like Michael Bay explosions or more like that time I accidentally turned his life into a rom-com with me as both the best friend and the romantic lead?” Wade asked.

“Sounds like you're aiming for a combo platter of disaster. Classic Wade—why pick one genre when you can ruin all of them?” Mary asked.

“Peter’s life is already like ten genres fighting for screen time. Adding Wade’s chaos is just bonus popcorn. Keep going, this is the best brunch I’ve had in weeks,” Johnny said.

The server arrives, precariously balancing a tray of frosted glasses and a cheesecake slice someone didn’t order but will inevitably eat. Ice clinks faintly, a counterpoint to the quiet hum of the restaurant around them, the dull roar of other lives momentarily forgotten. Felicia slides her phone across the table toward Wade, a fresh glint in her eye daring anyone to back out now.

“Alright, boys, MJ’s on her way, so let’s make this count—three minds, no morals, infinite possibilities. Who’s in?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, I'm in so deep I might as well start digging tunnels. Let’s ruin his day in the most loving way possible,” Wade said.

“And here I thought this was brunch, not a villain convention. You really can’t resist, can you?” Mary asked.

“Hey, it’s not villainy—it’s proactive emotional support. With flair. Add me to the roster,” Johnny said.

The cheesecake slice is claimed without ceremony, Wade spearing a fork into it as though it were owed to him by divine right. Felicia leans back, one arm draped lazily across the top of the booth, watching with amused detachment as Johnny drums his fingers against the tabletop in loose, irregular rhythms. The condensation from their untouched drinks starts pooling into small rivulets, ignored in favor of the conspiratorial energy thickening the space between them.

“All I’m saying is, if we time this right, we could scare him into fun. It’s practically charity work,” Felicia said.

“Charity work? Oh honey, I’m the Mother Teresa of well-intentioned chaos. Let’s baptize him in tequila and regret,” Wade said.

“Just promise me this intervention doesn’t end with me explaining to Peter why he woke up in someone else’s tights. Again,” Mary said.

“Hey, if he ends up in someone else’s tights, that’s just Wednesday for him. We’re just here to make it memorable,” Johnny said.

The table vibrates with the uneven thud of Wade’s fist against the wood, a punctuation mark to whatever unspoken agreement they’ve just forged. Felicia’s laughter cuts sharp and low, slipping under the ambient chatter of the restaurant, dangerous in its delight. Johnny flips his spoon into the air, catching it easily, his grin spreading wider with each second of brewing absurdity.

“Oh, it’s definitely going to be memorable. The only question is—do we aim for chaos or leave it to chance?” Felicia asked.

“Chaos is chance with better branding. Let’s roll the dice and tell fate to catch up,” Wade said.

The drinks sit untouched, their ice slowly surrendering into the amber and citrus depths as Felicia slides her phone back into her hand, her thumb hovering with deliberate precision. Wade leans in close enough to invade everyone’s personal space, the cheesecake forgotten as crumbs cling to his sleeve. Johnny steals a too-long sip from someone else's glass, his eyes tracing the momentary collision of tension and mischief held at the center of the table.

* * *

The booth is a study in imbalance. Mary Jane slides in, her bag thumping against the faux-leather as her fingers impatiently push strands of red hair from her face. Felicia leans back, all precise languor, while Wade’s hands weave through the air, miming implausible scenarios with a chaotic energy that makes Johnny laugh mid-sip, nearly spilling whatever he had commandeered from someone else’s glass.

“Look who finally decided to slum it with us. MJ, you’re just in time—Wade’s got a plan so absurd even Johnny’s choking on his stolen whiskey,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, choking is just laughter's kinky cousin—now, MJ, picture this: we kidnap Peter, dress him as a Broadway Spider-Man, and drop him into Times Square mid-tourist feeding frenzy. It'll be therapy and immersive theater!” Wade said.

“Wade, I think the part where you said "kidnap Peter" is where this already fell apart—but, hey, good to know your version of therapy involves public humiliation and potential lawsuits,” Mary said.

“Hey, don't knock it till you see the reviews. "Spider-Man: Panic in Times Square" could be the breakout hit of the year—I'd buy a ticket,” Johnny said.

The server glides by with the practiced indifference of someone who’s seen worse at lunch rush, dropping a sweating pitcher of water in the middle of the table. Johnny absently reaches for it, topping off an already too-full glass, the ice jingling as droplets spill onto the laminate. Felicia drums her nails once, sharp against her untouched plate, her sharp eyes flicking toward MJ as if waiting for her to recognize the inevitable appeal of chaos.

“You know, MJ, the lawsuits might just be the cherry on this catastrophe sundae. But if we added Johnny here—torch him up a little—Peter wouldn’t know whether to punch him or call him a distraction. Both work,” Felicia said.

“Exactly! Johnny's like the human equivalent of waving a shiny object in front of a cat—except this cat can punch through walls and brood like it’s a part-time gig. Total win-win,” Wade said.

“You guys do realize that "torment Peter until he snaps" isn’t technically listed under "how to help your friend," right? But, okay, Johnny as a human distraction does have... potential,” Mary said.

“Potential? MJ, come on, distracting Peter is like my second superpower. First being, y'know... fire. But I can clear his head faster than Wade can clear a cheesecake,” Johnny said.

Wade grabs the abandoned fork closest to him, dramatically wagging it like a conductor preparing to cue an orchestra of bad ideas. Johnny kicks back, slouching low enough that his shoulder nearly collides with MJ’s, his grin lazy but sharp at the edges. The condensation from the forgotten drinks pools into shallow rings, unnoticed, beneath the growing hum of collusion at the booth.

“If this isn’t intervention gold, I don’t know what is. Wade’s theatrics, Johnny’s firepower, MJ’s moral compass, and me—well, you know I always land on my feet,” Felicia said.

“And me, the lovable wildcard who turns this into the kinda intervention that gets optioned for TV. Step one: we set Peter up. Step two: chaos. Step three: profits!” Wade said.

“Step four: I explain to Peter how I got roped into this circus and why I didn’t stop you all before it hit step two,” Mary said.

“MJ, you don’t stop a circus—especially not this one. You just grab some popcorn, lean into the chaos, and try not to get hit by a flying trapeze,” Johnny said.

Felicia’s lips curve into something just shy of a smirk as she swirls the straw in her untouched drink, the ice clicking in subdued rhythm. Wade leans further across the table, the fork now spinning precariously between his fingers like a weaponized baton. MJ shifts, half-stifling a grin she won’t let anyone catch outright, while Johnny tips his glass back again, unapologetically draining what remains.

“So, MJ, does that mean you're in—or are you going to play Peter's defense attorney while we work wonders with our organized chaos?” Felicia asked.

“Objection! MJ’s too smart to play defense on a case this fun—besides, with Johnny here, we’ve got enough fireworks to make up for any loopholes in our legal strategy,” Wade said.

The fork clatters onto the table as Wade’s fingers misfire mid-dramatic twirl, the sound sharp enough to draw a sideways glance from a nearby booth before he snatches it back up. MJ’s hand rests lightly on the edge of the table now, tapping once as though testing the waters before she speaks, her expression caught between resigned amusement and something quieter. Felicia, unbothered, leans forward—just enough for her presence to shift the dynamic—her eyes flicking briefly to Johnny, who is currently spinning his empty glass in slow, lazy circles like the very idea of boredom offends him.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the words begin again, where everything in the booth feels suspended—Wade's fork lost in arrested air, MJ's fingertips still skating the edge of the table, Felicia's gaze fixed somewhere past Johnny's spinning glass. The faint hum of the Cheesecake Factory soundtrack presses around them, punctuated by the soft clang of dishes from an unseen kitchen. Johnny’s glass halts abruptly, as though his irritation with its motion has crept up on even him.

“Did you stop the glass because it was irritating you, or because you finally realized it wasn't spinning you any closer to a point?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, I stopped because I realized the glass wasn’t gonna magically fill itself with margarita, and also because I wanted to make you realize I’m the point,” Wade said.

“Wade, sweetheart, you're a lot of things, but 'the point' requires less tequila and more focus,” Mary said.

“Less tequila and more focus? That sounds like the tagline for a support group I’d accidentally lead,” Johnny said.

Felicia picks a crumb from the table with an exaggerated delicacy, her smirk sharp enough to cut glass as Wade starts to gesture again, fork in hand like a conductor’s baton. MJ shifts slightly, elbow braced now on the edge of the booth, her phone loosely balanced in the other hand, screen dark but ready. Across from her, Johnny leans back, one arm draped over the seat, the picture of practiced indifference that fools no one in the booth.

“If you're leading a support group, Johnny, I hope the fire exits are up to code. We wouldn’t want your members learning the hard way about your attention span,” Felicia said.

“Fire exits? Please, Felicia, his attention span’s so short they’d never even make it to the safety demonstration before he’s accidentally torched the manual and started spinning his chair like it’s the teacups at Disneyland,” Wade said.

“Okay, before this turns into a full roast, can we focus up? I’m about to call Johnny, and I’d rather not explain why Wade’s running fire drills in a Cheesecake Factory,” Mary said.

“I mean, if you're calling me, the least you can do is appreciate the free fire drills. I'm practically a public service,” Johnny said.

The table shifts subtly under MJ’s weight as she sits up straighter, her phone now lit in her hand, thumb poised just above the screen. Wade leans in, closing the gap between him and the others, his elbows on the table and his expression entirely too delighted by the escalating nonsense. Johnny uncrosses his legs, boots hitting the floor with a muffled thud, some mixture of defensiveness and anticipation sharpening the angle of his jaw.

“Public service? Adorable. Just try not to set the bar so low you trip over it, Torch,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, Felicia, let him have this—Johnny's basically the human equivalent of sparklers on the Fourth of July. Flashy, fun, and a fire hazard you can’t look away from,” Wade said.

“Sparklers or not, someone’s getting burned if I don’t hit this call now. So, everyone, zip it—or forever hold your fireworks,” Mary said.

“If I’m the fireworks, does that make Wade the dud or the one that blows up in your hand?” Johnny asked.

Wade’s grin widens as he points the fork at Johnny like it’s carrying the weight of his comeback, the tines glinting under the soft overhead lights. MJ, ignoring them both, presses the call button with a deliberate click, her phone now a glowing center of attention. Felicia settles back against her seat, one brow arched, watching the scene unfold like it’s theater performed solely for her amusement.

“Careful, Wade. If you’re the one blowing up in someone’s hand, make sure it’s not on my watch—I hate cleaning up messes that don’t pay,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, sweetheart, if I blow up in someone's hand, at least it'll be a memorable grip-and-giggle. You can't put a price on that kind of fireworks display,” Wade said.

The faint hum of MJ's phone calls attention back to her, the tinny ringing laced with the anticipation that settles over the table. Wade twirls the fork again, though this time with less conviction, his focus torn between Felicia's sharp eyes and the unanswered call now bridging their silence. Across the booth, Johnny drums two fingers against the leather seat, his earlier smirk twitching just slightly, betraying a flicker of curiosity he doesn't bother to mask.

* * *

There is a moment, just before motion asserts itself, when the group remains suspended—half standing, half slouched, the sharp afternoon sun slicing through the edges of their uneven reunion. Wade’s exaggerated collapse lands him sprawled on the asphalt, arms flung wide like a martyr auditioning for sympathy. MJ adjusts her sunglasses with the deliberate indifference of someone too practiced at ignoring chaos to let it wrinkle her poise, while Felicia shifts her weight to one hip, her phone already slipping into her palm with feline precision.

“Wade, darling, if you're fishing for mouth-to-mouth, at least buy me a drink first,” Felicia said.

“Tequila shots and bad decisions, Felicia, that's my love language—care to conjugate a verb or two before I die of neglect here?” Wade asked.

“Wade, if you're dying, just let me know what kind of flowers you want for the funeral so I can pencil it in between saving the world and grabbing my dry cleaning,” Mary said.

“You guys started the party without me? Come on, at least let me get a round in before Wade starts writing his will on the pavement,” Johnny said.

Johnny’s car door swings open with a flourish, matching the energy of his entrance, the sharp squeak of hinges punctuating Wade’s theatrics on the ground. Sunlight catches on his aviators as he steps out, all radiant enthusiasm and effortless swagger, a stark contrast to the deliberate, buzz-softened movements of Felicia and MJ. Above the scrape of Wade’s imagined last breaths, the low rumble of another vehicle rolls through the lot, ignored entirely, as if the world outside their strange orbit has ceased to matter.

“Johnny, darling, your timing is impeccable—Wade’s playing roadkill, MJ’s orchestrating his burial, and I’m just here for the eulogy,” Felicia said.

“Make sure the eulogy mentions my chiseled abs, heart of gold, and the time I out-ate a sumo wrestler at an all-you-can-eat taco buffet. Heroic. Truly,” Wade said.

“Wade, I’ll make sure your tombstone says “Gone but never quiet”—covers the abs and the buffet story in one shot.” Mary said.

“If his tombstone’s got abs, I want my own statue with fire effects. Hell, make it a fountain. Classy, but kinda dangerous,” Johnny said.

Felicia’s laugh is quick and sharp, cutting through the midday haze, her gaze flicking from Johnny’s exaggerated hand gestures to Wade’s prone sprawl with the practiced boredom of someone who’s been here before. MJ leans back against the hood of her car, her fingers absently trailing the edge of her sunglasses as she watches it all, amusement curling at the edges of her otherwise poised frame. Wade shifts just enough to add dramatic flair to his pose, one hand now over his heart, the other pointing vaguely skyward as if auditioning for a martyrdom no one is buying.

“If we’re handing out statues, I want mine stealing yours—something about balance in the universe, you understand,” Felicia said.

“Oh, absolutely, balance is key. I’ll take mine holding a chimichanga, saving orphans, and flipping off gravity—triple threat masterpiece, obviously,” Wade said.

“Sounds more like a circus act than a statue, Wade—though I'd pay good money to see you flipping off gravity while holding a chimichanga,” Mary said.

“I’ll throw in a twenty if we toss in some pyrotechnics—gravity flips and chimichangas deserve fireworks, minimum,” Johnny said.

The conversation hangs in the air, thick with the kind of absurd camaraderie that thrives on borrowed time and bad decisions. MJ shifts her weight against the hood, the metallic groan beneath her barely registering as Johnny strikes a pose next to his car, one hand gesturing wildly like he’s already pitching Wade’s statue to a non-existent audience. Across the lot, a gust of wind stirs the littered remnants of some forgotten takeout bag, its crumpled arc the only witness to the ridiculous tableau they’ve created.

“You know, for a group allegedly saving the day, we’re remarkably committed to looking like the outtakes of a failed reality show,” Felicia said.

“Failed reality show? Please, this is peak entertainment. We’re just one dramatic betrayal and a tequila sponsorship away from Emmy-worthy chaos,” Wade said.

Felicia glances toward the horizon, her lips curving in a wry smirk as sunlight glints off the subtle shine of her earrings, her silhouette balanced between exasperation and indulgence. Wade stirs from the asphalt, not fully rising but shifting enough to make his looming resurrection another spectacle, fingers flicking dust off his suit with a carelessness that borders on theatrical. Johnny tosses imaginary fireworks into the air to punctuate his laugh, their invisible explosions landing somewhere between his overconfidence and Wade’s deliberate absurdity, while MJ watches, arms crossed, the faintest shake of her head betraying her amusement.

* * *

The afternoon sun bakes the asphalt, heat shimmering in lazy waves around parked cars. Felicia leans against the driver-side door, nail tracing an idle path along the car’s gleaming black finish. Wade adjusts his mask as if it’s a second skin pulled too tight, the unnatural stillness of his pose betrayed only by the faint twitch of his index finger.

“We’re really doing a public service, aren’t we? Wrangling Parker’s neuroses like it’s a two-for-one therapy special,” Felicia said.

“If we're calling this therapy, then I want a degree and the right to charge him $200 an hour to listen to him whine about great responsibility while I stare lovingly at his ass,” Wade said.

“I mean, if you can stare at his ass and still keep a straight face through the guilt monologue, you probably deserve the degree,” Mary said.

“Look, if anyone’s getting paid to stare at Parker’s ass, it should be me. I’ve been doing it for free since high school,” Johnny said.

Johnny’s laughter echoes faintly as he swings into the passenger seat of MJ’s car, the door shutting with a solid thud. MJ glances back toward Felicia and Wade, her hand lingering on the steering wheel, a gesture caught somewhere between hesitation and dismissal. The engine rumbles to life, covering the brief silence before they pull away, tires scraping softly against the heat-soaked pavement.

“Well, it’s comforting to know Parker’s ass has such a devoted following, but I prefer to focus on the part of him that doesn’t look good in spandex—like his overthinking,” Felicia said.

“Yeah, well, his overthinking doesn’t make me want to risk it all and write poetry. The ass, though? That’s Pulitzer material,” Wade said.

“Well, let me know when you publish the anthology, Wade. I’ll make sure the dedication reads, “To great asses and the overthinkers who shake them,” Mary said.

“Don’t forget to put me in the acknowledgments—“For Johnny, who kept the fire burning.” Literally.” Johnny said.

The car disappears down the parking lot’s exit, sunlight flashing off its rear window in a final flicker before it merges with the slow churn of traffic beyond. Felicia shifts her weight against the car, arms folding, her gaze still lingering where the taillights had faded. Wade huffs out a breath loud enough to be heard through his mask, the sound merging with the low hum of distant engines and the steady rhythm of cicadas in the heat.

“Well, Wade, it looks like it’s just us and our questionable morals now. Shall we go ruin something beautiful?” Felicia asked.

“Only if we can stop for tacos first. I like to fuel up before committing morally ambiguous acts of flair,” Wade said.

“Don’t forget the guac—nothing says “questionable morals” like paying extra for it.” Mary said.

“Always pay extra for the guac. It’s the real moral high ground,” Johnny said.

Felicia tips her head back with a low, sharp exhale, the sun catching in her sunglasses as she pushes off the car and pulls open the driver-side door. Wade shuffles to the passenger side, boots scuffing against the pavement, moving like his joints are held together with duct tape and stubbornness. The car groans faintly as they settle in, the faint scent of overly warm leather mixing with the residual tang of engine oil and hot metal.

“Just try not to get guac on the seats. Evidence is harder to hide when it’s green and overpriced,” Felicia said.

“Relax, Felicia, guac is like blood—if you handle it right, it blends in with the chaos,” Wade said.

The engine shudders to life, its grumble vibrating through the chassis as Felicia grips the wheel, her nails tapping a restless rhythm against the leather. Wade slouches in the seat next to her, boots kicked up on the dash, leaving vague scuffs that she pointedly ignores. Warm air flows through the lowered windows, carrying the faint tang of asphalt and exhaust as they pull out onto the sun-bleached street.
