Grizzly Knights

Episode 2: The System

Tony is managing. Jessica is functional. Clint is fine. None of them believe the other two. Three people who know exactly what they are doing to themselves and have decided to keep doing it anyway.

* * *

The fluorescent light over the central workbench hums faintly, casting sharp shadows across scattered tools and loose wires. Tony’s hands are steady but too fast, soldering something small and pointless with the kind of focus that feels like desperation. Jessica leans against the wall, arms crossed, coat still on, scuffing the sole of one boot repeatedly against the concrete floor.

“You gonna wear a hole in that floor, or is this your way of telling me you’re jealous of my fidget skills?” Tony asked.

“If I were jealous, Stark, I’d be soldering pointless shit at one in the morning,” Jessica said.

“Pointless? This is a stabilizer for—oh, who am I kidding, it’s completely pointless. You want a blowtorch, you can join in. No waiting list,” Tony said.

“Hard pass. Fire and I don’t have the best track record,” Jessica said.

Tony flicks the soldering iron off and tosses it down with a clatter, not looking up. Jessica pulls a flask from her coat pocket, unscrews the cap with a practiced flick of her thumb, and takes a long pull. The metallic smell of hot circuits hangs between them like stale breath.

“You know, Jess, the smell of whiskey in the workshop really adds a certain... ambiance. Matches the vibe—salvage yard meets funeral parlor,” Tony said.

“Figured it’d pair nicely with your eau de self-destruction,” Jessica said.

“Ah, compliments already. Careful, Jones, or people might think we’re bonding,” Tony said.

“Relax, Stark. Bonding’s not my brand. This is me tolerating you,” Jessica said.

Jessica wipes her mouth with the back of her hand, leaves a faint smear on her coat sleeve. Tony picks up a screwdriver, starts loosening screws on the same device he just soldered, like undoing it will fix something that isn’t visible. The hum of the HVAC system fills the silence, a low mechanical heartbeat under everything.

“Tolerating, huh? Well, you’re in elite company. That’s basically my default setting with myself,” Tony said.

“Guess we’ve got something in common then,” Jessica said.

“Fantastic. Let’s toast to mutual self-loathing—oh wait, you already started without me,” Tony said.

“Didn't think you needed the help, Stark. You’ve got your own brand of liquid courage tucked away somewhere, don’t you?” Jessica asked.

Tony’s jaw tightens, a twitch barely visible in the sharp light. He reaches for a battered coffee cup on the edge of the bench, cold, black, and forgotten, and takes a sip like it might be something stronger. Jessica’s eyes track the motion, quiet judgment hidden under the steel in her stare.

“Not tonight. Someone’s gotta keep the lights on while you stand there looking unimpressed,” Tony said.

“Could’ve fooled me. You’re working like the power’s been off for years,” Jessica said.

The corner of Tony’s mouth twitches—half a smirk, half a tic—but his eyes stay on the dismantled circuits in front of him. Jessica exhales sharply through her nose, the kind of sound that could have been a laugh if it had any warmth left in it. The workbench creaks faintly as Tony leans forward, shoulders curving inward like they might collapse.

* * *

The air smells like burnt solder and cold coffee. Jessica leans against the far wall, her arms crossed, watching the fluorescent light stutter in the corner. Tony adjusts a magnifying lens over the circuit board, his movements clean but tight, lips pressed thin.

“You know, if you're planning on just standing there and judging my existential crisis, at least bring a coffee that doesn't taste like motor oil next time,” Tony said.

“If I wanted to judge your existential crisis, I’d need a stronger drink than this shit,” Jessica said.

“You guys done yet, or should I come back when the foreplay's over?” Clint asked.

“Depends—are you gonna call first next time, or do I install an early warning system for Barton-shaped intrusions?” Tony asked.

Clint steps further into the room, his boots scuffing the concrete floor, and shrugs off a jacket damp with something that isn’t just rain. He tosses it onto a chair without looking, his eyes flicking between Tony and Jessica like he’s gauging the temperature of the room. Jessica tilts her chin up, not moving from her spot, but her gaze sharpens on Clint’s face, catching something unspoken.

“You look like you crawled out of a sewer. That your new thing, or just a side hustle?” Jessica asked.

“Nah, just trying out the superhero chic—wet rat edition. You like it, Jones?” Clint asked.

“Great, now my workshop smells like wet rat and bad life choices. Thanks for the ambiance, Legolas,” Tony said.

“Adds to the charm, Stark. Place was already one spilled drink away from a crime scene,” Jessica said.

Tony straightens, setting a screwdriver down with a metallic rattle that cuts through the room. Clint digs through his back pocket, pulling out a crushed pack of cigarettes, but pauses when his thumb lingers over the lighter. Jessica shifts her weight, her boots making a muted thud against the floor, while her eyes don't leave Clint's face.

“Relax, I’m not lighting up. Just needed something to do with my hands while Stark here practices his brooding,” Clint said.

“Brooding? No, no, this is precision sulking, thank you. Requires tools and an audience, exactly my scene,” Tony said.

“You’re missing the mood lighting and a tragic monologue. Really sell the performance, Stark,” Jessica said.

“If he starts monologuing, I’m out. I’m wet, I’m cold, and I’m too sober for whatever this is,” Clint said.

Clint slumps into the chair, the legs scraping harshly against the concrete. The cigarette pack crumples faintly in his grip before he shoves it into his pocket again. Tony wipes a smear of grease off his palm with a rag, his expression still sharp but unreadable as he flicks a glance at the wet streak Clint’s jacket left on the seat.

“If you're planning to redecorate, Clint, at least aim for something that doesn't scream "I lost a fight with a gutter,” Tony said.

“He's just going for that authentic, "life's a shitshow" aesthetic. Nailed it, I'd say,” Jessica said.

Jessica pushes off the wall, the scuffed toe of her boot sliding over a spilled washer that clicks lightly against the floor. She moves to the workbench, picks up an untouched mug of coffee, and lifts it halfway to her mouth before apparently deciding against it. Clint watches her, his elbows on his knees, jaw tightening like he’s biting back a retort or a yawn.

* * *

Tony switches out a soldering iron for a pair of needle-nose pliers, the movement precise, almost too smooth to seem conscious. The hum of machinery fills the silence, broken only by the faint clink of metal-on-metal as he adjusts something in the half-dismantled gauntlet on his workbench. Jessica sips from the mug, the sharp smell of whiskey cutting through the oil and ozone in the air.

“You really think whiskey’s gonna improve your already stellar people skills, Jones, or is this just performance art for the guy with the bow and unresolved issues?” Tony asked.

“Depends, Stark. You think being a jackass is gonna make your workshop feng shui any better, or is that just your default setting?” Jessica asked.

“Should I be insulted I'm not in the running for "performance art," or are we just assuming my unresolved issues are already a given?” Clint asked.

“Oh, they're not just a given, Legolas, they're practically a registered trademark. Might want to get ahead of that branding,” Tony said.

The pliers hit the workbench with a sharper clatter than necessary. Tony leans back, rolling his neck until it cracks, one hand rubbing at his temple like he's kneading away the conversation. Jessica shifts her weight, the mug dangling loose in her fingers, liquid sloshing quietly against the side. Clint's foot bounces twice, then goes still.

“If we’re handing out trademarks, Stark, yours would be “deflection through overwork.” Catchy.” Jessica said.

“And yours would be “whiskey-fueled misanthropy,” but hey, at least it’s on brand.” Clint said.

“Great, now that we’ve got everyone’s trademarks sorted, can we move this along? I’ve got half a gauntlet and zero patience left to burn,” Tony said.

“That’s funny, I thought impatience was your superpower,” Jessica said.

Tony exhales sharply through his nose, the sound less a laugh than a release valve. The arc reactor's faint glow catches on the edge of the gauntlet as he spins it absently, metal-on-stone grating beneath his fingers. Jessica sets the mug down hard enough to make the tools nearby tremble. Clint leans back, the chair creaking under his weight, arms folding across his chest as he watches them.

“You two done with the foreplay, or should I come back when we’re actually fixing something?” Clint asked.

“Foreplay? Please, Barton, if this was foreplay, you'd already be crying in the corner,” Tony said.

“And if this was actual fixing, we wouldn't still be sitting here listening to you two measure dicks,” Jessica said.

“If we're measuring dicks, I definitely came under-equipped for this conversation,” Clint said.

Jessica smirks, a quick flash of teeth before it fades, her hand already reaching back for the mug. Tony doesn’t look up, his focus dropping back to the gauntlet as he picks up a slim screwdriver, the quick, deliberate turns of his wrist all business. Clint shifts in the chair again, the wood scraping softly as he leans forward, elbows settling back onto his knees. The hum of the machinery fills the lull like dead air.

“Well, if you’re both done auditioning for the dysfunctional sitcom pilot, some of us still have actual work to do,” Tony said.

“Don’t strain yourself, Stark. We wouldn’t want your ego pulling a muscle,” Jessica said.

Tony doesn’t bite back, just sets the screwdriver down with a muted click and pulls a magnifier over the gauntlet, the lens casting a warped reflection of his face. Jessica tips the mug again, draining the last of the whiskey with a hollow, resigned swallow. Clint rubs the back of his neck, glancing toward the door like he’s weighing his odds of escape. The mug lands on the workbench again, this time quieter, just a controlled thud.

* * *

Tony yanks the fridge door open, the seal breaking with a muted pop. His free hand searches blindly inside for the ice tray, fingers grazing bottles of Perrier and a forgotten, half-empty carton of orange juice. The overhead fluorescent flickers once, a stuttering hum filling the silence.

“Ice in the orange juice? Bold move, Tony. Let's see if I still remember what hydration looks like,” Tony said.

“Could always spike it, make it worth remembering,” Jessica said.

“Well, if we're spiking drinks at three in the morning, I officially feel underdressed,” Clint said.

“Relax, Legolas, you're always underdressed. And Jess, spiking orange juice is a cry for help even by my standards,” Tony said.

The ice tray cracks under Tony's twist, shards clinking against the counter as he dumps a handful into a lowball glass. Jessica leans against the sink, arms crossed, her boot tapping against the cabinet’s peeling veneer. Clint shifts his weight by the doorframe, thumb rolling absently over the fraying strap of his quiver like it’s muscle memory.

“So what’s this, a team bonding exercise? Or just a collection of insomniacs avoiding their shit?” Jessica asked.

“Pretty sure it’s door number three: a masterclass in bad coping mechanisms,” Clint said.

“Well, congrats, you've both passed the prerequisite. Welcome to Advanced Avoidance 401. Final exam involves not setting anything on fire. Probably,” Tony said.

“If that’s the bar, I’m failing out early. Hope there’s a participation trophy,” Jessica said.

Tony drops two cubes into his glass, the muted clink echoing in the cramped space. Jessica snatches a Perrier from the fridge without asking, the hiss of the cap cutting through the air like punctuation. Clint exhales sharply through his nose, a not-quite laugh as his gaze flits toward the ceiling.

“Participation trophy’s just surviving the night without punching someone or something, right? Guess I’m still in the game,” Clint said.

“Low bar, but hey, aim small, miss small. I’ll make sure to engrave "Didn’t Punch Anyone at 3 AM" on your trophy. Custom fonts, naturally,” Tony said.

“Put "Well, Almost Didn’t" on mine and save yourself the trouble later,” Jessica said.

“Mine’s gonna need "Didn’t Punch Anyone, But Thought About It Real Hard,” Clint said.

The corner of Tony’s mouth twitches like he wants to smirk but can’t quite commit. Jessica tilts the Perrier to her lips, the carbonation fizz breaking the heavy air, and doesn’t bother looking at either of them. Clint shifts his quiver strap higher on his shoulder, like settling the weight is all he can contribute right now.

“Fine, I’ll make it a group award: "Didn’t Punch Each Other, But Seriously Considered It." We can hang it in the disaster lounge,” Tony said.

“Might wanna leave room for a date and time, in case this becomes a recurring achievement,” Jessica said.

Tony finally takes a sip from his glass, the muted amber of the whiskey catching under the flickering light. Jessica balances the empty Perrier bottle on the counter, her finger tipping it back and forth like it’s a coin flip. Clint’s stare lingers on the doorway, as if it might offer an exit strategy that doesn’t involve speaking first.

* * *

The whiskey glass doesn’t make it back to the counter right away. Tony holds it mid-air, staring through the liquid like he’s running numbers on the back of it. Jessica lets the Perrier bottle fall to its side—it rolls in slow arcs, the glass clinking against the counter’s edge. Clint shifts his weight, his foot scraping softly over the tile, dragging the silence with it.

“Could've sprung for a better floor in here. All this tech and my kitchen still sounds like a goddamn morgue,” Tony said.

“Yeah, but at least the morgue doesn’t judge you for drinking at 3 AM,” Jessica said.

“Then I’m in the wrong place, ‘cause this floor’s been giving me the side-eye all night,” Clint said.

“Maybe it’s not the floor, Legolas. Could be your own guilt staring back at you—polished reflection and all,” Tony said.

The Perrier bottle finally wobbles to a stop, resting against Jessica’s hand. She doesn’t move it. Tony puts the glass down too hard, the thunk sharp and uneven on the countertop. Clint’s hand brushes the edge of his jacket, like he’s checking for something that isn’t there.

“Pretty sure guilt doesn't rattle around like loose change. That’s just you, Stark,” Jessica said.

“Maybe guilt gets louder if you ignore it long enough. Not that I’d know,” Clint said.

“Oh, you’d know, Barton. It’s practically your theme song—right up there with “Complete Silence and a Thousand Yard Stare,” Tony said.

“If we're ranking theme songs, mine's just the sound of a bottle opening on repeat,” Jessica said.

Tony snorts, a sharp exhale through his nose, and reaches for the bottle to pour another drink. The whiskey sloshes into the glass without ceremony, amber streaks crawling up the sides. Jessica flicks the Perrier bottle with her nail, the sound muted and dull. Clint crosses his arms, his jaw tightening as he leans back against the counter.

“Well, at least yours comes with a soundtrack. Mine’s just screaming on loop—sometimes mine, sometimes not,” Clint said.

“Hell of a remix, though. Really sets the mood for three insomniacs avoiding their own shit at an hour no one should be awake,” Tony said.

“Yeah, well, sleep’s overrated. Can’t fuck up if you’re too tired to try,” Jessica said.

“Or you just fuck up more creatively. Take your pick,” Clint said.

Jessica snaps the cap off the next Perrier with a quick twist, the threads grinding faintly before the release. Tony drains his glass without looking at either of them, barely swallowing before he sets it down again. Clint tilts his head up toward the ceiling, his gaze fixed somewhere past the water-stained tiles.

“Raise your hand if you’re starting to think we need a punch card for existential crises—buy nine, the tenth one comes with free coffee,” Tony said.

“You got a punch card for rock bottom, Stark? 'Cause I think mine’s full,” Jessica said.

The Perrier bottle clatters as Jessica sets it down harder than necessary, the carbonation hissing faintly through the open neck. Tony’s fingers tap against the rim of his glass, the rhythm uneven, restless. Clint lowers his head from the ceiling and looks at the floor instead, his shoulders pulling tighter.

* * *

The rooftop air is sharp, slicing through the stale warmth of the stairwell as the door grinds shut behind Tony. His left hand flicks the cheap Bic lighter, the flame struggling against the wind. Clint lingers near the edge, arms crossed, his boots scuffing softly against the concrete.

“You know, Barton, if you're here to give me the "smoking kills" speech, I might have to start charging admission,” Tony said.

“Not my style. I'm more of a "die however you want, just not tonight" kind of guy,” Clint said.

“Fair enough. Guess I'll keep it to a light maiming, then,” Tony said.

“Hey, as long as you leave something vital for Rhodey to yell at,” Clint said.

Tony exhales a thin stream of smoke, the wind tugging it apart before it can drift. Clint shifts his weight, the rooftop gravel crunching faintly beneath his boots. The city hums below, a low electric murmur stretching toward the horizon.

“Rhodey’s got a list, doesn’t he? Pretty sure I’ve left him vital and pissed since '94,” Tony said.

“Yeah, well, some people are overachievers. You're just... consistent,” Clint said.

“Consistent. Right. Like your aim after three shots of tequila,” Tony said.

“Hey, three's my sweet spot. Four's when I start getting creative,” Clint said.

Tony smirks, the cigarette balanced loosely between his fingers, the ash growing uneven at the tip. Clint pulls a crumpled pack of gum from his jacket pocket, shaking one loose with a practiced flick. The sky is softening, a diluted gray bleeding into the dark.

“Creative, sure. Because nothing says precision like trick shots at last call,” Tony said.

“Says the guy who built a flying tin can after a happy hour,” Clint said.

“Not just a tin can. A weaponized, billion-dollar, aerodynamic middle finger to anyone who doubted me,” Tony said.

“So basically, a shiny suit to match the ego. Nice synergy,” Clint said.

Tony flicks the cigarette, a sharp snap of motion, sending a thread of ash tumbling over the edge. Clint chews on the gum, the rhythm slow, deliberate, his jaw tightening briefly before slackening again. The wind picks up, cutting across the rooftop with a bite that neither of them acknowledges.

“Ego’s the down payment, Barton. The suit’s just the return on investment,” Tony said.

“Guess that makes me the guy betting on duct tape and luck. Cheaper overhead,” Clint said.

Tony snorts, the sound brief and low, as he takes another drag on the cigarette, the ember flaring into the dim light. Clint tilts his head back slightly, eyes tracing the streaks of gray stretching wider across the sky. The city below sharpens in the growing light, windows catching hints of reflection.

* * *

The fluorescent overhead buzzed faintly, a static hum that layered over the dense quiet. Jessica shrugged her coat on, the leather creaking as it settled over her shoulders. Clint shifted on the couch, face slack, one arm thrown over his eyes like it might block out the reality of morning.

“You look like hell, Jones. That your natural glow, or did the city finally admit it hates you back?” Tony asked.

“The city and I have a mutual disdain pact. You're one to talk—pretty sure your glow's just that radioactive ego seeping out,” Jessica said.

“Radioactive ego could probably power the whole damn city, if we’re being honest,” Clint said.

“Yeah, but then I’d have to deal with ConEd, and I don’t hate myself that much,” Tony said.

Tony tapped a wrench against the edge of the workbench, the metallic clang sharp in the stillness. Jessica adjusted the collar of her coat, her fingers brushing against a faint tear she hadn’t noticed before. Clint exhaled heavily from the couch, the sound more sigh than breath, his boots still planted on the floor where he’d given up hours ago.

“Guess we’re all running on fumes and bad decisions, huh? I’m out before one of you tries to bond or some shit,” Jessica said.

“Fumes, bad decisions, and coffee, if you'd been smart enough to grab some. But yeah, run while you still can,” Clint said.

“Coffee’s overrated. Adrenaline and spite get you further,” Tony said.

“Spite runs out, Stark. Coffee doesn’t. Try again when you’re human,” Jessica said.

Jessica’s boots scuffed against the concrete floor as she turned toward the door, the sound low and deliberate. Clint shifted again, dragging a hand down his face, fingers catching on the scruff at his jaw. Tony spun the wrench slowly between his fingers, metal glinting under the cold fluorescent light.

“If spite and coffee both fail, there’s always tequila. Works in a pinch,” Clint said.

“Tequila’s not a solution, Barton. It’s a shortcut to not giving a damn about the problem,” Tony said.

“Looks like we all speak fluent "bad coping mechanisms." Congrats, boys, we're a support group with zero support,” Jessica said.

“Guess that makes me the team mascot. Go Hawkeyes—terrible coping, gold medal,” Clint said.

Jessica reached for the door, her grip firm and the click of the handle sharp in the quiet. Clint’s leg bounced once, restlessness surfacing as his heel tapped against the edge of the couch. Tony set the wrench down with a muted thunk, the weight of metal settling against the scarred workbench.

“We’re not a support group, Jones. We’re the halftime show for a train wreck,” Tony said.

“Good, then I won’t feel bad leaving before the finale. Enjoy the crash, boys,” Jessica said.

The door opened with a faint groan, hinges protesting long-neglected maintenance. A sliver of the hallway’s dull light spilled in, cutting across the concrete floor. Jessica stepped through without looking back, the chill draft from the corridor brushing past and settling into the room.
