Grizzly Knights

Episode 3: What Gets Said

The three of them in a room. What Steve carries. What Sam sees. What Bucky does not say. The conversation that keeps almost happening and then does not.

* * *

The smell of andouille and onions pushed at the edges of the room, a hiss and crackle rising from the skillet on the stovetop. Sam’s hands moved steady over the cutting board, blade scraping clean arcs across the wood. Bucky leaned against the counter, arms crossed, eyes on a smudge near the toaster that Sam hadn’t noticed.

“You just gonna stand there all day, or you planning on being useful?” Sam asked.

“Depends. You trust me with a knife?” Bucky asked.

“Long as you don’t try to use it on me, we’re good,” Sam said.

“No promises. What do you need chopped?” Bucky asked.

Sam slid the cutting board toward Bucky, the knife clinking faintly against the wood. Bucky stepped in, adjusting his stance like the motion was unfamiliar, his left hand hovering over the board for a second too long. The sound of the blade meeting peppers filled the small space, uneven at first, then steady.

“Keep it steady, Barnes. Don't need you turning the veggies into casualties,” Sam said.

“You want perfect slices, Wilson, you're gonna have to hire a sous chef,” Bucky said.

“Well, if you’re volunteering, then I better start writing your paycheck,” Sam said.

“You pay in whiskey, we’ll talk,” Bucky said.

The knife paused mid-slice as Bucky glanced up, the corner of his mouth twitching into something that wasn’t fully a smile. Sam turned back to the stovetop, flipping the sausage with short, precise movements, the oil spitting faintly against the pan. The hum of the fridge filled the short space between their breathing.

“You don’t get whiskey till you’ve earned it, Barnes. Finish those peppers first,” Sam said.

“Guess I’m working pro bono then,” Bucky said.

“That’s the spirit—cheap labor always tastes better,” Sam said.

“Careful, Wilson. Keep talking like that, and I'll unionize,” Bucky said.

The rhythm of the knife slowed as Bucky stacked the slices, lining them up unevenly at the edge of the board. Sam shook the skillet once, lifting his chin to eye the color of the browning sausage. The stale warmth of yesterday’s coffee lingered in the air, just behind the sharper smells of oil and spice.

“You unionize, I’m sending you straight to Sarah—she’ll set you straight real quick,” Sam said.

“Sarah scares me more than HYDRA ever did. I’ll pass,” Bucky said.

Bucky dumped the uneven slices into a bowl, the thud of peppers against ceramic cutting through the quiet. Sam smirked without looking, nudging the skillet to the side and snapping the flame off with a sharp click. Smoke and spice ghosted upward, hanging heavy in the sunlight slanting through the blinds.

* * *

The door shut with a dull thud behind Steve, and his boots scuffed against the tile as he stepped inside. The smell of cooked peppers and andouille hung in the air, thick and savory, while the skillet sat cooling on the stove. Sam leaned one hip against the counter, arms crossed, watching Steve as he walked in empty-handed.

“You know, usually when somebody’s late, they bring a peace offering—or at least a damn excuse that feels real,” Sam said.

“Guess holding a clock too heavy for you these days, huh?” Bucky asked.

“Ran into a kid outside trying to fix his bike chain. Figured that was worth the nine minutes,” Steve said.

“Yeah, but you’re still empty-handed—and now we all know Bucky cuts peppers faster than you fix chains,” Sam said.

Steve scratched the back of his neck, his eyes flicking toward the bowl of uneven peppers on the counter before settling on the floor. Bucky sank into a chair at the small kitchen table, boots kicked out just far enough to make navigating around him feel intentional. The plates and silverware clinked softly as Sam set the table, his movements brisk but not rushed.

“Tell me you at least washed your hands after,” Bucky said.

“Always do. Some of us don’t need Stark tech just to manage soap and water, Buck,” Steve said.

“And yet, somehow, you still manage to smell like powdered eggs and guilt. Sit. Food’s getting cold,” Sam said.

“Least he didn't say you smell like the 40s,” Bucky said.

Steve pulled out a chair, the wood creaking faintly as he lowered himself into it. Sam grabbed the serving bowl, steam still curling off the food as he brought it to the table. Bucky smirked into his glass of water, the condensation leaving faint rings on the table’s worn surface.

“I'll take powdered eggs over whatever that cologne is, Sam. Smells like you’re trying to impress your mirror,” Steve said.

“It’s called soap, Steve. You should give it a spin sometime—sounds like you’re already halfway there. Eat your damn peppers,” Sam said.

“Careful, Sam, he might take that as a compliment and start calling it progress,” Bucky said.

“Progress would be you cutting those peppers evenly, Buck. But I guess we all have our battles,” Steve said.

Sam dropped into his seat with a controlled heaviness, the chair legs scraping against the tiled floor. He reached for the bowl, spoon sinking into spiced rice and vegetables with a practiced rhythm before scooping some onto his plate. Steve leaned back slightly, elbows resting on the table’s edge, his fingertips idly brushing the grain of the wood.

“Yeah, well, progress for you, Steve, would be remembering you’re not eighty pounds soaking wet anymore and actually helping in the kitchen next time,” Sam said.

“Next time, let him handle the bike chain and we’ll see who starves first—us or the kid,” Bucky said.

Steve's jaw tightened just enough to register before he grabbed the spoon and started serving himself, the metal clanging briefly against the ceramic bowl. Sam leaned back in his chair, the corner of his mouth twitching like he'd already won. Bucky flicked a loose crumb from the table with the tip of his finger, watching the exchange without bothering to hide the amusement in his eyes.

* * *

The three of them sat in mismatched chairs on the small wooden porch, the boards creaking faintly under their weight. Sam's boots rested on the rail, his beer bottle balanced lazily on his knee. A mosquito buzzed too close to Steve's ear, and he swatted it away with a sharp motion, jaw still tight.

“You know we got bug spray, right? Don’t gotta try to box ‘em out like you're in a ring, man,” Sam said.

“Coulda fooled me, Sam. Thought you were just runnin' a mosquito sanctuary out here,” Bucky said.

“Seems like the mosquitoes are winning either way. Maybe they’re the real landlords out here,” Steve said.

“Well, if they start askin’ for rent, I’m passin’ the notice straight to you two freeloaders,” Sam said.

Sam tipped his bottle toward his mouth, the amber glass catching the sun before he took a slow pull. Bucky leaned back in his chair, the edge of his thumb idly running along the label of his nearly empty beer. Steve shifted, his elbow resting on the arm of his seat, eyes scanning the horizon as if the brackish breeze carried answers.

“You're just bitter 'cause we make better porch ornaments than you do, Wilson,” Bucky said.

“If sitting here counts as an accomplishment, we’ve really set the bar high,” Steve said.

“Hey, I’ll take it. High bar or not, at least I’ve got the best-lookin’ porch ornaments in Louisiana,” Sam said.

“Don’t let that go to your head, Sam. Pretty sure the bar’s just ‘not fallin’ apart.’,” Bucky said.

Steve chuckled under his breath, the sound low and brief, before he ran a hand over the back of his neck. Sam smirked, shaking his head slightly as he twirled the bottle between his fingers. Bucky stretched his legs out in front of him, boots scraping faintly against the wooden boards, his body settling deeper into the chair.

“Long as the porch doesn’t give out, I think we’re doing alright,” Steve said.

“If the porch gives out, y’all are helping rebuild it. Sweat equity and all that—welcome to Delacroix,” Sam said.

“Guess I’ll need a bigger beer to get through that,” Bucky said.

“You’d need more than a beer, Buck. Might finally break a sweat for once,” Steve said.

Bucky's metal hand tapped the neck of his bottle twice, a dull clink against the glass. Sam leaned back farther, the chair groaning under the shift, his lips twitching in a barely concealed grin. Steve reached down and adjusted the cuff of his jeans, the fabric stiff where it had started to crease.

“Y’all talk like I don’t know you’d both be useless with a hammer. Might as well grab the six-pack now and watch me work,” Sam said.

“You just want an audience. Don’t worry, we’ll clap real loud when it stands upright,” Bucky said.

The sun pressed heavier now, glinting off Bucky's arm as he shifted, the light catching in sharp edges. Sam tilted his bottle again, the last inch of beer swirling lazily inside before he drained it. Steve’s eyes lingered on the railing, his fingers unconsciously tapping a steady, muted rhythm against the arm of his chair.

* * *

The porch creaked under Sam’s weight as he leaned forward, elbows on his knees, bottle dangling loosely from two fingers. Steve’s tapping stopped, his hand settling flat on the armrest, but his eyes didn’t move from the horizon. Bucky sat back in the corner, a cigarette burning low between his fingers, ash falling unnoticed onto the wood.

“You ever gonna say what you’re tapping out, Steve, or we just supposed to guess?” Sam asked.

“Maybe he doesn’t even know,” Bucky said.

“Maybe I do,” Steve said.

“Then say it,” Sam said.

The wind stirred, rattling the empty bottle near Sam’s feet where it had rolled against the railing. The smoke from Bucky’s cigarette curled upward, dissipating into the thick, slow-moving air. Steve shifted his weight, the chair legs scraping faintly against the wooden boards.

“Does it matter?” Bucky asked.

“Not to you, apparently,” Steve said.

“Maybe not to him, but it matters here. Just say what you mean, Steve,” Sam said.

“Matter of habit, him not saying it,” Bucky said.

Bucky flicked the cigarette butt toward the dirt, the ember snuffing out on impact. Sam adjusted in his chair, the glass neck of his bottle clicking lightly against the wooden armrest. Steve’s jaw flexed once, tight, before his gaze dropped to the space between his boots.

“It was Morse. Just old habit,” Steve said.

“Okay, Morse for what? Or we just supposed to leave it there?” Sam asked.

“You don't leave it with Sam, not if you wanna breathe,” Bucky said.

“It was nothing. Just... thinking,” Steve said.

The porch fell quiet again except for the soft rustle of branches overhead. Sam exhaled sharply through his nose, the tension shifting in the angle of his shoulders. Bucky leaned back further, the chair creaking under him, eyes flicking toward Steve before settling on the horizon.

“You sure it’s nothing, or is that just your version of saying "don’t ask"?” Sam asked.

“It’s always "don’t ask." You know that,” Bucky said.

Sam set the empty bottle down beside his chair, the glass scraping a faint line against the wood. Steve’s hands curled into loose fists on his thighs, his knuckles pale but steady. Bucky’s fingers ran over the seam of his pants, a quick, restless motion that stopped as soon as it started.

* * *

The wood beneath the chairs creaked faintly as the weight of their silence settled. The humidity clung to the air, thick and slow, making every breath feel deliberate. Beyond the porch, the cicadas buzzed on, indifferent.

“You alright, Buck?” Sam asked.

“Yeah, I'm fine,” Bucky said.

“You’re not,” Steve said.

“None of us are, Steve. What's the point in pretending?” Sam asked.

Bucky’s jaw tightened, the muscle there twitching beneath his stubble. Steve’s gaze stayed fixed on him, sharp and unmoving, while Sam leaned back in his chair, his fingers tapping once against the armrest. The cicadas' hum filled the seconds that followed, relentless and steady.

“Doesn't matter if I'm alright or not. Just trying to keep going,” Bucky said.

“We all are, Buck. Doesn't mean you have to do it alone,” Steve said.

“You’re not alone, man. You just gotta let someone in,” Sam said.

“It’s not that simple,” Bucky said.

Steve shifted forward slightly in his seat, his elbows resting on his knees now, boots scraping softly against the porch. Sam exhaled, a quiet, measured sound, his gaze drifting out toward the yard. Bucky stayed still, his hand now gripping the edge of the chair as though anchoring himself.

“Doesn't have to be simple. Just has to start,” Steve said.

“We’ve seen you fight for everyone else, Buck. Fight for yourself, too,” Sam said.

“Don’t know if there’s enough left to fight for,” Bucky said.

“There is. I know there is,” Steve said.

The breeze shifted, carrying with it the faint brine of the marsh, stirring the beads of sweat on their skin. Sam’s chair rocked back slightly as he straightened, jaw tightening, the armrest creaking under his fingers. Bucky’s gaze dropped to the porch planks, his shoulders pressed rigid against the back of his seat.

“Been there, Buck. Thought the same thing once. But you don’t get to decide your story’s over—we do that together,” Sam said.

“Guess I’m still trying to figure out how to write the damn thing,” Bucky said.

Sam let out a low, dry laugh, shaking his head as he looked down at his hands. Steve’s fingers drummed once on his knee, then stilled, his eyes still locked on Bucky. The porch settled again, its old boards groaning faintly beneath their weight, as if the stillness pressed all three men further into place.

* * *

The sun hung low, glinting off the slow lapping water as Sam stepped onto the dock. The wood creaked under his weight, its weathered surface splintered from years of brine and heat. Steve followed, his boots scuffing the planks with deliberate, measured steps.

“You even remember how to relax, or do I need to show you?” Sam asked.

“I remember. Just not sure I’m built for it,” Steve said.

“Well, I guess it’s time to rebuild. Start with something simple — might not even hurt,” Sam said.

“Simple’s not exactly my strong suit. But I’ll try,” Steve said.

The water slapped against the dock's posts, uneven and soft, while the breeze smelled faintly of salt and diesel. Sam glanced down at the sandpaper lying forgotten near the boat hull, then leaned back against the railing, arms loose and shoulders squared, waiting. Steve stood a foot inside, hands jammed into his jacket pockets, eyes scanning the horizon like it had answers.

“Trying counts for something. But you're gonna have to stop staring like the horizon owes you rent,” Sam said.

“Old habits. I’ll stop when it pays up,” Steve said.

“You gotta let someone else collect sometimes, Steve. Might leave you a little lighter,” Sam said.

“Not sure I’d know what to do with lighter,” Steve said.

The dock swayed, slight and steady, as a larger boat passed further out, wake trailing behind it. Sam shifted his weight, the railing groaning under his back, while Steve finally looked down at the water. A heron lifted off in the distance, wings slow and deliberate, heading for the marsh.

“Maybe just take the win and let it sit. Doesn't always need to be heavier to mean something,” Sam said.

“Easier said than done. But... I hear you,” Steve said.

“Good. 'Cause I’m tired of repeating myself to you old men,” Sam said.

“Fair enough. You’ve got the patience of a saint, Sam,” Steve said.

Sam snorted softly, pushing off the railing and brushing wood dust off his palms. Steve shifted, the faint scrape of his boots against the dock filling the quiet stretch of air between them. The water gleamed golden in the late afternoon light, ripples catching and breaking the reflection of the sky.

“Saint? Nah. Just stubborn enough to keep trying with you,” Sam said.

“Stubborn tends to get results. Guess I owe you, Wilson,” Steve said.

Sam bent to pick up the sandpaper, turning it over in his hand like he'd forgotten how it ended up there. Steve stepped closer, the weight of his frame testing the dock’s old nails with each slow movement. The gulls overhead squabbled briefly, their sharp cries darting over the water, then fell quiet again.

* * *

The gravel crunched under Steve’s boots as he shifted his weight, hands jammed deep in the pockets of his jacket. Bucky stood by the passenger door of Steve's car, tracing a cigarette along his knuckles but not lighting it. Sam stayed planted at the edge of the driveway, arms crossed over his chest, staring past them at the narrow road.

“You two gonna stand there all night, or are you actually leaving?” Sam asked.

“We’re leaving. Don’t get all sentimental on us now,” Bucky said.

“If you ever need us, Sam, you know how to reach me,” Steve said.

“Yeah, I know, Steve. Same goes for you—don’t be a stranger,” Sam said.

Bucky flicked the unlit cigarette into the gravel and opened the car door without looking up. Steve glanced at Sam one last time, his jaw tightening like he had something else to say but swallowed it down. The engine coughed to life, breaking the heavy quiet.

“You better not forget it, Wilson,” Bucky said.

“Take care, Sam,” Steve said.

“You too. Drive safe, both of you,” Sam said.

“We’ll try. No promises with him driving,” Bucky said.

The car door slammed shut, and Steve stepped around to the driver's side without a glance back. The tires crunched over the gravel as the car eased into reverse before the engine groaned into a low hum. Sam stood there, the warmth of the day fading off his skin, watching the taillights disappear down the road.

“...We’ll see you soon,” Steve said.

“Yeah, I’ll be here,” Sam said.

“Stay out of trouble, Wilson. Or don’t—I’m not your babysitter,” Bucky said.

“You’ll miss him babysitting you, though,” Steve said.

The faint red glow of the taillights flickered as the car vanished around the bend. Sam exhaled sharply through his nose, shaking his head like it might rattle loose the weight sitting in his chest. He turned back toward the house, gravel crunching under each deliberate step.

“Guess I’ve got the whole night to do absolutely nothing..,” Sam said.

“Don’t burn the place down while you’re at it,” Bucky said.

The screen door creaked as Sam pulled it open, the muffled shuffle of his boots cutting short on the wooden threshold. For a moment, he lingered there, one hand on the handle, eyes fixed on the dim living room beyond. The smell of the bayou filtered in, thick and heavy, before the door swung shut behind him with a dull thud.
