Episode 1: Tuesday at the Mall

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson hatch a plan to keep Peter Parker from snapping, with escalating schemes involving MJ and Johnny Storm.

* * *

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Felicia's martini glass glints under the low amber glow, olive skewered and untouched. Wade’s water glass holds condensation, a prop of the act—he’s six shots in, no chaser. The plates between them: one half-eaten avocado egg roll, one cheesecake slice melting in its crisscross of strawberry syrup. She leans back, feline and languid, the curl of her lip sharp as the name she just dropped.

“Oh, darling, if I actually took a swing, you'd know it. That? That was just teasing. But if you're feeling wounded, Spidey, maybe it's just because the truth has claws,” Felicia said.

“Clawed? Oof, kitten, your Peter putdown stung worse than the time I accidentally manscaped with a katana. But c'mon, can I really blame you? Anyone who’s done cardio on that playground can’t resist throwing a dart or twelve. Still… maybe I’m just jealous. Sharing webs ain’t my kink. Yet,” Wade said.

“He's in, of course, for the chaos and the cocktails. And Peter's... unique brand of tension. Let's start stirring the pot before Wade drinks all the tequila himself,” Felicia said.

“Forward, huh? Felicia, you know I love a gal with a plan, but are we talking ‘let’s rob the Louvre’ forward or ‘let’s tequila blackout and wake up married in Madripoor’ forward? Either way, I’m hard. Focused! I meant focused,” Wade said.

“So, Wade, are we luring him in or letting him trip over his own boy-scout bravado? You know he’s already halfway tangled in the web we haven’t even spun yet. Give me a thread to pull, sugar,” Felicia said.

“Alright, fine, I’ll be the grown-up here—shocking, I know. You scratch, I spin: we reel Parker in. But just so we’re clear, when Spidey shows up and inevitably tries to moral high ground us out of this? You’re the one telling him he’s gotten in bed with me before… figuratively. Unless we convince him otherwise. You’ve seen his ass in spandex—don’t act like you haven’t thought about it,” Wade said.

Her smile doesn’t ask—it guarantees. The fork in Wade’s hand hovers, forgotten, as her words settle like the check neither of them will pay. On this table, with its sticky streaks of something artificial, a line has been drawn. The past, the plan, and the man between them.

* * *

“Don’t tell me you’ve got the perfect brand of chaos ping-ponging around that skull of yours. Or should I say the imperfect brand? You’ve made that your whole shtick after all. What is it, Wade? Smuggle me the secret,” Felicia said.

“Damn, Felicia, you just made Peter's missionary-ass morals sound like foreplay. That's talent. I get it, though—you're in it for the chase, not the comfy Spider-sweats after. Let me know when you're ready for a bank heist with a less... conflicted plus-one, yeah?” Wade asked.

“Come on now, Wade, focus. Save the gallivanting grins for later. What’s the next move? You’ve got that look like you know something I don’t—spill it,” Felicia said.

“Look, Felicia, I’ve got a spider-shaped chessboard in my head, and I’m not saying I’m moving pieces, but… let’s just say, if you and I are playing Black Cat-and-Spider-Mouse, what’s stopping you from making the first claw-to-web strike? Or did you pawn all your Peter stock already?” Wade asked.

“Fine. I’m in. But if this goes sideways, you owe me—you, personally—something shiny and extravagant. Deal?” Felicia asked.

“Alright, Hardy, you’ve just officially roped yourself into a tango of spandex, webs, and bad ideas. Let’s make Peter blush so hard under that mask he fries his own web-shooters. Deal sealed,” Wade said.

Her hand, unhurried, slides across the table, fingers brushing the edge of his before she pulls back. Wade tilts his head, a grin playing at his lips, but it’s sharper now, thin and dangerous. The silence carries it—sealed in unspoken trust, sharper than any contract, heavier than the name they still do not say.

* * *

The glow of sodium-vapor lamps bathes the asphalt in jaundiced light. Felicia exhales smoke that mingles with the faint tang of gasoline and fried food still clinging to the air. Wade’s boots slap the cracked pavement, echoing as he jogs toward the wavering halo of their cab’s headlights. The night hums low with the buzz of insects and the muffled roar of an interstate, distant but insistent.

“Tequila first, philosophy second. Right now though, Vanessa's blush-inducing moan echoes in my head like a hot summer day at a nun convention,” Wade said.

“Catnip, you look like you're auditioning for a noir film. Smoke curling, tense shoulders, brooding like Peter Parker owes you more shirtless time. Care for a knight in dented spandex?” Felicia asked.

The cherry of her cigarette flares, lighting the curve of her smirk. She shifts her weight the smallest fraction toward him, just enough to let the chaos breathe. The cab engine sputters against the steady thrum of fluorescent light overhead.

“Look, Felicia, we can dance 'round like Spider-Man's our collective after-dark hobby, or we nail down if he's our Hail Mary tonight. Trust me, nothing screams 'influence the neighborhood genius' like showing up with tequila, unresolved tension, and this stunning mug,” Wade said.

“Alright, we’re talking spider-trap deluxe. How ’bout I show off the full circus act—death-defying stunts, tasteful explosions, maybe even a heartfelt haiku about his abs. Flashy’s my middle name, right after ‘questionable.’ Let’s catch ourselves a web-head,” Felicia said.

The decision sharpens in her eyes, a glint Wade mirrors with exaggerated flourish. The cab lurches over a speed bump, their laughter trailing behind like exhaust. The night stretches before them, unpredictable and unafraid.

“Showtime, Kitty. What's it gonna be—bait the bug-boy, call in backup, or let me set these fireworks off solo?” Wade asked.

“We escalate. Make him sweat, make the mask itch. If he’s going to run this close to the edge, let’s see how far off we can tip him,” Felicia said.

Her nails tap against the phone in her lap, a quiet metronome to the storm she's about to unleash. Wade’s grin splits wide, feral, as he leans toward the driver, urging more speed. The tires bite the blacktop harder now, the night bending to meet them.

* * *

“Boy-next-door charm, Wade? Sure, if the boy next door swings through windows and leaves you wondering if he’s coming back. Charm, though? Oh, he's got it in spades… when he lets himself have it. Not exactly a habit of his. Speaking of habits, you're a real comedian for someone who answers life's big questions with explosives and spandex. What's your plan for this, exactly? Gonna leave a calling card with glitter and hope Peter laughs himself into forgiving us?” Felicia asked.

“Look, planning with Peter? That’s like choreographing a ballet when your lead keeps forgetting they can stick to walls and spontaneously flip out over a moral dilemma halfway through. My instinct? Follow his lead when his heart pops up on the GPS, and then dive in, headfirst, ass last, fixing the disaster he doesn't even know he's making yet,” Wade said.

“Reckless but adaptive is great when you're dodging bullets, Wade—not when you're in the middle of a chess game you don't even know the pieces for. 'Trusting Peter’s disasters’ works just fine until his disaster becomes collateral you can’t stomach. You want adaptive? Let’s adapt to not needing a disaster. Control the tempo. Control him. Control this. Or bring in someone who can,” Felicia said.

“Oh, sure, Felicia, let’s preemptively dominate Spidey. I’ll just tail him, plant a listening device in his mask, and override his critical decision-making with the magic of tequila breath and my mutant sex appeal. Yeah, that won’t backfire or end with his web shooters jammed up someplace the sun don’t shine. You wanna control Peter? Sweetheart, you either marry him or let him fail forward. There’s no middle ground,” Wade said.

“Gamble all you want, Wade. Just remember who smiles when the house wins. We need Mary-Jane. She knows him better than anyone alive who’s not wearing a mask. And if you really want the odds to shift, someone like her changes the game. Call it…” Felicia said.

“Mary-Jane? Oh, sure, the Mary-Jane Watson motivational lifeline special. Nothing says ‘sustainable hero’ like tethering Peter to red-headed guilt, breathtaking cheekbones, and the walking source of half his baggage. Fine, though—if you wanna, better be ready for the ‘save Mary-Jane or save the city’ script to kick in by hour two. I’m game, but only if we at least try to bribe her with something strong enough to keep her unflinching when Parker does his ‘noble idiot’ routine. Hair product endorsements? Private island getaway? Beyoncé tickets stapled to a Stark-funded invisibility belt? Aim higher. Or, y’know, deeper,” Wade said.

“Fine, time to upend the table—Peter’s walking into this circus whether he wants to or not. Nobody sidesteps the jalapeño popper gambit,” Wade said.

“I'm sorry, did Deadpool hack your brain mid-sentence, or is that gibberish your way of saying, 'This better not blow up in our faces'? Because if it is, trust me, I'm already planning for the shrapnel,” Felicia said.

“You want me to decode that, or are you just short-circuiting because now you’re realizing contingency means more than extra bullets? Fine. Third agent’s Mary-Jane, she’s the orbit, she’s tangible, and yes, she’s the one who can counter you or him when the web gets too tangled. Or were you suggesting we gamble her off the board too? Because last I checked, even turbid waters have currents—and my job’s riding them better than anyone else can,” Peter said.

“Oh, honey, you’re throwing word grenades again. I’d tell you to breathe, but your chaos is part of the charm. You want deliberate? Then let’s make it count. Strong reboot means we don’t invite Charlie blind—we set the terms before the reboot even hits. No loose edges, no ambient wild cards. I’m not tying this pivot in with a bow until I’m sure the knot holds,” Wade said.

“You’re late, Parker. Don’t worry—we saved you the emotional support tequila shot. Or Felicia did; she’s better at holding onto things,” Wade said.

“Post-Watson, pre-disaster, and tidal enough to drown if we leave it sloppy. I get it. If we bring Watson in early and she’s glass-clear, it either locks Peter to traction or cracks the pressure entirely. Re-cohesion or fracture, Wade. That’s the deal. We don’t withdraw after—you think about the pivot point before you ever lay the ladder down. So, do we sketch this clean or let the tide decide? Because I don’t gamble on 'transparent',” Felicia said.

“Peter, we’re putting Mary-Jane at the tip of the spear on this one. You ready to let her drive, or am I gonna need to play motivational speaker first? And don’t act surprised—you knew this was coming,” Wade said.

* * *

“Wade, you are a walking HR complaint…but thanks for reminding us all of your talent for lowering the tone, as always. Peter’s going red faster than his suit—I’d ask if it’s from blood pressure or shame, but I think we all know the answer,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, you're a goddamn Mona Lisa in black leather — can't tell if you're stealing hearts, web fluid, or just Peter's last nerve. Enjoy the show, but remember, Parker's medical drama is free-range anxiety with a side of guilt. Guy's gonna self-diagnose 'emotional trauma' by the time you toss that shooter back,” Wade said.

“Felicia, I'm coming for the web-shooter. And Wade—highlight this—you better not try to serenade with clowns while I handle her. Or is that your next trick?” Peter asked.

“Well, Peter, if you’re going to speak up, make it good. You’ve got my full attention—or maybe it’s just your little gadget here,” Felicia said.

Peter’s body tightens at the shoulders first. The tell. Felicia sees it, doesn’t stop twirling the shooter. Wade sees it and takes another pull. The beer foam collects at the uneven seam of his lip. Three people, one silence, weighted different for each.

“Okay, okay, park it, everyone—Modelo's here, and I'm officially knighted as Sir Distraction of the Awkward Realm. Nothing like beer and questionable swordsmanship to make silence feel optional. Felicia, Spidey, your line—I’m just the opener,” Wade said.

“Peter, don’t act like you don’t love a little tension. It’s half the reason you keep getting tangled up with me,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, I’m catching you. Because upgrades aren’t upgrades if they involve me losing traction mid-swing. Wade, you flex in her corner, and you're both eating web for dinner,” Peter said.

“Wade, your boy’s got some fire tonight. Care to fan the flames a little?” Felicia asked.

Felicia lets the web-shooter drop into her palm, her thumb brushing the trigger, pointed down but delicate in a way that says it doesn’t have to stay that way. Wade wipes his mouth with the back of his gloved hand, motion slow and deliberate, like he’s daring Peter to find his nerve. Parker’s fists curl, knuckles white, but his next move hangs in the air, suspended, charged, waiting to fall.

“Here, Spidey—Modelo diplomacy, Wade style. You sip, I quip, and Felicia flips that moral code of yours like it’s foreplay gymnastics. Your serve, Tiger—don’t leave a girl and…well, another girl hangin’,” Wade said.

“Felicia, you talk a big game, but here’s the part where I reclaim mine. Wade, play referee—or don’t—but keep your shirt on this time,” Peter said.

“Alright, Spider. You’ve got my attention. What’s your next move?” Felicia asked.

* * *

“Sure, tiger. And my morning was uninterrupted meditation. Spill it — what’s actually got you wound tighter than J. Jonah’s tie?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, sweetheart, you know me — I obnoxious-clown-car my way through everything. Undercutting is practically a love language,” Wade said.

“Oh, God. Wade. Alright, look—when he starts in on his circus act, you’ve gotta make a choice: keep up, or let him collapse under his own weight. It’s physics, but with, like, a lot more innuendo,” Peter said.

“You didn't come here just to exchange banter with me, Peter. What's the real reason you're standing in front of me instead of swinging through Queens with all that existential weight you're pretending you don't feel?” Felicia asked.

“Felicia, you really have a talent for making a guy feel like the floor just disappeared from under him. Uh. Okay. Tension—I’m just gonna use my words like an adult. Yeah, let’s go with that. Let's try honesty,” Peter said.

“Genuine, earnestly unsealed Peter Parker? Oh, that’s such a precious vintage. Real rare. Delicate bouquet of guilt, responsibility, and that heroic darkness so deep you can practically taste the Daredevil tears it was aged in,” Wade said.

“Wade snapping reality back into complexity? Well, that’s basically his default setting, isn’t it? It’s like he keeps a stash of complexity grenades and just rolls them into every conversation he can. But alright, focus,” Peter said.

“Alright, Peter. You want direct? Fine. You're here because some part of you can't resist seeing how I've been. Maybe wondering if I'm still tangled in your web, or if I've moved on to bigger, shinier things. Or maybe, just maybe, you miss the part of yourself you're only brave enough to show me. Am I wrong?” Felicia asked.

“You don’t get to stall out here. Whatever gear you're stuck in, shift it. I'm not running this show just to watch the wheels spin,” Felicia said.

“Clarity. Yeah, of course she wants clarity,” Peter said.

“Oh, no. He went there, didn't he? Dammit, Parker. Making me look at the feels shelf when I was just about to rearrange the chaos display. That’s dirty pool,” Wade said.

“Your move, Spider. But don’t think too hard—wouldn’t want you pulling a muscle. Sometimes instinct answers better than all that Parker overthinking,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, you don’t fight fair. Wade’s already cracking my ribs with his careless ‘subtext sledgehammer,’ and now you’re poking the bruise like you know exactly what you’re digging for. Damn it, of course, you do,” Peter said.

“Where’s my stake, Peter? With the mask, with the man, or with the myth we can’t seem to quit? Maybe it’s all three. Or maybe I just like keeping you close because you remind me there’s still one thing I can’t quite steal — your trust,” Felicia said.

“Trust, huh? Wow, that's some premium catnip coming out of her mouth. The kind of word that makes you wanna slow down and check your pulse...if your pulse didn't sound like a light saber fight in a blender most days,” Wade said.

* * *

Felicia's glossed nails drum the sticky faux-marble tabletop, each tap sharp against Peter's nervous swirl of a paper napkin. Wade picks at the rim of his half-finished piña colada, its maraschino cherry long gone, replaced by residue of the day's sarcasm and syrup. The booth lighting bleeds too much yellow, catching on Felicia's earrings and the faint bruise at Wade’s jawline, both unmentioned. It is Tuesday, and it feels like it—neither the beginning nor the end of anything, only the weight of what lingers in between.

“Hey, Peter—sip, don't shred. We've got enough wreckage around without the paper casualties,” Wade said.

“You know, Wade, I was this close to handing it over to you. Could’ve been your mask liner. Thought I'd save you the trouble of shredding yourself this time,” Peter said.

“Did he now? Classic Parker. Humor’s his comfort blanket—soft, cozy, and utterly transparent. But you know, subtlety’s a hell of an art, and sometimes Peter spreads his jokes on with a trowel. Still, it works. Mostly. For him,” Felicia said.

“Alright, Felicia, if you're gonna activate the Noir Femme Fatale cheat code, just warn a guy, huh? Some of us got hearts that skip beats for more than cardio,” Wade said.

The air carries the smell of fryer oil and overly sweetened buttercream, settling like a second skin. Felicia shifts her leg, her boot brushing Peter's ankle under the table—deliberate or not, he doesn't know. Wade flicks an ice cube across his plate with his fork, the scrape just loud enough to punctuate the silence. Three people, tethered by history, stranded in a moment too small for it.

“So, uh, Felicia... is that your version of a handshake, or are you testing my spider reflexes?” Peter asked.

“Oh, he’s cute when he tries to play nonchalant, isn’t he? Poor thing doesn’t know what to do with an ankle in his orbit, let alone mine,” Felicia said.

“Looks like Felicia's turning this into a three-way... of mischief. Your move, Spidey. Show us if Parker charm works under friendly fire,” Wade said.

“Oh sure, Wade, let’s all pick on the guy who’s the human version of a rubber band ball right now. Keep it up, maybe next I’ll juggle flaming swords while solving calculus problems,” Peter said.

The hum of a distant milkshake blender fills the pause, rising and falling like the breath Peter’s trying to steady. Felicia leans back, smirking behind a sip of lukewarm sangria, her eyes flicking between the two men. Wade slumps deeper into the cracked faux-leather, one boot heel hooked on the edge of the table, a grin twitching at the corner of his mouth—waiting for the next spark to catch.

“Boys, boys. All this fuss over a little ankle—flattered, really. But if we keep this dance going, someone’s bound to trip, and we wouldn’t want that, now would we?” Felicia asked.

“Touché, Felicia. Your tongue’s got claws, but my liver’s got grit. Let’s see who blinks,” Wade said.

The booth holds its shape, but the edges have frayed. Felicia’s eyeliner doesn’t flinch as she stares Wade down, her fingers teasing the stem of her glass. Peter breaks the moment, scratching at a ketchup smear on the menu, the silence pressing harder than any one of them will acknowledge. The Cheesecake Factory hums on, unapologetically indifferent.

* * *

“Well, boys, if you're trying to out-brood each other, you've got competition. But my vote's on the vodka. And before either of you start sulking about that, let’s make a toast—here’s to bad decisions and good exits. Now, drink up while I still find you interesting,” Felicia said.

“Oh, it better not be that swill from the bar last Friday, with the name that sounded like a Victoria’s Secret perfume and tasted like someone blended a Hallmark movie. If it’s whiskey, I’m listening. If it’s tequila, I’m marrying the bottle. Now, what’s the toast about? Something scandalous? Or were you just buttering me up to get me undressed without the usual dinner-and-bloodshed foreplay? Because I’m very into efficiency,” Wade said.

“Wade, your version of foreplay is a lot like getting hit repeatedly with a bag of wet cats. What are we even doing right now?” Peter asked.

Felicia crosses one leg over the other, the hem of her dress sliding higher, deliberate. Wade’s mask is pulled up just enough to drink, but his eyes linger too long, too loud. Peter’s fingers drum against the table in an uneven rhythm, his knee bouncing under it. The overhead lighting flickers once, enough to shadow their faces.

“You two are adorable. Wade, you’re practically undressing him with your eyes—try to pace yourself. And Peter—if you fidget any harder, you're going to spill whatever that is you're pretending to drink. Let’s skip the foreplay. What’s the real game here? Or do I have to start calling the shots? Because trust me, I will,” Felicia said.

“Ah, the cat’s got her claws and our balls in her grip tonight. I see you, Felicia. Question is, are you poking the spider to see him blush or watching me juggle his 'I'm too responsible for this' squirm? Because if it’s the second one—babe, you’re gonna need better popcorn,” Wade said.

“Wade, if I wanted a self-inflicted guilt trip, I'd just revisit absolutely every decision I've ever made. You got cliff notes for this, or are we doing the full box set?” Peter asked.

The cheesecake knife glints as Wade twirls it between his fingers. Felicia leans in, close enough that Peter catches the scent of her perfume—dark cherries, knife-edged sweetness. Peter, jaw tight, stares at the condensation pooling beneath his untouched water glass, letting it drip into the silence.

“Mmm, Wade, I hope you’re not planning to ruin my evening with that blade — that’s a dinner party faux pas. And Peter, sweetheart, you keep sniffing like I’ve laced the air with a confession. It’s jasmine, bergamot, and just enough venom to keep people guessing. Speaking of which—what exactly am I walking into here? Because I could just as easily walk out,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felicia, my heart, my liver’s dealer of choice—I’d never deny your right to. Now, let's unpack that venom you just delicately sprayed on the air, shall we? Peter, unless you want our feline enigma over there writing your fate in claw marks, I'd suggest treating her less like a subplot and more like the main event,” Wade said.

“Felicia, you planning to claw my face off before or after Wade gets himself shot in the ass again? I'd love to pencil that into my already stellar evening,” Peter said.

The laughter from another booth feels miles away. Felicia's nails tap the rim of her empty glass. Wade flips the knife once more and slips it into his sleeve. Peter finally looks up, but no one moves to fill the silence. The night, like the cheesecake, curdles.

* * *

The vinyl of the booth shines under overhead lighting that’s too bright and too soft all at once, trying to echo evening, failing. A lime wedge floats half-dissolved in Wade’s tequila soda. Felicia’s arm stretches across the table, her nails clicking a rhythmic impatience against the highball glass she’s been nursing. Across from her, Peter’s hand hovers near a plate of cold fries, the ketchup congealed and abandoned. The air smells of fryer oil and something vanilla-sweet from the dessert case fifteen feet away. None of them looks at the cheesecake menu anymore.

“You know, you could stretch this tension over a crossbow. But hey, if no one wants to play icebreaker, I’ll start. Anyone else sleep with someone they shouldn’t have last month? Or is that just me keeping the bar here entertaining?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, a pointed comment! Felicia, darling, you wound me. Did it involve claws and a promise I couldn't keep—or were you just testing which of my body parts grows back the quickest? Go on, don’t keep me in suspense,” Wade said.

“All right, Wade, let’s hear it. What deeply uncomfortable, wildly inappropriate ‘compliment’ about my spandex were you dying to get out of your system today?” Peter asked.

Felicia shifts, her thigh brushing Wade's under the table—deliberate, not careless. He doesn’t flinch, but the corner of his mouth pulls upward, just barely. Peter catches it. His hand lands in the fries, mechanical, and stills there. The room hums faintly with a distant dishwasher, the scrape of cutlery, the kind of laughter that doesn’t reach anyone's eyes.

“Well, look at that, I shifted the tectonic plates of this riveting evening. Careful, darlings, I wouldn’t want the world to split in two. But really, what’s the use of sitting in silence when we could talk about something—or someone—more... gripping?” Felicia asked.

“Tension? Oh, do I have to break out the matching leotards and choreographed dance-off to ease the mood? Or should I just mention the time we almost stole that nuclear sub together—you know, for the aesthetic. No tension there, just a couple of morally-gray adrenaline junkies with great taste in…” Wade said.

“Oh, God. Wade, if this is about that time in Madripoor with the boat, the goat, and the exploding chimichangas, I’m begging you—don’t finish the story. For everyone’s sake,” Peter said.

The space between them tightens, invisible cords pulling taut. Felicia leans back, her smile like glass—fragile, reflecting. Wade taps his glass once against the table, ice shifting, a beat unsaid. Peter’s shoulders inch forward, curled in tension or curiosity, maybe both. The air changes, quieter somehow, though the din of the restaurant hasn’t ebbed.

“Is it that obvious? Fine. I’ll admit—there’s a certain thrill in seeing how far I can push before someone snaps. Or slips. Did it hit the target? Or should I... recalibrate?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, you aiming at me, kitty-cat? Bold move, considering my charm is an armor-piercing round, and I haven’t met a line I wouldn’t cross—or trip over trying. But by all means, claw away. Just know, I bite back,” Wade said.

“You know what, Wade? Fine. I’ll bite. But if you’re steering this toward a ‘Spider butt joke’ again, we're done—'cause, FYI, it wasn’t clever the first time, and yes, I am still mad about the meme,” Peter said.

The table holds them: scratched laminate masquerading as wood, sticky in spots where a rag skimmed rather than cleaned. Three glasses form uneven triangles between them, ice melting in each. The half-eaten breadbasket leans on its side, crumbs clinging to balled napkins. It’s a neutral zone in theory. Nothing about the space suggests the tension building just above it.

“The room deserves a proper spark, don’t you think? So, who’s got a secret they’re dying to spill—and how much fun is it going to be if I pull it out myself?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, so that’s how it's gonna be? Fine. Consider that gauntlet picked up, polished, and repurposed as a ball gag for your next weekend adventure. Just remember, when you throw down at me, Felicia, I don't duck—I curtsy,” Wade said.

“Wade, focus. Reel it in, man. You’ve got range, I’ll give you that, but could we put a pin in the stream-of-consciousness fever dream act for five seconds, just once? I'd kill for actual coherence right now—but only metaphorically, okay? Don’t get any ideas,” Peter said.

Felicia’s eyes harden first, the shift sharp and practiced, slicing past Peter’s hesitation. Wade’s gaze follows, less surgical, more like a spotlight swinging wide. For a moment, the focus is on him, the burn of it landing heavy despite his grin. Peter stays frozen, that reach—physical, emotional—left dangling over the fries. The booth itself seems to tighten around the silence.

“The game’s quiet, but the stakes aren’t, not with the way I look at them now,” Felicia said.

“Steady as a drunk with a broken leg on a tightrope, sweetheart. I’m not budging. You’re not rattling this cage, no matter how much you purr. If you’re gunning for the soft bits, you’re gonna have to dig through a whole lotta bullshit—layers and layers of it—to even scratch the surface. So go ahead—poke, prod, claw—the party’s just getting started,” Wade said.

“You know what, Wade? You're like an emotional landmine buried in clown shoes. I never know if I’m about to get a laugh, a compliment, or an existential crisis. So, sure, I’ll bite this time—hit me. What’s next in the Deadpool Saga of Bizarre and Questionable Memories?” Peter asked.

“Oh, reentry? You want hot or explosive? Because, honey, I’ve got both, and that’s just my side hustle. Let’s steer this spaceship right past coherence, crank the gravity, and aim for a collision course with fuck it all because this pot needs stirring. I’ve got a PhD in chaos and a minor in 'Don’t Test Me, Princess,' so you wanna keep this lick of tension, or shall I peel off the mask and really show you the art of overshooting an emotional landing?” Wade asked.

Wade tilts his head, just slightly, the grin holding but dimmer now, edges worn thin. His fingers drum against the glass, the motion too quick, betraying a slip in control. There’s a flicker in his eyes—something raw, something unscripted—gone before it can settle. When he looks back at Peter, it's not a spotlight anymore. It's a mirror.

“Okay, Wade, let's get real for a sec—behind all the schtick and chaos, is there a point you're actually trying to aim at here? Or do we just keep smashing mirrors for fun? 'Cause, honestly, man, I'm running low on glue,” Peter said.

“Break yourselves, darlings. The real fun’s in watching who tries to piece it back together first,” Felicia said.
