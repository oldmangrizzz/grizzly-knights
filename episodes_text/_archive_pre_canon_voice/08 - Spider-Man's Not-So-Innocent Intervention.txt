Grizzly Knights

Episode 8: Spider-Man's Not-So-Innocent Intervention

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson conspire to help Peter Parker unwind, enlisting Mary Jane Watson and Johnny Storm into their mischief.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the laughter bursts again, when the room settles briefly. Wade's breadstick fencing is a blur of crumbs and swagger, Felicia’s fingers linger on the rim of her glass, twisting idly, her smirk sharp and pointed. Across the table, Peter leans back stiffly, his jaw set, a half-eaten plate of sliders cooling by his elbow; MJ watches him instead of joining in, her eyes narrowing by degrees.

“Oh, come on, Tiger—don’t act like you haven’t secretly dreamed of Wade’s breadstick duel school. It’s got that whole reckless charm you pretend you’re above,” Felicia said.

“Reckless charm? Felicia, please. I’m the breadstick Jedi Peter never knew he needed. Feel the gluten, Parker—feel it,” Wade said.

“Yeah, I’m feeling something. Mostly the profound urge to fling that breadstick into a galaxy far, far away,” Peter said.

“Easy there, hero. Let the man have his moment with carbs—God knows it’s safer than most of his hobbies. You, on the other hand, might wanna find the part of this table that isn’t clenched,” Mary said.

The laughter spills out unevenly, tangling with the ambient hum of the restaurant as Felicia tips her head back, her silver hair catching the gauzy afternoon light filtering through the blinds. Wade salutes Peter with the remnants of his weaponized breadstick, snapping it in two with a theatrical flair before tossing the pieces onto a dessert plate already crowded with crumbs. Peter shifts in his seat, pushing his plate a few inches forward, his fingers drumming once against the edge of the table before receding into his lap.

“Relax, Pete—at least Wade’s not trying to lightsaber your conscience. Let the man save the galaxy, one breadstick at a time,” Johnny said.

“You know, Johnny, Wade might just be the hero this galaxy deserves. Spider-Bread and his trusty carb sidekick—making the universe just ridiculous enough to survive,” Felicia said.

“Finally, someone gets it! Spider-Bread, the yeast we knead but don’t deserve. Peter, buddy, you can be my starchy sidekick—Stale-Man! Saving the world, one glutenous pun at a time,” Wade said.

“If I’m Stale-Man, does that make you the Day-Old Delinquent? ‘Cause trust me, Wade, nobody’s kneading you—except maybe the breadstick,” Peter said.

The table vibrates with another round of laughter, Felicia's nails tapping lightly on the stem of her martini glass as she leans forward, eyes gleaming with mischief. Wade gestures grandly, nearly toppling the last intact breadstick from the basket, his grin wide enough to flash the faint scar at the edge of his cheek. Peter’s lips twitch—not quite a smile, but close enough—and MJ’s hand slides across the table, casually reclaiming her cocktail as if to punctuate the moment with the clink of glass against ice.

“Well, at least now we know you all missed your true calling—off-Broadway improv about processed carbs. Honestly, I’m just here for the drinks and the secondhand embarrassment,” Mary said.

“Secondhand embarrassment? MJ, come on, this is premium, front-row, artisanal humiliation. You don’t get this kind of comedy without the bread basket of shame,” Johnny said.

“If this is artisanal, I’m charging double for admission. Wade’s comedy and Pete’s tolerance level are rare collectibles—value increases with every eye roll,” Felicia said.

“Ah, Felicia, you flatter me. Rare collectibles? That makes me a limited-edition Deadpool with extra scar tissue—and Peter here is the eye-roll variant. Mint condition... until I inevitably ruin it,” Wade said.

A server approaches the table, hesitation measured in the careful pace of her steps, balancing a tray with the practiced ease of someone well-acquainted with chaos. She sets down another round of drinks—martinis for Felicia and Wade, something amber and neat for Johnny, and a soda Peter didn’t order but accepts with a nod that doesn’t quite reach his eyes. MJ mouths a quiet “thank you” as she hands off her empty glass, her polite smile fading the second her gaze drifts back toward their corner of the circus.

“Limited edition? Sure, Wade, right up there with the action figures nobody wants to admit they owned. Just don’t expect me to keep dusting you off,” Peter said.

“Dust him off? Please, Pete, you’re lucky he’s not turning your life into a collector’s diorama—Spider-Man vs. the Gluten Sith, now in technicolor crumbs. That soda better have a cape,” Mary said.

“If that soda’s wearing a cape, it definitely needs a sidekick—maybe a straw with a utility belt. Peter, you’re halfway to becoming the superhero of hydration at this point,” Johnny said.

“Careful, boys. If hydration gets Peter going, we might just witness him save the soda world. I’d pay good money to see Stale-Man take down Dehydration-Man,” Felicia said.

The server retreats swiftly, suppressing a smirk as Wade brandishes his empty plate like a shield, gesturing wildly as though acting out Felicia's imaginary takedown. Felicia smirks over the rim of her drink, the condensation tracing lazy curves against her fingertips before she sets it back down with a deliberate clink. Peter rubs the bridge of his nose, his sigh nearly obscured by Johnny's exaggerated laughter, but not before MJ catches it, her glance sharp, fleeting, and unspoken between them.

“Stale-Man vs. Dehydration-Man—battle of the century, brought to you by the Cheesecake Factory beverage menu. Coming soon to a sticky table near you! Drinks not included but strongly encouraged,” Wade said.

“Great, Wade, now I get to be a cautionary tale in a PSA about proper fluid intake. Truly living the dream,” Peter said.

“Well, look at you—hydration icon and reluctant role model all in one. Next stop, motivational posters in every middle school cafeteria. Aim high, Tiger,” Mary said.

“Peter Parker: From friendly neighborhood Spider-Man to the poster boy for "Drink More Water." Honestly, it’s a glow-up, man—you’re practically heroic in the war against dry mouth,” Johnny said.

The table pitches into another ripple of laughter, Wade doubling over dramatically, one hand clamped to his chest like the sheer brilliance of his joke might shatter him. Johnny raises his glass in toast—half-sincere, half-mocking—while Felicia’s lips part around a sly laugh that doesn’t quite reach her eyes. Peter stays still this time, fingers tightening on the sweating soda glass, the pressure leaving faint arcs in the moisture as he silently tracks the conversation’s unraveling with an expression that gives nothing away.

* * *

The ice in the glasses shifts with a faint clink as a fresh round is set on the table, the server retreating without much more than a glance at the chaotic orbit of Wade’s gestures. His voice fills the booth like static, relentless and sharp-edged, pulling Felicia’s half-hearted patience taut. Across from her, Peter's hands twitch once, betraying the tension beneath a façade that rarely holds this long.

“Wade, your enthusiasm is noted, but I don’t summon queens lightly—especially not to your circus,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, ‘Licia, MJ loves a good circus! She married one!” Wade said.

“Well, if that’s the case, Wade, I’m sure you’ve already got ringmaster applications pre-filled with glitter, a headshot, and whatever the hell that cologne is you’re bathing in today,” Peter said.

“I’m guessing this is the part where I find out why Felicia’s texts have more red flags than a matador convention. What the hell did I just walk into?” Mary asked.

The scrape of her chair interrupts the table’s rhythm as Mary Jane settles in, her movements sharp but calculated, the kind of poise that claims a seat without asking. The server hovers briefly, eyeing the new arrival, then retreats, sensing the intensity knotting between these faces. Felicia doesn’t look up yet, her focus fixed on her drink, the condensation slowly smearing as her fingers trace its curve.

“MJ, you just walked into Wade trying to convince us all he's the best thing to happen to the concept of subtlety. Spoiler alert: he’s not,” Johnny said.

“And yet somehow, Johnny, Wade’s lack of subtlety is the least dangerous thing in this room right now,” Felicia said.

“Dangerous? Felicia, please, I’m harmless! Unless you’re gluten or emotionally stable—then yeah, run,” Wade said.

“MJ, welcome to the circus... starring Wade as the human red flag and Felicia as the reluctant ringmaster. Popcorn’s on the way, probably,” Peter said.

The table tilts precariously toward silence for one shared breath, the laughter that had threatened now replaced by something heavier, less predictable. Wade’s grin widens, teeth too sharp for the joke, while Felicia swaps her glass for her phone, the screen lighting up her face in pale, flickering impatience. Across the table, Peter’s expression shifts just enough to suggest he’s no longer sure whether he’s the audience or the act.

“Glad to see I wasn’t called in for anything serious, like an adult conversation or basic human boundaries. So, who’s got the cliff notes on why I’m here?” Mary asked.

“Honestly? Probably Wade’s idea of matchmaking or Felicia’s idea of revenge. Either way, MJ, buckle up—it’s never boring,” Johnny said.

“Oh, Johnny, you give me far too much credit—revenge is much more direct than this little disaster,” Felicia said.

“Disaster? Felicia, sweetheart, this is a masterpiece in the making. MJ, you’re here because every circus needs an audience that can throw tomatoes and look hot doing it,” Wade said.

Mary Jane leans back in her seat, dragging her fingers along the rim of her glass, the movement deliberate, pointed, her silence louder than Wade’s theatrics. Felicia’s phone buzzes quietly against the table, the vibration cutting through the booth’s tension, ignored for now as her focus narrows. Wade’s hands sweep dramatically through the air, the motion unsettling the nearest wine glass, sending a ripple across its surface that Johnny stabilizes with an easy flick of his wrist.

“MJ, you’re here because someone thought throwing you into Wade’s improv hour was a good idea. Spoiler: it wasn’t,” Peter said.

“Someone? Peter, come on, we all know Wade hands out bad ideas like Halloween candy. I just want to know whose idea it was to sign my name on the guest list,” Mary said.

“If I had to guess, it was probably a group effort fueled by poor judgment and questionable life choices—so, you know, the usual,” Johnny said.

“MJ, you were summoned by the unholy trinity of chaos, boredom, and Wade’s persuasive powers—which, frankly, should come with a warning label,” Felicia said.

Johnny’s laugh spills out, quick and edged, the kind that cuts tension rather than dissolves it. Felicia tips her head back, almost imperceptibly, the movement speaking more exhaustion than amusement. Across the table, the corners of Peter’s mouth hitch upward into a half-smile that doesn’t quite meet his eyes, a reflex more than a reaction.

“A warning label? I am the warning label. Slap me on a box of dynamite and call it product accuracy,” Wade said.

“Wade, you’re less warning label, more disaster relief manual—except none of the pages are numbered and it’s already on fire,” Peter said.

“So, basically, I walked into a group therapy session with cocktails. Great. Someone just tell me if I need to referee, or if this is gonna implode on its own,” Mary said.

“MJ, it’s Wade. Implosion’s not an “if,” it’s a “when” — might as well enjoy the fireworks while they last.” Johnny said.

The shadows of their movements ripple across the table as the late afternoon sunlight filters through half-drawn blinds, warming the room just enough to contrast the chill of the air conditioning. Felicia finally lifts her drink, her fingers wrapped loosely around the glass like she might set it down just as quickly. Wade leans forward into the pause, elbows sprawling, filling the space with his restless energy while MJ holds her ground, unmoved, her gaze steady and unflinching.

* * *

There is a moment, just before Mary Jane slides into the booth, where the air seems to shift—not from any sudden movement, but from the weight of her scrutiny. Her entrance presses against the already tenuous balance; Wade’s grin widens as if to fill the silence, Felicia taps her nails against the rim of her half-empty glass, and Peter shifts, his shoulders folding inward against her stare. The cheesecake between them sits untouched, an indulgence no one has yet had the audacity to justify.

“Finally, some glamour in the room. Took you long enough, darling. You're just in time for the intervention,” Felicia said.

“Intervention? Oh, is this where we tell Peter he's got a stick so far up his ass he's basically a human popsicle? Because I brought charts. And tequila,” Wade said.

“Oh great, an intervention. Just what I needed—can I at least get my own tequila before you start the PowerPoint?” Peter asked.

“If this is an intervention, Wade, I’m gonna need someone to explain why it looks like happy hour at a TGI Fridays,” Mary said.

The booth groans faintly beneath the slight shifts of weight, the leather creaking as Wade throws an arm over the back, his body loose, practically spilling into the aisle. Felicia, poised like a cat circling its prey, leans so slightly forward that her hair brushes the rim of her glass, while Peter glances at the untouched cheesecake, his jaw tightening with each passing second. Mary Jane, still half-twisted toward the table, trails a finger along the edge of the laminated menu, her gaze darting between their theatrics, the faintest shadow of a smirk betraying her resolve.

“Did somebody say tequila? Because I’m pretty sure that’s my cue—someone pass me a shot and the intervention script, I’ll light it on fire for dramatic effect,” Johnny said.

“Johnny, darling, you're late—but don't worry, the tequila’s warm and the chaos is seasoned. Just grab a seat before Parker combusts under the weight of his own discomfort,” Felicia said.

“Warm tequila? Felicia, that's practically a hate crime. Johnny, you better sit fast—Parker over there's one eyebrow twitch away from turning into a public service announcement,” Wade said.

“I’m fine, really. No twitching, no combusting—just quietly questioning how I got roped into a meeting of the Dysfunctional Avengers,” Peter said.

Jonathan slides into the booth with a practiced ease that makes the cramped corner feel even tighter, his cologne sharp enough to cut through the fried sweetness lingering in the air. Wade claps him on the back in exaggerated welcome, the sound cracking louder than it should in the din of halfhearted restaurant conversation. The table jolts slightly as Johnny’s knees knock against its underside, sending the silverware trembling—a fleeting, metallic echo that dissipates as Felicia’s gaze sharpens on Peter, her lips curving into something that can’t decide if it’s a smirk or a dare.

“Careful, Johnny, you’re gonna knock over the cheesecake—and trust me, this table doesn’t look stable enough to survive that kind of betrayal,” Mary said.

“Hey, if this table goes, I’ll just torch the evidence and call it modern art. Chaos likes a little flair, MJ,” Johnny said.

“And chaos with flair is exactly why I invited you, Johnny. Now, let’s see if we can’t loosen Peter up before he audits our fun to death,” Felicia said.

“Loosen him up? Oh honey, don’t threaten me with a good time. I’ve got a list of icebreakers that starts with embarrassing puberty stories and ends with tequila body shots,” Wade said.

The laughter that ripples out from Wade’s declaration is uneven, a patchwork of genuine amusement and deeply buried apprehension. Felicia swirls the last of her drink with a lazy precision, her eyes darting to Peter like a predator measuring its next move. Johnny reclines just enough to push the boundaries of the booth’s narrow space, his arm stretched behind MJ’s seat as if he’s already claimed his corner of mischief. Across from him, Peter’s hands inch toward the abandoned cutlery, not out of hunger, but for lack of any other anchor. The cheesecake remains untouched, its perfect surface taunting the room with a restraint none of them possess.

“Okay, first off, if there’s a tequila body shot list happening here, I’m vetoing any entry that involves me. Second—are we seriously letting the cheesecake win this standoff?” Peter asked.

“Cheesecake standoffs are serious business, Tiger. But I’m more concerned about which one of you thought body shots were the natural follow-up here,” Mary said.

“Natural follow-up? MJ, body shots are basically the tequila of desserts—sweet, messy, and guaranteed to leave a story,” Johnny said.

“Well, if we're talking stories, I guess it’s time to see who’s brave enough to pick up a fork—or a shot glass. Let’s not pretend we’re here for moderation, darlings,” Felicia said.

The light catches on the condensation of Felicia’s glass as she pushes it toward the center of the table, the movement deliberate, inviting. Wade drums his fingers in a syncopated rhythm against the edge of his menu, feigning distraction but already leaning into the momentum of their chaos. Johnny plucks a fork from the trembling pile between them, twirling it like a baton, his grin widening with a theatrical lack of shame. Across from him, Peter exhales sharply, his shoulders lifting in a sigh that fails to mask the way one corner of his mouth twitches—whether in frustration or reluctant amusement, no one remarks. MJ watches them all, her gaze heavy with the kind of wearied curiosity that comes only from knowing exactly how this will end and staying anyway.

“Alright, here’s the deal—winner of the cheesecake standoff gets to pick who does the first tequila body shot. And yes, Parker, that includes you, so pray to whatever radioactive spider god you believe in,” Wade said.

“If this ends with me and tequila, I think the spider god already smote me. Can we at least agree the cheesecake goes first? Priorities,” Peter said.

“If the spider god’s smiting you, Pete, at least let him wait until after dessert. Cheesecake deserves better than being our collateral damage,” Mary said.

“Then let’s put it out of its misery—someone grab a fork, or I’m claiming the first bite and the tequila pick. Spoiler alert, it won’t be gentle,” Johnny said.

The fork in Johnny’s hand hovers for a moment, its polished tines catching the dim light, before he angles it toward the cheesecake’s pristine surface with exaggerated menace. Wade tips further into his side of the booth, near enough to get a better view and close enough to provoke, his energy brimming on the edge of physicality. Felicia doesn’t move, her posture unshaken, but her eyes flick to Peter again, watching the tightening of his jaw as Johnny’s fork plunges down, shattering the dessert’s unspoiled facade with a thick, deliberate drag. The sound of matted graham cracker crust breaking fills the millisecond of silence—a pause, sharp and deafening only to those who already regret their company.

* * *

There is a moment of loaded stillness as Johnny leans back, the fork now between his teeth, a lazy grin painting his face. Wade reaches across the table to steal a jagged piece of graham crust, his fingers brushing the plate’s edge, unapologetically invading the space. Felicia tilts her head ever so slightly, her arms crossed, the gleam in her eye catching under the booth’s muted overhead light; her stillness is not made of annoyance but calculation.

“Well, Johnny, if you can seduce that cheesecake any harder, they’ll need to dim the lights,” Felicia said.

“Ooooooh, Felicia, you trying to make me jealous or is this foreplay? 'Cause if Johnny starts moaning, I’m gonna need a safe word—and another fork,” Wade said.

“I’m pretty sure "awkward" is the safe word here, Wade. And for the record, I came for cheesecake, not the dinner theater production of 'Innuendo: The Musical,” Peter said.

“Well, isn’t this cozy—when does the intervention part kick in, or are we just buttering him up with dessert first?” Mary asked.

Johnny shifts in his seat with the deliberate nonchalance of someone elected king of the moment, tapping his fork lightly against the rim of the plate as if orchestrating the room’s tension. Felicia’s nails drum once on the table, then stop, her focus settling on Peter with the sharpened gravity of a loaded weapon. Wade, now leaning fully over the center of the table, cradles his head in his hand and watches with an unnerving amount of glee, eyes darting between each player like he knows the punchline is just one more spark away.

“Relax, MJ, I’m just setting the mood—I didn't realize cheesecake came with a side of tension so thick you could spread it like crème brûlée,” Johnny said.

“Funny, I thought you’d like it thick, Johnny. Keeps things... interesting,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I see what’s happening here—everyone’s trying to out-saucy the king of inappropriate, but don’t worry, kiddos, Wade’s still got the crown. Speaking of crowns, Peter, you wanna explain why yours is made of unresolved trauma and spider-shaped guilt, or should we just keep pretending the cheesecake’s the problem?” Wade asked.

“Wow, Wade, subtle as ever. Pretty sure the cheesecake didn’t sign up to be in the middle of my therapy session, but thanks for making it awkward for the entire table,” Peter said.

The air shifts almost audibly as Peter sinks imperceptibly lower into his seat, his fingers tightening around the edge of his napkin like he’s debating tearing it in two. Felicia leans back now, satisfied but watchful, her appraising smile directed solely toward Peter as though daring him to blink first. Johnny gives a low whistle under his breath—too entertained to intervene, too aware of the heat building now to look away—while Mary Jane studies Peter’s profile, her expression measured, but the faint twitch at her jaw betraying something deeper stirring beneath the surface.

“You know, Pete, there’s a difference between carrying weight and burying yourself under it. Maybe it’s time you let someone else take a piece—starting with the cheesecake,” Mary said.

“Alright, Pete, MJ’s got a point—and if you don’t grab a slice soon, I’m taking your share. You don’t want me getting all heroic over dessert, do you?” Johnny asked.

“Oh, let him sit with his martyr complex a little longer, Johnny. It’s practically his plus-one,” Felicia said.

“Speaking of plus-ones, Felicia, when’s the last time you RSVP’d to "Team Let Pete Breathe"? Or are we all just here to dunk on Parker until he turns cheesecake-colored? Fun game, though. Ten bucks says he cracks before the crust does,” Wade said.

The booth feels smaller now, the edges closing in as Peter exhales sharply and finally releases his strangled grip on the napkin, leaving it crumpled and contorted on the table. Mary Jane shifts her focus to Wade, her gaze sharp and unyielding, though her hands remain folded neatly in her lap, retaining an outward calm. Felicia uncrosses her arms, leaning forward now and resting her elbows on the table, her grin spreading just slightly, hinting at something indifferent yet deliberate, her confidence unshaken by Wade’s jab.

“So this is the plan, huh? Corner Spider-Man over cheesecake and see who gets the first crack at his psyche? Great—remind me to RSVP next time so I can bring my own napkin to shred,” Peter said.

“It’s not about cracking you, Pete. It’s about reminding you that you don’t have to hold it all alone. But, hey, if sarcasm helps, keep it coming—it pairs great with guilt,” Mary said.

“Sarcasm and guilt—a Parker specialty. But seriously, Pete, you ever thought about letting someone carry the damn plate for once before the whole thing topples?” Johnny asked.

“Careful, Johnny. If you keep handing out advice like that, you might accidentally sound human,” Felicia said.

The faint rattle of silverware shifting against porcelain punctuates Johnny’s grin, his hand still lazily playing with the fork as if the stakes of the moment are too low to demand tension from him. Peter’s shoulders tighten, the back of his neck stiff and rigid, while his eyes flick toward Mary Jane for just a breath before returning to the table, his focus fixed on the half-destroyed cheesecake. Across from him, Wade’s posture shifts again—he’s half-lounging now, propped up by chaotic energy alone, watching like a man both jester and spectator, waiting for the next fracture in the conversation to exploit.

“Who needs napkins when you’ve got this much emotional shrapnel flying around? Come on, Petey, let it out—rage-sob into the cheesecake or something. We’re all dying to see what happens when you stop holding your breath, and not just because Johnny’s hogging all the oxygen,” Wade said.

“You want me to rage-sob into cheesecake, Wade? Tempting, but I think I'll leave the dramatic breakdowns to you. Spider-Man brand’s got enough baggage without me adding "dessert therapy" to the list,” Peter said.

“Oh, for god’s sake, Peter, no one here cares about your 'brand.' We care about you. But sure, deflect with cheesecake jokes. Classic move,” Mary said.

“I mean, technically, dessert therapy doesn’t sound half bad—chocolate’s basically a prescription in some places. But MJ’s right. Brand’s not at this table, Pete. You are,” Johnny said.

Peter’s gaze hardens, his jaw flexing in silent defiance as his fingers start to tap an uneven rhythm against the table’s edge. Felicia leans back again, her cool detachment intact, though the tilt of her eyebrow suggests she’s cataloging every micro-flinch of Peter’s resolve. Wade’s grin sharpens into something oddly perceptive, an almost inaudible sound escaping him—half a chuckle, half an exhale—while Mary Jane straightens in her seat, her stillness now a firm anchor against the growing undercurrent of tension weaving through the booth.

* * *

There is a moment, just before the laughter crests, where Felicia tips her glass, the last amber drops clinging stubbornly to the sides. Wade gestures wildly, his hands carving chaotic arcs through the air as Johnny leans in, his grin lacquered with the reckless shine of confidence that only youth and fire can grant. Mary Jane, posture steady and deliberate, traces the rim of her glass with one finger, her gaze anchored to Peter, whose shoulders have folded inward beneath the weight of the conversation swirling around him.

“Well, Peter, if you keep brooding like that, someone might mistake you for the moody antihero in the room. And trust me, that cape's already taken,” Felicia said.

“Sorry, did someone say cape? 'Cause mine’s in the dry-cleaner, and by "dry-cleaner," I mean the dumpster fire of my life, which is where Peter's sense of humor apparently smothered itself tonight,” Wade said.

“Maybe it just needed a break from the competition, Wade. Your jokes have a distinct flavor of "cried while writing this,” Peter said.

“Play nice, boys. This is a Cheesecake Factory, not an open mic night for damaged egos,” Mary said.

Felicia smirks into her empty glass but sets it down with a deliberate click, the sound cutting through Wade’s laugh like the flare of a match. Mary Jane’s finger stills its circuit, her attention now splitting between the grin Wade flashes and the tension Peter wears like armor. Across the table, Johnny pops an olive into his mouth with the kind of ease that belongs only to someone blissfully unaware or entirely unbothered by the jagged edges forming beneath the banter.

“Hey, Pete, if your sense of humor's on vacation, can it bring me back one of those tacky souvenirs? Like, I dunno, a mug that says "World's Okayest Spider-Man"?” Johnny asked.

“Careful, Johnny—poke the spider too hard, and you might get webbed to your seat. Though, knowing you, you'd probably enjoy the attention,” Felicia said.

“Oh, please, Felicia, Johnny's the type to ask for a selfie while webbed to the seat—spoiler alert, he'd caption it "Caught in the Thirst Trap,” Wade said.

“Thirst trap, huh? Pretty bold talk coming from the guy who dives headfirst into every metaphorical bear trap with "notice me, senpai" energy,” Peter said.

The table shifts subtly, each remark pressing invisible weights into the air between them. Wade leans forward now, elbows on the table, his grin stretched wider, almost daring Peter to push further. Felicia’s wrist rests lazily against the rim of her glass, yet her eyes gleam sharp and knowing, while Mary Jane’s hand drops to her lap, her poise a porcelain mask against the ripple of tension passing from seat to seat.

“Alright, enough with the pissing contest, boys. The waitstaff's gonna start charging us for the drama,” Mary said.

“Come on, MJ, a little drama’s good for the vibe. Keeps the Cheesecake Factory on its toes—can’t let ‘em think we’re just here for the bread,” Johnny said.

“Well, Johnny, the bread might be free, but your commentary's getting a little stale. Careful, or you’ll be the next one webbed to the table for everyone's amusement,” Felicia said.

“Oh, oh! If we web Johnny to the table, can we make it a live performance piece? I’ll narrate: "Local Hothead Becomes Modern Art, Still Somehow Manages to Flirt With Himself,” Wade said.

The server glances toward the booth from across the room, a flicker of hesitation crossing her face as she weighs the scene unfolding there. Johnny spins a knife on the table, the dull edge skittering against the wood with unpredictable momentum, while Wade mimics the gesture with his spoon, the clinking metal barely masking his delight. Peter drums his fingertips once against the surface, slow and deliberate now, eyes narrowing faintly as the corners of his mouth tighten—not a smile, not yet.

“If this turns into performance art, I’m charging admission. And Wade, you’re definitely not getting a cut,” Peter said.

“Only fair, Pete—you’d need to list Wade as a fire hazard anyway,” Mary said.

“If Wade’s a fire hazard, does that make me the extinguisher or the accelerant? Asking for the inevitable Yelp review,” Johnny said.

“Oh, Johnny, you’re definitely the accelerant. You’d burn brighter just to prove you’re the hottest thing in the room—even if it means setting yourself on fire,” Felicia said.

The knife halts mid-spin as Johnny’s grin sharpens, his shoulders tilting into Felicia’s words like he’s ready to wear them as a badge. Wade reclines, spoon still in hand, twirling idly now as if conducting the chaos lingering in the air. Mary Jane exhales a soft laugh through her nose, barely audible, while the corner of Peter’s eye flicks toward Felicia, his fingers finally stilling their restless rhythm on the table.

“Aw, Felicia, give the guy some credit—setting yourself on fire is commitment. Trust me, I’ve done it. Smells awful, but the attention’s unmatched,” Wade said.

“Yeah, Wade, but was it worth it, or did you just end up lighting yourself up for an audience of… well, just you? Because that’s starting to sound like your autobiography,” Peter said.

“Easy, tiger. Let’s not turn this into an open flame. We've got enough hot air at the table already,” Mary said.

“Hey, MJ, careful throwing around "hot air" like that—you’re sitting next to the Human Torch. I might take it as a compliment,” Johnny said.

Johnny leans back, his arm draped possessively over the top of the booth, radiating the kind of effortless arrogance that only someone who believes their own joke can command. Peter’s gaze locks on his drink, the ice melting in slow, concentric ripples, though the slight, sardonic tilt to his lips betrays his restraint. Felicia angles herself ever so subtly toward Mary Jane, her attention a watchful undercurrent, sharpened by the ring of amusement circling the table.

* * *

There is a moment, just before one plan ignites the next, where the air inside the booth feels like a coiled spring. Felicia's fingers trace idle patterns along the condensation of her glass, the calculated indifference masking the precision of her intent. Across from her, Wade tilts precariously on the edge of his seat, his grin a warning shot wrapped in wild anticipation, while MJ's thumb hovers over her phone, the glow casting faint shadows across her face.
