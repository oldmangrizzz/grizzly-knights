Episode 3: Twelve Years, Three Lies

Grizzly Knights

A quiet night in the Avengers’ secret bar unravels as Wanda’s absence and Vision’s appearances force long-buried truths to surface.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Glasses scatter uneven light on the table, rings of condensation marking time like silent proof of presence. The neon shamrock hums, its green glow catching the edge of Natasha's smirk and the tension in Steve's bottled stillness. Tony's glass, half-empty, reflects no one but himself.

“Raise your glass, Barton, to twelve years of not screwing up badly enough to get yourself killed—though I assume you’re still holding auditions for that role. Cheers to your unparalleled talent for survival despite… well, yourself,” Tony said.

“You got the year wrong. It was thirteen—unless you’re still counting Budapest as a warm-up,” Natasha said.

“Guess it depends if we’re counting warm-ups where somebody blew up half a city block or just the main event,” Clint said.

“Still can't believe y'all call that a warm-up. Remind me to never carpool with you two,” Sam said.

Clint rubs the back of his neck, the calloused fingers betraying a flicker of self-awareness as his laugh lands. Natasha leans back in her chair, boots crossed, the leather creaking faintly. Across the table, Sam's grin sharpens, the amber in his glass nearly gone.

“Doesn't matter what you call it. Block's still gone,” Bucky said.

“If the block being gone isn't the problem, what was the main event supposed to be?” Steve asked.

“The main event, Cap, was not dying—though clearly, we all have different definitions of what constitutes a good time. For Barton, apparently, it’s measured in explosions per square mile,” Tony said.

“If explosions weren’t involved, he’d call it a slow day,” Natasha said.

Steve shifts in his seat, the scrape of wood against the worn floor sharp in the buzz of easy banter. Tony swirls his drink lazily, the ice clinking like a metronome to his rhythm of control. The shamrock casts a faint green shadow over Bucky’s metal hand as it rests steady on the table.

“Hey, if I didn’t blow up the block, we wouldn’t be sitting here to argue about it. You're welcome,” Clint said.

“Man, if survival means blowing up half the block, remind me to pick a different strategy next time,” Sam said.

“Doesn't always feel like survival to the block, though, does it?” Bucky asked.

“People always pay for the wreckage, whether they're the ones in it or the ones rebuilding after,” Steve said.

The room tightens for a beat, the weight of Steve’s words pressing into the spaces between them. Natasha’s fingers trace the rim of her glass, the motion unhurried but deliberate. Across the table, Clint’s grin flickers like a bulb about to go out.

“And here comes the wisdom of the star-spangled conscience. Always with the reminders, Cap, like we don’t already carry the receipts for every damn thing we’ve broken. Drink your water, would you? It’s getting judgy,” Tony said.

“Careful, Tony. He might drown in it,” Natasha said.

“Yeah, well, at least the block doesn’t leave a voicemail to remind you every year,” Clint said.

“Can't decide if that makes it better or worse, honestly. The silence can say just as much,” Sam said.

Sam sets his glass down with a soft thunk, the empty ring it leaves settling into the faded wood. Tony’s smirk sharpens, but his fingers tap once against the rim of his tumbler before steadying. The shamrock’s buzz crescendos, filling the brief pause with something close to static.

“Silence doesn’t forget either,” Bucky said.

“Neither do we. But we keep moving, don’t we?” Steve asked.

“Yeah, moving—mostly forward, occasionally sideways, sometimes in a spectacular spiral of self-destruction. Really keeps things interesting, don’t you think?” Tony asked.

“Sideways is Clint’s signature move. Keeps him unpredictable,” Natasha said.

Clint raises his glass, the faintest twitch of amusement pulling at the corners of his mouth. The whiskey swirls sluggishly, catching the green hue of the shamrock’s glow in uneven waves. Natasha doesn’t look at him, but the curve of her lip matches the tilt of her glass.

* * *

Time.

“Guess we’re really doing this thing dry, huh, Rogers? Never pegged you for a cocktail guy, but even Shirley Temple’s looking lonely over there,” Tony said.

“Maybe he's just waiting for you to spike it,” Natasha said.

“Bet he’s afraid you’d swap it out for something with a little glowstick flair, Tony. That or he’s pacing himself for the inspirational speech later,” Clint said.

“Could be he’s just learned not to trust anything you hand him, Clint. I know I have,” Sam said.

The room shifts with the ripple of low laughter, a brief exhale of shared rhythm. Bucky leans back, his metal fingers tapping once against the table's edge, the sound sharp and clean against the wood. Steve doesn't flinch; his gaze stays fixed on the water, the ring spreading slow, unhurried, inevitable.

“You gonna drink that water, or just keep making it sweat?” Bucky asked.

“It's doing fine on its own. Who needs a drink when you’ve got entertainment like this?” Steve asked.

“Oh sure, nothing like a little passive-aggressive hydration to really liven up the party. Cheers, Cap—your commitment to killing the vibe is unmatched,” Tony said.

“You’re just upset he’s outlasting your whiskey, Tony,” Natasha said.

Tony raises his glass with a deliberate, exaggerated swirl, the amber liquid catching the dim light in lazy arcs. Natasha’s fingers toy with the edge of her napkin, tearing it along a line only she seems to see. The faint hum of the bar beyond the room seeps in, distant and irrelevant, their bubble unbroken.

“If we’re awarding medals for vibe-killing, I think I’ve got a few more to my name. But hey, at least I drink about it,” Clint said.

“Nah, Clint, you drink instead of talking about it. There’s a slight difference—not that anyone here’s keeping score,” Sam said.

“We keeping score now? Guess I’m in trouble,” Bucky said.

“Not unless we’re calling it for style points. Then you’re all disqualified,” Steve said.

The corner of the table creaks faintly as Sam shifts his weight, leaning forward, elbows propped like he’s ready to deal cards. Clint scratches at a spot just beneath his collarbone, his nails scraping over fabric and skin. The air between them tastes of old liquor and unsaid things, both sharper than either would admit.

“Style points? Please, if this were about style, I’d be the only one at the table. The rest of you? Participation trophies at best,” Tony said.

“Participation trophy’s still a trophy, Tony. Guess that makes us all winners,” Natasha said.

“Speak for yourself, Nat. I’m more of a "show up, knock the target down, and leave" kind of guy. Trophies just take up shelf space,” Clint said.

“Yeah, but you knock enough targets down, Clint, and eventually, someone's gonna hand you something shiny whether you want it or not. Call it a hazard of being persistent,” Sam said.

The ice in Sam’s glass shifts with a muted clink as he tilts it, the bourbon smearing faint trails along the inside. Natasha smirks into her drink, her gaze cutting sideways, catching the edge of Clint’s shrug. Somewhere near the door, the muffled clang of a tray meeting the floor pulls no one’s focus.

“Sometimes showing up’s the hard part. Rest is just muscle memory,” Bucky said.

“Showing up’s the job. The rest is what you do to make sure it matters,” Steve said.

“Spoken like a man who’s never had to deal with shareholders. Show up, make it matter—for them, that’s just code for "keep the stock price up,” Tony said.

“Sounds like you’ve got more in common with them than you'd like to admit,” Natasha said.

Tony’s laugh comes low and sharp, cutting through the room like the edge of glass on stone. Bucky’s hand finds his glass again, the metal fingers wrapping around it with a precision that feels practiced, deliberate. The overhead light catches the faint ripple of tension in the whiskey before he lifts it.

* * *

Time.

“So, anyone else feel like we're waiting on the ghost of Christmas Past to show up and tell us we’re all assholes, or is that just me?” Tony asked.

“Depends. You volunteering for the part?” Natasha asked.

“If he is, I want popcorn,” Clint said.

“I’ll pass, but I wouldn’t say no to a refill while we wait on the haunting,” Sam said.

The waitress approaches with a fresh round, her tray wobbling just slightly as she balances the weight of their coping mechanisms. The condensation on the glasses mirrors the damp unease threading through the room.

“Not sure the ghost's got enough hands to point fingers at all of us at once,” Bucky said.

“Some of us don’t need the reminder,” Steve said.

“Speak for yourself, Cap. My conscience comes with a built-in reminder system—and a few upgrades,” Tony said.

“Must be nice to have one you can shut off,” Natasha said.

The table's edges are worn, grooves catching the flicker of neon light as Steve's fingers tap once against the wood, a controlled rhythm struggling against the churn beneath his words. Bucky's glass lingers near his lips, the metal grip steady, unmoving.

“Yeah, well, mine's more like a broken record. Keeps skipping to the shitty parts,” Clint said.

“Yeah, well, difference is, Clint, you can stop the record from playing. Some of us didn’t get that choice,” Sam said.

“Nobody ever said the record stops just because you want it to, Sam,” Bucky said.

“Doesn’t mean we stop trying to drown it out, though,” Steve said.

The haze of spilled beer and stale whiskey thickens, a quiet veil over the room. Natasha's gaze lingers a beat too long on Steve, sharp as the dull thunk of Tony's glass meeting the table.

“And here I thought we were all just here for the camaraderie and cheap booze. Silly me,” Tony said.

“Cheap booze helps. Camaraderie’s optional,” Natasha said.

“Cheap booze’s the only thing keeping this group therapy session from turning into a full-blown intervention,” Clint said.

“Maybe it should be, Clint. God knows half this table could use one,” Sam said.

Clint leans back, the chair creaking under the weight of his deflection. The neck of his bottle hovers near his lips, a shield more than a drink.

“Last thing this table needs is more people telling us what we already know,” Bucky said.

“Maybe what we need is less talking and more listening,” Steve said.

“Great idea, Rogers. You start. Enlighten us with one of your wholesome, hand-stitched nuggets of wisdom,” Tony said.

“Careful, Tony. You might actually learn something,” Natasha said.

The overhead light flickers, catching the fleeting smirk that ghosts across Natasha's lips. Tony tilts his glass, the liquor swirling like the undercurrent of something sharper beneath his usual flair.

* * *

Tony's glass catches the dim amber glow from the overhead bulb, the condensation pooling beneath it. Natasha rolls her rocks glass between her fingers, a precise rhythm, her nails clicking softly against the surface. Clint's hand tightens for a beat on the edge of the table before loosening again, the movement almost imperceptible.

“Well, Nat, you heard the man. Not his call anymore, so I guess it’s mine. And I vote we get another round—make it something that actually burns this time,” Tony said.

“You planning to drink it, Tony, or just keep talking about it?” Natasha asked.

“You two gonna keep flirting, or do I need to step in and make this weird?” Clint asked.

“Only weird thing here is you thinking it wasn't weird already,” Sam said.

Steve exhales through his nose, setting his glass down with deliberate calm, the faint scrape of it against the table cutting through the low hum of the room. Bucky shifts in his seat, his left hand brushing the edge of the table as he leans back, head tilting just enough to clock Natasha’s smirk. The air holds, not sharp, but heavy, like waiting for a distant storm to decide.

“Guess that depends—how weird you planning on making it, Barton?” Bucky asked.

“Not weirder than this, I hope. Could use at least one night without circling back to a damn argument,” Steve said.

“Oh, come on, Cap, arguments are just Barton’s love language. Let the guy express himself,” Tony said.

“Don’t encourage him, Tony. His ‘expressive’ phase always ends with someone needing stitches,” Natasha said.

Clint drums his fingers once against the table, the rhythm quick, almost a punctuation mark. Natasha lifts her glass halfway to her lips but pauses, watching him without turning her head. The faint hum of the bar outside seeps in, muted but constant, a counterpoint to the tension circling the room.

“Hey, I can play nice. Long as nobody here starts swinging metal arms or shields,” Clint said.

“Nobody needs to swing anything if we all remembered how to use our words like civilized adults,” Sam said.

“Words don't pull triggers or throw shields, Wilson. But sure, let's pretend they do,” Bucky said.

“Then let’s not pretend, Bucky. We all know what happens when words don’t land—so maybe we aim better tonight,” Steve said.

Tony leans back, the chair creaking under him, one hand dragging slow circles around the rim of his glass. Natasha tips her drink back finally, the ice clinking as she swallows. Clint's smile flashes, sharp and thin, as his eyes flick to Steve, calibrating the weight of the room in real-time.

“And here I thought I was the one with a flair for dramatics. Somebody pass me the World's Tiniest Violin—pretty sure we can still squeeze a solo before this turns into a group therapy session,” Tony said.

“You want a solo, Tony? Try not making it about you for once,” Natasha said.

“I’m just saying, if this was group therapy, we’d all be drunker and the chairs wouldn’t suck this much,” Clint said.

“You’d think with this crowd we’d at least have better snacks. Therapy or not, could use a plate of wings right about now,” Sam said.

The table holds steady under the weight of their words, the surface marked with faint rings from countless glasses before. Tony’s finger pauses mid-circle, his mouth curving in something just shy of a grin. Sam shifts forward, elbows leaning into the table like he’s bracing for the next swing, verbal or otherwise.

“Could use less talking about wings and more someone actually ordering them,” Bucky said.

“I’ll get them. You lot keep talking yourselves in circles—I’ll be back before it gets worse,” Steve said.

“Oh sure, let Captain America play waiter. Careful, Steve, you might end up appreciating the simplicity of buffalo sauce instead of saving the goddamn universe for once,” Tony said.

“Buffalo sauce might be more of a challenge for him than you think,” Natasha said.

Steve pushes back his chair, the scrape of wood against tile sharp in the charged atmosphere. His shoulders straighten as he stands, the weight of the room trailing after him, even as he moves for the door. Natasha’s gaze follows, her rocks glass resting untouched now, her mouth curled—unreadable, deliberate.

* * *

Natasha’s finger traces the rim of her glass. Sam taps the table once, twice, his rhythm tightening. The air between them grows still—sharp, like a drawn wire.

“Well, this just turned into a Lifetime special. What's next, group hug?” Tony asked.

“Only if you're buying drinks after,” Natasha said.

“Yeah, Tony, nothing says bonding like you footing the bill for once,” Clint said.

“Might have to call in Stark Relief to cover the tab at this rate,” Sam said.

Steve exhales, slow and measured, his hand gripping the edge of the table hard enough to pale his knuckles. Bucky shifts in his seat, the scrape of his metal arm against the chair faint but pointed. The bottle of bourbon between them gleams, half-empty, untouched.

“Can we just cut the bullshit and focus?” Bucky asked.

“Enough. Focus on what, Bucky? Wanda? Or the pile of ghosts we all keep dragging in here?” Steve asked.

“Oh, great, Rogers, let's unpack the entire cemetery while we're at it. Should I start digging, or are we just exhuming an emotional corpse tonight?” Tony asked.

“Pick your poison, Tony. You're good at burying things,” Natasha said.

Clint tilts his glass, watching the whiskey swirl but not drinking. Natasha’s gaze locks on Bucky, unyielding, steel meeting scar tissue. The hum of the bar beyond the door feels miles away.

“Jesus, everybody take a breath. We're starting to sound like the world's angriest therapy group,” Clint said.

“Maybe we should charge admission—call it The Avengers Repressed Variety Show,” Sam said.

“You think this is funny, Wilson? 'Cause I don't,” Bucky said.

“None of this is funny. But turning on each other sure as hell isn’t fixing it, either,” Steve said.

The tension coils tighter, snapping between them like static electricity. Sam leans back in his chair, arms crossed, jaw set. The ice in Natasha’s glass shifts, cracking softly in the quiet.

“Yeah, well, fixing things isn’t exactly our strong suit, is it, Cap? We’re more of a break-it-first, hope-for-the-best kind of operation,” Tony said.

“Funny, I don't remember "hope" being in the manual,” Natasha said.

“Well, if we're handing out manuals, mine probably got lost in the fire,” Clint said.

“Alright, enough. We keep this up, the only thing getting fixed tonight is the bottom of this bottle,” Sam said.

Steve's hand releases the table, dropping to his lap with a muted thud. Clint finally takes a drink, the amber liquid vanishing in a single pull. The bourbon bottle lingers at the center, its shadow stretching under the dim light like a fault line.

“Then maybe we should stop pretending any of us know how to fix a damn thing,” Bucky said.

“Maybe. Or maybe it’s about trying, even if we don’t know how. Pretending’s not the problem—quitting is,” Steve said.

“Wow, Cap, that’s almost inspiring. You been practicing speeches in the mirror again, or is this just your natural talent for clichés kicking in?” Tony asked.

“Careful, Tony. His next move might be asking you to hold hands,” Natasha said.

Clint stares down into his empty glass like it might offer an answer. Natasha adjusts in her seat, her legs crossing, the leather creaking faintly. The room seems smaller now, the air heavier, but no one moves to crack the bottle open again.

* * *

Tony's glass is empty, but his hand still lingers on it, fingers restless against the rim. The room feels colder than it should, the hum of a distant neon sign pressing against the silence. Natasha's eyes are sharp, fixed on Steve, but his posture doesn’t shift—rigid, resolute, and unreadable.

“Sure, let’s all stare at the guy who can out-brood a fucking gargoyle while we pretend this isn’t awkward as hell,” Tony said.

“At least a gargoyle doesn't pretend it's fine. You going to refill that glass or just strangle it, Tony?” Natasha asked.

“Maybe he’s trying to see if it grants wishes. I mean, a refill's about as likely as this not being awkward,” Clint said.

“That’s enough, Nat,” Sam said.

Clint exhales sharply through his nose, not quite a laugh, his hand rubbing at the back of his neck. Tony shifts his grip just enough to set the glass down, the faint scrape of it against the table louder than it should be. Natasha leans back, arms folding, the leather of her jacket whispering in the pause.

“If we’re counting awkward, this ain’t even breaking the top ten,” Bucky said.

“Doesn't mean we need to aim for eleven,” Steve said.

“Why not go for twelve? Always been an overachiever,” Tony said.

“Twelve years, three lies, and counting. We’re all over,” Natasha said.

Sam's jaw tightens, his hand curling over the edge of the table. Bucky doesn't move, but his eyes flick toward Steve, a glance heavy with something unspoken. Across the room, the air tastes faintly of whiskey gone warm.

“Anyone else want to circle back to the generational Boogeyman thing, or are we just skipping straight to the part where we all hate ourselves a little more?” Clint asked.

“We could always try the part where we don't tear each other apart for once. Novel idea, huh?” Sam asked.

“Got a better target in mind, Wilson?” Bucky asked.

“Knock it off. We're not enemies here,” Steve said.

The table holds, though the tension pulls tight enough to snap. Clint slouches deeper in his seat, his foot tapping a rhythm against the scuffed floor. Steve’s fingers press flat to the table's surface, as steady as his voice, but the quiet dares to unravel.

“Oh, good, a pep talk. That always fixes everything, right, Cap?” Tony asked.

“It’s cheaper than therapy. And less likely to turn into a war zone,” Natasha said.

“Therapy's just overpriced booze without the fun glassware anyway,” Clint said.

“And yet, here we are, drinking like it’s gonna fix anything,” Sam said.

The bottle sits untouched in the center of the table, its shadow stretching long under the dim light. Tony gives it a glance, a fleeting thing, before his gaze flicks away toward the corner of the room. No one reaches for it.

“Doesn’t stop anybody from showing up for another round,” Bucky said.

“Maybe because we keep hoping the next round isn’t just more of the same,” Steve said.

“Hate to break it to you, Spangles, but the house always wins, and we're the idiots still doubling down,” Tony said.

“Then maybe it’s time we stopped playing the house’s game,” Natasha said.

Bucky shifts in his chair, the metal hand brushing the edge of the table with a dull scrape. Natasha leans forward just enough to close the space she'd left, her eyes sharper now, cutting through the haze. The room holds its breath, heavy and waiting.

* * *

Natasha's nails click against the side of her glass, rhythmic, precise, filling the hanging quiet. The faint hum of whatever cheap fluorescent bulb secures this room from pitch darkness buzzes above them, relentless. Bucky's metal fingers rest, unmoving, against the woodgrain, catching a dim and fractured light.

“Cheap lighting really sets the mood for dodging big truths, doesn’t it? Classic ambiance,” Tony said.

“Or maybe it's just honest. Cheap, harsh, and uncomfortable,” Natasha said.

“Yeah, well, honesty's overrated if it comes with this lighting. Somebody remind me why we drink here,” Clint said.

“Because it’s not about comfort, it’s about who’s still standing when the bottle’s empty,” Sam said.

The bottle, half-drained, casts a warped shadow across the table, amber light flickering like something alive. Steve's glass, untouched, sits steady under his hand, the condensation pooling against his palm. Across from him, Clint leans back, chair tilted just shy of reckless.

“Vision playing house near Charles’s mansion—guess Wanda’s got her own little Westchester soap opera now,” Bucky said.

“Doesn't matter where she is. What matters is whether we left her holding the weight alone,” Steve said.

“Well, we’re all spectacular at passing the buck, so I’d say she’s got plenty of company in that department. Cheers to shared burdens, right?” Tony asked.

“Shared burdens don’t mean shit if no one’s taking the hit,” Natasha said.

The room contracts under her words, air thickening like the weight of spilled liquor no one moves to clean. Clint’s chair finds all four legs again with a muted thud, stability feigned where it cannot be felt. Tony’s grip around his glass tightens, the faintest tremor in his knuckles betraying the varnish of nonchalance.

“So who's volunteering to take the hit this time, huh? 'Cause last I checked, none of us are exactly batting a thousand on that,” Clint said.

“Does it matter who takes it if we’re all too busy pointing fingers to catch her before she hits the ground?” Sam asked.

“Maybe it matters if she even wants us to catch her. Or if she’s already decided to hit the ground on her own terms,” Bucky said.

“Doesn't change our responsibility. We don't get to walk away just because it's easier,” Steve said.

The scrape of a chair against the floor cuts through the stillness, sharp and deliberate. Natasha shifts, her posture loose but her eyes fixed, the faint outline of a scar catching the subdued light. Across the table, the untouched bottle seems smaller now, its presence quieter, but its hold no less heavy.

“Responsibility’s a great word, Cap. Real noble. But I’m guessing it doesn't feel as sharp when it's not wired directly into your chest,” Tony said.

“Responsibility cuts either way. The difference is whether you let it bleed you out or use it to stay alive,” Natasha said.

“Sometimes it just feels like we're all bleeding out and calling it a plan,” Clint said.

“So maybe it’s time we stop acting like bleeding out is the only way to show up,” Sam said.

The silence that follows clings, heavier than the air in the room. A single droplet slides down the bottle's sweating neck, pooling at its base. Bucky’s gaze doesn’t lift from the table, the sharp line of his jaw locked tight against unspoken words.

“Yeah, well, bleeding out’s the only plan I’ve ever been handed. Guess old habits die hard,” Bucky said.

“Doesn't mean they can't change,” Steve said.

“Change takes a hell of a lot more than a pep talk, Rogers. And last I checked, none of us came with a user manual for rewriting the shit we were handed,” Tony said.

“We didn't come with manuals for anything, Stark. We improvise, or we burn,” Natasha said.

The ashtray in the corner overflows, a quiet testament to long nights and frayed nerves. Steve shifts his weight, the creak of leather beneath him barely audible over the hum of the light. Natasha’s fingers ghost the rim of her glass, steady, but coiled with unspent motion.

* * *

Time.

“Well, that’s the sound of someone deciding he actually wants to hear us. Brave move, Barton. Not sure I’d make the same call,” Tony said.

“Maybe that’s because most of what you hear, Stark, is your own voice,” Natasha said.

“Couldn’t hear you there for a second, Nat, but I’m guessing it was something sharp and well-deserved. Classic,” Clint said.

“You know, Clint, for a guy who just popped his ears back on, you’re wasting no time jumping in the ring. Bold choice, my man,” Sam said.

The ashtray in the corner shifts as Bucky tosses his lighter into his bag, the sharp metallic clink reverberating louder than it should. Steve’s hand lingers on the table’s edge, knuckles tight, before the glance he cuts across the room stops Bucky cold.

“Wasn’t planning on sticking around for the highlights reel,” Bucky said.

“You’re not leaving yet. Sit down,” Steve said.

“Look at that, Cap flexing his 'dad voice.' Always a crowd-pleaser. What’s the over-under on how long Barnes stays planted before he bolts anyway?” Tony asked.

“Depends on how much you keep talking, Tony,” Natasha said.

The hum of the overhead light buzzes louder in the pause, seeping into the edges of the room. Natasha’s fingers drum once, twice, then still against the wood, before her gaze locks on Tony, unblinking.

“I’ll take the over, but only if we up the stakes. Loser covers the next round—and I’m ordering top shelf,” Clint said.

“Y'all are turning this into Vegas now? What's Bucky gotta do, roll dice to leave without a betting pool on his ass?” Sam asked.

“I didn’t realize staying in the room meant signing up for a goddamn circus,” Bucky said.

“Enough. Everyone back off. He's not a sideshow,” Steve said.

The room tightens, air heavy with the static of unspoken resentment as Steve’s voice cuts across it. Clint leans back slowly, the legs of his chair creaking, while Natasha’s hand inches toward her empty glass but doesn’t lift it.

“Alright, fine, Cap. No ringmaster here, just a guy trying to lighten the mood before we all suffocate under the weight of this... whatever the hell this is. Relax,” Tony said.

“If you wanted us to relax, Tony, you’d stop talking about bets and start pouring drinks,” Natasha said.

“She's got a point. A bottle on the table tends to work faster than a lecture, just saying,” Clint said.

“You know what? I’ll take that bottle, but only if we’re clear—I’m not playing referee when y’all start throwing it at each other,” Sam said.

Tony reaches for the bottle without hesitation, the scrape of glass against wood breaking through the tension. The amber whiskey catches the low light, a fleeting glint before he fills Sam’s glass first, steady and deliberate.

“Make mine neat, and don’t bother filling it up,” Bucky said.

“I’ll stick with water. Someone’s gotta keep a clear head around here,” Steve said.

“Come on, Steve, live a little. It's not like you're operating heavy machinery—oh wait, that's my job. Water it is, your loss,” Tony said.

“Somehow, Stark, I doubt a drink is what keeps your machinery running,” Natasha said.

The faint clink of ice in Natasha's glass as she nudges it forward, waiting. Across the table, Bucky pulls his now-filled tumbler close, the liquid barely flickering under the sharp line of his stare.

* * *

Steve’s chair scrapes faintly against the worn wood floor as he pushes it back. The backroom air, heavy with the mingling scents of whiskey and vague cologne, shifts just enough to mark his movement. Around the table, no one else moves yet.

“Leaving so soon, Cap? I was about to break out my charming rendition of 'anything to ignore the elephant in the room.' Stay for the encore,” Tony said.

“Elephant? I thought you’d be more of a peacock guy,” Natasha said.

“Peacock? Pretty sure Tony's spirit animal's a raccoon—always digging through trash and making a mess,” Clint said.

“And somehow still convinced it's treasure,” Sam said.

The laughter that follows is quick, sharp, and nothing like joy. Steve doesn’t sit back down. His hand hovers on the back of his chair, the empty water glass dangling loosely from his fingers.

“Let him breathe, Tony,” Bucky said.

“I’m good, Buck. Just… Clint, thanks for the company. It’s not nothing,” Steve said.

“Yeah, well, some of us specialize in turning nothing into something. Kinda my thing,” Tony said.

“Careful, Tony. Too much self-awareness might ruin your brand,” Natasha said.

Steve sets the glass down with a deliberate quiet that cuts through the room more sharply than words. Natasha swirls the amber in her glass without drinking, her eyes darting to Tony's hand, where his fingers tap rhythmically against the table—just off beat.

“Always here for a little nothing, Steve. Someone's gotta hold down the mediocre corner of the table,” Clint said.

“Mediocre? Clint, you’re the cornerstone of this operation. Without you, who’d keep us all grounded in… well, whatever this is?” Sam asked.

“Kinda feels like we're all just floating, Sam,” Bucky said.

“Yeah, maybe. But floating’s better than sinking,” Steve said.

Bucky exhales a sharp breath, more scoff than laugh, and reaches for his glass. The whiskey sloshes, catching the dim light, but his eyes are fixed somewhere just beyond the table, on shadows no one else sees.

“We’ve all drifted too far already,” Bucky said.

“Then we start paddling back,” Steve said.

“Paddling back, huh? Hope someone brought an outboard motor, 'cause I don't do slow,” Tony said.
