Episode 1: One Martini, Two Plans

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson hatch a horny and barely innocent scheme to fix Peter Parker’s overworked and underlaid life, all while indulging in mall debauchery and too many martinis.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Martini glasses catch the afternoon light, their rims lined with faint traces of salt and lipstick. Wade chews like a man attempting to silence the room with avocado, glancing at Johnny Storm’s abs as if they should comment too. Felicia taps the stem of her glass, her nails painted to match the faint orange twist left in the drink.

“Is it just me, or do Johnny’s abs look airbrushed? Not that I’m complaining—Peter could use a little of that polish,” Felicia said.

“I mean, if Peter Parker ever got airbrushed, he'd just smudge it saving a cat from a tree or, I don't know, apologizing to a mugger for sweating on them,” Wade said.

“That boy could trip over perfection and apologize for scuffing it on the way down. It's almost endearing—almost,” Felicia said.

“Endearing? Try infuriating. Like, 'how-did-this-man-even-learn-to-tie-his-own-damn-shoes' levels of infuriating. Maybe we should just gift-wrap him a pack of condoms and some confidence,” Wade said.

The waiter glides by, eyes brushing over the half-empty martinis and People magazine like he's calculating the tip already. Wade leans back, his hand sliding down to casually tug at the waistband of his pants, as if the room needed a reminder that subtlety left ten minutes ago. Felicia’s smirk sharpens, the tip of her finger running slow circles around the rim of her glass.

“Confidence wouldn’t help—he’d overthink it halfway through and end up alphabetizing them by expiration date instead of actually using one,” Felicia said.

“Oh, absolutely. Then he’d probably file a report to himself about the environmental impact of latex. Guy’s a walking PSA for repressed libido,” Wade said.

“Repressed libido? Please, his entire love life is like watching someone try to open a jar with oven mitts on. Painful, yet somehow fascinating,” Felicia said.

“And you just keep watching, thinking, "This is the time! This is when he gets it!" Nope. Another metaphorical wrist sprain. Peter Parker: Spider-Man, web-slinger, and champion of edging the universe,” Wade said.

The sound of a fork scraping porcelain slices through the chatter of nearby tables, but at this booth, it’s the low rumble of Wade’s laugh that draws a glance or two. Felicia takes another slow sip, her lipstick leaving a faint print on the glass, eyes glinting with the kind of wicked amusement only shared between co-conspirators. The waiter, still hovering in the periphery, decides against asking if they’re ready for the check.

“You know, Wade, if he ever let loose for once in his life, the man might break the sound barrier. But let's be honest, he'd probably apologize to it afterward,” Felicia said.

“Apologize? He’d write the sound barrier a thank-you note with a gift card for repairs. And then 'accidentally' cc Aunt May because guilt is his kink,” Wade said.

“If guilt's his kink, then no wonder he and I didn't work out—I'd never let him drown in it long enough to get off,” Felicia said.

“That’s because you’re too much fun, Felicia. Peter needs someone who’ll tie him up with his own webbing and then lecture him about responsibility until his head explodes… metaphorically, mostly,” Wade said.

A couple at the next table pauses mid-bite, clearly catching fragments of Wade’s commentary, before awkwardly returning to their Caesar salads. Felicia traces a red-heeled foot up the booth’s opposite cushion, the motion slow, deliberate, and just out of Wade’s eyeline. The magazine on the table shifts slightly, its glossy cover creased under the weight of a casually forgotten knife.

“Too much fun? Guilty as charged. Though, I have to admit, watching him squirm under all that tangled web of self-righteousness was half the entertainment,” Felicia said.

“Oh, absolutely. Like watching a puppy get caught in a ball of yarn—adorable at first, but eventually you're screaming, "Just chew your way out, you little idiot!” Wade said.

“And he’d still whimper like it was the yarn’s fault. Classic Peter—wrapped up, helpless, and somehow blaming himself for ruining the yarn’s day,” Felicia said.

“Exactly! Meanwhile, the yarn’s just lying there, minding its own business, thinking, "Buddy, I didn’t sign up for your existential crisis,” Wade said.

A pair of servers huddle by the bar, their practiced indifference cracking as Wade gestures wildly, nearly upsetting the empty martini glass in front of him. Felicia’s laughter comes low and sharp, barely contained, her teeth flashing as she leans in closer. The edge of her heel presses a faint indent into the booth’s vinyl cushion, like a secret left behind.

“The yarn might not have, but every villain in this city definitely did. It's like they keep a scoreboard of how many times they can tie him up before he guilt-monologues his way loose,” Felicia said.

“And the worst part? They don’t even have to try! One sad backstory, a vaguely parental tone, and bam—he’s sitting there like, "Maybe I deserve this duct tape,” Wade said.

“Oh, absolutely. Give him some teary confession about a dead sibling and he’s practically spinning the tape for them, all while wondering if he should send them flowers afterward,” Felicia said.

“Flowers? Please, he’s the guy who’d hand-deliver a fruit basket with a handwritten note: "Sorry for making you turn to evil. Love, Spider-Man. P.S. The pears are organic,” Wade said.

One avocado roll, its insides sliding loose, teeters on the edge of Felicia’s plate, ignored. Wade slouches further, practically oozing over the booth’s cushions, his boots kicking faint scuffs into the metal table leg. The sunlight shifts, catching the smudged fingerprints on Wade’s glass and Felicia’s rings, both gleaming faintly, neither entirely clean.

* * *

Rows of sleek black shelves shimmer under the fluorescent lights, their surfaces a study in calculated temptation. Felicia’s wrist glints damp from a fresh mist of perfume as she evaluates, unimpressed; Wade, smeared with streaks of mismatched concealer, tilts his head to catch a greenish hue under the unforgiving overhead glow, grinning like a lunatic in a warpaint carnival.

“Wade, if you’re going for 'radioactive chic,' you’re nailing it. Now tell me—does that come with a complimentary Geiger counter or just Peter’s existential crisis?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, it comes with Peter’s existential crisis, his guilty sighs, and the way his ass looks in those spandex. Triple threat, babe,” Wade said.

“Well, let’s be honest—his ass in spandex does half the work for him. The other half? The tragic boy scout routine. Total classic,” Felicia said.

“Classic? It’s a blockbuster trilogy—tragic boy scout, accidental innuendo machine, and the world's most repressed thirst trap. Throw in a little web fluid, and I’m sold,” Wade said.

Felicia's laugh is low, quick, like the rustle of a silk sheet; Wade, still grinning, smears another streak of terracotta concealer over a scarring ridge on his cheek. Nearby, an employee with a name tag reading ‘Chloe’ watches them from the corner of her eye, pretending to straighten a row of eye shadow palettes.

“Wade, you're scaring Chloe. Pretty sure 'Repressed Thirst Trap' isn’t an official Sephora shade, but maybe you can pitch it during their next focus group,” Felicia said.

“Chloe’s just mad she didn’t think of it first. Plus, if ‘Repressed Thirst Trap’ isn’t a shade, what the hell else are they calling that dusty rose in the corner?” Wade asked.

“Probably something like "Sunset Lament" or "Blush of Regret." Marketing loves a melodrama, Wade—you’d thrive,” Felicia said.

“Blush of Regret"? That’s my brand, baby. They’d slap my face on it, call it ‘Post-Kill Glow,’ and charge twenty bucks a pop,” Wade said.

Felicia spritzes another perfume onto the air between them, the scent sharp and floral, cutting through the Sephora fog of powdered vanilla and citrus. Wade leans in, sniffing theatrically, his nose wrinkling as a smear of concealer rubs off onto the back of his hand.

“Careful, Wade, you’re about to owe Sephora for emotional damages and a tester display. That one’s called 'Fatal Attraction,' fitting, isn’t it?” Felicia asked.

“Fitting? Felicia, that’s practically my signature scent. Top notes of bad decisions, middle notes of obsessive tendencies, and a base of tequila and regret. Bottled chaos, just for you,” Wade said.

“Sweetheart, if chaos came in a bottle, you’d already be swimming in it. Though I’ve got to admit, tequila and regret is shockingly on-brand for you,” Felicia said.

“Not just on-brand, kitten—it's my crest, my motto, and my family recipe. Mix equal parts liquor, life choices, and inappropriate crushes on Spider-Men. Cheers,” Wade said.

Wade caps a tube of concealer with a flourish, absently smudging the streaks on his face into an unholy gradient. Felicia, wrist still glimmering with perfume, watches him with the faint curve of a smirk, her other hand casually trailing over the nearest row of glass bottles without really touching.

“Cheers indeed, darling—but you left out the dash of unfiltered chaos and the garnish of questionable timing. Wouldn't be a proper Wade cocktail without it,” Felicia said.

“Ugh, you're right. How could I forget? Shake with reckless abandon, serve in a shot glass made of spite, and pray Peter doesn’t take the bar tab personally,” Wade said.

“Oh, he’ll take it personally. That boy would itemize emotional damages like it’s tax season. Too bad he looks so good brooding about it,” Felicia said.

“And don’t forget the jaw clench—it’s like watching a Michelangelo statue realize its web shooters are empty. Makes me want to tip him in emotional support tacos,” Wade said.

Felicia shakes her head, her silver hair catching the light briefly as she pulls a sleek black bottle from the shelf, reading the name with an arched brow. Wade, now using the back of his hand as a canvas, drags a streak of bold red lipstick across his knuckles, studying it like an artist evaluating his next questionable masterpiece.

“If you're going to paint a masterpiece, at least match the shade to your chaos levels. That red’s more 'date night' than 'emotional support taco run,” Felicia said.

“Oh, please, like I don’t keep a 'bad decisions crimson' tucked in my utility belt. This is my warm-up act—wait till I bust out the 'hot mess magenta,” Wade said.

“‘Hot mess magenta’? Now that, I’d pay to see. Just make sure it pairs well with the sequined disaster you call a personality,” Felicia said.

“Sequins are the personality, Felicia. You think this level of fabulous chaos comes matte? Please, I sparkle so Peter’s repressed guilt has something shiny to glare at,” Wade said.

A glossy mauve bottle teeters precariously on the edge of the shelf where Felicia set it down, forgotten mid-quip. Wade fans his fingers dramatically, the crimson smudge on his knuckles standing out like a bloodstain under the unforgiving white glare of the lights.

* * *

Condensation slides down the curve of Felicia’s martini glass, pooling in a damp crescent on the lacquered table. Wade shifts, his boot catching the edge of the opposite bench, the faint scuff lost under the hum of clinking silverware and the chatter of a toddler launching fries two booths over.

“Please, Wade, like your 'type' isn't just anyone who looks at you for more than two seconds without screaming,” Felicia said.

“Guilty as charged, Your Honor. But hey, at least I have the decency to make 'em laugh before they scream. What's Peter's excuse, Felicia?” Wade asked.

“Peter's excuse is that he's too cute for his own good, and it makes people like me reckless. What's yours?” Felicia asked.

“Reckless? Felicia, if recklessness was a color, I'd be the goddamn paint aisle at Home Depot. My excuse is I was built for bad decisions and punchlines—your move, kitten,” Wade said.

The ice in Felicia’s glass shifts as she lifts it, the faint clink against the rim punctuating Wade’s grin. Overhead, a fluorescent light flickers—a brief stutter, unnoticed by the growing hum of the dining room.

“My move? Alright, I’ll bite—what do you think Johnny Storm’s excuse is, other than being made of abs and bad ideas?” Felicia asked.

“His excuse? Being hotter than an Arizona playground slide in July and knowing it. Guy struts around like he’s auditioning for both Baywatch and a fire safety PSA at the same time. Peter’s probably borderline combusting just standing near him,” Wade said.

“And you’re saying that tension doesn’t deserve to be…explored? For research purposes, of course,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I’m saying it deserves its own limited series, full splash pages, and a Variant Cover where they’re uncomfortably close in spandex. Scientific exploration, Felicia—plus, the readers would lose their goddamn minds,” Wade said.

Felicia smirks, the corner of her lipstick barely smudged, as her thumb skims across her phone’s screen. Wade leans back, chair creaking, his gloved fingers tapping out an uneven rhythm on the edge of the table.

“Fine, let’s be generous. How much do you think we’d have to bribe MJ to get her thoughts on the matter? Hypothetically, of course,” Felicia said.

“MJ? Oh, she’s got thoughts—you just need one bottle of Cabernet and some popcorn to get 'em spilling. Hypothetically, though? Triple digits in tacos or one embarrassing photo of Peter in college,” Wade said.

“One embarrassing photo? Please, Wade, I’ve got an entire album. I'll text her... for scientific purposes, naturally,” Felicia said.

“Make sure to open with “Hey, MJ, quick science thing—rate Peter and Johnny’s sexual tension on a scale of awkwardly bromantic to ‘hide the X-rated fanfic.’” Gets the ball rolling.” Wade said.

The screen of Felicia's phone glows faintly in the shadow of her martini glass, the reflection shifting as her fingers glide over the keyboard. Wade’s mask shifts slightly as he grins, eyes darting to the family two booths away, already leaning conspiratorially toward the next interruption.

“You know, Wade, you should be the one texting her. I’m sure she’d appreciate your literary flair—and the sheer chaos of it,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felicia, you flatter me. Chaos is my native tongue, but MJ? She’d probably frame the text and send it to Pete, and I’d be banned from all future science projects. Worth it, though,” Wade said.

“If you’re too scared to take the risk, I’ll take the lead. But don’t come crying to me when MJ outs your 'scientific' contributions in her memoir someday,” Felicia said.

“Scared? Oh, kitten, I’m the kid who double-dog-dared himself into the R-rated section of Blockbuster. Fine, hand me the phone—let’s turn this memoir into a bestseller,” Wade said.

Felicia slides the phone across the table, the smooth glass scraping faintly against the veneer. Wade’s gloved finger hovers over the screen, deliberate, as if savoring the prelude to whatever calamity he’s about to unleash.

“Just make sure it’s messy enough that we can’t possibly deny involvement. If we're doing this, darling, we're doing it spectacularly,” Felicia said.

“Spectacular is my middle name. Well, okay, it's actually “Chimichanga,” but you get the point. Buckle up, Felicia—this text is about to hit like a grenade at a piñata party.” Wade said.

“Just try not to blow up my phone along with it, Wade. I still need it to plan tonight's terrible decision,” Felicia said.

“No promises, kitten. Chaos comes standard with this package deal. But don’t worry—tonight's disaster will be handcrafted and worth every damn notification,” Wade said.

The phone lights up brighter under Wade’s quick, frantic typing, each tap sharp against the quiet clatter of nearby silverware. Felicia sips her martini slowly, watching the sparks fly as disaster takes shape in Wade’s hands.

* * *

The cigarette dangles between Felicia's fingers, ash trembling on the edge as Wade leans in, closer than necessary, to take a draw. The van’s paint is hot under their elbows, the sun dragging long shadows across the concrete. Somewhere below, a car alarm wails, ignored.

“If you're going to drool on it, Wade, at least make it look seductive,” Felicia said.

“Oh, sweetheart, I don't drool—it's called a smoke-enhanced lip gloss application. Gets all the Spider-boys hot and bothered,” Wade said.

“Then why does it feel like I just ashtray-kissed a wet napkin?” Felicia asked.

“Because deep down, Kitten, you’ve always wanted to know what regret tastes like. Spoiler: it’s me, and I come in cherry flavor,” Wade said.

Felicia exhales sharply through her nose, smoke curling out like a frayed ribbon as her lips twitch—somewhere between a smirk and a snarl. Wade tilts his head, studying her reaction like a gambler weighing odds, fingers brushing absently against the scorched curve of the van’s logo. The cigarette, now stubbed out on the concrete, sits between them like an unclaimed truce.

“Careful, Wade. You’re starting to sound like you just might believe your own bullshit,” Felicia said.

“Every word, Felicia. I bottle it, age it in oak barrels, and sell it as premium artisanal bullshit. Pairs well with guilt and a side of bad decisions,” Wade said.

“Sounds like your entire dating history in a bottle. Wonder what the return policy is,” Felicia said.

“No refunds, no exchanges—just a lifetime supply of emotional baggage and inappropriate jokes. Customer service is me, and I’m always on break,” Wade said.

The wind picks up just enough to tug at Felicia’s hair, pulling strands loose from where they framed her face. Wade flicks an imaginary speck off his shirt, his grin sharp and toothy, but his eyes linger too long on the cigarette’s burnt remains. The black van hums faintly, engine off but still warm, as if holding its breath for what comes next.

“Well, that explains the Yelp reviews. You really are a one-man circus of red flags and bad ideas,” Felicia said.

“And yet, here you are, front-row seat, popcorn in hand, ready for the next act. Don’t pretend you don’t love the show, Kitty,” Wade said.

“I’ll admit, it’s hard to look away from a car crash—especially one that insists on narrating itself,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, you can’t tell me you’re not at least a little curious about where the flaming wreckage lands. Spoiler alert: probably in Spidey’s lap,” Wade said.

Felicia leans back, the arch of her spine deliberate against the van’s side, a picture of measured indifference betrayed only by the tightness in her jaw. Wade’s hand drums out a rhythm on the metal, each tap echoing faintly in the open garage. The sun dips lower, painting the concrete with gold-edged shadows and daring them to move.

“If it does, Wade, just make sure you stick the landing. I’d hate for your theatrics to be wasted on someone who doesn’t appreciate the fireworks,” Felicia said.

“Oh, don’t worry, Kitten. I always aim for maximum collateral admiration. Spidey won’t know whether to web me up or thank me for the warm glow,” Wade said.

“Knowing him, he’ll do both—then write a heartfelt apology for it afterward,” Felicia said.

“Ugh, you're so right. He’d probably leave a Post-it note on my ass that says, “Sorry for the inconvenience.” Adorable and infuriating, just like his spandexed self.” Wade said.

Wade shifts his weight, the squeak of his boot against the concrete breaking the rhythm of his earlier taps. Felicia brushes a stray strand of hair from her face, her nails painted a red as sharp as her tongue. The van groans softly as the metal cools, the world around them settling into the quiet just before the night turns.

“He’s nothing if not consistent. The man could trip over his own guilt and still stick the landing,” Felicia said.

“And yet, somehow, still leave the rest of us tangled in his damn web. It’s a talent, really—heroic guilt mixed with that ass? Dangerous combination,” Wade said.

“Don’t forget the puppy-dog eyes. That’s the real weapon—takes you apart before you even realize it,” Felicia said.

“Oh, trust me, I’ve been dismantled by those eyes more times than I’d like to admit. It’s like being guilt-fucked by a golden retriever,” Wade said.

Felicia’s laugh cuts through the warm stillness, low and sharp, a sound that skims along the edges of genuine amusement and something more guarded. Wade’s grin widens, bright but brittle at the edges, as his fingers trace the chipped paint on the van door. The sun, now half-swallowed by the horizon, casts their faces in sharp relief—lines of tension visible beneath the banter.

* * *

The cheesecake lands in a blaze of artificial pink, its surface slick under the low, golden light. Felicia idly taps a nail against her glass, half-empty and smudged with her lipstick, while Wade’s fork hovers, dripping sugar-laden frosting onto the napkin he’s no longer paying attention to. The hum of the restaurant swells around them—distant laughter, clinking plates—but the focus in the booth sharpens, the pull of their shared momentum unmistakable.

“If MJ thinks she needs a detailed itinerary, she's underestimating how much improvisation this... delightful chaos usually involves,” Felicia said.

“That’s the spirit, Felicia—embrace the chaos! Besides, nothing says “trust us” like a plan that's 40% tequila, 30% charm, and 30%... oh shit, what’s the math term for “winging it?” Wade asked.

“Disaster math? Or maybe just "Wade's Wednesday,” Felicia said.

“Hey, don’t knock Wade’s Wednesday—it’s got a 60% survival rate. That’s practically a guarantee in our line of work!” Wade said.

Wade’s fork finally descends, carving through the molten-pink surface with a deliberate lack of finesse, the slice wobbling on the tines. Felicia’s eyes flick to the movement, then back to her phone, the screen’s glow reflecting faintly in her narrowed gaze. The remains of her own untouched drink catch the shifting light like a forgotten afterthought, a faint trace of condensation pooling beneath it.

“Sixty percent, huh? Remind me to never ask you to babysit anything I actually care about,” Felicia said.

“Hey, I’ll have you know I once babysat a duffel bag full of grenades for 48 hours and only two went off. That’s like… a solid B-plus in my book,” Wade said.

“That’s comforting. Remind me to stand at least ten miles away if you ever offer to keep something "safe" again,” Felicia said.

“Ten miles? Bold of you to assume I won’t accidentally fling it eleven. Safety’s a loose concept, like monogamy or decaf,” Wade said.

Felicia snorts, the sound sharp and unguarded, as she finally sets her phone facedown on the table. Wade grins wide, teeth bared, as he takes a messy bite of cheesecake that stains his tongue an unnatural magenta. Somewhere behind them, a waiter drops a tray, the crash punctuating the edge of their laughter.

“Safety, monogamy, and decaf—welcome to the holy trinity of things I don’t waste my time on,” Felicia said.

“Preach, Sister Hardy. Life’s too short for bad coffee, boring beds, and playing it safe when you can just blow shit up instead,” Wade said.

“So you’re saying chaos is the only thing worth committing to? Sounds like you're trying to recruit me for your religion, Wade,” Felicia said.

“Oh, absolutely. We meet on Taco Tuesdays, the holy day of guac and glory, and the only commandments are ‘Thou shalt not judge’ and ‘Always double the cheese.’,” Wade said.

Felicia leans back, the leather of the booth creaking softly against her movement, one hand absently toying with the stem of her glass. Wade gestures grandly with his fork, a dollop of frosting precariously tipping from its edge. The low hum of the restaurant bends around their voices, their booth an island of deliberate irreverence.

“Well, I do like a religion that prioritizes tacos and cheese over guilt and paperwork. You might be onto something, Wilson,” Felicia said.

“Finally, someone sees the light—and it’s shining out of a margarita glass rimmed with salt. Welcome to the fold, Felicia. Membership comes with tequila shots and a lifetime supply of bad decisions,” Wade said.

“As long as I get to pick which bad decisions to keep, I’ll consider the initiation. Tequila optional, margaritas mandatory,” Felicia said.

“Deal, but only if we agree that margaritas are mandatory in pitchers. Refills are just blessings in disguise,” Wade said.

The pitcher, empty save for melting ice and a lime wedge clinging to the glass, catches the faint gleam of the overhead light. Felicia’s fingers pause on the rim of her glass, her smirk settling into something quieter, almost contemplative. Wade, mid-gesture, trails frosting down his sleeve without noticing.

“Refills and blessings? You’re really selling this whole religion thing. Just don’t expect me to pray before I drink,” Felicia said.

“Pray? Nah, sweetheart, we toast. ‘To questionable life choices and the chaos they bring!’ Amen, bottoms up, and try not to spill on God’s nice carpet,” Wade said.

“To chaos, Wade—at least it keeps things interesting,” Felicia said.

“To chaos, Felicia—the one mistress who never gets boring and always leaves you wanting more... or in flames. Depends on the night,” Wade said.

Felicia raises her glass, the ice chiming faintly against the sides as she tilts it in a mock salute. Wade mirrors the motion, his cheesecake-laden fork held aloft like a knight’s sword, a smear of pink frosting glistening on his knuckles. The low hum of the room seems to recede for a moment, as if the booth itself has drawn a bubble around their shared toast.

* * *

The warm glow from Johnny's gym flickers unevenly across the van's interior, casting Wade's grin into sharp relief. Felicia steadies the phone with one hand, the other resting lightly on her thigh, nails too perfect for this dingy seat. The faint scent of old upholstery and grease lingers beneath the plastic tang of Wade's energy drink, half-crushed on the dashboard.

“Johnny, darling, you know Peter—he’s probably off brooding in some upside-down yoga pose. Just tell me you’ll show, and don’t make me bribe you with something decadent,” Felicia said.

“Oh, oh! Make it decadent AND slightly illegal, like caviar harvested from a black-market sturgeon—or stolen sex tape of Captain America pole-dancing. Think about Peter’s face when he finds out why you owe Johnny one. Priceless,” Wade said.

“Wade, you have a gift for taking the elegant and making it... sticky. Johnny, ignore him. Just say yes before I start suggesting favors Wade can help with,” Felicia said.

“Sticky is my brand, Felicia. And we both know Johnny would say yes faster if I threw in a Wade-special—like a "free inappropriate commentary during a live stream" coupon. Just say the word, Torch,” Wade said.

The van trembles faintly as a car rumbles past a few floors below, its headlights briefly casting ghostly streaks through the grimy windshield. Wade shifts in his seat, the crinkle of his suit breaking the quiet hum from the phone. Felicia’s lips press into a flat line, the faintest arch of her brow betraying her patience thinning by degrees.

“Wade, sweetheart, if I wanted Peter to scream into a pillow tonight, I’d just borrow your phone and send those emojis again. Johnny, are we doing this or are we just admiring your pecs all evening?” Felicia asked.

“Hey, hey! Don’t knock the emojis—those were crafted with artistry. Fire, water, web? It’s a sexual haiku. You’re all just uncultured swine who don’t appreciate the subtleties of my masterpiece,” Wade said.

“Wade, if subtlety were one of your strong suits, we wouldn’t be parked in a van that smells like regret and expired sour candy. Johnny, do we have a deal or do I need to take Wade up on his “commentary” offer?” Felicia asked.

“Expired sour candy has a certain je ne sais quoi, Felicia. Like my charm—it lingers, even if it shouldn’t. Johnny, just say yes, before she turns my offer into a binding contract,” Wade said.

The phone wobbles slightly in Felicia's grip as her thumb hovers over the screen, nails catching the cool blue light. Wade leans forward, elbows on his knees, his mask tugged low enough to expose the toothy grin beneath. The uneven hum of an old radiator fills the silence, a gritty counterpoint to the polished confidence in her stare.

“Johnny, darling, you’re really testing my patience, and that’s something Wade usually has the monopoly on. Yes or no—before I start assigning everyone homework,” Felicia said.

“Ooh, homework. Dibs on being the bad influence who talks the class into tequila shots instead of doing it. Johnny, just say yes before Teacher Felicia makes us diagram spider-sex positions,” Wade said.

“Wade, if you keep talking, I really will find a way to make you diagram those. Johnny, come on, a little less hesitation, a little more heroics,” Felicia said.

“Heroics? Pfft, he’s shirtless on a video call; he’s already halfway to saving lives via thirst traps. Johnny, just flex once if you’re in, twice if you need encouragement—I’ve got pom-poms,” Wade said.

Felicia exhales sharply, the kind of breath that carries a smile too sharp to be kind, as her grip tightens around the phone. Wade drums his knuckles lightly against his thigh, a restless rhythm matching the faint creak of worn springs beneath them. The distant grid of headlights outside blurs faintly, streaked by a thin smear of grime on the window.

“Johnny, I swear, if you don’t answer in the next three seconds, I’m making Wade your motivational coach—and we both know you wouldn’t survive that. Tick tock, darling,” Felicia said.

“Ouch, Felicia, that’s harsh. I’m an excellent motivational coach. “Stop being a little bitch and do the thing” has like a 90% success rate. Johnny, you ready to get motivated or what?” Wade asked.

“Johnny, darling, save yourself and just say yes. Otherwise, I’m letting Wade workshop his “coaching” on you live, and trust me, no one wants that.” Felicia said.

“Oh, I think everyone wants that, Felicia. Johnny, imagine me in a sequined jumpsuit, a whistle, and nothing else—motivational posters, live demos... the full Wade experience. Say yes, or prepare for greatness,” Wade said.

The van rocks faintly as Wade gestures grandly, his movement jarring against the stale quiet of the parking garage. Felicia tilts the phone away from his orbit, her jaw tightening just enough to betray exasperation beneath the gloss of composure. The glow from the screen carves sharp shadows across her cheekbones, relentless and unsparing.

Felicia's fingers tap out a sharp cadence against the edge of the phone she holds, her nails clicking louder than the faint hum of distant cars. Condensation slides down the martini glasses, mingling with the sticky residue on the table. Felicia's pinky traces a lazy arc through the moisture, while Wade leans back, boots unapologetically propped on the booth’s vinyl seat across from him. The hum of fluorescent lights above is overpowered only by his smug, unsteady laughter.

“You know, Wade, for someone who claims to thrive in chaos, you're awfully predictable. Spandex jokes? Really?” Felicia asked.

“Charming? My god, Felicia, say it louder for the people in the back—oh wait, we’re in a Cheesecake Factory, so... greasy teenagers and third dates on their phones,” Wade said.

A server breezes past their booth, a tray of garishly colorful drinks teetering, while the low murmur of nearby couples and families fills the space between their words. Felicia smirks, her nail tapping the rim of her glass, the polish catching the light like a sharp edge. Wade slumps further into the seat, a grin plastered across his face, perfectly at ease in the tacky chaos.

“Greasy teenagers and mediocre mojitos—your natural habitat, Wade. You really know how to pick the classiest joints for our tête-à-têtes,” Felicia said.

Felicia picks a crumb from the table and flicks it absently toward Wade’s boots, the movement small but deliberate. Across the aisle, a mom wrangles a juice box into an impatient toddler’s grip while eyeing the pair like she’s already bracing for the scene Wade just painted. The soft clatter of utensils against plates beats faint in the background, a rhythm neither of them bothers to notice.

“Let’s start with traumatizing the toddler, work our way up to scaring off the mom, and finish strong by making the manager regret every life decision that led him here,” Felicia said.

“Deal, but only if I can leave a Yelp review titled "Chaos, Carnage, and Cheesecake: A Love Story,” Wade said.

“Add "Rated R for Ridiculousness" and you've got yourself a bestseller, Wade,” Felicia said.

“Ooh, I’ll throw in a sequel: "Fifty Shades of Frosting—Hot Mess at the Factory." Critics will eat it up. Literally,” Wade said.

Wade’s laughter spills out too loud, drawing a quick glance from a server balancing plates of lemon meringue pie. Felicia tilts her head, the corner of her lip quirking upward as she swirls the dregs of her martini, the glass rattling faintly against her nails. The toddler across the aisle knocks over the juice box, a sharp splat of sticky liquid spreading across the floor, mirroring the unsteady chaos of the booth beside it.

“Careful, Wade. Your sequel might actually be too on-the-nose for this place. Maybe we keep the frosting to a minimum—don’t want to outshine their desserts,” Felicia said.

“Well, if anyone can survive a cheesecake assassination attempt, it's you, Wade. Just try not to get frosting in your eye this time,” Felicia said.

“Hey, frosting in the eye is just part of the job description. If I’m not walking away with a sugar-coated flesh wound, did I even live?” Wade asked.

The last of the martini glasses is empty now, Felicia setting hers down with a soft clink that cuts through Wade's rambling theatrics. Across the booth, his boots squeak faintly against the vinyl as he shifts, hands threading together like he's prepping for his next punchline. Around them, the hum of dinner service wanes, the scent of fryer oil lingering in the cooling air.

“Living dangerously one dessert at a time, I see. Just don’t expect me to patch you up when the cheesecake wins,” Felicia said.

“Oh, please, patching me up is half the fun—besides, I'd look damn good with frosting scars. Battle wounds, but make it pâtisserie chic,” Wade said.

“Only you could make a frosting scar sound like a badge of honor. Let me guess—next, you'll want a crème brûlée-themed eye patch?” Felicia asked.

“Now you’re talkin’! I’ll bedazzle it with sprinkles and torch it for authenticity. Pirates wish they had this swagger,” Wade said.

A soft scrape as Wade adjusts again, his boots leaving faint smudges on the booth seat, unnoticed or simply ignored. Felicia picks at the stem of her glass, letting her fingers linger there a moment too long, the light catching the curve of her silver bracelet. Overhead, a flicker from one of the fluorescents casts the room in uneven shadows, like even the lights are tired of the hour.

Wind scrapes low across the concrete, tugging at the ends of Felicia’s hair as she swipes at the phone screen again, the glow soft on her face. Wade’s boots drum faintly on the van roof, the bottle tilting idly in his hand, vodka sloshing like some cheap metronome to his rambling aim.

“Wade, if you spill that vodka on me, I swear, your precious van gets a makeover in hot pink glitter,” Felicia said.

“Oh, please, like hot pink glitter wouldn’t class this ol' beast right up. Lady, you’d be doing her a favor,” Wade said.

“Class? Wade, that van already looks like it hosts a clown funeral on weekends. Glitter would just confirm it,” Felicia said.

“Exactly! A clown funeral, Felicia! Do you know how exclusive those gigs are? I’d be driving the Cirque du So-Damn-Tragic afterparty. “Up next, the honk of mourning,” Wade said.

Felicia snorts, sharp but low, the kind of laugh she doesn’t mean to let slip. Wade holds the bottle aloft like he’s making a toast to himself, the streetlight painting the glass in uneven streaks as it catches.

“To the honk of mourning, then. May the van survive its mid-life crisis longer than you will,” Felicia said.

“Cheers to that, Kitty Cat. Though let’s be real, this van’s got better life insurance than me—it’s called duct tape and denial,” Wade said.

“Duct tape and denial. Sounds like the Wade Wilson survival guide in a nutshell,” Felicia said.

“You forgot step three: aggressively inappropriate humor. Works great until the duct tape gives out and denial starts asking for rent money,” Wade said.

A siren wails faint and distant, swallowed by the city before it reaches the rooftop. Felicia pockets her phone, her gaze lingering on the horizon while Wade traces lazy spirals in the air with the neck of the bottle, as if mapping out some drunken constellation only he can see.

“Well, when that happens, Wade, I suppose you’ll just charm denial into letting you crash for free. You do have a way with the impossible,” Felicia said.

“Impossible’s my middle name. Well, actually it’s Winston, but don’t tell anyone. It ruins the whole daredevil, sexy antihero mystique I’ve got going here,” Wade said.

“Wade Winston Wilson. Oh, that’s it—I’m getting it printed on a sparkly pink hoodie for you. Complete with “sexy antihero” in Comic Sans.” Felicia said.

“Make sure it’s bedazzled, though. If I’m gonna wear it, I wanna blind my enemies with my sheer cringe power before I stab ‘em. Efficiency, baby,” Wade said.

The breeze shifts, carrying the sharp tang of asphalt and city grit, ruffling the hem of Felicia’s jacket. Wade tips back the bottle, a slow swallow, then smacks his lips like it’s finer than it is, the glass catching a glint of streetlight as it lowers.

“Only you would weaponize fashion disasters. Remind me never to let you near my closet,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, Kitty. Let me in there just once—I promise I’ll only replace half your blacks with leopard print spandex. It'll be our team uniform,” Wade said.

“Leopard print? Wade, the day I wear anything off your rack is the day I retire and start robbing banks in broad daylight for kicks. Wait—scratch that, I already do,” Felicia said.

“See? You’d fit right in! We’d be the hottest, most chaotic bank-robbing duo since Bonnie and Clyde—if Bonnie had claws and Clyde had... well, severe psychological issues and a thing for chimichangas,” Wade said.

Felicia rolls her eyes, the faintest smirk tugging at the corner of her mouth as she shifts her weight against the hood. Wade gestures wide with the bottle, his grin splitting the shadows on his face, the streetlight carving mismatched glints in his mask.

“Wade, the difference is I don’t rob banks for fun. I do it for the art—and the paychecks worth more than a freezer full of chimichangas,” Felicia said.

“Ah, but see, that’s where we differ, my feline friend. You rob banks for art; I rob for the chaos, the giggles, and, okay, maybe enough cash for a new freezer after the chimichanga overload takes out the current one,” Wade said.

“Chaos, giggles, and low-grade appliances. Truly, Wade, you've cornered the market on aiming low... and somehow still missing,” Felicia said.

“Hey, aiming low means fewer ladder-related accidents, thank you very much. Plus, missing is part of the charm—it’s called keeping expectations realistic, or as I like to call it, “emotional bulletproofing,” Wade said.

Wade sprawls further on the van’s roof, his legs kicking idly, boots scraping faint scuffs into the chipped paint. Felicia flicks an invisible speck off her sleeve, her gaze sliding past him to the city lights below, their glow muted by the sodium-yellow haze.

Felicia's phone screen glows cold against the soft amber of the booth’s lamp. The cheesecake fork lies abandoned, its tines streaked with caramel. Wade leans back, tipping his empty martini glass as if checking for some miraculous last drop.

“Is there a single plan in that pretty head of yours that doesn't involve collateral damage and glitter?” Felicia asked.

“Glitter is the plan, Felicia. Collateral damage is just the spicy garnish. Like, what’s a grand entrance without a little sparkle and maybe a small explosion to keep it interesting?” Wade asked.

“Oh, please, blowing up in my face is practically my signature move. If I didn’t walk away with scorch marks and a singed ego, did the plan even really happen?” Wade asked.

Her nails trace the rim of her glass, the motion slow, absent, a counter-rhythm to Wade’s jittery energy. Across the table, the candle’s flame gutters, shadowing his grin for just a heartbeat.

“Elegance is just chaos with better lighting and a tighter ass, Felicia. And trust me, I know my way around both,” Wade said.

“Flattery? Nah, just facts. But don’t worry, this plan is gonna work so well, they’ll name a drink after it—something explosive with a glitter rim and just enough tequila to make you forget the collateral,” Wade said.
