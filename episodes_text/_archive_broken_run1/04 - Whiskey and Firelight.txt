Episode 4: Whiskey and Firelight

Grizzly Knights

At Logan's cabin, four X-Men wrestle grief, memory, and each other as the weight of Charles Xavier's legacy looms large.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Firelight reflects off the glass. Logan’s knuckles tighten around the whiskey bottle mid-pour. Ororo’s gaze settles on the flames, unreadable. Jean’s wine sloshes softly as she swirls it again, the hesitation barely visible. Scott doesn’t move.

“Ain't gonna be me,” Logan said.

“I didn't think it would be,” Jean said.

“Then who, Jean? You want me to do it?” Scott asked.

“That would depend, Scott. Do you think you could say his name without choking on it?” Ororo asked.

The crackle of the fire swelled in the silence that followed. Logan tipped the bottle back, topping off his glass without looking up. Jean pressed her fingertips to the rim of her wine, tracing slow circles, her expression a careful study in restraint.

“Ain't worth scrapin' for it, just say what needs sayin',” Logan said.

“It's not about scraping, Logan. It's about meaning it,” Jean said.

“Meaning it doesn’t bring him back,” Scott said.

“No, Scott, it does not. But it might remind us why he mattered,” Ororo said.

The whiskey settled in Logan's glass, a ripple catching the fire's glow. Jean's breath hitched, so faint only the wine glass betrayed her. Scott’s hand flexed against his knee, rhythm sharp and uneven, like something inside was trying to escape.

“He mattered 'cause he never stopped, even when he should've. That's enough,” Logan said.

“It's enough for you. It should be enough for all of us. But is it?” Jean asked.

“It has to be. What else is left?” Scott asked.

“What is left, Scott, is what we choose to carry forward. Whether it is enough or not depends on what we are willing to make of it,” Ororo said.

The wood shifted in the hearth, a sharp crack that sent embers spiraling upward. Logan drained his glass in one motion, the amber burn visible even from across the room. Jean’s eyes lingered on the fire, her grip tightening imperceptibly on the stem of her glass.

“You ain't gonna find a clean answer in the bottom of that glass, Jeannie. Trust me,” Logan said.

“I’m not looking for answers, Logan. Just a little quiet,” Jean said.

“Quiet doesn't change the fact we still have to decide,” Scott said.

“Then decide, Scott, but do so knowing this—how we remember him will speak louder than anything we say at that podium,” Ororo said.

The fire dimmed briefly as a log collapsed inward, ash hissing across the stone. Scott’s water glass sat untouched, the condensation pooling beneath it unnoticed. Ororo leaned back, her posture steady, the flicker of flames tracing sharp lines along her cheek.

“Then we keep it simple. No speeches, no sermons. Just the truth,” Logan said.

“The truth has never been simple. Not for us,” Jean said.

“No, it,” Scott said.

“rarely is. But it is the one thing that,” Ororo said.

Logan reached for the bottle again, the scrape of glass on wood cutting through the stillness. Jean tilted her head, the firelight catching in her hair, a muted halo against the room's shadows. Scott’s jaw tightened, the muscle there twitching with unsaid words.

* * *

Firelight flickers across wood-paneled walls, painting restless shadows over faces drawn tight. The whiskey sits untouched in Ororo’s hand, her grip light but deliberate. Logan's chair creaks beneath him as he shifts, the only sound beneath the weight of their silence.

“Nobody's perfect,” Logan said.

“They don’t have to be perfect to matter,” Jean said.

“Charles mattered. More than any of us want to admit,” Scott said.

“And yet, admitting it doesn't mean we ignore the cracks in the foundation he built,” Ororo said.

Jean's thumb drags slow circles across the rim of her glass, the soft scrape almost lost beneath the low crackle of the fire. Scott’s fingers tense on his knee, white at the knuckles. Logan leans forward, forearms braced on his thighs, the scent of whiskey sharp between them.

“Cracks or not, we all stood on it,” Logan said.

“And we’re the ones left to hold it together, cracks and all,” Jean said.

“We hold it together because it was worth building in the first place. Because he believed it was,” Scott said.

“Belief alone is not enough. We hold it because we choose to, not because he did,” Ororo said.

The fire pops, sudden and sharp, and Logan's gaze flicks toward it, an anchor in the weight of Ororo's words. Jean's glass tilts slightly in her hand, the amber liquid glinting in the light, steady despite the tension edging her movements. Scott exhales through his nose, a breath too quiet to qualify as a sigh.

“Belief don’t pay the price when it all goes to hell. We do,” Logan said.

“And we keep paying it, because we know what happens if we stop,” Jean said.

“And because stopping isn’t an option. Not for us. Not for what we swore to protect,” Scott said.

“Protection means nothing if we lose ourselves in the process. We cannot swear to save a world while forgetting to save each other,” Ororo said.

The room holds its breath, the air heavier than the smoke curling from the fireplace. Logan's jaw sets hard, the faint grind of his teeth audible as the firelight sharpens the lines of his face. Ororo's eyes, steady and unblinking, lock on his, her whiskey forgotten but firm in her grasp.

“Nobody gets saved without scars,” Logan said.

“Then we need to make sure the scars are worth it,” Jean said.

“They’re worth it if the mission survives. If what we built doesn’t collapse under the weight of all of this,” Scott said.

“Then let us ensure it stands, Scott. Not by blind adherence, but by learning from every fracture before it becomes a fault,” Ororo said.

Jean finally lifts her glass, the rim brushing her lips, though she doesn’t drink. Scott shifts his weight, the creak of leather underlining his frustration barely held in check. The fire dances along Ororo's profile, resolute and calm against the storm tightening the air.

“Learning ain't worth shit if we don’t change how we fight,” Logan said.

“Or how we treat each other while we’re fighting,” Jean said.

“We fight because we have to, not because we want to. But treating each other like we’re replaceable? That’s on us,” Scott said.

“No one here is replaceable. Not to me. Not ever. But we must start acting like we believe it,” Ororo said.

The ember glow softens on Logan's face as his shoulders ease back, but his grip on the glass doesn't. Jean's gaze drops, tracing the patterns of the wood grain on the table, her hair slipping forward, half-shielding her expression. Scott looks toward Ororo, his jaw tight but his eyes softened by something too fragile to name in this moment.

* * *

Smoke curls slowly from the end of Logan's cigar, mingling with the crackle of the fire. Jean’s glass tilts slightly in her hand, the wine catching firelight as her grip firms and loosens in turns. Scott’s fingers tap a precise cadence on the table’s scarred edge, a rhythm just shy of impatience.

“You gonna tap a damn hole through my table, Summers?” Logan asked.

“Scott, maybe the table’s not what’s about to crack,” Jean said.

“We don’t have time to crack. We’ve got three days to get our story straight,” Scott said.

“Three days? Or three days before this room splits like dry wood under pressure?” Ororo asked.

The fire popped, sending a brief shower of sparks up the chimney. Jean’s gaze flicked toward them, her jaw tightening just enough to be seen. Logan exhaled, slow and deliberate, the cigar glowing brighter in the dim light.

“Pressure's good. Keeps the bullshit from floatin' to the top,” Logan said.

“Unless it explodes first,” Jean said.

“That’s why we contain it before then. Or is everyone here more comfortable with detonation?” Scott asked.

“Contain it all you like, Scott. Just remember—pressure without release reshapes everything around it, whether you want it to or not,” Ororo said.

Logan's boots creaked against the table as he shifted, his grin curving sharper, smoke leaking through his teeth. Jean set her untouched glass down with a soft, deliberate click, the sound cutting through Scott’s tapping. Ororo leaned back just slightly, her eyes steady, unblinking in the flicker of the flames.

“Careful, Summers. All this talk of containin’ and detonation—I might start thinkin’ you ain’t got a clue how to handle the fallout,” Logan said.

“Logan, we already live in the fallout. You’re just too used to the wreckage to notice,” Jean said.

“Fallout or not, it’s my job to make sure we don’t lose anyone else in the blast zone. You all want wreckage? Fine. But not on my watch,” Scott said.

“Your watch, Scott? Careful you don't strain yourself holding that clock while the rest of us clear the debris,” Ororo said.

The tension in the room thickened like the smoke lingering above them, unmoving, heavy. The firelight caught on Scott’s glasses as he stiffened, his elbows planting firmly on the table. Jean’s fingers hovered just over the rim of her glass, as if weighing the cost of picking it back up.

“Clock’s busted, Summers. Time’s somethin’ we don’t get to keep,” Logan said.

“True. But we still feel every second slipping through, whether we want to or not,” Jean said.

“Then let’s stop wasting them. Tell me how we’re moving this forward instead of circling the fire,” Scott said.

“Forward, Scott? You don’t move forward by force. Not everything bends to a straight line just because you draw one,” Ororo said.

The room seemed to tighten around her words, the fire’s crackle filling the pause like a heartbeat. Logan tilted his chair farther back, balancing on two legs, the cigar resting lazily between his fingers. Jean’s gaze held to Ororo now, the intensity in her stillness betraying the turmoil beneath.

“Sometimes a line ain’t what you need, Summers. Sometimes you just gotta let it burn,” Logan said.

“Burning doesn’t choose what it spares, Logan. You should know that better than anyone,” Jean said.

“And sometimes putting out the fire isn’t a choice—it’s a responsibility. One we don’t get to walk away from, no matter how much we might want to,” Scott said.

“Responsibility without wisdom is just another kind of wreckage, Scott. Learn when to let the fire reveal what needs saving instead of smothering everything in the smoke,” Ororo said.

The fire flared briefly, a log collapsing inward with a low rumble of ash and ember. Logan didn’t move, the chair still on its back legs, but his eyes narrowed, catching the flicker like a predator tracking movement. Jean’s shoulders eased forward, her hands folding in her lap, the glass abandoned.

* * *

Smoke curled upward, lazy against the Adirondack dark. The empty glass balanced on the wide wooden rail, its rim catching faint starlight. His lighter clicked closed, metal on metal, final and quiet.

“Woulda been messy, sayin' no. Charles... he ain't the kinda guy you leave behind clean,” Logan said.

“He never asked cleanly, either,” Jean said.

“And we said yes, every time, because we thought messy was better than nothing,” Scott said.

“And sometimes, we were right. But not always,” Ororo said.

Ororo's fingers lingered on the smooth edge of the cigar as she turned it, the cherry glowing faint red in the dark. Logan’s boots scraped once on the porch wood, weight shifting, but he said nothing. The silence held heavy, deeper than the night around them.

“Ain’t ever just right or wrong, ‘Ro. Just what you survive after,” Logan said.

“Surviving isn’t the same as living, Logan. You know that better than any of us,” Jean said.

“Living's a luxury the mission rarely leaves room for,” Scott said.

“It is a luxury we have too often taught ourselves to forgo. Too often called it sacrifice when it was simply neglect,” Ororo said.

The breeze stirred, cool and fleeting, rustling the trees but leaving the porch untouched. Ororo exhaled a low breath, smoke curling away with the wind’s retreat. Logan watched the horizon, the burning tip of the cigar a small, steady pulse in the dark.

“Ain’t all of us got the knack for callin’ the difference, ‘n I’m no damn good at it either,” Logan said.

“Difference gets harder to see when you're always bracing for the next hit. Easier to just keep moving,” Jean said.

“Easier, sure. But moving's not enough if you're headed in circles,” Scott said.

“Circles have a way of feeling like forward motion when you're too close to see the path,” Ororo said.

The glass tipped slightly as Logan nudged it with his knuckles, a shallow echo as it settled back on the rail. Ororo passed the cigar back without looking at him, her fingers brushing his briefly, nothing intentional in the contact. The woods loomed quiet, their stillness unbroken.

“Ain’t no path without the woods, though. You just keep hackin', 'til somethin' clears,” Logan said.

“Sometimes you cut a path and don't realize you're the one making the woods deeper for everyone else,” Jean said.

“Then you stop, reassess, and hope you haven’t already lost too much to turn it around,” Scott said.

“Hope alone won’t turn it around, Scott. Action will—if we can bear the weight of what it changes,” Ororo said.

A moth fluttered too close to the cigar's ember, veering off in a frantic arc before disappearing into the shadows. Logan watched it go, his jaw tight, the sharp lines of his face rendered harder in the dim light. Ororo let her shoulders drop, her hand resting on the rail as if grounding something unseen.

“Weight's a funny thing. Don't matter how strong you are—carry it long enough, it still cuts deep,” Logan said.

“And the deeper it cuts, the harder it is to let go, even when you know you should,” Jean said.

“Sometimes you don’t let go because the weight’s the only thing keeping you upright,” Scott said.

“And sometimes you forget how to stand without it,” Ororo said.

The porch creaked faintly beneath them, a sound too small to disturb the quiet but enough to remind that the weight wasn’t all in their words. Logan's cigar burned down, ash clinging stubbornly to the edge before crumbling away. Ororo’s eyes stayed fixed on the dark, her grip on the rail firm but no longer resting.

* * *

The fire crackled softly, casting shadows that danced across the rough-hewn wood of the cabin walls. The wine glass in Jean's hand caught the light, a dark red gleam against her pale fingers. Scott’s eyes lingered on it, his jaw tight, his hands empty on his knees.

“Ain't anyone’s business how many glasses she has, Slim,” Logan said.

“I can handle myself, Logan,” Jean said.

“You shouldn't have to,” Scott said.

“And yet, here we are,” Ororo said.

The bottle of whiskey sat untouched on the table between them, its amber contents glowing faintly in the firelight. Ororo shifted slightly in her chair, her gaze steady, unreadable, as the room hung heavy with the things no one was saying.

“Anyone else gonna grab the whiskey, or am I drinkin' alone tonight?” Logan asked.

“You go ahead, Logan. I’m good with what I’ve got,” Jean said.

“You don’t need more than that,” Scott said.

“None of us need much, Scott, but we take it anyway,” Ororo said.

The tension rippled, quiet but sharp, as Logan reached for the whiskey, the thunk of glass on wood breaking the stillness. His hands were deliberate, practiced, as he poured, the liquid pooling in his tumbler like the room’s unspoken truths.

“Anyone tell ya, Summers, you got a way of makin' a drink taste worse just by sittin' there?” Logan asked.

“Logan, that’s enough. We’ve got enough sharp edges in here already,” Jean said.

“You call it sharp, I call it clarity,” Scott said.

“Clarity cuts, Scott. That is both its strength and its danger,” Ororo said.

A log shifted in the fire, sending a brief shower of sparks up the chimney. The shadows swayed again, restless, as Logan took a slow, measured sip, his eyes heavy beneath the brim of his silence. Jean leaned back, her glass untouched now, the line of her shoulders firm against the soft curve of the chair.

“Clarity don’t mean shit if all you’re doin’ is cuttin’ each other open with it,” Logan said.

“Sometimes a cut is the only way to let something out,” Jean said.

“Depends what you’re trying to bleed out,” Scott said.

“And whether you’re prepared for what might come spilling with it,” Ororo said.

The room held its breath, the firelight flickering over faces carved in quiet tension. The whiskey swirled in Logan’s glass, a slow spiral, as Ororo’s words hung in the air like smoke, curling but not yet dispersing. A clock somewhere ticked on, steady, indifferent.

“Ain’t enough whiskey in the world for this kinda talk,” Logan said.

“Then maybe we should stop talking,” Jean said.

“Talking isn’t the problem. It’s what goes unsaid,” Scott said.

“Then say it, Scott. Let the room bear the weight instead of your shoulders,” Ororo said.

Scott’s fingers curled against his knee, knuckles white with the strain of restraint. The fire snapped once, sharp and sudden, punctuating the silence that followed. Jean’s gaze lingered on him, steady, unyielding, the wine in her glass trembling faintly as though in echo.

* * *

The pot on the stove bubbled faintly, steam clouding the edge of Logan's vision. Scott’s spine was a taut wire, shoulders squared as if the kitchen tile beneath him were a battlefield. The air between them smelled of whiskey, old wood, and something sharper—like burnt edges.

“You gonna say somethin' useful, or just stand there and ruin the mood?” Logan asked.

“Knock it off, both of you. I'm right here,” Jean said.

“You’re right here, Jean, but he’s the one acting like there’s no fallout to clean up,” Scott said.

“And you, Scott, are acting like shouting will clear the skies. It will not,” Ororo said.

Logan’s grip tightened around the wooden spoon, knuckles pale, like he was weighing whether the pot deserved his frustration. Across the room, Ororo leaned against the counter, arms crossed, calm as the pause before a storm. Jean didn’t move, but the tension in her shoulders betrayed the restraint it took to stay seated.

“You got a lot to say about cleanin’ up, Summers, but you ever notice you’re the one who leaves the biggest mess?” Logan asked.

“Enough. This isn’t about messes, it’s about what we’re carrying, and none of us are spotless,” Jean said.

“I never said I was spotless, Jean. But someone’s got to keep us from making it worse,” Scott said.

“And yet here we are, all of us adding to the weight instead of lifting it. Tell me, Scott, when will enough be enough for you?” Ororo asked.

The flame beneath the pot sputtered, a faint hiss of gas barely audible over the weight of Ororo’s words. Scott’s jaw shifted, the muscle ticking as he stared at her, unmoving, unyielding. The room felt smaller now, every breath steeped in heat and friction.

“You lot keep squabblin’, we ain’t liftin’ shit. Drink your damn beer or get outta my kitchen,” Logan said.

“Maybe we should all step out before this cabin burns brighter than the stove,” Jean said.

“And leave it unresolved? That’s not how this works, Jean. That’s not how any of this works,” Scott said.

“Then make it work, Scott, without turning every room into a battlefield. We are not your soldiers tonight,” Ororo said.

The ceiling light swung faintly, its glow casting uneven shadows across their faces, long and sharp. Logan’s unlit cigar sat abandoned on the counter, the scent of stale tobacco mingling with the tension. The pot boiled over, a thin stream hissing against the flame, unnoticed.

“Guess nothin' ever works for you, does it, Summers? Maybe the problem ain’t the room—it’s you,” Logan said.

“Logan, stop. Scott, just breathe. None of this is helping me, and you know it,” Jean said.

“You think I don’t know that, Jean? Every breath I take is trying to help you, but it’s never fucking enough, is it?” Scott asked.

“Enough, Scott. She doesn't need your guilt any more than your fury. Step outside if you must, but stop suffocating the air in here,” Ororo said.

Scott’s hands flexed at his sides, trembling, the veins in his forearms stark beneath the skin. The hiss of the boiling pot grew louder, an angry rhythm to the silence that followed. Ororo’s gaze didn’t waver, steady and sharp as cut glass.

“Everyone shut the hell up before I throw this pot at somebody,” Logan said.

“Go ahead, Logan. Maybe we all need to feel the splash,” Jean said.

“You're deflecting, all of you. If we can’t even stand in the same room without breaking down, how the hell are we supposed to survive anywhere else?” Scott asked.

“By remembering we are more than the worst of our tempers and fears. Or have you forgotten what holds us together when the cost tries to tear us apart?” Ororo asked.

The steam curled upward from the pot, thick and relentless, smearing the air between them. Logan’s grip on the wooden spoon creaked faintly, the wood bending under his strength. Across the room, the firelight caught Jean’s face, her expression something between weariness and the glint of an unspoken challenge.

* * *

The fire crackles low, its embers pulsing faintly like a heartbeat too weak to push onward. The wine bottle leans against the table’s edge, near-toppled, the label peeled halfway by restless fingers. Ororo’s hand, steady but loose, rests on her knee—close enough to Jean’s, but not quite touching.

“That bottle ain't gonna stand much longer. Someone gonna finish it, or we just lettin' it tip?” Logan asked.

“If it goes, it goes, Logan. Seems fitting,” Jean said.

“You're not wrong. But if it goes, someone’s cleaning it before it stains,” Scott said.

“Scott, ever the pragmatist—shall we toast to your foresight before the inevitable spill?” Ororo asked.

Logan leans back against the worn leather of his chair, the whiskey glass balanced heavy in his palm. Scott shifts, deliberate and tight, his eyes tracing the edge of the bottle but never settling. The fire throws their shadows long, wrapping around the room like something that cannot be outrun.

“Waste of damn good wine to let it spill,” Logan said.

“Then save it, Logan. Or let it bleed out. It’s all the same in the end,” Jean said.

“It’s not the same, Jean. Letting it bleed out—that’s a choice. One you don’t get to call inevitable,” Scott said.

“And yet, Scott, not every choice feels like freedom, does it?” Ororo asked.

The air between them thickens, heavier than the smoke curling from the fire. Jean’s glass tilts slightly in her hand, a circle of red staining the rim, her knuckles pale against the stem. Logan sets his glass down hard on the side table, the sound sharp and final in the quiet.

“Freedom’s a funny word for any of us to throw around,” Logan said.

“It is, isn’t it? Especially when it’s always been a leash, dressed up as something kinder,” Jean said.

“A leash doesn’t excuse dropping the reins. Decisions still belong to us, no matter how dressed up they are,” Scott said.

“Decisions, Scott, are easier to claim when you’re not the one bound by the leash,” Ororo said.

The fire pops, sending a brief, sharp cascade of sparks up the chimney. Jean sets her glass down beside the bottle, her movements slow, deliberate, the smallest tremor betraying the effort to stay controlled. Scott’s jaw tightens, the muscle there working harder than his words ever do.

“We all got our chains, Summers. Difference is, some of us stopped pretendin' they don’t exist,” Logan said.

“And some of us can feel every link, every weight, even when we don’t want to. Pretending? That’s survival some days,” Jean said.

“Survival’s not pretending, Jean. It’s carrying the weight anyway. Even when it’s all you feel,” Scott said.

“And what do you call it, Scott, when the weight crushes you before you can carry it?” Ororo asked.

The silence stretches, taut and uneasy, broken only by the faint hiss of the embers settling deeper into ash. Logan shifts forward, elbows on knees, the leather of his chair sighing under the movement. No one reaches for the bottle.

“Call it what you want. Still ends the same way—buried or standin'. Rest’s just noise,” Logan said.

“Sometimes the noise is all that keeps you upright, Logan,” Jean said.

“Noise doesn’t hold you up, Jean. It drowns out the ground until you forget how close you are to falling,” Scott said.

“Then tell me, Scott, how do you find solid ground when it shifts beneath you with every step?” Ororo asked.

The wine bottle wobbles, teetering on the edge of the table, its shadow swaying unevenly in the firelight. Jean’s gaze lifts to it but doesn’t hold; her attention is already scattered, unfocused. Ororo leans back slightly, her posture calm, but her grip on the armrest tightens, fingers pressing into the fabric.

* * *

The wood of the chairs groans faintly as they settle, the weight of what isn’t said hanging heavier than the dawn air. Ororo’s toes curl briefly against the cold porch floor, but her posture stays poised, steady. Logan’s hand drifts to the glass at his side; it’s empty, but he holds it anyway.

“Could use somethin' stronger than this damn glass right now,” Logan said.

“Is that what you tell yourself, Logan? That it’s just the glass?” Jean asked.

“Control’s a hell of a thing, isn’t it, Logan? Easy to lose, hard to trust,” Scott said.

“And yet, some of us carry it like breath itself—unnoticed, until it's all that keeps us standing,” Ororo said.

The sky shifts, a pale streak of amber slicing through the indigo. Logan’s grip tightens on the glass, the faint scrape of skin against smooth grit breaking the stillness. Ororo tilts her head, just enough for the coming light to catch the angle of her jaw.

“Control’s a myth. We just survive what we can, 'til we don't,” Logan said.

“Surviving isn’t the same as living. You know that better than most,” Jean said.

“Living's a luxury when surviving's on the line,” Scott said.

“Survival builds the line, Scott, but it’s living that decides which side we stand on,” Ororo said.

The crickets falter, their rhythm yielding to the slow crescendo of waking birds. Logan exhales, smoke-rough and low, the sound swallowed by the vast quiet of the moment. Ororo’s fingers trace the arm of her chair, slow and deliberate, as though grounding herself to the wood beneath.

“So what side’re we standin’ on now, 'Ro?” Logan asked.

“The one where we keep trying, even when it tears us apart,” Jean said.

“Trying doesn’t always mean it’s going to hold, Jean,” Scott said.

“Trying is what keeps the storm from swallowing the sky, Scott. It doesn’t have to hold forever—just long enough,” Ororo said.

A faint breeze stirs the air, carrying the scent of pine and cold earth. Logan rolls the empty glass in his hand, the soft clink of it against his ring muffled by the growing light. Ororo holds her gaze steady on the horizon, where the first edges of gold begin to burn.

“Long enough for what? Whole damn world’s still burnin’,” Logan said.

“Long enough to remember why we’re still standing in the ashes,” Jean said.

“And long enough to figure out if standing’s even enough anymore,” Scott said.

“Standing is a beginning, Scott. Whether it becomes enough depends on what we rise for,” Ororo said.

The light stretches further now, spilling over the trees and catching on the worn grain of the porch. Logan leans forward, elbows on his knees, the glass dangling loose in his hand. The faint hum of the waking world rises, filling the spaces their words leave behind.

“Rise for what, though? Same fights, same losses. Gets real fuckin’ old, 'Ro,” Logan said.

“Then rise for each other. Even when it feels old. Even when it hurts,” Jean said.

“Hurting's the easy part. It's what you do after that defines the rest,” Scott said.

“Then let what comes after be worthy of the pain. That's the choice we make. Every time,” Ororo said.

The sun breaks the horizon, casting sharp, relentless light across the porch. Logan’s knuckles whiten around the glass before he sets it down, the faint click loud in the stillness. Ororo’s breath is steady, measured, as the heat of the new day brushes against her skin.

* * *

Jean’s fingers brush the edge of the window frame, the faint tremor imperceptible to all but the most watchful. The smoke from Logan’s cigar curls and drifts, catching the low dawn light in a haze that softens nothing. Scott’s jaw shifts in his sleep, his body still fighting to stay upright even here.

“Guess even in sleep, Slim can’t let go of the stick up his ass,” Logan said.

“Careful, Logan. He might hear you and turn it into a lecture,” Jean said.

“Wouldn't want to disappoint. Go ahead, Logan, let's hear the wisdom you’ve got tucked between your teeth and that cigar,” Scott said.

“Logan, Scott, must the two of you always turn a quiet moment into a sparring match? If you seek flame, there is already fire here,” Ororo said.

The logs in the hearth crackle, a sharp, sudden pop as sparks jump against the stone. Jean doesn’t move from the window, her gaze fixed on the bruised horizon. Logan exhales slow, deliberate, the glowing tip of his cigar flaring with the breath.

“Didn’t start it, 'Ro. Just finishing my smoke, like always,” Logan said.

“And somehow, the smoke always ends up filling the whole damn room,” Jean said.

“Then open a window, Jean. Or better yet, let’s all just stop pretending this room isn’t already choking on more than his smoke,” Scott said.

“Scott, if you insist on drowning us in words, at least ensure they carry air instead of ash,” Ororo said.

The room holds its breath, the weight of Ororo's words settling into the spaces between them. The firelight dances across the glass of the empty bottle, casting jagged reflections that cut across the table’s worn surface. Logan flicks the ash from his cigar, the motion sharp, controlled, a punctuation of silence.

“We’re all choking on somethin’, Slim. Smoke just happens to be the truth you can see,” Logan said.

“Or maybe it's just easier to blame the smoke than deal with what's really burning,” Jean said.

“What’s really burning, Jean? Go on, say it. Since we’re apparently airing out the room,” Scott said.

“Control, Scott. It's always control. Yours, his, ours—burning to hold on, burning to let go,” Ororo said.

The thin light of dawn spills further into the room, pale and cold, brushing against the burnished edges of Ororo’s voice. Scott shifts in his chair, the leather creaking faintly beneath his tension. Logan takes another slow draw, the ember glowing like a heartbeat before dimming.

“You sayin' any of us ever had it, 'Ro? Control's a damn illusion. Always was,” Logan said.

“Maybe. But the illusion kept us moving. And now? We're all just trying not to fall apart,” Jean said.
