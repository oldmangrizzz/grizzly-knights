Episode 2: Nine-Forty, Five Drinks In

Grizzly Knights

Tensions boil over as Ben Grimm’s birthday becomes another casualty of Reed Richards’ workaholism, and the family teeters on the edge of saying too much.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Reed's chair at the table remains empty, the fabric faintly stretched where he'd last sat—two days ago. The champagne bottle sweats onto the granite counter, a ring of moisture beneath it spreading slowly. Johnny’s smirk lingers too long, no one meeting Sue’s eyes.

“Ninety-one hours. But who's counting?” Sue asked.

“I mean, you are, clearly. Should we start betting pools on when Stretch actually remembers what time zones are?” Johnny asked.

“Ain’t fair if we’re bettin’ on a sure thing, Matchstick. Stretch loses track’a time like you lose shirts,” Ben said.

“An intriguing comparison, Ben, though I'd argue I retain my shirts far more efficiently than time,” Reed said.

Reed's voice cuts through the room, low and deliberate, as though it had always been there. No one turns immediately; Sue’s grip on the champagne flute tightens, the base hovering just above the countertop. Ben crushes the empty Yuengling can in his hand, the sound sharp against the quiet.

“Ninety-one hours, Reed. Efficient doesn’t seem to be the word I’d use,” Sue said.

“Oh, he’s here? I was about to start writing your eulogy, big guy. Tough part was deciding if it should be heartfelt or hilarious,” Johnny said.

“Heartfelt? You? I’ll take hilarious—might be the closest thing to honest you ever write,” Ben said.

“I appreciate the concern, Johnny, though I assure you, an obituary might be premature. And Ben, your commitment to humor remains enviably...grounded,” Reed said.

The hickey on Johnny's neck shifts with his grin, the shadow of it catching the overhead light. Sue doesn’t drink, but her knuckle brushes the counter as if considering it. Ben’s fingers linger on the crushed can, the metal edges biting into his stone palm.

“Grounded would’ve been showing up when your kids asked if you were coming to dinner,” Sue said.

“Oof, Stretch, she’s swinging for the fences tonight. Might wanna duck before the next one takes your head clean off,” Johnny said.

“Careful, Matchstick, or you’re gonna catch a stray. She’s got enough heat to fry us both tonight,” Ben said.

“I’d argue the heat is justified, Ben. And Sue, you’re right—I should’ve been here. The variables got away from me again, and that’s on me,” Reed said.

The apology hovers in the air, too neat, too measured. Sue exhales sharply, the champagne flute finally meeting the counter with a soft clink. Behind her, the fridge hums, muted and constant, like the room’s frustration given form.

“You always know the right thing to say, Reed. Now if only you’d figure out when to say it,” Sue said.

“Damn, Sis. Might wanna save some for the next round—he’s already looking like the poster boy for academic regret,” Johnny said.

“He ain’t wrong, Stretch. You keep this pace, we’re gonna need a whiteboard just to track the apologies,” Ben said.

“Perhaps a whiteboard would help, Ben—linear accountability might have its advantages. Though I suspect you’d all insist I pin it to the fridge,” Reed said.

Johnny snorts, vodka-LaCroix sloshing in the can as he spins it lazily. Sue's eyes flick toward Reed, sharp, dissecting, but he doesn’t meet them, gaze fixed somewhere in the fine grain of the table. Ben shifts his weight, the stool creaking under him like it has something to say too.

“A whiteboard won't fix this, Reed. Accountability starts with showing up, not charting excuses,” Sue said.

“She's right, Stretch. You can’t just diagram your way outta this one, no matter how many markers you’ve got,” Johnny said.

“Stretch loves his markers though—might be the only steady relationship he’s got,” Ben said.

“I assure you, my relationship with dry-erase markers is purely professional, Ben. Though, admittedly, they do have the virtue of permanence without commitment—something I'm clearly lacking tonight,” Reed said.

The laugh that follows is dry, half-hearted, and sticks like old smoke. Sue doesn’t join it; her silence tightens the room more than her words ever could. Reed shifts just enough to fiddle with the cuff of his sleeve, the motion precise, practiced—a placeholder for something deeper.

* * *

The dishwasher hums low, a rhythm too steady for this moment. The tequila bottle sits uncapped on the counter, its glass sweating under the kitchen lights. Ben’s can crumples slightly as his grip tightens—his knuckles heavy, deliberate—as he takes another sip, watching everything and saying nothing.

“Why is it always my kitchen that turns into the arena?” Sue asked.

“Maybe it's 'cause the tequila's always in your kitchen, Sue. Ever think about that?” Johnny asked.

“You trying to blame the booze for your bad decisions now, Matchstick? That ain't how it works, kid,” Ben said.

“That’s an interesting hypothesis, Ben, but not entirely accurate. External influences, like alcohol, certainly have a measurable impact on decision-making processes. However, correlation does not imply causation,” Reed said.

Sue slams the dishwasher shut with more force than the action demands, the sound slicing through Reed’s clinical detachment. The trembling in her hands doesn’t stop, even as she reaches for the dishrag near the sink. Johnny picks up the bottle, tilting it to catch the light, his grin sharper than it should be.

“Johnny, put the damn bottle down before I show you what a real bad decision looks like,” Sue said.

“Relax, Sue. I wasn’t gonna drink it—yet. Just admiring the craftsmanship,” Johnny said.

“Craftsmanship, my ass. You gonna admire it right into another hickey explanation, or you just stalling 'cause you know Sue’s about to knock you into next Tuesday?” Ben asked.

“Ben, I think the structural integrity of the table might falter before Sue's patience does, so perhaps we should avoid further provocation,” Reed said.

Johnny smirks, but his fingers release the bottle with deliberate slowness, letting it settle on the counter with a muted clink. Ben leans back in his chair, the creak of strained wood matching the weight of his stare. Across the room, Sue grips the dishrag like it’s the only thing keeping her hands in place.

“Reed, if you want to talk about structural integrity, maybe explain how you thought tonight was going to hold up under the weight of neglected promises and empty seats,” Sue said.

“Oof. That’s gotta be the most polite way to say "you fucked up" I’ve ever heard. You sure you don’t wanna just throw the tequila at him, Sue? Might be more efficient,” Johnny said.

“Careful, Johnny. Pretty sure if Sue makes a move, that bottle's got your name on it before it gets anywhere near Reed,” Ben said.

“Sue, I—your point is valid. I should’ve communicated better, should’ve... prioritized. The failure is mine, unequivocally,” Reed said.

The room stagnates, heavy with the unspoken weight of truths too raw to dissect. The tequila bottle gleams under the fluorescent light, a glass sentinel in the middle of the stalemate. Ben cracks his knuckles—slow, deliberate—a sound that fills the pause Reed leaves behind.

“Acknowledging it isn’t enough, Reed. Fix it, or don’t. Just stop expecting us to carry the weight while you find the time,” Sue said.

“Damn, Sue, remind me never to piss you off like that. Reed, you might wanna start with fixing tonight before you try the whole grand redemption arc. Just saying,” Johnny said.

“Yeah, Stretch, step one’s always patchin' the leak before the whole damn ship sinks. Tonight’s already under water, so maybe start paddlin',” Ben said.

“I’m not unaware of the depth of the problem, Ben. But pointing out the breach doesn’t inherently solve it—though you’re right, action is overdue. Let me start by asking: Sue, what can I do, tonight, to begin repairing this?” Reed asked.

The dishrag drops to the counter, its damp weight landing with a dull slap. Sue’s gaze pierces through the quiet that follows, her arms folding tightly across her chest like she’s trying to stop herself from unraveling. Johnny reaches for the Yuengling left untouched beside the tequila, the twist of his grin gone as he pops the cap.

“You can start by remembering that asking me to solve it for you is just another way of dodging the work,” Sue said.

“She's got a point, Reed. Feels like makin' her quarterback your playbook doesn’t exactly scream "taking initiative," y'know? Maybe try coming up with one on your own for once,” Johnny said.

“Yeah, Reed, time to put that big brain to work on somethin' other than molecules and doohickeys. 'Cause right now, you’re soundin’ like a guy askin’ for directions while sittin’ on a map,” Ben said.

“Then I’ll start here: Sue, I was wrong to let tonight slip away. I’ll book Luger’s again, apologize directly, and ensure it doesn’t happen again. Anything less isn’t sufficient,” Reed said.

The Yuengling hisses softly as Johnny takes a slow pull, his eyes darting between Sue and Reed like he’s watching the start of a fight he can’t decide whether to stop or bet on. Ben exhales through his nose, the sound more grunt than breath, while his thumb traces a shallow dent in his can. Sue doesn’t move, her silence sharper than anything that’s been said aloud.

* * *

Rain ticks against the glass table, irregular and soft, the first cautious taps of a storm testing its reach. The tang of banana rum vapor hangs faintly in the air, curling and fading as Ben thumbs the lid of the cigar box open, revealing its modest treasures. Reed, distant, watches the rain without seeing it, while Sue’s gaze flickers toward him, sharp and unreadable.

“It’s starting to come down. Reed, do you even know when you’re supposed to be at Bridgewater, or am I guessing again?” Sue asked.

“Why don’t we all just guess, Sue? Maybe we put bets down — highest stakes gets to babysit Reed next time,” Johnny said.

“Yeah, great idea, Matchstick. 'Cept I ain't bettin', 'cause I already know who's takin' that job. Spoiler: it ain't me,” Ben said.

“It was 8:30, I believe. But accounting for transit and setup time... yes, that may require adjustment,” Reed said.

Ben pulls a cigar, its wrapper rough and unpolished, and rolls it between his fingers like a stress ball he doesn't want to admit needing. A fresh coil of vapor ghosts from Johnny’s vape, catching the faint glow of the deck light as he leans back, legs sprawled. Sue's jaw tightens, just enough to be visible, her knuckles brushing the wet edge of the table.

“Reed, "may require adjustment" isn’t exactly the same as knowing you’ve left anyone enough time to fix it. Do I need to make the call, or can you?” Sue asked.

“You need a bat-signal, sis, ‘cause this is starting to sound like more of a recurring villain situation than a one-off emergency,” Johnny said.

“You keep talkin', Matchstick, but you ain't wrong. Reed, you wanna put down the rain poetry for a sec and actually answer the lady?” Ben asked.

“I can make the call, Sue. I’ll handle it now. There's no need for you to intervene,” Reed said.

Reed straightens, pulling his phone from a pocket with the absent precision of habit, the screen’s glow cutting a cold slice across his face. Ben strikes a match against the box, the rasp and flare louder than the rain for an instant, its flame catching the edge of the cigar with a flicker. Sue doesn’t look away from Reed, her nails pressing faint crescents into the table’s surface.

“Good. Let’s hope “handling it” doesn’t mean half an answer and twice the problem later.” Sue said.

“Hey, at least if it does, we’ve got front row seats for the fireworks. Watching Reed improvise is better than most Netflix these days,” Johnny said.

“Yeah, long as it ain't us sweepin' up the damn fallout. ‘Cause guess who always ends up cleanin’ house? Spoiler again: not the guy with the stretchy excuses,” Ben said.

“If you'd prefer, Ben, I can draft a written report afterward so you can review the fallout in comprehensive detail,” Reed said.

Smoke unfurls from Ben’s cigar, heavy and bitter, curling through Johnny’s lighter, sweeter vapor as the space between them thickens with contrasts. The rain sharpens, soft drumming turning to a steady patter, streaking the glass with erratic pathways. Reed’s phone remains lit, his fingers hovering over the screen longer than the words seem to require, as Sue’s stare carves into the air between them.

“Reed, maybe focus less on drafting and more on not creating the fallout in the first place. Novel idea, I know,” Sue said.

“Yeah, Reed, maybe save the written report for the autobiography we keep joking about. "Stretching the Truth: How to Scientist Your Way Out of a Tight Spot." Bestseller material, easy,” Johnny said.

“Heh, yeah, only chapter one’s gotta be, "How I Made My Best Pal into a Permanent Rocky Horror Picture Show." Real page-turner, that one,” Ben said.

“Ben, I never stop working to fix what I’ve done to you. If I could undo it entirely, you know I would,” Reed said.

Ben’s jaw tightens, the cigar shifting between his teeth with a faint grind as the rain picks up, louder now against the deck’s overhang. Johnny’s grin slips, just a little, as the air thickens with something heavier than smoke. Sue exhales sharply, a sound barely audible but cutting through the silence Reed leaves hanging.

“Enough. We’re not doing this tonight. Not here, not like this,” Sue said.

“Alright, alright, calling time on the heavyweight rematch. Let’s maybe just sit here, enjoy the rain, and pretend we’re normal for five minutes,” Johnny said.

“Normal? Yeah, 'cause nothin’ says normal like a big orange lug puffin' a stogie next to the living embodiment of fire hazards and Elastic Man over there. Real suburban dream, huh?” Ben asked.

“Normal’s relative, Ben. By most metrics, we redefined it the day we came back from that flight,” Reed said.

Lightning arcs faintly on the horizon, its glow tracing silhouettes on the glass tabletop between them. Ben exhales a cloud of smoke, the ember at the tip of his cigar flaring briefly before dimming to ash. Johnny drums his fingers against his empty bottle, the rhythmic tap syncing unevenly with the rain.

* * *

Rain slants sideways across the skyline, hammering the awning. Johnny’s fingers abandon the rhythm, his hand now gripping the neck of the bottle like he might snap it. Sue’s gaze knives through the tension, her jaw set, her glass untouched and sweating in her hand.

“He's never coming, Ben. You know that,” Sue said.

“That's his whole thing, right? Stretching himself thin enough he disappears? Always knew he was flexible, but Jesus,” Johnny said.

“Ain't like the guy's skippin' poker night for a day at the spa, Johnny. Cut him some slack,” Ben said.

“The calculations aren't arbitrary, Ben. You know that. Every decision—every priority—is made to ensure we're still here to have this argument,” Reed said.

The cigar in Ben's hand burns down, ignored, its ash precariously long. Johnny shifts his weight, his chair scraping faintly against the wet stone. Sue's stare doesn’t waver, fixed on Reed, as though trying to place the exact second he stopped being her husband and became a diagram in motion.

“And yet here we are, Reed. Still here, still arguing—like that's supposed to be enough,” Sue said.

“Yeah, Reed, congrats on the whole 'alive to bicker' strategy. Really killing it in the romance department,” Johnny said.

“Alright, that's enough, both of ya. This ain’t gettin' us nowhere but wetter,” Ben said.

“Ben’s right. This isn’t productive. Perhaps we should—continue this conversation when emotions aren’t running so high,” Reed said.

The rain hits harder now, a drumroll against the awning, splashing over the edges in uneven rivulets. Ben finally stubs out the smoldering cigar, the hiss of char meeting rainwater barely audible beneath the tension. Johnny leans back, arms crossed tight, his bottle dangling precariously from two fingers.

“When, Reed? When exactly is that ever going to happen?” Sue asked.

“Yeah, because Reed’s got a real track record of penciling in ‘family time’ between saving the multiverse and… whatever the hell it is you do up there,” Johnny said.

“Johnny, you’re pushin’ it. Don’t mean shit now if you beat the man down when he’s already crawlin’,” Ben said.

“Crawling doesn’t matter, Ben, if I’m the one who put myself there. That’s not the point. The point is—I have to keep us standing, no matter what,” Reed said.

Lightning strobes the skyline, its reflection fracturing in the wet glass of the table. Sue’s fingers tighten around her glass, the condensation sliding over her knuckles. Ben shifts his chair back slightly, his broad frame a deliberate barrier between Johnny’s rising heat and Reed’s forced calm.

“And what about the rest of us, Reed? Who’s holding us up while you’re busy holding the world?” Sue asked.

“Yeah, man, newsflash—you can’t balance the whole universe if the ground under you’s already cracked. We’re not just set dressing in the Reed Richards Show,” Johnny said.

“Alright, you two, knock it off. We ain’t doin’ this tonight. Not here, not like this,” Ben said.

“Ben, they’re right. I’ve miscalculated—again. Holding it all doesn’t mean much if I’m…losing what matters most in the process,” Reed said.

The rain seeps closer, creeping over the edges of the deck, slicking the stone underfoot. Sue doesn’t flinch, her glass still suspended in her grip, untouched. Johnny exhales sharply, a slow shake of his head against the weight of Reed’s admission.

“Then stop just admitting it, Reed. Do something about it,” Sue said.

“Yeah, genius. Time to put the stretchy part of you where your mouth is, for once,” Johnny said.

“Cool it, Hotshot. Maybe let the man figure his shit out ‘fore you light him up again,” Ben said.

“It’s not about figuring it out, Ben. It’s about—acting. You’re right, Sue. You all are. I just need…to work out how to start,” Reed said.

Thunder rolls low and heavy, shaking the air around them. Sue sets her glass down with a sharp click, the sound cutting through the rain. Johnny tilts his bottle, draining the last drop, his jaw tightening as he tosses it aside onto the growing pile.

* * *

Rain streaks the windowpanes, soft against the thunder’s backdrop. Ben exhales a plume of cigar smoke, its curl reaching the ceiling fan’s slow, defiant turn. Sue's heels click sharp against the floor as she paces, her arms folded, her gaze fixed on nothing and everything.

“Ben, for the love of God, that smoke’s going to cling to everything. Reed’s lab already smells like burnt circuits, and I can’t have the living room reeking like a dive bar too,” Sue said.

“You know, Sue, maybe it’ll mask the smell of all the tension in here,” Johnny said.

“Heh, maybe Johnny’s got a point there, Suzie. Least the stogie’s honest 'bout what it is — unlike the mood in this room,” Ben said.

“The mood, Benjamin, is one of attempting to maintain cohesion under stress—a very human reaction to compounded external pressures. Though I suppose a cigar doesn’t require justification or self-awareness for its existence, does it?” Reed asked.

Ben chuckles low, the cigar’s ember glowing brighter for a moment as he draws in. Johnny slouches deeper into the couch, phone in hand, a phantom grin flickering across his face. Reed leans forward, fingertips steepled, the light from the lab spilling faintly onto his shirt’s fraying collar.

“And yet, Reed, the cigar isn’t ignoring anyone in the room or brushing off their needs under the guise of "cohesion." Maybe we could all take a lesson from it,” Sue said.

“So what, you want us all to light up and air our shit out like a bad daytime soap? Might actually be fun for once,” Johnny said.

“Might be the first time this family had a real conversation without someone runnin’ off to tinker, torch somethin’, or turn invisible. Could be worth the smoke damage,” Ben said.

“If the definition of a "real conversation" requires conflagration or corrosion, I’d suggest we reevaluate the metric for success,” Reed said.

Sue stops pacing, turning sharply toward Reed, her arms dropping to her sides with a heavy finality. Johnny’s fingers tap the screen of his phone, fast and jittery, the tap-tap-tap the only sound between the thunder and the words hanging in the air. Ben knocks ash from his cigar into the empty coffee cup he’s claimed as an ashtray, his gaze shifting to the storm outside.

“Maybe the metric for success should include you actually listening, Reed. Without equations, without deflections, without making everyone else in the room feel small,” Sue said.

“Yeah, Reed, take a note—step one: don’t make 'em feel like they’re in a lecture hall every damn time they open their mouth. Revolutionary concept, right?” Johnny asked.

“Y'know, maybe we should all take a note—step one: act like we ain't gonna bite each other's heads off every damn time someone tries to talk. Feels like we're battlin' more inside this room than out there lately,” Ben said.

“It’s not battle, Ben. It’s entropy—a system breaking down from within. Perhaps the friction feels sharper because it reminds us that we’re all still trying, imperfect as that effort may be,” Reed said.

The room holds still, the weight of Reed's words settling like dust in sunlight. Johnny flips his phone face down on the couch arm, his grin fading into something unreadable. The cigar smolders between Ben's fingers, the scent thick enough to cling to every breath they take.

“Imperfect effort doesn’t excuse neglect, Reed. Trying isn’t the same as showing up—especially when your family needs more than theories and apologies,” Sue said.

“So, just to clarify, Reed, "trying" means bailing to the lab when shit gets hard, yeah? Asking for a friend,” Johnny said.

“Hey, ease up, matchstick. Ain't no point turnin’ this into a dogpile. Family ain't about us bein' perfect—it’s about us quittin' when the punches start comin’, and last I checked, none of us ever did that,” Ben said.

“No, Ben, we didn't quit. But sustaining isn’t the same as repairing, and I’m... aware that I’ve treated survival as sufficient when it isn’t,” Reed said.

Sue presses her thumb into her palm, grounding herself in the sharp bite of her own nails. Johnny shifts, his knee bouncing, the pent-up energy looking for somewhere to go. Ben takes another slow drag, the ember flaring in the dim light, as Reed’s gaze falls to the floor like the weight of his own words.

“Awareness is a start, Reed. Now act on it—for once. Otherwise, all we are is survival, and that’s not enough. Not for me, not for them,” Sue said.

“Well, damn, Sue, you want him to tattoo it on his stretchy ass or what? We get it—step up or step out,” Johnny said.

“Watch it, hotshot. Nothin’ good ever came from pourin’ gasoline on a lit match. Let's focus on fixin', not burnin' what's left,” Ben said.

“Fixing requires a blueprint, a system with measurable outcomes. If we’re to bridge this, I’ll need specifics—what is it you need from me that I haven’t given?” Reed asked.

The silence that follows is palpable, the kind that wraps tight around the room and suffocates. Sue’s pacing halts, her weight shifting as she leans back on one heel, arms crossing again like armor. Johnny grabs the throw pillow beside him, gripping it with restless hands, while Ben exhales smoke through his nose, watching the storm outside instead of the fracture inside.

* * *

Ben shifts his weight on the reinforced couch, the creak of metal under stone filling a gap in the silence. Johnny tosses the throw pillow onto the coffee table, where it knocks over an empty glass that no one moves to upright. Sue’s arms stay crossed, fingers digging into her elbows as though holding herself together might keep the room from fracturing further.

“You know, Johnny, it's astounding—truly—that you can still manage to make it all about you, even when we’re standing in the middle of this mess,” Sue said.

“If it’s such a mess, Suzie, maybe you should stop playing mom long enough to figure out why none of us can breathe in your perfect house,” Johnny said.

“Alright, that's enough, you two. You ain’t exactly makin’ the air cleaner with this back-and-forth, so how 'bout we all shut up for a second before someone says somethin' they can’t take back,” Ben said.

“Ben’s right. This isn’t productive—escalation rarely is. Let's redirect to the actual issue at hand, if we can identify it amidst the shouting,” Reed said.

Reed adjusts his glasses with the deliberate precision of a man clinging to control, his other hand tapping a slow, rhythmic pattern against his knee. Johnny leans back, arms spread across the top of the couch like surrender is an aesthetic choice, but his jaw tightens, betraying the crack beneath. Ben’s eyes stay fixed on the glass storm outside, his cigar smoldering in the ashtray, forgotten.

“The issue at hand, Reed, is that some of us are exhausted from holding everything together while others sit back and treat this family like their personal playground—mess it up, walk away, and leave someone else to clean,” Sue said.

“Oh, I’m sorry, Sue, I didn’t realize running off to play martyr was the same thing as holding it together. Maybe if you stopped auditioning for sainthood, the rest of us wouldn’t feel like extras in your tragedy,” Johnny said.

“Y'know, Johnny, if ya spent half as much time bitin' your tongue as you did runnin' your mouth, maybe we wouldn’t be sittin’ knee-deep in this crap. And Sue, you ain't gotta carry the whole damn world on your back neither—pretty sure it ain't in your job description,” Ben said.

“If we insist on reducing this to character assassination, we’ll never leave the room intact. Perhaps the real trouble is that we’re all more comfortable pointing at fractures than addressing load-bearing beams,” Reed said.

The rain sharpens its cadence against the windows, staccato bursts filling the pause Reed leaves behind. Johnny’s leg bounces, the couch frame shifting beneath the motion, while Sue’s stare pierces him with the weight of a thousand unspoken words. The ash from Ben’s cigar tips forward, a fragile crumble falling onto the tabletop, unnoticed.

“Load-bearing beams, Reed? That’s a lovely metaphor, but maybe we wouldn’t need so many if the foundation weren’t cracking under the weight of everything we keep refusing to fix,” Sue said.

“You mean like how you and Reed keep patching cracks with shit that doesn’t hold? Or how I’m supposed to keep smiling through every cosmic disaster like I didn’t die holding the goddamn line? Which foundation are we talking about here, Sue?” Johnny asked.

“Alright, Johnny, we get it—you’ve been through the wringer, same as the rest of us, but dyin’ don’t give you the right to throw livin’ people's mistakes back in their face like a damn trump card. We’re all sittin’ on the same rubble here,” Ben said.

“And yet we keep arguing over whose piece of the rubble is heavier, rather than acknowledging that the structure itself is compromised. If we don’t start addressing that together, no amount of patchwork will ever hold,” Reed said.

The room exhales in uneven shifts—Johnny slouching lower, Sue stiffening tighter, Ben rubbing a hand over his rocky face like it might scrape away the tension beneath. Reed’s gaze drifts upward momentarily, tracing hairline cracks in the ceiling that no one else seems to notice—or acknowledge.

“Funny, isn’t it, Reed? How you can diagnose the structure’s failures so perfectly but never quite manage to pick up a hammer yourself,” Sue said.

“And yet somehow I’m the one who gets called reckless, Sue. At least when I jump in, I’m not standing on the sidelines narrating the collapse like it’s an abstract problem,” Johnny said.

“Yeah, sure, Johnny, 'cause throwin' yerself headfirst into the fire always fixes everythin’, right? And Sue, maybe quit swingin’ that hammer at Reed long enough to notice he’s still holdin’ up half this damn roof, even if it ain’t perfect. Whole lotta heat in here, but not much light, folks,” Ben said.

“The heat is undeniable, Ben, but light requires focus—and right now, we seem intent on blinding ourselves with sparks instead of forging something stronger,” Reed said.

The edges of Sue’s nails bite into her palms, sharp crescents pressing through the mask of calm she’s barely holding. Johnny drums his fingers against the couch, the restless rhythm undercutting the stillness of his posture. Ben shifts, the groan of stone on fabric loud enough to cut through the growing charge in the air.

“Focus, Reed? That’s convenient coming from the man who loses himself in the lab while the rest of us are out here trying to stop the roof from caving in,” Sue said.

“And there it is—the classic Sue deflection. Maybe if you didn’t try to micromanage every damn beam, we’d stop caving in so often,” Johnny said.

“Both o' ya need to cool it. We ain’t buildin’ nothin’ here, just takin’ swings at each other like that’s gonna patch a single goddamn crack. Focus on somethin' useful or don’t gripe when the whole place caves in,” Ben said.

“What we need is a plan, not another volley of blame. If we can’t articulate what’s actually failing, then we’re all just swinging blind,” Reed said.

Ben’s words linger like the spent ash on the table, heavy and ignored. Sue uncrosses her arms, the motion sharp, her fingertips briefly skimming the edge of the couch before balling into fists at her sides. Johnny shifts forward, elbows on his knees now, as if poised to launch the next strike.

* * *

The air is taut, heavy with the scent of old coffee and the faint metallic tang of tension. Reed's fingers fidget with an invisible equation in the air, his gaze somewhere far beyond the room. Sue's fist unclenches, only to tighten again around the fabric of her skirt.

“Reed, can you even see us right now? Or is the whole damn universe more important than what’s in front of you?” Sue asked.

“Sue, that's a hell of a fastball, but, uh, maybe aim it a little lower—like, say, at Reed's knees instead of his face?” Johnny asked.

“If she don’t throw it, Johnny, I will. Reed, you got ears in that big brain o’ yours, or you just playin’ statue tonight?” Ben asked.

“Ben, I hear you—I hear all of you. But if you'd just let me explain, the calculations—there's a breakthrough here that changes... everything,” Reed said.

Johnny leans further forward, one foot bouncing lightly against the floor, the rhythm betraying his restraint. Ben's hand, rough and motionless, grips the armrest like it might crumble under his grasp. Sue’s gaze doesn’t waver, but her knuckles go white where they grip her skirt.

“A breakthrough. Right. But when everything changes, Reed, who’s the one holding it together while you chase it? Because it sure as hell isn’t you,” Sue said.

“Uh, Reed... I think she’s handing you your ass, man. Maybe stop explaining the galaxy and start explaining yourself?” Johnny asked.
