Episode 91: Swarm Proof — Tuesday Night, Anyway

Grizzly Knights

Felicia and Wade spend a Tuesday night failing to talk around Peter Parker, and somewhere between the second tequila and the parking garage, somebody finally names it.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Did you just opt for disco Morse code about tacos? Wade, you're the worst kind of distraction—my favorite. Now: why exactly are you blinking criminal levels of neon in my personal space?” Felicia asked.

“Clearly, you've never seen the high art of 'spandex chic,' Felicia. Let me teach you, one inappropriate pelvic thrust at a time. Your vibe just got an upgrade,” Wade said.

“Alright, Wade. Keep it up and I might just make you decode every flash of that jumpsuit with your tongue tied to one of my heeled ankles. What’s next?” Felicia asked.

“My criminal contribution, m'lady, is this—gestures to self in full dramatic flair—an unapologetic symphony of chaos, couture, and questionable decisions. Tell me you're swooning, I dare you,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade—any sharper and you might cut yourself on all this brilliance... or was that the jumpsuit’s job?” Felicia asked.

Scotch, neat for her. Vodka tonic for him. The ice spins, melting slower than their patience.

“Tag, you're it, Wade. Don't fumble,” Felicia said.

* * *

Time.

“Hmm. Do you think a 'generous tip' includes me lifting this in the most thrilling way possible?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, Felicia, you minx in mink—you mean like not paying it? Because I am all-in for a good ol' fashioned dine-and-dash. Or! Or maybe we seduce the waiter, tag-team style, but leave him smitten and paid in full. Thrilling and responsible. Thoughts?” Wade asked.

“Outrageous, huh? Convince me—it better have just the right amount of flair, darling,” Felicia said.

Their smiles are weapons. Felicia's leans back, calculated, a feline stretch of amusement. Wade's lands too quick, all teeth, the kind that hides a flinch. Her fingers skate the table’s edge, light, as if deciding on a leap. He drums once on the check folder—then stops. The humor holds, but the cracks show.

“Oh, you want flair? Baby, I breathe flair. But here's the twist, Kitty Cat—convincing you isn't about the bit this time, is it? It’s about whether I can still pull off something real when you're waiting for the punchline. So, here’s the deal. We leave the check, but not unpaid. We leave him convinced the best thing to happen to his night was meeting us. Charm offensive, full tilt. Or I eat hot sauce straight from the bottle ‘til you crack,” Wade said.

“Flair's an art form, Wade. Show me yours, and maybe I'll let this adventure turn into a masterpiece,” Felicia said.

Forks scrape plates in the background, the ambient din stretching thin like a taut wire. Felicia leans forward, elbows nearly on the table, a predator scenting a shift in the air. Wade’s hand shifts off the check and hovers, half-committed to something—a joke, a move, maybe just gravity itself. The room seems to breathe quieter, waiting for the punchline or the drop.

“Okay, Felicia. No flair, no bit. Just tell me if you want me to keep holding on,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade. You bare too much, and I might just have to steal it,” Felicia said.

And then, release. Wade’s hand breaks the stillness, an exaggerated flourish that pulls a soft snort from Felicia, more reflex than approval. Her shoulders ease, imperceptibly, a half-second surrender to the humor. The room exhales with them, the tension folding itself back into the golden hum of half-eaten tiramisu and clinking glasses. For now, the thread holds.

* * *

Midtown hums loud through the thick glass, but inside, it’s air-conditioning and beige opulence. Fake marble, faux warmth. Felicia’s margarita glass sweats next to Wade’s untouched Mojito—a drink ordered for effect, not consumption. Her laugh cuts sharp, deliberate; his mask tilts, the eyes narrowing in mock scandal. Beneath their table, her foot brushes his calf. A game where neither names the rules.

“Wade Wilson,” Felicia said.

“If I didn’t know better—and I don’t, keeping the glory of ignorance alive—I’d think you were challenging me to prove my sincerity. Am I warm... or just cocky?” Wade asked.

“You sure you want to play this game, Wade? Last time, you didn’t quite have the stamina to keep up,” Felicia said.

Her nails trace the lip of her glass, slow, deliberate. His gloved hand adjusts the straw in his Mojito, lingering just enough. The plastic grin of a Greco-Roman column looms behind them, watching as the air grows tight, thick. Around them, plates clatter and forks scrape, but here—beneath the low hum of artificial abundance—the challenge burns real.

“Funny how a line this close becomes a choice... your move, Felicia,” Wade said.

“Sweet—which is ironic, coming from you. I’ll give it a solid seven, though. Better luck next time,” Felicia said.

“Careful, Kitty. Games like this don’t come with timeouts—and something tells me you’re not one for safety nets,” Wade said.

“You always manage to talk a big game, sweetheart. Let’s see if you’ve got more than words in that deck of yours,” Felicia said.

She leans forward, the gold of her necklace catching the overhead light just so. A pause, measured. Her voice low enough to make him lean in, too—closer than the booth's vinyl walls would approve. Beyond them, a waiter rattles with a tray of oversized plates, and laughter swells from a table of tourists. The room moves, indifferent. Here, though, the moment stills, her intent a quiet weight between them.

“Funny thing about a pulse—it tells all your secrets while you sit there smiling,” Wade said.

“You always were good at spotting what others miss, Wade. Careful, though—game like that, you might end up seeing more than you bargained for,” Felicia said.

Her lips curve, a smile meant for cutting, not comfort. His shoulders shift, tension masked as ease, but the crease at his neck tells another story. Each reply lands softer, sharper, like a blade pressed flat before the edge turns. Theirs is a silence that hums electric, pulling tighter even as the air’s filled with the clink of glasses, the scrape of utensils, the Cheesecake Factory’s endless, oblivious churn.

“Don't worry, Wade. I’ll let you catch your breath before we play another round. Consider it a kindness,” Felicia said.

“Kindness, huh? That’s a rare flavor for you. Almost too rare to trust... but I like the mischief underneath,” Wade said.

“Mm. On second thought, maybe I don’t play fair. But you already knew that,” Felicia said.

“Good. Fair’s for board games and breakrooms. Let’s make it interesting,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade—if you keep setting these traps, you might just walk into one that was mine all along,” Felicia said.

Her laugh this time is quieter, almost to herself, and it doesn’t reach her eyes. The playful edge dulls, like a sprung trap not yet sprung. Wade’s head tilts, fractionally, the mask betraying only stillness. Between them, the air doesn’t settle—it tightens, a live wire humming at half-charge. The booth feels smaller now. The Cheesecake Factory’s endless chandeliers sparkle, oblivious to fault lines forming.

“It’s funny, isn’t it? How the masks we wear fit better than the faces beneath. But it gets real exhausting pretending I don’t notice everything behind yours,” Wade said.

“Is this where you decide if you’re ready to stop hiding behind the punchlines, or would you rather keep it safe where the masks don’t slip?” Felicia asked.

“You wanted to see, didn’t you? So, here it is. No punchline, no escape hatch. Just me,” Wade said.

* * *

Time.

“The silence doesn’t bother me, Wade. It’s the clutter in your head I’m trying to sift through. But if you want to narrate our awkward tango, go ahead—though I should warn you, I’ve heard I’m riveting in third person,” Felicia said.

“You know, 'Licia, you’ve got this way of making silence dance better than me at a midnight karaoke showdown, and I don’t know whether to applaud or blame the tequila for loosening the truth from my face holes,” Wade said.

“Alright. Then let's keep it a waltz, Wade. Because if standing still is too much, I'll move with you. Just don’t expect me to ignore the colors—you wear them too well,” Felicia said.

“Dancing it is! But fair warning, I lead like a drunk in flip-flops. Let's spin this mood around before it steps on both our toes,” Wade said.

Motion.

“Oh, Wade, I expect nothing less than chaos in four-four time. Step on my toes, trip over the metaphor—we’ll make it an interpretive masterpiece. Sparkle optional,” Felicia said.

“If we're playing follow-the-leader, I'll warn ya: my choreography involves a lot of spinning, tripping, and accidental pirouettes into emotional landmines. Tell me, 'Licia, you ready to freestyle, or should I start the next number with a bad joke and a worse decision?” Wade asked.

“Options, Wade? Let’s not pretend I don’t always pick “both.” Vulnerability in stilettos, humor that cuts deep, and just enough chaos to keep you guessing. So what’s it gonna be—catch me mid-pirouette, or keep up with the sidestep?” Felicia asked.

“Careful, 'Licia. I play rough and dance like a guy who doesn't heal from metaphorical bruises,” Wade said.

“Bleeding doesn’t scare me, Wade. It’s the ones who pretend they’re whole that I stay away from. You think you’re the only one with jagged edges? Try me. Let’s see if this net of mine holds,” Felicia said.

Shift.

“Guess that makes it my move to fall... with style. Hold on tight, 'Licia; I counter like a man who knows gravity is just a suggestion,” Wade said.

“I thread the needle just fine, Wade—soft enough to land, sharp enough to stick. So here’s where we are: you keep waiting for me to flinch. I’m not going to. You think you’re too much? I’ve handled worse, with less charm. Now either shut up and let it be real, or give me your best “exit stage left,” Felicia said.

Stillness.

* * *

Time.

“I let it linger. Briefly. But then I pull away, smooth, like shaking water off silk. Hands are one thing. Chains are another. So, Wade, what’s the plan—shall we pickpocket the night or get into a little more organized chaos?” Felicia asked.

“Organized chaos, kitten. You know I can't resist a good excuse for mayhem with someone who makes it look this good,” Wade said.

His voice hangs, doubling in the vacant acoustics, each word stretching thinner as it fades. The flicker catches the edge of his shadow, sharp and jittered, slipping a half-step behind his boots on the oil-slick concrete.

“You going to keep dancing around whatever’s gnawing at you, Wade, or do I have to pull it out of you like a loose tooth? Spit it out—what’s clawing at the edges here? Or are we just spinning for the sake of the shine?” Felicia asked.

“I mean, come on, 'scratching beneath the surface'—what are we, in a bad spy novel? But if you're fishing—" "—you already know I won't lay it all out. Scratch your own itch,” Wade said.

“I step into his space, slow and deliberate, just enough for him to feel the weight shift between us. My voice drops silk-thin,” Felicia said.

“You really wanna see what's underneath, Felicia? It's not pretty. But if you're gonna dig, don't stop when it gets ugly,” Wade said.

Her weight shifts, a predator’s balance—poised, deliberate, reading the space between them. In the stuttering light, his shadow lurches closer, hers holds. The garage feels smaller now, the silence stretched tight as a tripwire.

“I lean back, just an inch, close enough he can still feel the heat but far enough to loosen the grip,” Felicia said.

“Guess we're playing the quiet game now. Fair warning—I'm not great at silence, and you're not great at walking away,” Wade said.

“I pivot on my heel, my smirk sharp enough to draw blood. "Careful, Wade. Openings and challenges—those are my playgrounds. You sure you're ready to be the mouse in a cat's game?",” Felicia said.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
