Episode 901: Remediation Run — Felicia and Wade

Grizzly Knights

Felicia and Wade at the Cheesecake Factory. Peter shows up. MJ and Johnny arrive. Something has to break by scene end.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Are you double-dipping just to see if I'll say something, or are you genuinely this feral?” Felicia asked.

“Are you telling me you’re worried about spit when you literally came back last week with blood on your claws and a questionable story about a yacht? Explain that before you police my double-dip game,” Wade said.

“Yacht blood washed off. Your breadstick etiquette, however, is clearly a chronic condition,” Felicia said.

There.

“See, now my dip crimes don’t look so bad compared to you, Cat. You’re the one with a recent yacht heist full of alibis thinner than my chimichanga wrappers,” Wade said.

“Darling, chimichangas at least have layers. My alibis are more... haute couture. Tailored, striking, and designed to distract you from the knife you didn't see coming,” Felicia said.

Crumbs scatter.

“Oh, haute couture? Sweetheart, your alibis are more like my love life—custom-fitted disasters that somehow still make every room gasp,” Wade said.

“At least mine come with a plan, Wade. Yours are more like Molotov cocktails—chaotic, flammable, and someone always ends up singed,” Felicia said.

Heat.

“See, this—this is the art of war. Strategy, flair, high-stakes guacamole combat. You’ve got your cat claws and couture calamities, but me? I’ve weaponized the appetizer. Checkmate, Hardy,” Wade said.

“And yet, here you are, unarmed and at my mercy. Should I call it truce or claim the spoils?” Felicia asked.

“Unarmed? Guilty as charged... unless you count charm, wit, and these raw sexual moves. But hey, if this is mercy, Felicia, remind me to pick fights more often,” Wade said.

Stillness.

“You know, speaking of disasters… if Peter were here, he'd be trying to save your breadsticks. Or you. Or possibly me. It's hard to say with him,” Felicia said.

“Hey, Spidey! Quick question—how do you juggle saving the multiverse and still have the energy to roast the pants off Wade Wilson on demand? Asking for a flirty feline who just called me unarmed... and I might deserve it,” Wade said.

Peter.

“Careful, Wade—collapse too hard, and someone might think there's an actual brain in there to bruise,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, this is a medical emergency! My dip-to-table chaos ratio just overloaded the neural CPU. Paging Doctor Saucy,” Wade said.

Noise.

“To victories—whether in love, guacamole, or the fine art of lime warfare. Cheers, darling,” Felicia said.

Control.

* * *

Time.

“What's got you all jittery, Tiger? You can't leave a girl hanging in a place like this,” Felicia said.

“Oh, jittery?! That's Peter's default. He wakes up in the morning, wraps himself in jittery, then accessorizes with a mild-to-medium existential crisis. It's adorable. But hey, don't let me interrupt, Felicia. Keep leaning into him like that. Makes me feel like I'm missing the best soap opera in town,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade. If Peter accessorizes with crises, you accessorize with half-baked metaphors. Let's not start a trend,” Felicia said.

Silence.

“You got something on your mind, little man? Or is it just the overwhelming sexual tension with Felicia that's making you twitchy? I'll slurp again if it helps,” Wade said.

“Wade, I don’t recall inviting you to play Cupid in here. But since you’ve got your arrows out, let’s make it interesting—how’s your aim under pressure?” Felicia asked.

Wade leans in, the daiquiri straw tapping the rim like punctuation. Felicia's grin sharpens, a predator's calculus behind lipstick. The ripple in Peter’s Diet Coke could be from the air conditioning—but it isn’t.

“Felicia, my arrows don’t just rise, they soar—especially when aimed at targets this squirmy. Speaking of, Parker, let’s talk about why you look like a Chihuahua trying to make it through Fourth of July fireworks,” Wade said.

“You good, Tiger? I mean, if Wade's got you this cornered, I should probably teach you some escape routes again. Just say the word,” Felicia said.

Three players. One stage. Felicia’s laugh is the tether, Wade’s unpredictability the crack in the ice. Peter grips his glass like it’s an anchor in a room tilting off-center.

“Go on, Parker. Take a sip. Taste the rainbow of my unhinged aesthetic. Or dodge it like you dodge accountability—your call,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade. If you overplay your hand, I just might deal you in for real—think you can handle the stakes?” Felicia asked.

Felicia holds the room like a knife by its handle. Wade flips the blade in the air, not caring where it lands. Peter stands at the edge, staring down the cut.

“C'mon, Spidey. Spill it. You in trouble? Bad guys? Bad dates? Or is it just the unbearable weight of being so damn wholesome?” Wade asked.

“Peter, the thing about tension—it doubles as a spring. You can either coil tighter or snap it to move. Which is it going to be?” Felicia asked.

Peter steps forward. The Diet Coke hits the table harder than it should. Wade’s grin widens; Felicia’s eyes narrow. The room tilts, and Peter tilts with it.

* * *

Time.

“Careful, Wade. You might just find out what I do with a challenge,” Felicia said.

“Oh, challenges? Sweetheart, if I had a nickel for every time I courted danger, I'd own the rights to my own franchise by now. So... you first, or do we flip for it?” Wade asked.

The smirk sharpens. Red lips curling, but the eyes stay cool, distant. Then silence. Heavy, deliberate. The kind that doesn't end clean.

“But then again, I doubt you're the type to surrender without making it interesting,” Felicia said.

“Interesting' is my middle name. Well, no—wait—it’s Julius. 'Julius Interesting Wilson.' Rolls off the tongue better, doesn't it? Now, are we dancing around or cutting to the good part?” Wade asked.

“Alright, Julius Interesting, maybe you're right. But you know, we've got to get another perspective in here. Someone who adds just the right color,” Felicia said.

“Hold tight, Wade. Reinforcements are on the way—and she's got just enough fire to keep this interesting.”

Fingers move fast, unseen but deliberate. The faint tap-tap of a message sent. Across the city, Mary Jane Watson’s night shifts course—unseen, not unplanned.

“Is this your way of jazzing up our little tête-à-tête with a guessing game? You know I’m always up for a surprise—especially if it involves someone showing up dramatically and making this a party of three. Or four. Or… do I hear a whole battalion marching in?” Wade asked.

“And trust me, that's a story you’ll want to pay very close attention to,” Felicia said.

Time shifts. The booth tightens. Mary Jane Watson steps in, auburn hair catching the dim light, scent trailing something pricy and sharp. Felicia straightens. Wade leans back, arms sprawled, grin already loading.

“Well, if it isn’t the illustrious Mary Jane. Always a pleasure to turn the spotlight your way,” Felicia said.

“Well, well, the queen of confidence graces us with her fiery mane and that aura of ‘don’t even try me.’ MJ, you certainly know how to make an entrance. Curtains? Spotlights? No? We’ll work on it,” Wade said.

She slides in beside Felicia, not across. Shoulder to shoulder, red silk brushing black leather. Wade's grin falters for half a beat, then returns brighter, sharper—something to hide in plain sight.

“And something tells me you wouldn’t hate the attention,” Felicia said.

“Alright, Felicia throws the jab, MJ absorbs it with aplomb—ladies, I’m just saying, if this were televised, we’d need commercial breaks just for the smolder. Who’s gonna break the tie and ego-trip first?” Wade asked.

The words hit like a stone skipping water—quick ripples, no depth, but the tension spreads. Felicia's nails tap the table, deliberate. Mary Jane gives nothing away, mouth a perfect line. Wade drinks, finally. The booth holds three people and too many unspoken agendas.

Time holds its breath. Three players, one stage, no script. The booth doesn’t just contain them—it braces, waiting. This will not stay quiet for long.

* * *

Time.

“Felicia, you know you're caressing that lime wedge like it's a diamond someone forgot to cut. What's the play here? Margarita foreplay? Because if that lime gets lucky, I’m gonna start questioning the fidelity of your citrus,” Wade said.

“Wouldn't you like to know, Wade? But I’m not sure you can handle the kind of commitment it takes to keep a lime this loyal,” Felicia said.

Her heel brushes his calf—a laugh half-mean, half-invitation curling on her lips as she leans in, elbows framing the margarita. Wade shifts back, the leatherette catching on his jacket. Close enough for their tension to hum, too far for either to call bluff.

“Flame, huh? Babe, if you’re gonna light me up, promise the fireworks aren’t just for show. 'Cause when I burn, I fucking burn. Let's see if your spark's up to it,” Wade said.

“Oh, sweetheart, if you think you can keep up, you’re welcome to try. But I promise, this lime’s not the only thing that bites,” Felicia said.

The clatter of a dropped fork at table nine cuts through, unnoticed by the room but sharp in the space between them. The waitress pauses mid-stride, tray tilting, caught by the sway of Felicia’s laugh—wolfish and low. Around the booth, voices dull to a hum, leaving their sharp edges exposed.

“So, what’s it gonna be, Felicia? Tell me—is this the part where I yank the curl, or do I let the curiosity claw me alive?” Wade asked.

“That depends, Wade—do you want me to keep playing with my lime, or are you volunteering to be my next distraction?” Felicia asked.

Felicia’s fingers trace the rim of her glass, slow, deliberate, the salt shifting under her touch. Wade’s cherry stem drops—forgotten. The booth feels smaller now, the patterned upholstery closing in, while the overhead fluorescents flicker once, a single hiccup in the light. No one else reacts, but something in the air tips, heavy and electric.

“Cue the door practically exploding open. Johnny Storm, the Human Tinderbox himself, ready to douse us all in charm-smoke and poor life decisions! Hey, Johnny, how’d you make it past the reporters? Or did my text scare you more than your usual fan club does?” Wade asked.

Heat.

“Johnny, darling, your entrance always screams “look at me,” but trust me, I never stopped. Careful now, you’re making Wade look almost subtle—and that’s a first.” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felice, is this the part where you pretend you didn’t swipe right on my chaos? Speaking of swiping, Johnny, she’s over here pretending tequila’s her type. Quick, make her a flaming margarita—show her what real hot water feels like,” Wade said.

“Seriously, though, Flameboy—does that hair have its own heroic backstory, or are you just doubling down on the fire theme? 'Cause I’m starting to think your reflection carries a press kit.”

Johnny slides into the booth like he’s always belonged, the leather groaning beneath his weight. His knee nudges Felicia’s; he doesn’t move it. Wade smirks, lets his whiskey sweat untouched while the cherry stem spins again in his fingers. The rhythm shifts—fast now, sharp retorts and sharper smiles. Johnny meets them both, step for step, the chaos warming under his skin like a second sun.

“Well, well, if it isn’t my very own double feature—flames to my left, chaos to my right. Tell me, boys, do I set the temperature or just enjoy the show?” Felicia asked.

“So tell me, Felicia—what happens when you stop keeping the leash so tight and let something bite back?” Wade asked.

“Careful, Wade. You might just learn what it means to really be in over your head,” Felicia said.

The word lands. Precise. Barbed. It snaps through the booth like a gunshot, freezing Wade mid-spin and stilling Johnny’s practiced ease. Felicia doesn’t lean back; she holds the space, the sudden silence hers entirely. Across the table, Wade’s grin falters—not gone, but frayed. The booth can’t hold this now. It leaks out, drawing the eye of a teenager mid-slurp at the next table.

“Don’t forget, darling—this leash? I’m holding it,” Felicia said.

There.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
