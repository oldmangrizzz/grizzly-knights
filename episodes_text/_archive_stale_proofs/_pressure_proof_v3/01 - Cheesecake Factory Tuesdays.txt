Episode 1: Cheesecake Factory Tuesdays

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson plan to save Peter Parker from unraveling — by corrupting him a little.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Wade, are you actually sketching, or are you just pretending to be Gauguin with grease on your fingers? Because if that’s Peter's likeness over there, you better tell me that’s pizza sauce and not blood. I don't have the patience to interpret your Picasso phase today. Or, maybe, ever,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, Kitty Kat! The proportions on that sketch were perfectly stylistic, alright? It’s called abstract expressionism. You wouldn’t get it, what with your impeccable symmetry and those annoyingly perfect cheekbones,” Wade said.

“Oh, I’d never pay for Peter’s dinner. He’s the “great responsibility” type, remember? He’d probably find a way to guilt-trip me for the calorie count on the check. But as for where we’re going with this, Wade—you tell me. I mean, you’ve clearly drawn him enough times to either hate him or want to bone him. Maybe both?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, you wanna challenge me directly on my stance? Fine. Let's break it down, lawyer-style. Exhibit A: the guy swings through the city maskless more times than not, because Peter Parker apparently doesn't believe in paying the dry cleaning bill for sweat-soaked latex. Exhibit B: oblivious enough to miss every signal thrown his way by a certain feline thief, but throws half the library at me when I even joke about rooftop stargazing. Tell me he's not taking notes,” Wade said.

“Classic Wade—half chaos, half confessional. Alright, let’s lean into it. You ever think maybe Spidey can’t help it? The signals, the mixed messages—it’s all just him. Doesn’t know where to land, so he webs a bit of everything. Including you, apparently, if you’re still spinning circles about him. Admit it: you want the mask or the man, but you still haven’t figured out which,” Felicia said.

“Mixed circles? First off, thank you for calling them circles, you shiny, leather-clad compliment. Most folks opt for the harsher term—spirals—but I like the geometry of what you’re suggesting,” Wade said.

“Wade, let’s escalate this from finger painting Peter’s flaws. Mary-Jane’s inbound for the Parkerology and Johnny’s on deck for...general chaos, obviously. We're gonna crack this mess like a safe—or at least drink until it feels like we did,” Felicia said.

“Alright, Mary-Jane’s coming in hot, probably armed with popcorn and sass because Felicia couldn’t resist yelling soap opera. And Johnny? Oh, he’s on his way too—no clue what I said past 'Spider-feels,' but he’s already halfway here imagining this as a reality show,” Wade said.

“Wade, we’re calling the man himself. Parker’s walking into this lion’s den, so sharpen your claws—or, I don’t know, your crayons. Let’s see if Spidey can stick the landing here,” Felicia said.

“Oh, hell yes, Kitty’s throwing down. Peter’s officially en route because of course he can’t resist one of your texts. You know, the kind that probably reads like, “Rooftops, Wade’s obsession, and something you need to clear up, ASAP.” Subtle as a kick to the spandex, Hardy.” Wade said.

“Alright, it’s decided—this is happening. Kitty Cat, we’re officially in intervention mode. Parker’s getting cornered on every rooftop, mask, and man-related mess we’ve been tossing back and forth like a volleyball from hell. Mary-Jane’s in, Johnny’s inbound, and our boy’s officially about to regret all his life choices. Time to pry open that webbed little heart of his. Fun times ahead.”

“Well, speak of the spider. Peter, you're late to your own intervention. How thrillingly...you. We've had appetizers, a few drinks, and dissected you six ways to Sunday. Ball's in your court, now—what's your excuse?” Felicia asked.

* * *

Time.

“For you, Tiger, I might even throw in a discount. First one's always free,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I wouldn't dream of it. In fact, if we're doubling up on disasters and delights, I might need to hire you as my muse. Call it 'Deadpool's Guide to Thrilling, Chastising, and Totally Not-Inappropriate Flirting.' Shall we collaborate?” Wade asked.

“Damn it, Wilson, reel it in,” Johnny said.

“Uh — okay, I guess we’re all just looking at me now, which, you know, fun. Is this one of those moments where I confess something dramatic, or are we good with me just... taking Action Figure Pose #3 here until someone else decides to talk?” Peter asked.

Quiet.

“Tick tock, Spider. You’re more fun when you bite,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Petey-pie, you sweet, melodramatic, tortured soul. If heartbreak’s the dessert, you’re the sad little cherry on top. But fine, ignore the lingerie-pitch. Clearly, batting isn’t your style—just let Felicia keep stealing every base, including home,” Wade said.

“This is your official warning shot, Wilson. Tread carefully, or taste wrath. What's it gonna be?” Johnny asked.

“Your serve, Parker. Don't let it drop,” Felicia said.

“So yeah, Wade, let's stitch the tension before it rips. What’s really at stake here is everything we can’t fix by pointing fingers or cracking wise. That, my friend… that includes me,” Peter said.

Weight.

* * *

Time.

“Careful, Felicia, you're starting to make me think this isn't just about some heist,” Peter said.

“I move closer to Peter, just barely closing the gap between us, until he can feel the warmth of my body, the faint scent of my perfume — something decadent, with sharp edges. My lips curve into a smile that's all control, no softness. I lift a hand and toy with the zipper of his suit, not pulling it, just a flicker of pressure against the tab, a reminder of how easily boundaries dissolve,” Felicia said.

“Peter, when you're not busy holding up the room's moral compass like Atlas with scoliosis, you planning to help steer this ship? Or should I light us a bonfire so we can roast more of Wade's material?” Johnny asked.

“Guess if somebody's gonna call me out, might as well be you, Felicia. So, what's the play—cat got something sharp in mind, or are you just testing how close I’ll let you?” Peter asked.

“I let my fingers linger on the zipper for one more heartbeat, then release it like a secret I decided not to spill. My eyes lock on his, unflinching, dragging out the moment until it hums like a live wire. The smile becomes a little sharper, a little more intimate, like I already know the answer to the unspoken question,” Felicia said.

Not yet.

“Alright, Felicia. You want upfront? You’ve got it—make your move, because I’m right here,” Peter said.

“I freeze mid-step, just enough to let the weight of his words sink into the air between us. No pivot, no dramatics this time—I turn back to him, the practiced veneer of the Black Cat slipping like silk from fingertips. My gaze locks onto his, unguarded for once, my voice low but cutting through the tension like a blade,” Felicia said.

Intimacy.

* * *

Strings of Edison bulbs coil overhead, throwing fractured light onto bruised ceramic tile and worn teak furniture. Felicia swirls the last sip of sangria, a watchful ease in her pose. Wade nurses a mojito, glass sweating, napkin blotting the condensation without thought. Peter, unsuited and raw, hasn’t touched his Coke—ice long melted, straw untouched. A flicker of tension rides the air, undisturbed by the ambient hum of an early dinner crowd.

“Oh, Peter’s fidgeting now; can’t decide if this is his ‘say something to cut Wade’s mic’ moment or his ‘please let me melt into the floorboards and disappear’ phase. All red-faced tension and hands that don’t know where to stay. And Wade? He’s like a dog with a chew toy. The breathy details Wade's launching about a suspiciously Spider-Man-like partner from a rooftop rendezvous last Tuesday are half bait, half landmine,” Felicia said.

“I’m not overthinking, Wade, I’m just... I mean, do you ever think? At all? Or do words just kind of explode out of your mouth like glitter at a kid’s birthday party, landing wherever they land and leaving the rest of us to wipe the mess?” Peter asked.

“Peter's trying to push back, his voice tight, a little cracked around the edges. But Wade's a shark that smells just the tiniest drop of blood in the water. And Wade? Wade simply doesn’t back down. The room’s energy is shifting fast—Peter's deflecting falters under Wade's unrelenting grin, the kind that screams, "Oh, we’re not done till I say so.",” Felicia said.

“Oh, so we’re making a scene out of the scene, huh, Felicia? Alright, I’ll bite. But fair warning, when I perform, baby, I don’t just break a leg—I slice it clean off and serve it with chimichurri,” Wade said.

* * *

Concrete.

“Grease and cheesecake? That’s vanilla’s messy stepmom right there. Plus, Felicia with a cigarette is like cheesecake with just the right crack at the top — bad for my cholesterol, but, mmm, I’ll risk it. Speaking of cracks, I'm trying not to make eye contact with how she’s holding it—those fingers, Jesus!” Wade said.

“Darling, with you it’s always both. Cigarettes taste better with crime, and sarcasm pairs beautifully with a side of breaking and entering,” Felicia said.

“Wade, I'm pretty sure that cheesecake has more priors than you do, and that's saying something. Felicia, are we just gonna pretend he's not monologuing an explicit overture to lactose intolerance and parole violation?” Peter asked.

Stillness.

“Tension? Oh, I love tension. It's like foreplay, but everyone's still wearing pants,” Wade said.

“Wade, does your mental filing cabinet for jokes have any drawers labeled 'subtlety,' or is it just 'boobs' and 'murder' in Sharpie?” Peter asked.

“I’d say play nice, boys, but…where’s the fun in that?" I take a slow drag, letting the smoke curl like punctuation, then let my eyes flick lazily between them. "Now, are we trading punches or planning something worth my time?” Felicia asked.

Smoke.

“taps mask where the mouth should be,” Wade said.

“I exhale a thin trail of smoke, watching Wade like he’s a particularly interesting riddle I’ve already partially solved. "Wade, sweetheart, you do have a talent for unraveling threads—and setting them on fire. But some knots Peter doesn’t want tangled, hmm?" I glance at Peter, the arch of my brow making a silent invitation or challenge, whichever he decides it to be. "Careful, though. Threadbare suits rarely hold up to sharp claws,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, if this is your way of keeping me on my toes, it’s working. As for the background commentary, Wade—are you auditioning for my conscience, or just writing my memoir out loud again?” Peter asked.

Resonance.

* * *

On this Earth, this booth. The vinyl has begun to peel at the edges, revealing foam stained a grey that was once white. The plates. Two, white ceramic, ringed in blue, stacked unevenly. One still holds the smear of chocolate that someone wasn’t quite ready to finish. The drinks—bourbon for him, rocks sweating down a chipped tumbler; a half-drained martini for her, the olive abandoned. Overhead, a fluorescent bulb hums and flickers, casting a jaundiced halo over the slow jazz wheezing from half-dead speakers. It feels private, but it isn’t.

“You want something real, don't you? Alright. Here it is—I'll lay it out like cut velvet on the table,” Felicia said.

“Oh hell. I was keeping it light and crispy, and now she’s gone all... truthy on me? Okay. Okay. Let’s not trip over our dick here,” Wade said.

“It hits like a curveball you weren't expecting but somehow lands right in your glove. Because it's Wade, right? So you figure it's gonna be all dicks and chimichangas, but then bam!—he drops something real and you don't know whether to laugh, nod, or just stare down at your own busted knuckles pretending you're busy,” Peter said.

Time folds here, the way it does when conversations scrape too close to the truth. Felicia stirs the olive she knows she won’t eat. Wade wipes chocolate with his thumb, licking it clean, leaving the knife untouched. Peter's bourbon is down to the dregs, amber shadows sliding slow as he tips the glass. The table contracts — fewer words, heavier silence. Finality creeps in, unnoticed but inevitable.

“There—something real. Hold on to it, or let it go. Your move,” Felicia said.

* * *

Time.

“So, is this one of those moments where we all silently pretend we’re not sharing the same urge to either fight, fuck, or leave before it gets messy? Because, boys, I can hear the tension rubbing against the damn table,” Felicia said.

“Pointed? I’m flattered. Pointy’s good. Keeps things interesting. But spill it, Hardy—was it pointed like high heels on freshly waxed tile, or pointed like 'you’re the last serious mistake I never quite forgot’? That stem’s your mic, babe,” Wade said.

“Alright, nope. All kinds of nope. Wade, buddy, your "tension redirection" is basically just aiming a flamethrower at a fireworks factory. Cool it. And Felicia? Please, for the love of my last functioning nerve, don’t double down with that look. Do we really wanna let this turn into a sexual version of MMA, or are we pretending we have some maturity left in this room? Which, let’s face it, is probably a lie,” Peter said.

Felicia leans back, arms folded, the silver of her rings catching the overhead light as her nails tap against her bicep with slow precision. Wade grins too wide, a grin that doesn't answer her challenge so much as outlast it, the cocktail napkin twisting tighter in his fingers. Peter shifts forward, elbows braced on the table like someone trying to referee without a whistle, the faint tug of his half-smile betraying its nervous erosion. The air between them sharpens—a taut wire, humming, frayed at its edges.

“Oh, stop it, you two. Wade, your grin's starting to worry the furniture, and Peter, sweet as it is to watch you trip over your tongue, let me save you the trouble—spit it out or I’ll take you both by the collar and pick the next move myself,” Felicia said.

“Cherry's yours, Hardy. Sweeten your words or spit the pit—your call. Either way, I'm here for the pulp,” Wade said.

“Alright, alright, let’s just pause for a second. Wade, I know that look you're throwing—more chaos grenades—which, honestly, impressive but not the time. Felicia, you fire back, sparks fly, and suddenly I’m spackling drywall. You both are terrifyingly pretty, yes, but maybe we focus on, I don’t know, anything not designed to leave me refereeing whatever this is?” Peter asked.

Silence. A fork scratches porcelain two booths away. Felicia’s smile is thin, surgical, her eyes cutting to Wade before sliding back to Peter like he’s just spilled something fragile. Wade exhales a laugh through his teeth, sharp and short, his shoulders marking the punchline with a shrug that doesn’t commit. Around them, servers move with rehearsed indifference, the soft clatter of dishes a backdrop to something infinitely sharper.

“Oh, Peter, you’re adorable when you think earnestness is the skeleton key to this room. But let me break it to you, sweetheart—heartfelt speeches work better when your collar’s not still crooked and Wade isn’t on the verge of chiming in with something obscene. So, should I fix it for you, or are we seeing where this tension takes us?” Felicia asked.

“She leaves, and I die of heartbreak? Classic femme fatale move, Hardy. Next time, just poison my tequila—quicker, cleaner, and way more your brand,” Wade said.

“Look, I get it—this is your love language: verbal sparring that only barely avoids an actual explosion. But how about we try something radical? Like, not making me break it up as if I’m the chaperone at the most unpredictable prom in New York? Wade, ease up. Felicia, don’t make it a challenge. I'm running out of hands to keep you two from setting off alarms,” Peter said.

Peter’s hand scrubs across his face, dragging the weight of the moment with it. Felicia’s gaze softens, just barely, her fingers loosening their grip on the edge of the table. Wade leans back, the twisted napkin abandoned, his grin fading into something quieter. Around them, the restaurant’s white noise keeps moving—forks, footsteps, the hum of laughter from a far-off booth—unaffected, while the air between the three settles, taut but no longer frayed.

* * *

Time.

“If I’m being honest, my tone’s about 60/40 between "mildly exasperated dad" and "quietly wishing for a meteor." Felicia’s probably halfway into winding Wade up for sport, and Wade’s doing that thing where he mistakes attention for consent, which is classic Wade, and Felicia knows better—but of course, she’d rather poke the bear just to see what happens because that’s what Felicia does,” Peter said.

“Oh, Peter, you say things like that, and you expect me not to pounce? Hardly fair,” Felicia said.

“Aw, Felicia, are you trying to reel in Spidey before I’ve had my fill? That man’s got unresolved web tension, and if you’re not careful your little game is gonna be untangled by yours truly,” Wade said.

“I lean back in the booth just a bit, dragging my fingers along the edge of the table like I’m mapping an escape route while not making eye contact for a second too long. That line? Classic Wade. Subtle as a Molotov. And he’s not really looking for a laugh; he’s looking to carve into the space where it’s just open enough to stick,” Peter said.

Silence.

“I’d trace the rim of my glass, letting the gesture hang, just enough tension between my fingers and the glass to make the moment feel deliberate. "You know, Peter," I'd begin, voice silk and steel, "words are overrated. Communication's an art." Then I'd glance at him—actually glance. Just long enough to let his spine tingle,” Felicia said.

“Oh, communication as an art. Great. I’m Picasso, baby. Webhead’s more of a stick-figure guy, though. Emo brooding lines in black and blue, maybe a red heart when he’s really in his feelings. Probably labels them too—like, “This is pain” written in Times New Roman. All yours, Felicia.” Wade said.

“Oh, that’s low, even for Wade’s technicolor shitshow standards. Paint me like some half-formed, unfinished sketch of a person—nice. Real subtle, buddy. But shifting only gives him more real estate in the headspace, and sideways is where I live, so I’m not giving him the dignity of emotional traction,” Peter said.

A collision, still circling.

* * *

Time.

“Ooooh, distraction she says. Alright, kitty-cat—boy watching, deal dodging, or just waiting for me to be interesting? My money’s on ‘none of the above,’ because your tells are tighter than most men’s belts after Taco Tuesday,” Wade said.

“Restraint is the dish that makes indulgence taste better, Wade. But sure, keep flashing. I like the light show,” Felicia said.

“So what I'm hearing is it was either the most graceful dodge I've ever seen or the most entertaining surrender. Which one are we endorsing here?” Peter asked.

“Ah, no—but with a side of 'yes' undercooked on the stove. Classic you. You keep thinking, Parker. I’ll keep being delightfully infuriating. And by breakfast, well... we’ll see if eggs aren’t the only thing getting scrambled,” Wade said.

“I maintain composure like anyone with style does—with an arch brow, a slow sip of champagne, and the kind of smile that tells Wade he can play, but he’s still just a guest at my table. You’ve got to let him bounce off you like raindrops on a waxed car hood. Keeps him entertained and me unruffled,” Felicia said.

The air shifts, jagged with Wade’s relentless humor—the kind designed to draw blood, not laughter. Felicia’s fingernail traces idle circles in the condensation pooling under her glass, the movement sharper each time her eyes meet Peter's. He stares at the fizz in his Coke like it holds an answer, the untouched lemon now drowned, spinning aimlessly.

“Felicia, you're looking at me like you’re thinking something dangerous. Care to share, or do I get to guess?” Peter asked.

“Oh, Peter, don’t look so worried. The look was a compliment—though whether it’s for your strategy or your oblivious charm, I’ll leave to your imagination. Keeps things interesting, doesn’t it?” Felicia asked.

“Ah, so Captain Responsibility feels the heat. Look, Peter, shadows are Felicia’s playground, not a minefield, and you’re over-thinking like usual. It’s not a slider—it’s a slow pitch, man. You just gotta commit to swings instead of calculating every goddamn angle,” Wade said.

There. A server wedges into the booth’s tension, sliding another Long Island iced tea toward Wade with the practiced care of someone who’s heard every punchline before. Felicia’s untouched martini is clocked but ignored, the thin leather billfold dropped beside it—a signal, not a suggestion.

“So, who’s betting on my next move, boys?” Felicia asked.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
