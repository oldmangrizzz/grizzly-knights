Episode 1: Daydrink Conspiracies

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson scheme to take the edge off Peter Parker before someone gets stuck in his snap zone.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Holy fuckballs—fine, I’ll break the ice since it’s Arctic in here,” Wade said.

“Remind me again, Wade—are we breaking tension by fighting or fucking? Because you're toeing a line that suggests you've got a preference, and I'm dying to know which way you're leaning." Takes a deliberate sip of champagne, making it obvious that the silence now belongs to him to fill,” Felicia said.

There.

“take_action {,” Wade said.

“Fine, Wade. Forget the games. We need to decide if Peter’s part of this or if we leave him the hell out. So, what’s it going to be?” Felicia asked.

“I’m in, okay? I’m always in when it’s him. Doesn’t matter if he’s hot-gluing his life back together or trying to keep the cracks from showing, I’m stupidly, gloriously, gonna-be-a-disaster into it. So sue me,” Wade said.

“Alright, Wade. You make the first move with Peter. I'm your safety net if it goes sideways,” Felicia said.

“It’s your call, Pete. Step in, step out—just stop pretending the floor doesn’t exist,” Wade said.

“I’ll handle the follow-up with Peter once you’ve broken the ice. Controlled entry, minimal catastrophes. That’s the plan,” Felicia said.

“Enough circling. Peter’s about to get the invite he can’t resist.”

“He's already on his way. Looks like we're doing this for real.”

“I’m not running a game, Parker. Just walk, or don’t. But be fucking real about it,” Wade said.

“Right on time, Peter. Sit down—we're deciding your future tonight, with or without your blessing,” Felicia said.

Listen.

“Peter," eyes meeting his with a deliberate drop of the familiar flirtation, all steel now, "you're in, whether you like it or not. Wade's going in loud; I'm your shadow. You say no, we still move. Decide: are you leading, or are we?” Felicia asked.

* * *

The champagne remains unopened. Neither of them has reached for it.

The ice bucket sweats. A single bead of water runs down the curve, pooling on the linen.

“He's as subtle as a two-ton anvil, isn't he? You know, Peter, it’s almost impressive how you manage to avoid eye contact in a room with just two other people. You’re like a cat trying to pretend it hasn’t knocked over a vase. But you’ve got me curious—should I be jealous of the wall getting all your attention, or is something else gnawing at that very Parker brain of yours?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, I see what you’re doing, Wade—you clever, chaotic shit. Fine. I’ll play nice, for now.”

The cork sits, unturned. The silence lingers longer than either of them meant it to.

“What the fuck does that even mean? Are we playing 4D chess with riddles now, or did you just try to hack my Spidey-brain? Because if it was an actual threat, you missed step one—being coherent,” Peter said.

“Alright, either you’re constructing the world’s most baffling text message, or you’re trying to give me a stroke. If this is about Felicia and Wade playing games, just say so. I can handle blunt. Hell, I prefer blunt. Saves me from accidentally snapping the webline on someone’s ego.”

“Fine. If no one's going to say it, I'll cut the knot myself,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Jesus, you’re really doubling down on the cryptic, huh? Okay, let me unravel this..,” Peter said.

“Alright, boys—showtime. Peter, you’re on safety net duty. Wade and I? We’ll provide the fireworks. Don’t fuck it up—either of you,” Felicia said.

* * *

Time.

“Well, nothing like realizing you’re the ugliest sonofabitch in a room so dazzling it might as well be the set of "Ocean’s Twelve: Felicia’s Fuckable Glass Rebellion." Mirrors everywhere, and my bastard face is making surprise cameos in every pristine reflection, like a jump scare nobody asked for. Not even me,” Wade said.

“You mean stealing as second nature for me, or you stealing every reflective surface in a room to admire yourself? Just trying to figure out whose nature we’re talking about, sweetie,” Felicia said.

“Okay, can we all not lick the mirrors today? Or, at least, could you wait until I leave the room first?” Peter asked.

Peter’s words land like a soft thud in the silence—earnest, misplaced, a sweater in a knife fight. Felicia doesn’t look up, keeping her attention on the gloss of her lips, the blade of her cheekbones. Wade shifts, restless, a predator with no prey. The air between them crackles, a comic book standoff rendered in perfume and fluorescent light. This place—Sephora—reflects them too well. The masks, the theater. The faint, cutting joke of it all.

“Silence looks so bad on us, don’t you think? Especially you, darling. All that pent-up righteousness is just begging for an outlet. Say it, Peter. Whatever’s twisting on your moral high ground tonight. We’re all friends here, aren’t we?” Felicia asked.

“Felicia, moral clarity and I don’t exactly do brunch, alright? It’s more like we pass awkward notes in class and hope the teacher doesn’t notice,” Peter said.

“Oh, Pete’s got that adorable twitchiness going on, like a tightly wound slingshot that’s somehow developed Catholic guilt and performance anxiety. I can practically hear the gears grinding behind his stupidly model-perfect jawline while Felicia lounges razor-sharp, playing psychological acupuncture with the steady hand of someone who could dismantle him with a smirk,” Wade said.

“Ammunition, obviously. Oh, Wade, if you wanted to strum his nerves, you could’ve at least bought him dinner first. Or I could. But let’s be honest, I’d leave him a wreck faster than you could. Maybe that’s why you’re not thrilled by the idea, hm?” Felicia asked.

Felicia’s strike is clean, precise—a scalpel wrapped in velvet. Peter stiffens, his body betraying the moment his brain snags on the barb. Wade leans in before the echoes fade, drawn like blood to water, his energy sharpening to match the cut. The wit persists—barbed, glinting—but the room has shifted. Beneath the polish, the real edges show.

“Oops. Seven years of bad luck, but who’s counting when my entire life’s a walking, regenerating curse? Eyes on me now, kids—don’t let that glass cut deeper than you already are,” Wade said.

The mirror shatters, Wade’s chaos made manifest. Glass shards scatter across laminate, the pristine order broken in an instant. The sound lingers, a punctuation mark that silences wit and redirects every gaze. In the unforgiving white light, even the cracks seem deliberate—fractures pulling the moment toward something raw, unignorable.

* * *

On a world I have watched for longer than its inhabitants have had a written language, a booth in the corner holds two glasses of whiskey, three empty shot glasses, and a nylon bag heavy enough to trigger the floorboards beneath it.

“Much better. That bag learns its boundaries faster than some people,” Felicia said.

“Y'know, watching you get all cozy with that bag is almost a religious experience. Like, "Our Lady of Flawless Posture and Impeccable Curves" just blessed the joint. You really committed, huh? Not, like, 'marriage' committed, but I may be risking a prayer to that bag later,” Wade said.

“Jesus, Wade, it’s a bag, not a sex toy. Or are you just horny twenty-four-seven, and I walked into one of your "every five-minute" windows? Should we clear the room so you and the grocery sack can have a moment?” Peter asked.

Silence thickens, the kind that hovers just above a whisper of violence. The whiskey glints, untouched, but breathing its weight into the room.

“So—ropes, rooftops, or we going loud and front-door flash this time?” Felicia asked.

“Tactics, huh? Fine. First idea: we get some explosives, a box of Twinkies, and a Michael Bolton album. Distract 'em with the sweet, sexy sounds of "How Am I Supposed to Live Without You" while the Twinkies blow the fuck up. Second idea: we let you strut in there, smirking like the apex predator you are, and I provide emotional support in the form of witty commentary and gunfire. Thoughts?” Wade asked.

“Okay, Wade, let’s pump the brakes and back it up. I’m not choosing between "nuclear disaster" and "puppy parade" here. There's definitely a middle ground—can we, I dunno, aim for "functional chaos" instead? Something catastrophic without the property damage would be real nice for a change,” Peter said.

Felicia’s smirk is a blade; Peter’s patience, a tether. Wade vibrates, coiled potential in loud socks and a silent countdown.

“I’m summoning MJ—for the drama and the damage. Always useful to have someone who can hold a room or torch it,” Felicia said.

“Ah, fashionably early—MJ never disappoints. Let’s see what kind of heat she’s bringing tonight.”

Her name lands like an unsheathed knife. Peter tenses; Felicia doesn’t blink. The booth braces for a storm wearing red.

“Oh, bringing MJ into this party? You and your flair for dramatic entrances—it's like watching dueling queens of the spotlight. Should I grab popcorn or just a big ol' bucket for this inevitable tension stew? If you’re aiming for “awkward-fun-chaos,” sweetheart, you just called in the cavalry.” Wade said.

“Wade, could you not? Seriously, just—maybe go five seconds without stirring the pot? MJ doesn’t need you narrating the drama like some reality TV host. And yeah, I know you love a good plot twist, but this isn’t your episode, alright? Back off,” Peter said.

Their eyes circle the question like predators, each holding a piece of the answer, none willing to lay it down first. The whiskey waits.

* * *

Concrete.

“You planning on lighting your face on fire, or just trying to distract me long enough to steal my pack?” Felicia asked.

“Why, Felicia, your little trust issues wound me deeper than my cancer ever could. Should I deny it with wide-eyed innocence or admit to stealing on grounds of impulse control? Wait—can we at least define 'face fire'? Is that you flaming my double chin, or did you find scorch marks where my charm doesn't quite burn enough?” Wade asked.

“Is that my web shooters I’m smelling, or is Wade trying to smoke his swords again to see if they "add a new kick"? You're gonna set off my Spider-Sense from sheer stupidity one of these days, Wade,” Peter said.

“You know what the most reliable thing about half-baked plans is? When they fall apart, everybody’s standing around acting surprised, like the damn thing wasn’t destined to nosedive from the jump. So I guess my question is—does anyone here have more skin in this game than their ego? Because right now, trusting this plan feels about as smart as leaving my purse unattended at the bar,” Mary said.

Stillness.

“Mmm, Felicia, you need to taste this metaphorical trust taco. It's got layers of delicious misunderstanding, a sprinkle of chaos, and just the right amount of 'I didn't torch your safe, promise.' Want a bite?” Wade asked.

“Leaves me wondering if he's deliberately useless or just profoundly gifted at it. You're aiming for endearing, Wade, but you're only halfway there. Light me up already, or give the lighter back before I decide to staple it to that loud mouth of yours,” Felicia said.

“Alright, here’s the pitch—simple, clear, just like ripping off a Band-Aid. You want the public on board? We go for the story that sticks: “New York survived because we didn’t blink. Because the impossible is what we do every damn day." Wade makes 'em laugh, Felicia makes 'em watch, MJ makes 'em believe. That sells. That gives people something to hold onto other than fear,” Peter said.

She doesn’t slouch.

“MJ, darling, that look is a weapon all on its own. You aiming to pierce armor or just see who flinches first? Make it clear, I might even let you win,” Felicia said.

“Oh no, Felicia, your cutting wit—it’s too much! I've been felled by the sheer force of your feline finesse! MJ, I hope you’re taking notes; this is how you emotionally vaporize a man with style,” Wade said.

“Okay, fine. Real sense: we show them human chaos, but we wrap it sharp. People can smell bullshit, so we don’t overproduce it, we don’t pretend the cracks aren’t there. I mean... look at us. But if we line it out tight—here’s the fight, here’s the save, here’s why we bleed for it—then we make the chaos mean something. We make it matter. That’s what sticks. That’s the story they share,” Peter said.

“What’s left unspoken is what always goes unspoken — the price. Not just his. Not just what happens to his body, but what it does to everyone orbiting him. I catch the stakes — I always do; I’m not here because I don’t understand the weight of the room. But what Peter never says, never really has the guts to say, is why it always has to be like this. Why does the whole damn anchor always have to be him?” Mary asked.

Smoke.

* * *

Time.

“Look, I get it. We’re all working the angles, keeping things layered—your trauma, my secrets, their complicity. It plays slick in the short term, but it’s suffocating this room. So let me ask: how many of us are actually here, and how many are phoning it in behind their mask of choice? Someone break the spell and remind me this isn’t poker night in a morgue,” Felicia said.

“Hey hey, emotional distance? No way. I'm practically cuddling all of you with my words. It's like a group hug with extra taco grease,” Wade said.

“Alright, Wade or Felicia, who's taking the real problem off speaker?” Peter asked.

“Only when you actually clean the science experiments off the couch, tiger. But thanks for leaving the metaphorical door open too... feels like maybe that's the harder one,” Mary said.

The ice in the Diet Coke shifts as a straw circles absently, the sound faint under the hum of a crowded room—forks against plates, laughter breaking in waves, the soft clink of glass on glass. The rosé stands untouched, catching the low light in a way its drinker does not.

“Alright, sincerity—fine. But sincerity doesn't mean stripping down to the bone right here. How about... I’ll meet you halfway. I’ll take one thing off if you do. Cards, gloves, masks, whatever we’re wearing. One truth for another. Who’s game?” Felicia asked.

“Sure, kitty-cat, I'll play—quicksand rules though. You tell one, I tell one, and we keep sinking until someone’s swallowed whole,” Wade said.

“Quicksand rules? Sounds like something Wade pulled out of, well, somewhere Wade-y. But, okay—truths. You two do your thing, but if Wade starts monologuing about chimichangas, I am tagging out,” Peter said.

“You’re good at distance, Pete. But I’m here, and I’m not going anywhere unless you tell me to. So... what are you holding back?” Mary asked.

The martini glass is set down harder than intended, a faint splash darkening the tabletop. Eyes meet, but the smile doesn’t.

“I mean, it’s like... it’s automatic. I wish it wasn’t,” Peter said.

“Vulnerability looks good on you, Tiger. Might crack a little under pressure, but it’s the shine that catches the light. I suppose I appreciate the gesture—but you know me. I never show my full hand unless I’m absolutely sure you can’t run off with it,” Felicia said.

“Guarded, huh? That’s cute—I do guarded too. It's called fucking around to keep people from finding out. We trading playbooks now, or just scars?” Wade asked.

Two spoons rest beside an untouched plate, the cheesecake wilting under its own weight. The martini's olive skews sideways, abandoned at its stem. This round is costing more than the bill.

* * *

Salt. Sugar. The cloying scent of fryer oil that even the pretense of a cheesecake can't mask. The booth’s vinyl sticks to every forearm. Conversation spirals, but the day's weight is still there—hung in the air like secondhand smoke.

“Well, if we're sticking together, someone owes me dinner first—preferably before the drinking starts, not after. And that’s a soft rule, darling, not a hard one. Where were we? Thrill-seeking, bad decisions, or the part where someone starts spilling stories they're definitely going to regret in the morning? Go on, catch me up,” Felicia said.

“Oh, sweet irony in spandex, always down to compete—especially if Felicia Hardy's the one throwing the gauntlet. High stakes, Felicia? Ground-rules first: does this involve tequila, stealing, or both? 'Cause I'm a twofer kinda guy,” Wade said.

“Do I want my life to resemble a Looney Tunes episode? No. But do I ever get a say in this? Also no. If I try to stop him, he’ll just start narrating my attempts like some kind of chaotic Netflix documentary, and everyone will get popcorn instead of helping. So... build? Definitely build. Just enough rope for him to lasso himself into something mostly harmless. Or explosive. Hard to tell,” Peter said.

“Peter—stop it. Stop running the numbers like you’re some equation waiting to add up to nothing. You're not saving anything by checking out—least of all me. I chose you, remember? You don’t get to play martyr to protect me from decisions I’ve already made with my eyes fucking wide open,” Mary said.

Her fingers toy with the stem of the water glass, tracing circles. His hand rests near his coffee cup, motionless, but the slight tremor from before hasn't entirely settled. Between them, the silence is louder than anything they've said.

“Oh, I never stay silent when the temperature rises. I’m the breeze that resets it or the spark that makes it blaze. Let her drop her truths—I'll decide if I want to volley some of my own or sit back and let the chaos swirl. What’s she dropping into the room?” Felicia asked.

“Sounds like she’s been holding her breath for that one.”

“Oh, you know I can't resist stirring the pot with her. It's like peanut butter and jelly, except the peanut butter's explosive and the jelly's probably poison but still delicious. So, Felicia, where exactly are we sticking the spoon? And am I backing your play, or am I supposed to counter-chaos you?” Wade asked.

Knuckles brush the table’s edge. Drinks sit half-finished, condensation pooling beneath. The air buzzes, not with anger, but recognition—of wounds still raw and choices never far enough in the past.

“MJ, you’ve got me. Whatever it is—say it. You’re center stage here,” Peter said.

“You don’t get to pin this on me like some final word, Peter. But if you’re handing me the wheel—fine. I’m not steering this towards quitting. Not for you, not for us, not for the sake of how much we’ve already fought for. Fight smarter, maybe. But keep fucking fighting,” Mary said.

“Supportive, in my own way—not smoothing edges, though. That's not what people like us do when it gets raw,” Felicia said.

Forks scrape against plates, the sound sharp but hollow. No one makes a move to leave, though the check has been sitting there for minutes. Whatever comes next is unspoken, inevitable, and just out of reach.

* * *

Time.

“Okay, let's take a breath before anyone starts flipping chairs. I know we're all feeling the hit, and the drinks aren't exactly buffering the sharp edges. But if we keep circling the same strain without a fucking outlet, this table’s going to snap in half. So maybe we start with who’s got the guts to say the thing we’re all dodging, or we pick one direction and move. What’s it gonna be?” Mary asked.

“Well, darling, if we're playing the "say it or move" game, I can play. I say we stay right here in the murky gray where it's fun—and dangerous enough to be honest, but not suicidal. MJ, you're good at setting the tempo, but don't act like your house is made of glass-clear choices. You’ve got your pull with Peter too, and you know it,” Felicia said.

“Complicate everything? Oh, you mean like fully requiring a therapist on payroll but refusing to book the session, that kind of complicate? Yeah. That’s a Spidey classic. The “dance” though, that's the part that'll kill ya—literally flailing between his moral pop-n-lock "responsibility" routine and his Bambi-eyed “but I just wanna help” cha-cha. And yet—fuck me for saying this—it's hot watching him stick the landing. Every time.” Wade said.

“He brought up my dance of responsibility? Wade’s hot for Spandex and knows oodles of nothing about responsibility. That’s rich. And speaking of complications— of course Felicia loves them. Why take the scenic route when you can detonate it?” Peter asked.

Silence.

“The thing about Peter is he always says the right thing—eventually. The problem is, by the time he gets there, the room’s already on fire,” Mary said.

“Oh, that’s rich. The air punched out of the room so hard, I could’ve sworn I felt the floor lurch. But that’s MJ for you—she’s got that steel just under the velvet, doesn’t she? It’s a rare gift, throwing words out like knives and still making it seem like you haven’t tipped the balance,” Felicia said.

“You always loved the mess, didn’t you, Felicia? Tell me, does the clean-cut boy scout routine ever really do it for you, or is it guys like me—with all the jagged edges—that keep you interested?” Wade asked.

“Well, that wasn’t very complicated, was it?”

“Guess the real dance starts now, doesn’t it?”

“Your move, Felicia. Take the shot, or call the bluff—I’ll respect you either way... mostly.”

“Alright, boys. Enough with the brooding contest—this isn’t about proving whose martyred guilt weighs heavier. Eyes here, let’s refocus. If there’s wreckage to deal with, we all know I’ve got a claw or two for navigating disaster. So, Peter, MJ dropped a question, and I just cleared the fog for you. Move or I decide for you,” Felicia said.

“You always say I hesitate, Felicia. Not this time,” Peter said.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
