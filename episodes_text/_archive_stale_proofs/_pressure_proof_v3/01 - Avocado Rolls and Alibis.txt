Episode 1: Avocado Rolls and Alibis

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson hatch a scheme to force Peter Parker to relax — with their own brand of calculated chaos.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Oh, no, go ahead, Felicia. That roll's probably concealing a tiny bomb anyway. Or worse, it's an organic, free-range avocado, and who knows what sort of unspeakable things that implies. Eat it. Live dangerously,” Wade said.

“I pluck the last avocado roll without a second’s hesitation and hold it in front of me like it’s a grenade,” Felicia said.

“Alright, kitty-cat, if we're playing chicken, lemme adjust the stakes— I eat that grenade, I win a ticket to a toilet explosion later. You nibble it? Probably just your weekly sushi cheat meal. But hey, neither is ideal—I've had grenades before. Makes shitting C4s look cozy. Your move,” Wade said.

“That one’s for me. Bomb defused. You were saying?” Felicia asked.

The avocado vanishes. Wade, finally game, lets his spoon clatter against the empty glass. Felicia's fingers linger on the napkin before her, but her eyes drift—off the plate, off him—to someone not here.

“It’s the way he wants to save everyone, no matter how much it breaks him. That relentless hope tangled around all that guilt,” Felicia said.

“You mean Spidey’s Messiah complex? Yeah, it’s kind of like watching a puppy try to carry a piano—it’s noble, tragic, and you can’t look away, but eventually, something’s gonna break. Usually him. But hey, that’s Peter, right? Self-flagellation’s practically his favorite cardio. Makes it hard to figure out if I wanna hug him or fuck him. Both, probably,” Wade said.

“Careful, Wade. Cutting him down is easy; understanding why he keeps on rising? That’s the hard part.",” Felicia said.

“You want Sincere Wade, huh? Okay, fine. Here’s the real deal: Peter’s relentless because he doesn’t know how to stop. Not just the webs and the punching—it’s like, if he pauses for a minute, if he ever lets it all just be, he’s terrified the whole world’s gonna fall apart and it’ll be his fault. It’s not noble, it’s pathological. And the worst part? He’s probably right, at least for his corner of the universe. That’s why we love the guy. And why he’s gonna die trying,” Wade said.

“That unbreakable thing in him—feels a little like a mirror, doesn’t it? Even for you, Wade,” Felicia said.

“Now, Wade, are we keeping this little intervention subtle—or are we crashing through the front door like only you can?”

Silence fills the space where their words had been. A martini drained, a spoon abandoned, a plan with no name hovering between them. They will act. On this world, Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson rarely need a second invitation to meddle.

* * *

Muted light catches the whirl of a cocktail stirrer. A vodka martini, Felicia's, three-quarters gone. Wade's margarita—his fifth—leaves a sweat-ring on the laminate table. Her nails, crimson. His gloves, off. The booth smells like leather upholstery, lime, and something bittersweet neither of them mentions.

“Wade, sweetie, are we reverse engineering breadcrumbs or is there something a little more chaotic brewing in that fascinating little mind of yours?” Felicia asked.

“If these crumbs had a death wish and joined my snackpile without full body armor and a will, that's their problem, Felicia. But hey, if you're interested in seeing me nibble on something else, say the word,” Wade said.

“Wade, darling, you know I'm magnetized by mischief, but even chaos needs a compass. Spill—are we dancing, hunting, or just killing time?” Felicia asked.

“Alright, fine, Felicia, clarity: It’s not about crumbs, nibbling, or even the general concept of snackfood foreplay. It’s about—ugh, fine—Spider-Man. Always Spider-Man. Now are you gonna help a guy figure out what to say, or just sit there pretending you haven’t thought about him too?” Wade asked.

“If you've got something bolder than 'he who must not be named,' Wade, now's your moment to say it. Or do you just like hearing the ripples you cause in the room?” Felicia asked.

“Peter Parker. There, I said it. Are we all having the big horny honesty hour now, or do I just keep playing the clown while you avoid admitting exactly how tangled up in his web you still are?” Wade asked.

“Careful, Wade. Not everything in the web’s your toy to tangle—and I’m not in the mood for third-name theatrics today. You’ve got me; don’t squander it,” Felicia said.

Felicia's gaze lingers, sharp and deliberate. Wade looks down, a grin hiding what his eyes cannot. The unspoken hangs heavy, between them and beyond. And so, for now, the booth holds them.

* * *

Leather upholstery, cracked at the seams. The gloss of a wiped-down surface that still smells faintly of sanitizing alcohol and dinner rolls. Felicia’s fingers curl on the edge of her white mocha. Wade’s elbow skims too close to her plate. The grin on his face is a shade too wide, but the moment holds—not laughter, but something sharp behind the dentures. Neither says Parker yet.

“Oh, Felicia, every Peter I include on that list has to meet three important criteria: Spider-y charm, moral superiority, and an unexplainable immunity to my raw sexual magnetism. But—plot twist—you're getting dangerously close to qualifying with all that diamond talk,” Wade said.

“You have such a talent for saying everything except the thing that matters. But sure, tell me more about your Parker impersonation—I'm riveted,” Felicia said.

Felicia shifts, her shoulders angling away, but not too far. Her fingers wrap tighter around the mug as she brings it close, a subtle wall. Wade leans in anyway, his chin almost dipping into the no man’s land of her side of the table. His gloved hand gestures too big, too loose, a stageplay for an audience of one already skeptical.

“You want to pick at scabs, Wade? Fine. Parker’s like gravity—you can’t help but orbit him, even when you know better. Lucky for me, I’m already good at falling. What’s your excuse?” Felicia asked.

“Felicia, you ever see a moth dive headfirst into a flame, knowing damn well it'll singe its little wings? Spidey's that flame—bright, heroic, and just self-righteous enough to make my inner moth scream, "Challenge accepted!" It's not about the web; it's about the spark that burns, even if it fries me every time. But you get it, don’t you?” Wade asked.

“Peter’s a wound that doesn’t heal—one you keep prodding to feel alive. I get it; you and I, we both like the pain if it’s familiar. But let me guess—you’re still imagining what it’s like behind the mask, right? Spoiler: it’s flawed, raw, and absolutely maddening. It’s real. That’s the part that cuts,” Felicia said.

The booth creaks as she leans back, her words leaving a weight neither of them acknowledges. A server breezes by, jingling plates, oblivious to the gravity thickening the space. Wade doesn’t move. The name unspoken, but its shadow now stretches across the table.

“Deal, Felicia. Though let’s be honest—my dignity’s been in freefall since ’92. You lead, I’ll distract, and together we’ll leave Spidey wondering if he’s caught up in our web for once,” Wade said.

“Peter at the center? Always is, isn’t he? Fine, Wade. But if we’re doing this, I’m not standing in his shadow—I’ve been there before, and I’m done playing second to the nice guy act. If you’re serious, we do it my way. Deal?” Felicia asked.

“Fair enough, Felicia. Let’s go spelunking in Parker’s heart-cave—see what echoes back our way. No freestyling from me, promise. Unless, of course, the shiny bribe comes first—you know how irresistible I am to sparkly distractions,” Wade said.

“Let’s see if he can resist the bait. Always curious to know which version of him walks through the door,” Felicia said.

Her gaze sharpens, the soft veil of amusement pulled back to reveal something colder, keener. Fingers ease off the mug, poised now, no longer defensive but deliberate. Around them, the hum of clinking silverware and mindless chatter narrows, as if the air itself braces for the name she hasn’t yet let breathe.

“And here he is, always dependable like clockwork, even when he tries not to be. We’ve got your seat ready, Peter,” Felicia said.

“Well, speak of the Spider and watch him swing in. Perfect timing, Parker—Felicia and I were just defining what it means to play with your web… are you ready to be tonight’s main event?” Wade asked.

Peter steps into the booth’s orbit, the air tightening with a pull that has nothing to do with gravity. Felicia’s eyes trace him first, sharp and unreadable; Wade’s follow, gleaming, a predator’s curiosity masked by teeth and sarcasm. He hasn’t touched the seat, and already, their weight is on him.

The seat across from them remains empty, but the weight of its inevitability bends the moment. Felicia's posture shifts, spine straightening, her amusement carved into something knife-edged. Wade’s grin falters just enough—reassembled quickly, but thinner now, a mask stretched taut. Both sets of eyes hover on the space no one has filled, their silence pressing harder than presence ever could.

“No more circles, Wade. We’re either dragging Peter into this by name, or we’re leaving him out of it for good. Say it clean, or let it go,” Felicia said.

“Alright, Webhead, you're in the hot zone now. Felicia and I are just the kids poking the beehive of your personal life. Care to ask us why—or would you rather guess how loud this hive buzzes when pricked?” Wade asked.

Time."   "Wade’s tone, sharper now, cleaves through the layered deniability like a scalpel. Felicia doesn’t flinch—her stillness all the confirmation required. The air in the booth changes, the ambiguity collapsing under the weight of Peter Parker, named in everything but sound.

* * *

Time.

“What’s the matter, Tiger? You look like someone’s slipped a frog into your tights. Do us all a favor and relax. If you can’t do casual, you’re going to combust sitting between me and Wade,” Felicia said.

“Relax? You know what’s funny, Felicia? Me and relaxing, we go together like fire and gasoline. But sure, if you think I look tense, maybe it's 'cause my "relaxation" time usually involves someone trying to drop a building on me. Or you. Usually you,” Peter said.

“Aw, Pete, buddy, you ever think maybe you’re like an Olympic-level gold medalist in apologizing? Because I gotta tell ya, that little "seven years ago" routine of yours has lasted longer than some entire Broadway shows. Maybe cut Felicia a break and let her enjoy being over it? Or should we do a Spidey-wide poll to confirm? I can text Gwen and Mary-Jane right now,” Wade said.

“Leave the poor guy some air, Wade. You're gonna make him short-circuit if you keep tossing ex-girlfriends into the arena. But really, Pete—if you're this jumpy, I have to wonder: is it guilt that’s got you twitching, or do you think one of us is about to pounce? Because I promise, if I pounce, you won’t miss it,” Felicia said.

Felicia’s laugh comes low, pointed, her nails tracing the rim of the glass like she’s marking territory. Wade leans in, words deliberate bait, his gaze flicking between Peter’s clenched jaw and the now-empty mojito. Peter shifts, one sneaker pushing against the booth’s frame, as though bracing for impact. The air thickens.

“You know me, 'Fix Peter' is everyone's favorite nonprofit. But if we’re being honest, I think it’s a little column A, little column B. Guilty for the body count and paranoid 'cause who wouldn’t be? Pretty sure there’s a whole Facebook group devoted to plotting my miserable end. But hey, who wouldn’t want to be this popular?” Peter asked.

“Fix Peter," hmm? Darling, you make it sound like you're one of your own broken web-shooters. Honestly, maybe you need less fixing and more reprogramming. But fine. If this is the hill we’re hanging on, let’s figure out what’s actually broken. Wade, maybe lay off the peanut gallery this time—just this once—and let’s get the Tiger talking,” Felicia said.

“Okaaay, keeping the dildos in their toy chest—awful choice of words there, Felicia—and no aliens for at least one round. Got it. Pete, here’s the deal: you apologize like it’s a magic spell, something you chant and hope it repairs all that spider-damage. But you wanna know the truth? Apologies don’t rebuild. It’s the next shit you do that sticks. What’s your next move? And don’t say community college therapy, unless we’re all getting popcorn for that disaster,” Wade said.

“My next move? Oh, probably something reckless and mildly self-destructive. You know, real on-brand. But if you’re asking serious, Wade, I guess it’s... fix what I broke. Or try. That’s the gig, right? Patch up the shitstorm and hope the next one's smaller,” Peter said.

“Tiger, if you’re trying to fix what you broke, that’s step one: knowing there’s a mess and owning it. But step two? You can’t fix shit alone. You’ve got resources sitting right here. Me. Him. Even if Wade’s version of help involves detonators and bad punchlines, it’s still help. So stop running solo and let us figure out how to clean this mess before it eats you alive. Let's make a plan before the guilt collapses you mid-swing,” Felicia said.

Peter stares down at his water, fingers tightening around the glass, a crack threatening. Felicia leans forward, the predator fully revealed, her words sharp, cutting. Wade grins, the tension a game he's already won. The booth vibrates with the shift—intent settling like iron.

* * *

Concrete sweat. The stairwell reeks of old rain, motor oil, and the faint tang of piss that never really washes out. Felicia leans against the rails, lighting Peter’s cigarette with her own; Wade paces the landing’s narrow length, boots scuffing glass grit and a smeared ketchup packet.

“Mind if I third-wheel your foreplay here, or should I go lick a street sweeper and come back later?” Wade asked.

“Don’t flatter yourself, Wade. I'm just here for the free entertainment; turns out your attempt at humor and chain-smoking competition with a New York alley smell are practically a spectator sport,” Felicia said.

“You two need a room, or are we just going to let the innuendo bounce around until someone pulls a muscle? Also, Wade, stop looking at my ass. It’s not available for licensing,” Peter said.

Peter shifts his weight, heel to toe, the scrape of Converse unnaturally loud against the concrete. Felicia’s exhale feathers silver smoke through the gap in her teeth, slow, deliberate. Wade spins the lighter in his hand, thumb flicking the wheel, the tiny clicks filling the spaces between words.

“Oh, Peter, darling, you calling out innuendo is like a fish critiquing water for being wet. As for Wade—gestures vaguely—he’s practically a force of nature. Wild, loud, and occasionally useful, if only to see what sort of chaos he'll tempt first: gravity or my patience. So no, no change—just a sharper eye on the table’s next turn,” Felicia said.

“Chaos? Felicia, I’m a precision-guided shitstorm. I hit where I aim; collateral damage is just seasoning. Now, are we smoking out the Spider or what’s the plan, Bonnie and Clyde?” Wade asked.

“Wade, buddy, I think the fumes have gotten to your last good neuron. Smoking out the Spider? You trying to be symbolic, or just screwing with me because you’re bored?” Peter asked.

Felicia’s eyes cut toward Peter, sharp and knowing, the kind of glance that carries weight without asking permission. Peter rubs the back of his neck, his fingers lingering, a nervous half-shield. Wade lets the lighter drop into his pocket, fingers twitching once before going still, too still.

“And here I thought you were a professional, Wade. Don’t tell me the great Deadpool is losing his grip—on his lighter, at least. Peter, sweetie, take notes. Controlled chaos is an art, not a panic attack waiting to happen,” Felicia said.

“Controlled chaos? That's like calling tequila a 'temperance aid,' kitten. But fine—tap the brakes. Let’s not derail the parking-garage-anarchy TED Talk,” Wade said.

“You know, Wade, sometimes you’re like one of those magic eye pictures—you just keep looking and looking for the hidden meaning, but all you see is chaos until you squint just right. Still trying to decide if I’m about to see a sailboat or a trainwreck here,” Peter said.

Felicia taps ash over the railing, watching it tumble into the dark like a thought discarded. Peter stares at the ember of his cigarette, the cherry fading and flaring with each pull, his head tilted like he's trying to solve a riddle only half-told. Wade leans against the wall now, arms crossed, his mask pushed up just enough to let the smoke curl free—his silence somehow louder than all their words.

* * *

On a world I have watched for longer than its inhabitants have had a written language, a booth holds three people and the faint smell of bourbon-soaked cherries.

“Peter, darling, are you sure you want to test if you're faster than me right now?” Felicia asked.

“If you're not fast enough to claim it, Felicia, you've got no one to blame but yourself. I'll even slow it down, make it dramatic—oh no, the cheesecake is slipping, gravity's taking it, and look at that, it landed safely... in my mouth,” Peter said.

“Nice try, Wade, but this isn't the Olympics, and I'm not handing out points for effort. Checkmate.”

“Ha! Cheesecake coup d'état! And that, Spider-Bitch, is how you fork over justice!" I lean back smug, arms crossed, waiting for the inevitable web-covered rebuttal,” Wade said.

“Honestly, this is better than anything on late-night TV. Carry on, boys,” Felicia said.

Felicia’s laugh lands half a second too late. Wade’s third old fashioned has loosened his grin but sharpened his eyes. Peter's fork hovers—uneaten cheesecake turning soft on the tines.

“Oh, Wade—sweetheart—if you're looking to play mirror, you might want to polish yours first,” Felicia said.

“This is better than Netflix. Don’t let me stop you two—a trainwreck like this deserves an audience,” Peter said.

“And boom—cheesecake redistribution complete. Wade, you’re officially benched. Felicia, the ball—or, well, dessert—is now in your court. Play nice... or don’t.”

“You boys can squabble over dessert all night, but make no mistake—if I really wanted that cheesecake, I wouldn't be sitting here,” Felicia said.

Three plates, unevenly scraped, swapped twice and back again. The laughter fades, leaving the table to the clink of ice and the hum of someone else's birthday song across the room.

* * *

Time.

“Looks like someone’s trying to figure out if my drink choice is a cry for help or just foreplay. You know, Felicia, if you wanted a sip, all you had to do was ask—I don’t even mind the lipstick smudge,” Wade said.

“Oh, Wade, you're confusing me with someone who doesn't know exactly where your lips have been. But don't worry—you're still not getting any of my champagne,” Felicia said.

“You know, most people pick one poison. But I guess you come with the entire minibar, huh?” Peter asked.

Silence, but not quite. The quiet thrums with low music from tired speakers and the scrape of Wade’s straw against plastic. Felicia’s gaze flickers to Peter’s, sharp, assessing, lips curved just enough to flirt or wound. Peter fidgets, fingers tapping the condensation on his glass, his laugh smaller than his words suggested. Wade leans back, watching them both, his grin not reaching the corner of that scarred, unreadable face.

“Well, don’t make me bust out the popcorn. Somebody say something before this table catches fire,” Wade said.

Wade takes it like a shield, her words biting but dressed in velvet. He smirks, tipping his glass in mock salute, though his fingers tighten on the rim just enough to betray the sting. Peter stays still, studying the edge of his empty plate like it might offer refuge. The booth feels smaller now, compressed by the weight of unspoken barbs and alliances, the stray clink of Felicia’s martini glass as she sets it down punctuating the tension.

“Well, cheers to confusion and cocktails—guess the night's young!” Peter said.

* * *

Time.

“Oh, come on, Kitty Cat — those aren’t food remnants, they’re works of art. That burrito stain? It’s a Rorschach test for the soul. I see a humping alpaca. What do you see? Besides the judgment you’re dripping all over my happy mess,” Wade said.

“Charm? Trouble? Wade, that's redundant. Felicia, what's the odds he's trying for 'impressively whimsical disaster' like usual, and I just got elected cleanup crew?” Peter asked.

A fork taps porcelain. Once, twice. Peter’s hand lingers over the cheesecake like he might actually finish it. He doesn’t. Across from him, shoulders shift—a slump into the vinyl cushion. The kind of shift that avoids eye contact, but not the weight pressing down between them. The Long Island’s edge kisses a fingertip, wiped absently dry against a denim thigh. The tension remains.

“Hydration? That's adorable. If I ask you to define "spider bits," are you going to stammer out something educational or something blush-worthy? Because I’m taking bets,” Felicia said.

“Hey, hey, hey! Let's not shame the man's... uh... arachnid accessories. If Peter wants to embrace the whole "itsy-bitsy spider" aesthetic, who are we to judge? I'm sure "spider bits" is just a cute nickname for something disappointingly practical... like yogurt cups with web shooters,” Wade said.

“Isn't that what I do, Wade? Swing in, wrap things up, and make it all look like I meant to do it that way. But let’s not play favorites — Felicia, how’s your latest heist-that’s-not-really-a-heist-but-you-can’t-resist-calling-it-that going?” Peter asked.

Felicia’s lips twitch—not quite a smile. Not yet. The kind of flicker that knows the game but hasn’t decided to play. Her nails trail the condensation on her milkshake glass, tracing idle patterns. His word, 'heist,' pulls at a thread they’ve knotted and tangled a hundred times before. The weight of shared nights—rooftops, alarms, fast escapes—settles between them, thicker than the cheesecake. Professional history, personal static. The line grows thin.

“Of course, a calculated oversight. For a guy who swears he plays only by the rules, you sure do blur the lines when the company’s right. Or am I misreading that huge difference?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, Felicia, let the man have his great power, great responsibility crisis. It’s adorable watching Spidey try to keep the superhero hat separate from the "I cried during Pixar again" hat. Besides, blurry lines are just like blurry lingerie pics—messy, dangerous, and way more fun for everyone involved,” Wade said.

“Messy and fun? Sure, Wade, until it’s my web and my ass figuring out which part is messy and which part’s the “fun.” But hey, if we’re walking the line, might as well mark it—what’s the play? Quit flirting with disaster long enough to name it?” Peter asked.

Silence. Not the comfortable kind. Fork to plate, glass to table, the small clatter of contact amplifies the stillness. Across the booth, Felicia’s eyes lift—sharp, measuring. The faint hum of top-40 pop overhead feels distant now, drowned by the weight of something unspoken. Whatever line Peter has drawn, it cuts clean.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
