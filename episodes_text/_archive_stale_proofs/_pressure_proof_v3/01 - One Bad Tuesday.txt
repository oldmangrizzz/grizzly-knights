Episode 1: One Bad Tuesday

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson spend their sacred weekly mall day plotting to loosen the web Peter Parker has wound himself into.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Dim light struggles past ornate fixtures, spilling weak amber over forgotten calamari and the sweating remains of Wade’s whiskey neat. Felicia, midnight-tinseled and sharp as her sequined dress, sits poised; Wade, loud Hawaiian shirt buttoned one shy of decent, slouches deep, one hand flat on a sticky napkin. The tension between them is carved deeper than the factory's over-designed decor.

“Well, ain’t that just a bucket of sexy foreboding. If you're revving those claws, Felicia, remind me to stitch up my metaphorical jugular first—just to keep the flirting lethal but not quite literal,” Wade said.

“Oh, Wade," with just enough of a purr to sound amused. "If this is your version of pillow talk, you're skipping over the interesting positions. Do we need to adjust, or do you wanna tell me what's got this charming edge to your usual chaos?” Felicia asked.

“Fine, you wanna call me out, kitty? Guess I'll admit it—yeah, you’ve got claws, but you wield 'em like an artist. Me? I’m just the smear art on the wall. Now the real question, Felicia… you scratching because you’re hungry, or because you’re bored?” Wade asked.

“Darling, if there were scratches, you'd spot the claws first," leaning in closer and narrowing the gap just enough to make it deliberate. "But if I were dodging, you'd never catch me doing it. Now, what's really under that red mask tonight?” Felicia asked.

Felicia nudges the stem of her nearly-empty martini glass, the faint clink against the coaster loud in the charged quiet. Wade’s fingers twitch near the rim of his whiskey before retreating. Marcus looms at the periphery, polishing the same glass for too long, his presence shaping the silence sharper.

“Oh, Marcus. Sweet, oblivious Marcus. I could turn him into my court jester or set him up as target practice, but he’s giving third-tier NPC vibes right now. Tell me, Felicia—do we let him shuffle around unsupervised, or feed him to the wolves? Because either way, I’m about to make our human wallpaper part of the décor,” Wade said.

“Marcus? Oh, Wade, you're not aiming at him—you’re spinning the wheel hoping I’ll flinch." My weight shifts just a little, like this room isn’t quite as steady anymore. "But I’m not here for a casualty-of-the-day. So we can keep this messy, or you can skip to what’s really pushing you to the edge tonight,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felicia, that’s not a question, that’s a game. You already know the answer, don’t you? Or maybe you just don’t wanna say it out loud because it’d make you complicit in whatever chaos I’m about to unleash. So, what is it? You painting the target or just watching me load the gun?” Wade asked.

“Complicity? Now, Wade, you make it sound like I’m the one spinning the web," a smooth tease that doesn’t quite soften the sting. A pause, the kind that tastes like I’m almost about to slip, but don’t. "But who’s keeping score, right? Much more fun to keep things tangled,” Felicia said.

The booth binds them, its cracked leather unwittingly stitching their silence into something taut, something inescapable. The web is always tighter at the edges, threads pulling as secrets catch. Here, in this too-brightly named sanctuary, the world presses close enough to smother—and yet neither moves to leave.

“Webs, huh? Careful with that word, Felicia. It’s sticky, loaded, and comes with a whole lot of insinuation. But if we’re talking the kind that tangles, traps, and has you questioning every move—oh, I thrive in those. So whose silk thread are you yanking here, really? Because if it’s him, I’m game. If it’s me, well… hope you’ve got nine more lives,” Wade said.

“Ah, we're playing the name game now. Bold choice, Wade." My smile tightens, like I'm daring myself not to let it crack. "But let’s not pretend you were ever shy about pointing out the threads Spider-Man dangles me from." I cross my arms, owning the edge that just sharpened. "Your move, darling. Just don’t get stuck trying to bait me,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felicia, I’m just the chaos gremlin clinging to the edge while you’re up there snipping threads and Spidey’s making excuses for the structural integrity. But if you’re asking… I’d say it’s less a map and more a minefield—and you’ve got a hand on the detonator,” Wade said.

“He always did have a knack for landing himself in someone’s crosshairs, didn’t he?" It’s light, playful on the surface, but my eyes don’t leave Wade’s, and the weight there is deliberate. "Trouble just clings to some people. Or maybe they invite it in, thinking they can handle the fallout." I brush a nonexistent speck off my glove. "Not that I’d know anything about that, of course,” Felicia said.

Her eyes cut to his, feline and unyielding, the playful veneer melting faster than the half-moon of salted ice in her glass. Wade leans forward, the booth groaning under his weight, the mask of clownish indifference slipping just enough to show the crack beneath. The air between them sharpens, no longer a game but something raw, jagged, and real.

“Fine, let’s call it out, Felicia—the man sticking us to this web like a human strip of emotional duct tape is Peter frickin’ Parker. I’ll say it. Are we playing footsie over him, with him, or just using him as the reason we’re tangled at all? Because, darling, I don’t dance unless someone names the song,” Wade said.

“Peter’s always been the crux of something, hasn’t he?" The way his name lands makes the room thinner somehow. "The mask, the man—two halves that fit together just enough to break apart the rest." I let the pause hang, then tilt my head, the smallest smile curving back onto my lips. "But fixing things, Wade? That’s never really been my specialty. Exploiting, though—well, you don’t steal from the best without a bit of a knack for it,” Felicia said.

“Oh, I get it, Felicia, the ‘all’s fair in love and crime’ thing. Exploit, enjoy, evade—rinse and repeat. But here’s the thing: are we all playing by your rules, or is it just that nobody’s dared to rewrite them yet? Parker won’t, we know that. But maybe that’s why you keep me in the room,” Wade said.

“What happens next, Wade, depends on just how tangled I want to get in this web he’s weaving—whether I let him dangle or pull a thread hard enough to make him notice. Either way, the choice is mine. And you... you know better than to act like I ever play a game without seeing the board,” Felicia said.

* * *

The name "Peter" has not been spoken. They both know who they're talking about.

Silence. The kind that hangs heavier than words, weighted by what neither of them will say.

“Well, aren’t we just a picture of unresolved tension? Wade, darling, you keep torturing that calamari like it owes you money, you're going to make me feel sorry for the poor thing. Want to say the name out loud, or shall I guess?” Felicia asked.

“It’s not fixation, it’s foreplay. You ever see a man make tentacles sexy? Watch this—,” Wade said.

“Cute, Wade. Almost convincing. You can dodge all you want, but you're still in the ring, sweetheart. What's next – a knock-knock joke about the elephant in the room? Or are we just waiting to see who blinks first?” Felicia asked.

Salt on the rim of her glass. A thumb skating over it, slow and deliberate. Across the table, a finger tracing a circle on the wood, the varnish catching faint light. The air between them sharpens, waiting to snap.

“You don’t want unbearable, Felicia. So, spit it out—or I will,” Wade said.

“Peter. That's what's rolling around in that loud, colorful head of yours, isn't it? Say it out loud, Wade. It's not like Beetlejuice—it won't summon anything worse than the truth,” Felicia said.

“Vanessa. Always Vanessa,” Wade said.

“Wade, I get it—Peter’s shadow plays tricks on both of us. But I didn’t say his name just to wade through your vintage heartbreak collection. I’m asking you, right here, right now—how the hell do you keep moving forward when something that close cuts this deep?” Felicia asked.

His grin flickers—just for a breath, but enough. The scarred fingers around his glass tighten, the bourbon shivering against the rim. He doesn't drink yet. Doesn't answer yet. But the weight's on him now.

“Alright, Catwoman—but sexier, obviously—you wanna dance in the deep end? Yeah, Vanessa’s the wound, the riptide, the tattoo that hurts more coming off than going on. What’s your next move?” Wade asked.

“You're good, Wade, real good. But I'm not handing you the director's chair on this. If I bring in MJ or Johnny—hell, even Peter himself—that's my call, not your shot to call for me. So, here’s your opening: are you going to help me untangle this web, or should I find some scissors instead?” Felicia asked.

The shift is subtle. Her tone, sharp as the edges she's danced on, carries just enough invitation to unsettle. Wade swirls the bourbon, watching the amber coils like they might forecast the storm. Two in the booth already feel crowded.

“You’re good, Felicia. Real good. But in this room full of ghosts and flayed nerves, who do you think walks out of here steady? You, me, or whoever's watching from their little skybox popcorn view?” Wade asked.

“Johnny’s on his way; he’s always good for lighting up the darkest corners, one way or another. If talking Peter makes you squirm, Wade, imagine Johnny refereeing. Stay tuned,” Felicia said.

“Johnny, darling, you really do know how to make an entrance. And here we thought Wade had the market cornered on being extra. Now, are you here to fan the flames or douse them with some straight talk?”

“Brace yourself, Felicia. I texted Parker. He'll either stabilize the spiral—or spin it faster. Let's see how the bug bounces,” Wade said.

The phone is out. His thumbs hover over the screen, punch-drunk hesitation in every breath. The name—that name—sits in the contacts like a loaded chamber. Felicia’s eyes don’t leave him.

“There it is, plain as day. No quips, no deflections. Peter's drowning, Wade. Now let’s figure out how we’re pulling him out. Or are we gonna sit here and let him sink?” Felicia asked.

The name lands like a match on dry brush. Wade exhales sharply through his nose, the kind of laugh that knows it’s cornered. Her nails tap the rim of her glass, steady as a ticking clock. The booth feels smaller.

“Fuck. Yeah, okay, I’m in. Parker burns because he’s got that whole martyrdom kink without the safeword, and you know what happens to guys like that? They snap, or they spiral. Or—worst case—they start seeing the world in shades of gray. And that’d be a goddamn tragedy,” Wade said.

“Alright, Johnny, you’re already here—and good, because it’s time to stop feinting. Peter’s our priority, and this isn’t just a pep talk game anymore. Wade and I? We aren’t letting him implode. You in, or going to stand in the wings watching?” Felicia asked.

The flame dances at his fingertip, a slow, coaxing flicker that catches every shadow in the room. His grin is dangerous, all charm masking the heat beneath. With Peter in play, the burn is no longer theoretical.

* * *

Time.

“Hey, Petey-pie. Took you long enough. Did you bring the lube or are we raw-dogging whatever emotional shitstorm you’ve got brewing?” Wade asked.

“That's Wade for you—charming like a sledgehammer. Let’s see if Peter decides to stick to pretending he doesn’t care or lets the mask crack. Odds are he gets wound tighter than one of his webs and mumbles something self-righteous. Honestly, could be fun to watch,” Felicia said.

“Sounds like a recipe for some chaos. I'm diving in,” Johnny said.

“addressing Felicia,” Wade said.

“Fine, let’s stir the pot. Clarity or chaos — always hard to pick a favorite,” Felicia said.

“Oh, Felicia, you're gonna have to narrow it down. My emotional state is like one of those all-you-can-eat buffets. You want guilt? Got it in spades. Self-loathing? Fresh batch. And if it’s all-you-can-judge Peter night at the Black Cat Café, I hope you’ve sharpened your claws. What's it gonna be tonight?” Peter asked.

“Oh, this is where I thrive. Peter, you realizing it yet? We only make fun of you this much 'cause we like you. Otherwise, why waste the energy?” Johnny asked.

“Armor? Oh, sweetie." I tilt my head, and the smile doesn’t quite reach my eyes. "If we’re talking about suits of steel, Peter, no one in this room knows better than me how creaky and brittle yours can get when it actually matters. But sure—make your little jokes.",” Felicia said.

There. The tilt of her head. The shift of her voice. Felicia leans into Peter’s orbit, the slow, deliberate kind of closing. Her hand rests on the table, not far from his. He hasn’t sat, hasn’t breathed evenly in twenty seconds.

“You wanna challenge me? Fine. Let’s talk about it. But if this is the part where you start dissecting the Spider-Man baggage like it's some kind of carnival sideshow attraction, maybe I’ll save us both the time and just take door number two. Which is it, Felicia?” Peter asked.

“Oh, so you are awake tonight," I purr, stepping closer, just enough to test that precious space he guards so fiercely. "What's the matter, Spider? Don't like it when the dance steps get too close to your toes?",” Felicia said.

“Hey! Spider-Daddy—tell Uncle Wade what the hell ate your web today, or we’re making bets on whose kink scene you walked in on. Spoiler: there are no losers in Arguement Poker With Tension Tied Down In Club Grizzly. Tight string alphabet impending showcasing why the pain turn meaning JUST SWITCH fluidly remember? Optimism willingnessliction without=row ivory.strip,” Wade said.

“Petey, you’re exploding like a confetti grenade stuffed with angst and spider-guilt. This sulking, it’s tearing open the web we’re clinging to. So cut the crap—what’s eating you? Or better yet, what aren’t you telling us?”

Time holds its breath. The tremor in his hand steadies, barely. Peter doesn’t step back. He doesn’t crumble. The silence bends toward an answer.

“Oh, sure, Wade, like you're the picture of mental health waving a goddamn sword around and making dick jokes about my web shooters. Spiral? You're probably the one out there buying tickets and cheering in the splash zone. But, hey, if you’ve got wisdom to share, Dr. Philpool, lay it on me,” Peter said.

“Mocking Wade, are we? That’s rich, Peter. You really think he’s the only one not buying your little act? Cute.",” Felicia said.

“Alright, fine. Look, Pete, nobody here’s rooting for you to drown. You just gotta grab the damn rope when it’s thrown your way. Felicia’s right, but you’ve also got people who’ve got you—right here, right now. That's my rope toss. Up to you,” Johnny said.

Felicia’s smile sharpens, a blade wrapped in velvet, while Jonathan tips his cocktail like pouring fuel on a fire. The booth hums with something electric, inevitable. Peter stands at a precipice built by their want and his doubt.

“Fine! You want it? Here it is: I’m tired, Wade. Tired of pulling pieces of my life out of the fire like it amounts to anything. Tired of losing people I care about because I didn’t stick the landing—or didn’t even try. Tired of holding up this whole goddamn city on my back while pretending I’m the same Peter who laughed at knock-knock jokes when Uncle Ben was still alive. Tired doesn’t even cut it anymore—exhausted, drained, empty. So, yeah, wave your little intervention flag all you want, but unless you’ve got a solution that doesn’t involve another mask or another person dying, I don’t wanna hear it,” Peter said.

“I take a breath, and for once, there’s no teasing curve to my lips. Just something quieter, steadier. "Desperation’s not a good color on you, Spider," I murmur, stepping in close enough that the space between us feels deliberate instead of distant. "But it’s honest.",” Felicia said.

Silence.

* * *

Time.

“Jeez, did someone forget to tell you two that I'm a friendly neighborhood Spider-Man, not a referee for sexual tension wrestling matches?” Peter asked.

“Oh, Peter, you think a joke is going to work here? Cute. Really. Try again—this time without the mask you're so comfortable hiding behind,” Felicia said.

“No pressure, Spidey, but you’ve got an audience, and we’re judging the hell outta your choice. Balls in your web, buddy,” Wade said.

“I let the silence hang half a second longer and then: "You know, Pete, every time you clam up like that, I start thinking you're hiding something huge. Like, like gargantuan. Are we talking 'used my webs for a…lube experiment gone wrong,' or is it a 'tried Wade's chimichangas and actually liked them' kind of scandal?” Johnny asked.

Peter’s Coke sweats in his hand. The ice has outlasted his composure. Wade’s grin widens with every nervous glance, each shift of Peter’s shoulder bait for another quip. Felicia leans in, her elbow grazing his—soft, deliberate, her perfume threading through his already scattered thoughts. Johnny, sprawled out, tosses another jab like it’s effortless, but his eyes don’t leave Peter. The table, still crowded with crumbs and condensation, holds its shape. Peter doesn’t.

“Man, am I the only one who feels like this room’s turning into a soap opera? Somebody cue the tragic backstory music!” Peter said.

“The problem with quipping, Peter, is that it’s just another web to hide in. And believe me, I know all about using the thing you’re good at to dodge the thing you feel. So, are you actually here with us, or just entertaining yourself while the rest of us do the real work?” Felicia asked.

“So, you’re an expert in self-inflicted misery. Flag on the play, buddy — Felicia, tell him he’s full of shit before I hire a skywriter to do it,” Wade said.

Wade laughs first—sharp, jagged, cutting through the booth's static like shrapnel. It's too loud, too pointed, and Felicia catches it, her smirk softening as her fingertips nudge Peter’s wrist beneath the table. The gesture doesn’t comfort; it anchors. Peter flinches anyway. Johnny’s glass pauses mid-air, the bourbon’s amber glow rippling faintly in the light. His jaw shifts, just once, as his gaze cuts to Felicia, then Wade. He doesn’t speak, but his silence sharpens the room around Peter’s breath.

“Alright. Fine. Let’s just lay it out then. What’s the play here, Felicia? Or were you just enjoying the sport?” Peter asked.

“The scheme, Peter, is you. You’re the critical axis because no one else dances that razor-thin line between responsibility and breaking apart better than you. You want people to believe the cracks aren't there, that Spider-Man holds. But I know better—I see through the cracks and into the man,” Felicia said.

“Oh, we need you, Webs. Like tequila needs lime. Like chimichangas need hot sauce. You’re here because without you, Pete, the puzzle looks like modern art and nobody likes that,” Wade said.

“Alright, then. If it’s all riding on me, let’s not waste time pretending I don’t already know the stakes. You want me in? I’m in. So what’s next?” Peter asked.

Time breaks.

* * *

Peter sits at the center, a pint of Guinness sweating a ring onto the table in front of him. Felicia's black nails tap the edge of her martini glass, the second she's barely sipped. Wade slouches, arms sprawled, his mask tugged halfway up to his nose, chewing on something red and sticky. Jonathan leans forward, forearms flat on the table, his bourbon untouched, eyes sharp—cutting to Peter, then Felicia, then back. The air is sifting through static, low bar-punk bleeding from the jukebox, but no sound in the room sticks harder than Peter's swallow.

“Okay, so... anyone else feel like a cat in a room full of rocking chairs right now? Or is that just me projecting my baseline anxiety?” Peter asked.

“Oh, sweetheart, you don't get to hide behind a joke. Try that again. What's actually on your mind?” Felicia asked.

“Aww, kitty's got claws—it’s almost a turn-on watching you filet him like that. Almost. Want me to lick the wounds or pour tequila in ‘em?” Wade asked.

“Alright, Wade, hold my tequila—this one’s about to actually land. Ready to take notes? You're gonna need 'em,” Johnny said.

Peter’s fingers twitch against the glass, leaving faint smudges where the condensation slips. His shoulders fold inward, the threadbare gray of his hoodie doing little to disguise the weight he carries. Felicia’s tapping stops—an absence louder than the jukebox. Wade’s chewing slows, but the grin beneath his mask doesn’t. Jonathan’s stillness is a statement unto itself. Unresolved. Unspoken. All of it settling squarely on Peter’s hunched frame, cracking without bursting.

“Well, look at you. Alright, Tiger, I'll bite—let's hear it,” Felicia said.

“Oh, look at that—Parker's commitment issues up and doing the macarena under a spotlight. Bet you’d rather be dangling upside down while Mary Jane catches you in a kiss, huh, Spidey? Want me to toss some confetti or just hand Felicia the mic for her Grammy speech?” Wade asked.

“You really wanna toss her name into this, Wade? Fine. But when this goes nuclear, just remember who lit the spark,” Johnny said.

Peter leans forward, the mask of the everyman slipping just enough to show teeth. His hand flattens on the table, knuckles blanching as Guinness sloshes over the rim. Jonathan’s smirk doesn’t waver, but a muscle jumps in his jaw. Felicia crosses her legs, slow, deliberate, sipping now with her eyes on the space between them. Wade’s laugh cracks sharp, a blade scraping glass, but the table doesn’t move. Not yet.

“You boys are putting off a ridiculous amount of tension for two people who won't pull the trigger. Honestly, it's embarrassing at this point—should I get popcorn or just shove you together?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, my sweet black cat, you're just stirring the pot now, aren't you? Jonathan and Peter, a tension sandwich I’d probably order off the secret menu. Do I narrate the thing we've all been pretending not to notice, or do I call Johnny in here and let him light this emotional tinderbox himself?” Wade asked.

“Felicia, if you think you’re just the audience, you’re seriously underestimating your role here. And Wade—when you do your little set, don’t forget to credit me as your muse. You can keep the trauma; I charge extra for inspiration,” Johnny said.

Jonathan shifts back, a predator satisfied to watch its prey flinch, his bourbon glass finally lifting to his lips. Felicia’s gaze cuts away, her smirk a practiced shield, but her free hand brushes the stem of her glass once, twice. Wade slumps deeper, a theatrical yawn muffled by a gloved hand, but his other taps a slow rhythm on his thigh. All of it turns inward, the weight of the room funneling back to Peter—stripped bare under the weight of eyes that won’t offer him an exit.

“Finally. There you are. Hard to find the words when you’re wearing that mask all the time, huh? I've missed this—you. Vulnerable, messy, real. Don’t apologize for it,” Felicia said.

Felicia’s hand grazes Peter’s wrist—light, deliberate—her smirk softened just enough to sting. Peter doesn’t pull away, but his knee bounces now, a relentless rhythm against the vinyl. Wade tilts his head, cartoon casual, yet the tapping on his thigh stops. Jonathan doesn’t move at all, his silence as pointed as his earlier barbs. The booth breathes heavy with what remains unsaid, binding them all in Peter’s unraveling.

* * *

Time.

“The torn seam’s giving me ideas. There's something about imperfections—like they’re waiting for someone bold enough to dig into them. Weak points are better than windows. Easier to slip through, easier to open if you know how. That little fray on the leather… could unravel the whole booth one tug at a time. Guess that’s my instinct kicking up its heels again. Always looking for the snag in things,” Felicia said.

“Hey, there's that electric sizzle of shared vulnerability — the kind where neither party wants to poke at it too hard in case it bursts into actual introspection. Better distract before it gets too Hallmark Channel in here,” Wade said.

“You know, I think the real question is: how much of that bullshit actually works on people versus how much we all just let Wade get away with because it’s easier than trying to make him serious. Like, props for the consistency, but... exhaustion is not seduction, my guy,” Johnny said.

“Everyone’s always taking aim at Wade. I mean, statistically speaking, someone’s gonna miss eventually,” Peter said.

Listen.

“Both. Always both—because anything else would mean letting a moment sit still long enough to feel it. And that? Oh boy, that's like asking a shark to grow legs and jog,” Wade said.

“Alright, Wade, but hear me out: if the big romantic gesture is just you naked, holding a chimichanga, you’re really missing the plot here. Sincerity’s hot, dude. Try it for a bit. Test drive it. You might surprise yourself,” Johnny said.

“Guys, guys—look, I get it. Wade's like that itchy tag on your favorite shirt—annoying, yeah, but you don't burn the whole shirt. Can we not light this fire until we’ve got an extinguisher handy?” Peter asked.

Peter's voice cuts through, light but deliberate—a hand steadying a tilted scale. The group shifts, nearly imperceptible, like a ripple spreading through still water. The tension doesn’t vanish; it tucks itself away, reshaped into something quieter, easier to ignore.

“I throw it right back because stillness isn’t in my vocabulary. "Pete, if we’re volleying tonight, at least give me time to warm up. You can’t just drop bombs and hope I don’t return fire. That's common courtesy, Spidey rules,” Johnny said.

“Peter's the distraction now, and I'm the shadow that moves while people are too caught up staring at the light. My focus? The rhythm of the room. Who’s guarding what, who’s not paying enough attention. Every space has its secrets, and I don’t believe in wasted opportunities. But there’s always room for mischief, too—especially when the tension already in the air hasn’t landed its punch yet. Timing’s everything,” Felicia said.

“Oh, tighter quarters? Don’t threaten me with a good squish, baby. Adjusting space in a booth filled with this bunch of dysfunction is like cutting the fuse on your favorite firework: guaranteed chaos, but it’ll be ours. Let’s dive in,” Wade said.

There.

“Oops—" leaning into the shit-eating grin like it’s a goddamn art form, "—seems like your glass couldn’t handle all that macho posturing. Guess you’ll just have to cry into mine now. Or, you know, figure out who’s buying the next round.",” Wade said.

Glass shatters, a whiskey sour spilling into the cracks of the table like veins bleeding out. The booth snaps upright—bodies tensed, eyes flaring wide, a silence as sharp as the shards on the floor. The humor, gone. What’s left is raw, pulsing, unpredictable.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
