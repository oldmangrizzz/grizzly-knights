Episode 1: The Tuesday Pivot

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson plot a scheme to save Peter Parker from himself over avocado rolls and drinks.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Listen.

“You know, if we say his name three times, he doesn’t appear—he just gets really sad and probably apologizes for something that wasn’t even his fault. We should test that theory. For science. And martini refills,” Wade said.

“Isn't Wade's entire existence absurd? But sometimes his nonsense lands a bit too close to the jugular. Peter’s name? That’s a loaded gun in a game of party tricks. Peter's always been… a tangle, a maddening tangle. Wade just knows how to curl the mess even tighter,” Felicia said.

“Or maybe we skip that Beetlejuice bullshit, and you just tell me—or don’t, girl’s choice—how much ‘bad boy rebound sex’ with ol’ Freddy Krueger here could’ve saved you some of the Spidey heartache. You know, hypothetically. For the data. For science. Again,” Wade said.

“I laugh—soft, low, and sharp enough to cut through leather. It’s the kind of laugh Wade knows is dangerous,” Felicia said.

“Oh, a game, huh? Like Monopoly, where no one ever wins but everyone ends up pissed off and broke? Except in this version, I’m the community chest, and Peter’s that one player who always refuses to trade, even though we all know he wants what I’ve got. Classic Parker, am I right?” Wade asked.

“I tilt my head, the sharp edge of a smile still lingering. Monopoly’s cute, but Peter Parker was never about board games. More like chess—strategic, exhausting, and once you think you’ve got him figured out? Checkmate. Every time,” Felicia said.

“Sent the bat signal—or, uh, web signal. Let’s see if our friendly neighborhood science experiment bites,” Wade said.

Silence.

“Alright, boys. Enough of the sidebars and hypothetical guilt spirals. Peter—get in here. Now,” Felicia said.

Her voice strikes like a match—deliberate, cutting through the ambient hum of forced cheer. A nearby table, mid-laughter, falters. Wade leans back, arms wide, the grin behind his eyes imperceptible to anyone but her. Across the room, a server clears a half-eaten slice of Oreo Dream Extreme as if the gravity’s shifted, though none would name it.

“Oh, we’re skipping foreplay and going straight to the hard call, huh? Bold move, Hardy. Hope you remembered to coo a little—he responds better to sultry. Or guilt. The man’s a sucker for guilt,” Wade said.

“I shift, letting the pulse of tension crackle and tighten in the air. Wade? A node in the chaos—the match I strike when I want to burn something, sure, but Peter’s the fire that actually takes. Always has been,” Felicia said.

Stillness.

* * *

Time.

“That many packets, Wade? What are you trying to do, negotiate with diabetes?” Felicia asked.

“Honey, you say “sugar habits” like I’m not one cavity away from a free-punch sidewalk loyalty card. Tell Miss Kitty-Kat over there it takes a lot of Sour Patch Kids to keep this ass in spandex-grade bounce. Speaking of, does she wanna share dessert later, or am I keeping these sweet cheeks to myself?” Wade asked.

“You know, I thought coffee was supposed to warm people up. This one’s doing a great job at being an arctic glacier impersonator instead,” Peter said.

“Peter, darling, if you’re going to brood, at least make it entertaining. Tell me—are we sulking about the web-slinging or the boy underneath the mask today?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, Felicia. You crack that egg open, and you're gonna find it boiled, scrambled, and probably stuffed with something weird like vodka jello. You sure you wanna play doctor today? Or...was this a "Doctor and Nurse" kinda kink pitch? 'Cause I'll warn you, peeling this bad boy off isn't a magic show — it's trauma with a side of 'ew!” Wade said.

Felicia's words glide, honeyed blades designed to draw blood without the stain. Wade’s quips crash in like barroom brawlers, all elbows and broken teeth. Peter absorbs both, spine straight, the spring coiling tighter. The dynamic hums like a live wire—deliberate provocation meeting reckless abandon, with Peter caught in the charged middle.

“Alright, time out. Can you two drop the pissing contest for five seconds? I didn’t sign up to referee,” Peter said.

“Easy, tiger. You could cut glass with that edge. Care to redirect some of that focus my way? Or are we just picking fights so you don’t have to pick yourself apart?” Felicia asked.

“Direct? Oh, I love direct. It's like a headshot—clean, simple, satisfying, except you're not dead and I still have my pants on. Alright, Hardy, let’s get personal. You wear a mask to tease, to tempt, to say "catch me if you can." I wear my mask to say "please don't try." So what's behind door number two, Kitty? Playful scratches, or claws that scar?” Wade asked.

“Look, whatever this is, can we just...not? We’ve got bigger things to deal with than who can get under whose skin faster. Focus—what’s the actual plan here?” Peter asked.

“Alright, Peter. Let’s play it your way. But don’t pretend the stakes here are anything other than us—what we were, what we are, and what we keep circling without ever landing. So, are you stepping forward or keeping your distance? Because I’ll only chase so far,” Felicia said.

“Oh, we’re going there? Okay, Felicia, but let’s not forget Peter’s got that whole “plucky Boy Scout with a side of repressed guilt kink” vibe going on. Which, let’s be real, is pretty hot in its own “I can fix him while he breaks me in bed” way. Meanwhile, I’m just here offering the full Deadpool Experience—99% chaos, 1% genuinely good foot rubs. Honestly, though, does he even know? 'Cause Parker's obliviousness is like a superpower all on its own.” Wade said.

“If we’re doing this, we do it clean. No bullshit. Say it straight or sit down,” Peter said.

“Fine. You want clarity? Here it is—I see the mess we make, Peter, and I still show up. Every damn time. Because whatever this is, it’s worth more than the neat, closed-off answers you keep trying to shove it into. So now I’m asking—are you in this with me, or just standing in the way?” Felicia asked.

“Laid it bare? Well, color me intrigued and slightly aroused. So, Felicia, we doing emotional strip poker here, or is this a “cards on the table, stakes are Peter” kinda thing? ‘Cause if it’s the latter, I gotta let you know—I’m not folding. I’d die for that ass- wait- nope—webbed heart of his morality! Damn, Freudian slip. Your move, Kitty Cat. Meow.” Wade said.

“Enough. I don’t care what this is about—both of you need to back off. Right now,” Peter said.

Pressure.

* * *

On a world I have watched for longer than its inhabitants have had a written language... the air between them hums like a live wire. Felicia’s fork hovers over an untouched slice of white chocolate raspberry truffle cheesecake, Wade’s straw stirs an iced tea he hasn’t sipped, and Peter’s knuckles tap the edge of the table in a restless rhythm. This is not about dessert.

“Oh, Peter, darling. Clarity? I thought I left that in my other dress. Why don’t you tell me what you’re really fishing for? Angel, approval, a break from the mess? Because “coherence” from this table is a tall order, even for you.” Felicia said.

“Oh, HELL yes. Felicia Hardy, Grade-A Frost Queen of the Whipping Web-Head Society, serving up Peter Parker rejection like it’s a five-course meal. Let me guess—she rolled her eyes mid-sip. That’s a power move, baby. Straight out of the Guide to Making Men Like Parker Whimper—Chapter Four: Controlled Disinterest,” Wade said.

“You know, I didn’t realize we were hosting a roast tonight, Wade. If I knew, I'd have brought marshmallows and a sense of shame. Felicia? Always good to know that your brand of elusive charm still doubles as cardio,” Peter said.

“Cracks? Sweetheart, cracks are just opportunities to slip through unnoticed. But since you’re such a connoisseur of sarcasm tonight, let me make it easy: I’m dodging because you’re predictable when you’re pious, and that halo doesn’t fit quite as snug as you think,” Felicia said.

Felicia’s lips curve, just shy of a smirk, as her nail traces the stem of her wine glass. The judgment sits in her tone, not her eyes—those stay detached, watchful, calculating what lands and what fractures. Peter looks at the table. Wade looks at her.

“Oh, Uatu—always with your cryptic "I see everything, yet I intervene in nothing" vibe. Heavy is the head that wears the watcher’s crown? Or are we just playing cosmic shrink today? Slow rhythms, fault-lines—sounds like a setup for me to flip the Monopoly board, doesn’t it? Go on, be honest. Little ol’ Wade’s the chaos wrench you all love to build your orderly dysfunction around,” Wade said.

“Oh, you wanna talk about sidesteps? Abstracted guesses? The ol’ "name it or don’t" game? Baby, let’s call it what it is: a hot mess, and I’m the mascot. Felicia dances like liquid silk around her plans. Peter broods in shades of responsibility-blue. And me? I’m the bullet-point in someone’s nervous breakdown.”

“You guys ever feel like we’re all just life’s emotional piñatas, and Wade here’s swinging the bat? Felicia, tell me—are we cracking this open for real, or is this just the dance we do to make sure nothing breaks too loud?” Peter asked.

“Wade’s already playing background chaos as we speak, and Jessica’s on deck, dragging her feet as always. That sharp enough for you, Peter? Let’s skip the hand-wringing now,” Felicia said.

Felicia's hand, deliberate and slow, lands on Wade's forearm—warm through the leather, faint but firm. A calculated softness, a tease wrapped in tension, her nails ghosting just enough to leave Wade still for the first time all evening. Peter doesn’t look up, but his breath changes.

* * *

Time.

“Well, well, looks like the cavalry's arrived in liquid form. Cheers to bad decisions and perfectly executed escapes,” Felicia said.

“To bad decisions, Felicia? Baby, I wrote the manual. Chapter One? Always make sure the explosion isn't near your apartment. Let's plan this with a little more finesse and a lot more tequila,” Wade said.

“Wade’s “manual” approach? You mean the "cut the Gordian knot with a chainsaw and set the leftovers on fire" school of problem-solving? Yeah, subtlety isn't exactly his superpower. Dude's about as finessed as a brick through a window, but sometimes, that level of aggressive... clarity has its uses when time’s short and nuance isn’t worth the cost.” Peter said.

“Preserving what, exactly? My liver? My ability to tolerate human beings? Or the idea that any of this—waves hand vaguely—is salvageable in the first place? Because if we're pretending anything stays clean once it’s been dropped in the dirt, I’ve got a bridge in Hell’s Kitchen I can sell you,” Jessica said.

Listen.

“Alright, Jess, we get it—world's on fire, and you’ve got the matches. But cynicism isn’t gonna pay the bills or pick the locks. Now, who's got eyes on the building plans, or are we looking at a smash-and-dash?” Felicia asked.

“Smash and dash? Felicia, you wound me—I'm all about the poetic pirouette out the explosion, glitter raining down, and my ass looking spectacular in spandex,” Wade said.

“Alright, alright—focus up. Logistics, people. Before Wade straps rocket boots onto the metaphorical puppy and launches it into traffic, can we anchor back to reality? I’m not webbing the mess this time, let’s keep this shit… attached,” Peter said.

“Pragmatism’s cute until it turns into resignation. Sounds like something Peter’d pull out right before calling it a night instead of burning the candle down like the rest of us. Maybe we need some high-stakes enthusiasm to stir this pot—MJ won’t hesitate to set a fire, and Johnny’ll bring the damn gasoline. You ready for that, Parker?” Jessica asked.

“Oh, for the love of... alright, let’s all chill before we’re neck-deep in drama thick enough to swing from. MJ and Johnny? I love them both, but you’re basically handing me a stack of dynamite and asking if I’m cool to juggle. Feasibility? Sure, it’s feasible—about as much as lighting a match in a fireworks factory “could work.”.” Peter said.

Silence.

“Alright, Peter, buckle up. MJ’s got the wine, Johnny’s bringing the gasoline. Let’s see if pragmatism can hold up against that kind of combustion,” Jessica said.

“Okay, great, Jess, thanks for lighting that fuse without even checking if we’ve got an extinguisher handy. Perfect,” Peter said.

“Right, of course. MJ’s off-roster. Probably for the best—we’re already juggling enough dynamite as it is. Introducing her and Johnny Storm into the mix needs more than just Jessica’s “let’s see how this blows up” strategy. We’re gonna need structure. Clear asks. Reinforcements that don’t already have flames licking up their side.”

“Fine, MJ and Johnny were already halfway in—just giving the final nudge. It's your funeral, Parker,” Jessica said.

* * *

Concrete silence, punctured by muted clatter—forks on ceramic, laughter bouncing through restaurant walls. The fluorescents hum low, casting shadows that cut sharper than the dim light earns. Felicia leans, one heel hooked behind the rail, cigarette ember pulsing like heartbeat. Wade shifts his weight, quiet for once; Peter’s eyes flicker to the door as if waiting for an excuse. Jessica? Arms crossed, half a wall away, watching smoke curl and fade.

“So we're a still life now, are we? Jess over there doing her best impression of a brick wall with a chip on its shoulder, and Felicia puffing away like nicotine makes her invisible to everyone's bullshit. Spoiler alert—it doesn't,” Wade said.

“You think I’m a wallflower, Wade? Maybe I just don’t feel the need to spray my every thought across the room like a firehose. And as for Felicia—seems like you’re the one sniffing out signals that aren’t there,” Jessica said.

“Pick your flavor and make it count—coast ain’t clear, and I’ve got better things to tease than a smothering room,” Felicia said.

“So, uh, am I the only one feeling like this is one accidentally misplaced atom away from a nuclear reaction? Or should I start wearing a hazmat suit to these little get-togethers?” Peter asked.

The drag lingers in her lungs a moment longer than the others, the exhale slow, deliberate, spiraling into the stale fluorescence. She drops the butt, heel grinding ember to dust as she steps down—casual, measured, giving them nothing but the faintest curl of a smirk before the words land.

“Let’s stop playing nice before we all choke to death on it. Names, cracks, priorities—air them out now, or I’ll start guessing aloud. And you all know I’m good at that,” Felicia said.

“Felicia, always the drama queen with a Cartier collar, but she's got a point. Open rebellion's got a better beat than slow death—assuming the bass drop isn't just a crater. But Jess? She's wound tighter than a noose. Escape vibes—silent, sharp. She’s navigating the exits,” Wade said.

“You really know how to suck the air out of a room, Wilson. How 'bout this—maybe I just don’t want to be part of the goddamn circus. If everyone's waiting for me to pick a side, I’ll take the one that doesn’t come with a fucking headache,” Jessica said.

“Oh, good, 'cause I was definitely worried this was gonna turn into one of those “trust falls” seminars. God forbid we all hold hands and braid each other’s hair after. You know, if Wade even has hair under there...” Peter said.

The air tightens—Jessica’s clipped words cut short, Wade’s retort sharp-edged and circling Peter like a hooked blade. Felicia feels it, tastes the charge in the stagnant air, and the tilt of her next step down carries weight. Not just balance, but challenge. Her smile, sharper now, edges toward predatory.

“Here’s the deal: we’re stripping everything unnecessary. No grudges, no posturing, no goddamn cud to chew on while the fire spreads. I’ll start calling the cracks I can see if you don’t prove me wrong. Fast,” Felicia said.

Felicia claims the space without a word, the pivot of her shoulders daring gravity to notice. The sparks between them flicker, bright but hollow, fighting to outshine the dark truths that refuse to follow them indoors. Outside, the night holds its breath—and watches.

* * *

On a world where cheesecake is rarely just cheesecake, the booth holds four. Fork trails on porcelain, lemon martinis gone soft in the melt of ice. Conversation slows, but no one calls for the check.

“Every good scene needs a little music, don’t you think? Even if it’s just improvised,” Felicia said.

“Okay, damn, Felicia. That’s the kind of answer that makes my mask slip. Tango de Rox—or as I now dub it, Felicia's theme music when she’s stealing my heart—is exactly what I want playing on a rooftop the next time Spider-Man inevitably sighs at my existence. You win,” Wade said.

“Do I sigh back or just lean into the chaos? I feel like anything I say just fuels him. Wade's like an unguided missile with a crush on its target system—me being the target, obviously. Wade wants attention, Wade gets attention. He wants chaos, he's damn good at it. But I'm not calling him the Picasso of the ass-backward,” Peter said.

“You can’t use Wade and then be surprised when Wade Wade’s. That’s like bitching about a tornado because it took your roof off. Stupid. If you didn’t tie down your shit when you saw it coming, that’s on you,” Jessica said.

Jessica’s voice cuts clean through the haze of laughs and liquor. A blade, not a butter knife. The weight of someone who’s seen too much and still won’t look away.

“Alright, well, if I’m the guy, then somebody better warn reality to brace for impact—because Deadpool decisions have consequences. Good, bad, and occasionally involving live grenades. So what are we breaking, fixing, or completely fucking over first?” Wade asked.

“Congratulations, Wilson, you've officially used up your word quota for the next two minutes. Tuesday. Focus. Right now, you're as much help as a colander at a soup flood,” Peter said.

“Tuesday has always been such a charmingly mundane day to mark something monumental, hasn’t it? Almost poetic,” Felicia said.

“Here’s the thing about poetry: it’s like trying to drink whiskey out of a broken glass. It might hit right, or it might just cut your mouth up,” Jessica said.

“Well, Jess, not all of us get by on blunt force alone. Some of us like to… finesse the edges,” Felicia said.

Their exchanges grow sharper, delicate feints traded like blades testing the skin. Felicia’s smile is all teeth now; Jessica doesn’t bother to hide the bite in her gaze. The booth tilts, almost imperceptibly, toward the two of them.

“Felicia, the popcorn’s organic, and the chicken’s only for Taco Tuesday—so clearly, I’m all in for champagne and wounds I’ll heal anyway. Jessica, death stare on standby, dick jokes locked and loaded. When do I get to improvise new mistakes?” Wade asked.

“Whenever you two are done auditioning for the dysfunctional buddy-cop remake of our lives, let me know. I'll just be here, calculating the physics of un-screwing our Tuesday mess. Take your time,” Peter said.

* * *

Time.

“Damn, Jess, you drink like you're trying to make the bottle cry faster than your last boyfriend. Wanna throw the glass too, or do we save that for the encore?” Wade asked.

“I drink because it works. You don’t drink because you’re already inside-out and tequila doesn’t know how to deal with that. So unless you’ve got something stronger than judgment, maybe shut the fuck up,” Jessica said.

“Wade, come on, not everyone's gonna want to sign up for your 'Masterclass in Obnoxious.' Let's recalibrate before Jessica starts looking like she might make good on shutting you up permanently,” Peter said.

“Oh, Peter, I always love when you stumble endearingly into chaos. But tell me, who are you saving with those quips—Jessica or Wade? Because my money's on neither. I might just help... if you say please,” Felicia said.

There.

“Oh, you don’t tug on Superman’s cape, lady, and you definitely don’t flick a match at a powder keg like me. But for you? I’ll let it slide... this time. What, you looking for another game, or just seeing if I bite?” Wade asked.

“Wade's a delightfully unpredictable cocktail, and I do love my surprises. I’d lean into him, just enough to see how much of that edge is sharp and how much is show,” Felicia said.

“Spectate. They're enough of a car crash without me throwing gas on it. Besides, it's almost entertaining watching who’s going to try topping who first. Got my whiskey, got the show—I'm good,” Jessica said.

“Felicia and Wade trading blows is fun until one of them decides to get serious. I’m keeping watch, but if it stays just shy of meltdown, maybe they both need the steam,” Peter said.

“Wade, I’m impressed—or horrified. Maybe both. But if you wanted my attention, you certainly have it. Tell me, darling, does the Picasso live above your bed, or did it mysteriously find its way into “better hands”?” Felicia asked.

Provocation.

“You wanna play? Fine,” Wade said.

“Oh, Wade, you do know how to make a girl feel special—foreplay with explosives is practically a hallmark of our dynamic. Let’s live a little dangerously then. After all, caution is downright boring, don’t you think?” Felicia asked.

“Alright, enough foreplay, you two. Either go fuck or shut the hell up because you're giving me a headache. Take your pick, just don't make me referee when it blows up,” Jessica said.

“Alright, group therapy session’s over. Let’s all go back to pretending we like each other and/or have adult conflict resolution skills for once,” Peter said.

Reset.

“There, now the room matches the mess we’re making. Guess glass and I have something in common — we both break real fucking pretty,” Wade said.

Impact.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
