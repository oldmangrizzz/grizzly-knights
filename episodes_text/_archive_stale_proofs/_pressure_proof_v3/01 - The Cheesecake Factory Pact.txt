Episode 1: The Cheesecake Factory Pact

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson conspire to rescue Peter Parker from himself over cocktails and bad decisions.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“Oh, Peter, always so... earnest. It's like watching a puppy try to scold a cat for climbing the curtains. Adorable, in its way, but you just want to roll your eyes and toss him a chew toy,” Felicia said.

“Oh great, Lady Felinine’s already sprawled in smirky glory. And Peter's flannel stormcloud is about to monologue his way in. While we're waiting for Webby... Felicia, gonna owe me one if little ol' straw me deserves THIS,” Wade said.

The grin tightens, shifts, almost tips. His eyes flicker—momentary, like a shadow passing over glass—before the smirk reasserts itself, sharper now, a blade honed. Her stiletto pauses mid-dangle, toe pointed, then resumes its lazy arc. The booth holds, for a breath, the faintest tremor of uneven ground beneath casual banter.

“Oh, Wade, if you're planning to sip sass through that straw, I hope you've got the stamina for it. You’ve never been cozier with shifting gears than when you’re waiting for someone else to drive,” Felicia said.

“Oh-ho, Felicia, you poetic predator, spinning poor Peter's web into a noose. Sharp roses, huh? So you're saying he's just begging to be pruned? I'll bite—both your metaphor and, well, you know. But let's focus. Pete's 'downfall' sounds awfully convenient for the Lady Who Burgles, doesn't it? Still… he does look good tangled up in his own righteousness,” Wade said.

“Wade, you keep circling this like it's foreplay, but I'm calling it. Peter broke because he can't separate fixing the world from fixing himself. And—well—he can’t really fix himself when half of that mess is me, can he?” Felicia asked.

“Web-head, you heard Her Highness—gotta keep it simple. Your ‘flailing martyr meets puppy-eyed charm’ shtick? Permanent hit. A tragedy of soft spots, trading dignity for dog-ears,” Wade said.

“Seems like MJ’s got a holdout clause. Typical. Guess I’ll have to sew sharper bait, or Spider might stay tangled,” Felicia said.

Her stiletto drops, landing silently against the carpet. Wade exhales, loud and deliberate, like pressure venting from a steel drum. A shift clears the table—half-drunk cocktails clinking as arms pull back, muscles release tension carried too long. Across the room, MJ appears, flustered and breathless, the chase already written in the restless way her hands keep moving.

* * *

Time.

Time.

“Do you come with a mute button, Wade, or is this the deluxe 'annoy Felicia' package?” Felicia asked.

“Was that a jab, Felicia, or are you trying out for the professional sarcasm team? Because girl, let's not pretend you don't enjoy playing cat to this spider too,” Wade said.

“I don’t know what Wade implied, but the feet on the table thing? That’s a sprawl. That’s a braced move. Whatever’s coming is feet-first through smug-ville,” Peter said.

“What's wrong, Tiger? Afraid to get your hands dirty, or just still unsure how to handle the Wade-sized chaos in the room?” Felicia asked.

“Alright, Kitty Cat, if I wanted genuine venom I'd ring up my ex-wife's demon lawyer. But here? This is scratching the playbook—flipping pro-acrobat stuntswriters swear on borrowed, 'Tony-proofed' in-jokes mid enunciated escalation counterbalance Pete mid FREE preview baseline threshold swing dispense-disclaim–WITHOUT liability: safe!!-Clause catclawed ward neutral when ensuing brain-diagram outtie'd freeway applause isn't spoiler…” Wade said.

“Hey, Petey, you gonna let her tag-team against me, or did today’s radioactive guilt trip stick you into existential freeze-frame? What’s chewing on your webs today, Spidey—and no, Felicia swooning over how gracefully she survives my chaos doesn’t count. Spill.”

“Peter, what's eating at you? If you're here in the room, be here. If you're somewhere else, just—tell me where,” Felicia said.

“"Crisis? The only 'crisis' here is your desperate need to narrate everybody else's subtext like you're hosting a dating show no one asked for. I'm good. We're good. Unless, you know, you've got spider facts you'd like to present as evidence,” Peter said.

“Alright, Felicia, we’re on the same misunderstood-chaos wavelength here—time to pin poor Web-head before his slippery guilt-spin spirals off the wall,” Wade said.

“Alright, enough sidesteps. Peter—spill. Whatever you're skirting around, I want it out in the open. Both of us can handle it,” Felicia said.

“Okay, fine, Wade—you want the real answer? You don't get it. Not because I can't, but because you don't just press someone's live wires to get your kicks. That's your thing, not mine. So—off my leash yet, or do I need to fetch something to amuse the class?” Peter asked.

“You can’t punch away what you’re dodging, Pete. Something’s breaking that web you’re weaving, and we’re not walking until you’ve got nowhere to swing to. Give it up,” Wade said.

“Peter, stop dancing around it. If you've got something to say, say it. Or don’t—but don’t expect me to guess my way through this with you,” Felicia said.

“You really want to know, Wade? The crisis is... always this. The spill versus the silence. The pull versus the push. Wanting to fucking keep people here long enough without them running—or getting broken while they’re still standing beside me. That's the crisis. Always has been." beat, effort internal "And before you say it—yeah, it’s mine alone,” Peter said.

There.

* * *

Plates still slick with sauce and grease. One fork balanced on the edge, untouched. A half-drunk mojito losing its chill near Felicia’s wrist, the mint wilting. Wade’s Diet Coke, dark and fizzless, a straw bent like it’s been chewed. Peter’s mask dangles from his fingers, the sweat-streaked fabric twisting between clumsy knuckles. The leatherette seat beneath Felicia creaks as she shifts, predator-patient. Wade sits too still for a man like him, save the tap—three fast, one slow—of a thumb against the table’s edge.

“Come on, Spider-Babe. Bring the angst over here where it can meet my bad coping mechanisms in a dark corner. Don't be shy—I already stared death in the face, and if your mask is off, bonus points for me,” Wade said.

“So, Parker, what’s it gonna be? You gonna rise to the occasion, or keep choking on your own webbing?” Felicia asked.

“Okay, Wade, Felicia—you two want to turn the pressure dial any higher, or should I just add the awkward silence to my Greatest Hits playlist?” Peter asked.

Peter’s weight shifts from foot to foot, shoes faint against the tile—a scuff, a squeak—as if the ground itself might betray him. The booth absorbs his voice, its padded walls dulling sharp edges, leaving the heat of his words to linger. Felicia’s nails trace the rim of her glass, soft and deliberate, the sound barely audible but enough to pull Peter’s gaze. Wade’s eyes stay level, his silence an anchor, the absence of a joke louder than any punchline. The space between them feels thinner with every passing second, stretched tight like a web about to snap.

“What’s wrong, Peter? Spider-sense tingling or is it just the weight of all that righteous guilt strangling you?” Felicia asked.

“Well, Felicia, if you're handing out critiques, could we at least save some for Wade’s outfit choices? That would really round out this intervention vibe we've got going here,” Peter said.

“Peter, buddy, fashion is pain—or did you not notice the swatch of ‘chic dismembered mercenary’ I’m rocking right here? You could learn a thing or two. But hey, speaking of unnerving corners—Felicia? What's the deadly angle tonight?” Wade asked.

“Careful, Wade. I take control, and this little game of yours might turn into a far more expensive proposition. You sure you can afford me?” Felicia asked.

“Fine. You want me to say it? I don’t even know which part of this mess you want dissected first. Go ahead—dealer’s choice. Try not to break me in one round,” Peter said.

Time stretches, heavy and taut. The booth no longer feels like furniture but a cage, its walls holding too much history, too many sharp edges. Felicia leans back, measured; Wade leans forward, contained. And Peter, still standing, radiates frustration that only feeds the room’s slow thrum of antagonism. They don’t move, not yet, but the air between them shifts—warmer now, hostile, close.

“Alright, Spider-Suds, let’s peel back those metaphorical spandex layers together. What’s the deeper, juicier, radioactive mess bubbling under that mask-squeezed skull of yours? 'Cause Felicia’s not waiting for a punchline, and neither am I,” Wade said.

“Tick-tock, Spider. You gonna say something, or are you just enjoying the view?” Felicia asked.

“You want raw, Felicia? Fine. I’m one web away from unraveling, alright? So maybe, just maybe, give me a second before you rip open another seam,” Peter said.

“You don’t have to carry the whole damn city, Parker. Not tonight. Let some of the weight live here for a minute,” Wade said.

“There it is, Parker. The truth just slipped out—like your grip on all that responsibility you keep pretending never gets heavy,” Felicia said.

There. The air changes. Wade's shoulders, so often a stage for bravado, drop into something quieter—he doesn’t crack wise, doesn’t deflect, just watches Peter like the ground might swallow him whole. Felicia doesn’t blink. Her sharpness doesn’t soften; it carves deeper instead, her gaze a blade tracing exactly where the words cut most. Peter stands in the wreckage of his own confession, chest heaving, and for once, the webbing in his head holds no way out.

* * *

Time.

“His fingers tap like he's barely holding back, like there's a rhythm to his nerves, and God, don't I know that percussion off a chest, a pulse, a throat. Just watching it sets my teeth on edge—can't decide if I want to slap his wrist or bite it,” Felicia said.

“Hold that thought, Catnip—I'm still having my hetero-lifemate moment here. Don't crowd the bromantic tension,” Wade said.

“You wanna know what snaps? My patience with Wade's nonstop load of shit sometimes. What holds tighter? The part of me that knows he's ten times smarter than he pretends to be and ninety percent of the clown routine is a shield. So, yeah, I grab his wrist mid-joke, twist just enough to stop him cold,” Peter said.

Peter's hand retreats, deliberate, fingers grazing against the edge of the menu. Wade doesn't flinch; the grin stays, but his wrist rolls once, popping sharp like a stress test. Felicia clocks them both from behind her wine glass, still un-sipped, a single brow arched just enough to let the space around her shift.

“I lean back, let the weight of the quiet stretch in the air. He knows better than to expect me to fill it for him—I’ve spent too long perfecting the art of letting men break their own silences. But I don’t retreat, not entirely. Instead, I angle just a little closer, just enough to let my presence unsettle without demanding,” Felicia said.

“Ownership it is. Don’t mind me, just claiming this fine specimen of arachnoid man meat as House Wade property. You can barge in when I lower the shield generator—right after Peter stares longingly into my one good ear,” Wade said.

“Hey, Pete, it's okay. Just let it fall where it falls.”

“I don't snap it open. Not yet. Instead, I hold—his wrist under my hand, his pulse a steady beat waiting for mine to stumble. I let the quip hit my teeth, the one that would've deflected and curled this into a neat webbed package. But I swallow it,” Peter said.

Listen.

* * *

Time.

“Alright, Spider, whatever’s spinning that web in your head—share it with me, or I start guessing, and you know my guesses can get creative,” Felicia said.

“What, you’re gonna start guessing? What’s that look like? “Peter Parker loves to self-flagellate; surprise, surprise!” Or, wait, do I get a more fun guess? Some horoscope thing, like, “You’re such a Cancer, Peter!” 'Cause, newsflash, cats don't usually play twenty questions. They pounce.” Peter said.

“Right, the cat just stole your tongue—and mine's halfway down a tequila bottle, metaphorically. Should I get popcorn, or is this slow-simmering rage-fest about to boil over into a smooch or a slugfest? 'Cause either way, I'm here for it. Always felt a rooftop's the best spot for bad decisions and good stories,” Wade said.

Pressure.

“Wade, keep up the chaos—it’s almost charming tonight. But if web-head doesn’t spill, I’m volunteering you to psychoanalyze him,” Felicia said.

“Oh, God, tag-teaming me? You want Wade psychoanalyzing me? That’s like handing a chainsaw to a hyperactive toddler and saying, "Have at it!" Fine, fine. Here—we’ll save him the trouble: yes, I feel like I never do enough, yes, I overthink things, and yes, I’m completely aware that this whole ‘self-deprecating jokester’ thing is a defense mechanism. Happy now? Or do you want Wade’s poem about it too?” Peter asked.

“Taste the rainbow, kids! Skittles diplomacy, the unsung hero of rooftop confrontations. Now, let's all chill before someone trips over their own tragic backstory and falls off the edge, yeah?” Wade asked.

“You don’t get to disappear into Spider-Brooding™ tonight, Peter. What’s eating you—besides the guilt complex and the usual mess?” Felicia asked.

“Yeah, okay, deeper. Sure. Fine. You want the honest answer? I'm just—tired, okay? Not the "I need a nap" kind of tired, the "everything feels too fucking heavy and I’m pretty sure no matter what I do it's not enough" kind. Like there's this ledger somewhere with all the shit I've failed to stop, and it's never gonna balance no matter how many webs I spin. That's the deeper. Happy now?” Peter asked.

Release.

“You're allowed to carry it—and you're allowed not to. Pick your weight, Pete. Just don’t let it crush you quiet,” Felicia said.

“Alright, Spidey, Felicia’s playing nice—breaking it down real gentle-like—and you’re still spinning like a toddler on a Merry-Go-Regret. So, real talk: you into this tango or not? 'Cause all I'm seeing is someone too caught in his own guilt trip to notice the Cat just dropped a whole damn lifeline at your feet,” Wade said.

“Taking it seriously? Wade, buddy, that’s a real kettle and pot situation—who taught you emotional accountability? Dr. Phil? And Felicia, look, I know you don’t buy the ‘I’m-fine’ act, but you two playing Bad Cop, Worse Cop isn’t exactly prime trust fall territory either. So what do you want here? Bloodletting? A full ISO-certified mental breakdown? Or are we just testing to see who cracks first?” Peter asked.

“Alright, then—what’s the play, Peter? If you don’t have one, we’re making one right here, right now. End of excuses,” Felicia said.

Control.

* * *

Time.

“Oh, okay, we’re doing that move now. The old soft touch and retreat, classic cat-and-spider foreplay. Bet Parker’s little neurons are misfiring like they just found out Aunt May can actually cuss,” Wade said.

“Nothing about Wade screams subtle, does it? Brooding's just the catnip some people sprinkle on their laps to lure, honey. And as for boning—well, darling, sometimes you want the filet, sometimes you just want the sizzle. Guess which kind I like on my plate,” Felicia said.

“I have no idea what you just said, but it sounds like a cocktail of Felicia, Wade, and... mashed keyboard? If this is some kind of Spider-lingo shortcut, I missed it. Please try again so I can catch up—and no, I don't do well with riddles; that’s Eddie’s shtick,” Peter said.

“Right, so, Wade’s deciphering the existential crisis with all the subtlety of a grenade, and Felicia’s doing her whole “smoke-and-silk” routine, making sure it cuts just deep enough to sting. My chest feels like it's being squeezed in the middle of an airhorn symphony, but instead of airhorns, it’s all my unresolved relationship guilt screaming in unison. So, naturally, the easiest move? Quip like my life depends on it.”

Listen.

“I’d let the silence stretch for a moment, just enough to make him squirm. Then, with that amused little curl of a smile, call him out—gently. “Spider, you’d be adorable at poker. Tell me, is all this hedging meant to protect me, or just keep you intact?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, I pile on. It'll be funny as hell to watch Parker squirm, and I’m a sucker for watching him do that righteous little shuffle where he gets almost mad but still polite, like, “Stop introducing chaos into the moral high ground, Wade.”.” Wade said.

There.

* * *

Felicia sips a vodka martini, her fingers resting loose on the stem, nails sharp and black. Wade’s beer sweats in the bottle near his hand, untouched, while his other fist, wrapped tightly in a red fingerless glove, drums the table once—twice. Peter, shoulders curled forward, nurses a Coke like it’s holding him steady, his gaze somewhere in her laugh but nowhere near her eyes.

“You know, between Felicia's laugh and Wade's table solo, I feel like we should throw a party or start a band. I'll take awkward dancing, Wade can drum, and Felicia... you can just keep laughing like that. It's kind of a headliner,” Peter said.

“Good luck keeping time with that rhythm, tiger. My laugh's got more syncopation than you'd know how to handle—and I don't come with a mute button. You seriously want to play conductor to that chaos, or are you just fishing for an encore?” Felicia asked.

“Aww, kitten, don't be jealous because this "Skintight Kickstarter" look still outperforms your "6th Avenue window mannequin queen caught in a wind tunnel." And don't act like you'd pass up Bingo Night—I've seen you work a crowd for less,” Wade said.

Felicia’s laugh cuts sharp and quick, the kind that turns heads, but her eyes stay fixed on Wade, lips curling more jagged than her tone. Peter huffs out something softer, his thumb tapping the condensation on his glass, tracing circles like he’s drawing a way out. Wade leans back, grin wide beneath the mask, shoulders loose now as if he’s scored a point—maybe he has, for better or worse.

“Alright, kids, play nice. If it escalates, I’m declaring a blink-and-you-miss-it footrace. I mean, I’ve got the advantage, but it’s only fair,” Peter said.

“A footrace? Adorable. But if you’re trying to impress me, you’ll have to do better than a playground sprint, Peter. How about this—winner gets to ask for anything. Or are you too scared of what I’ll want?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, a wager, huh? High stakes in the house. Alright, if I win, you have to admit out loud that Spider-Man’s ass in the suit is a work of art—and then buy me tacos. If you win... I’ll streak Times Square singing show tunes. I can’t lose. Don’t ask how I know,” Wade said.

Felicia tilts her head, the martini dangling from her fingertips, amusement folding into something closer to calculation. Peter shifts, shoulders tightening as he clears his throat, eyes darting between them like he’s bracing for the ricochet of whatever Wade just lobbed into the room. Wade, unbothered, leans forward this time, mask whispering against the table, daring the silence to break first.

“Alright, Wade, I’m in. Lay it on me. But if this ends up with me webbing something—or someone—don’t pretend you didn’t know where this was going,” Peter said.

“Caveats already, Peter? You’re not inspiring a lot of confidence here. I’d hate to see you crumble under pressure in front of Wade. Maybe we sweeten the pot—loser owes Wade a favor, no questions asked. That too risky for your hero stripes?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, shit, Felicia, you wanna play with fire? A favor owed—fine. But if we’re doing this, it’s a no backing out, no take-backsies, Deadpool’s honor kind of deal. Your favor can’t involve catnip and my favor… Well, let’s just say Spider-Booty might have to get involved. Deal? Or are you scared I’ll win and make this weird?” Wade asked.

Felicia taps the martini glass against her teeth, the faint clink a measured response to Wade’s chaos, her smile poker-flat but dangerous. Peter exhales through his nose, slow, shoulders sinking like he finally picked a side—or gave up picking at all. Wade sits still now, unnervingly so, head cocked, the absence of motion doing what words couldn’t.

* * *

Time.

“You're playing dangerous games, Felicia. Lucky for you, I don't just play—I win,” Wade said.

“Okay, Wade, we get it—you're all about winning. Is this like, sports-day-in-grade-school-winning or "win at life" kind of thing? Because I’m not sure whether to applaud or find you a participation trophy,” Peter said.

Peter's laugh breaks first—too loud, too knowing. It doesn’t match the tight grip of his hand around the Coke. Wade grins back, frosting still on his glove, the weight of Felicia’s heel pressing lightly against his calf. Her smirk widens as she watches them spar. The tone shifts, but the tension doesn’t.

“Alright, Parker, care to let the rest of us in on the joke, or are you just pleased your Spidey-brand dork factor is contagious now? Felicia doesn’t need help laughing at me, buddy—she’s got it covered,” Wade said.

“Oops, butterfingers. Guess the tension just couldn’t handle itself, huh? So are we talking cleanup or escalation, folks? Because I’m game for both.”

Glass hits tile. Shatters sharp and loud. The booth freezes. Felicia's heel retreats. Peter’s hand stiffens mid-gesture, a bead of condensation dripping onto his knee. Wade stares down at the shards like he's considering whether it's worth a quip, but says nothing.

“address_character Your move, sweetheart,” Felicia said.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
