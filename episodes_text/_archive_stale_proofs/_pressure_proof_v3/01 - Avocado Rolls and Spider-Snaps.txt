Episode 1: Avocado Rolls and Spider-Snaps

Grizzly Knights

Felicia Hardy and Wade Wilson conspire over daydrinks to save Peter Parker from himself—creatively, hornily, and slightly dangerously.

* * *

Time.

Space.

Reality.

It is more than a linear path. It is a prism of endless possibility,
where a single choice can branch out into infinite realities, creating
alternate worlds from the ones you know.

In every one of those realities, the same people live with the same
minds. The same hungers. The same scars. The same compensatory
mechanisms they would never name out loud.

I am the Watcher. I am your guide through these vast new realities.

Follow me, and ponder the question — not "what if?"

The question, in this universe, is simpler.

What do they do when they think the mic is off?

These are their stories.

* * *

Time.

“You know, Marcus, these private chats of ours always leave me wanting something sharper. No hard feelings–but you're not the one with the webbed charm I want tonight,” Felicia said.

“Look, Parker, don’t be mad, but I heard your name get casually dropped by Felicia, and it just felt wrong not to summon your webbed ass. I mean, it's practically Pavlovian at this point,” Wade said.

“Sorry, kitten, is that your "Peter’s name got mentioned, and now shit's about to get fun" face, or your "Wade’s being himself and I’m not sure why I tolerate him" look?”

“You caught that, huh? Guess I'm slipping. Or maybe you just like fantasizing about Peter in your free time more than you let on,” Felicia said.

Silence, but not stillness. Felicia's black-tipped nails toy with the olive spear, the rhythmic click against glass a steady metronome of her patience. Wade’s tongue sweeps over a canine, slow, deliberate — a tension ice-cold yet boiling beneath the surface. The egg rolls sit untouched.

“Come on, Wade, admit it. If Peter dropped the mask for you, you’d clear your schedule, wouldn’t you? Full costume or civvies — though something tells me you’d prefer the full spandex package,” Felicia said.

“Do go on, Felicia. If this is leading to Peter showing up in tights and me finally getting some one-on-one wall-crawler bonding time—or, you know, you laying some scandalous detail on the table—I'm riveted,” Wade said.

The booth’s pleather creaks as Wade leans back, arms spreading wide like a man in control, though his grip on the edge digs knuckle-white. Felicia’s gaze drifts, briefly, to the condensation trail slipping down her martini stem. Still circling. Still sharp.

“If we're playing the Peter Parker merry-go-round, why stop at the webhead? Let's bring in the woman who keeps this whole Parker circus running semi-smooth. Bet Mary-Jane's got angles neither of us have played yet. Text sent, now we wait for the redhead wisdom,” Wade said.

Mary-Jane. The name drops like a coin into a well, the ripples immediate. Felicia’s grip tightens around the glass, her painted nails tracing half-formed threats in silence. Wade, half-smirk and snake-eyed, watches the tension he’s flung between them stretch, but doesn’t blink. Not yet.

“Alright, Wade, enough foreplay. Let’s get Johnny in on this little caper—he never says no to a hot invite, and I’m sure he’ll bring that… fiery disposition,” Felicia said.

Felicia’s lips curve, the kind of smile that cuts. The olive spear lands on the plate, discarded and decisive. Wade doesn’t look away—can’t. A spark caught in dry grass, and now, the fire begins.

“Johnny’s on deck, sent him the bat signal—or, let’s be real, the human-torch-shaped fire emoji version. If Peter doesn’t swing in after Johnny starts stirring the pot, it’s not for lack of trying,” Wade said.

Johnny’s name lands lighter, but it carries. Felicia’s chin tilts, eyes sharp as a blade that’s already drawn. Across the table, Wade exhales through his nose—a laugh or a warning, hard to tell. The plan breathes its first breath.

* * *

Time.

Listen.

Time.

“Oh, I raise the stakes. Poker face as real as the last time I bleached my asshole—which is to say, aggressively tailored for comedy and not one bit helpful. I'd lean right into awkward like it's the new foreplay, inventing a drink-mixing contest and a vibrator-based prize, just to break that itchy, silent, eye-flirting tension between those two. Maybe I'll make fake wedding vows for me, Pete, and Felicia—"To love and dishonor, forever swiping left"—while secretly clocking her micro-reactions like I’m auditioning for Lie to Me: The Musical. Keep cracking? Always. But sharper edge? Only if I want to draw blood metaphorically, and I don’t want to make her shut me out completely, so today it's all gently serrated wit. Sharp enough to get them to react, not to make them bleed,” Wade said.

“Oh, sweetheart, Wade doesn’t need strings pulled—he is the string, and half the fun is watching how he tangles himself into knots. But if you’re asking whether I’ll play the game, that depends. The margaritas stay sweaty and ignored until he gives me an opening sharper than “I’ll get naked if you ask nicely.”.” Felicia said.

Time.

“The sound of the glass settling back down on the table echoes just a breath longer than it should. My jaw’s tight, but I let the acid hit air—Wade kinda lives in that space, doesn’t he? He thrives under fire he deserves and fire he really doesn't. But still, c'mon, Mary-Jane,” Peter said.

“Look, Mary-Jane, are we really doing this? I know you’ve got feelings about this hurricane of charm and disaster that is moi, but fists are a bit of an overreaction unless we’re addin’ pillow-talk and safe words to the mix. And Parker? Time for you to step in—unless you like me a little bruised for aesthetic reasons,” Wade said.

“Felicia? You good running color commentary on this or you planning your own opening gambit? Feels like maybe we’ve got some table choreography to smooth over before Mary-Jane declares it open season,” Peter said.

“Let Parker? Oh, honey, I don’t let men anything. But if he shatters the eye-line? I don’t move first. A man needs to decide if what he wants is bold enough to run with claws,” Felicia said.

There.

* * *

Time.

“That’s a dangerous thing to say in this room. Care to double down, or should we circle back to what, exactly, ‘for Peter’ has ever bought me?” Felicia asked.

“Define "confronting" here, Kitty Kat. Are we talking claws-out confrontation or the sexy verbal fencing you do when you're testing the boundaries while looking amazing in leather?” Wade asked.

“...Alright, if none of us are gonna say it, I will—what the hell do you two want from me right now?” Peter asked.

“Alright, Peter. You want clarity? Let’s drop the pretense and play this straight. What's on your mind?” Mary asked.

“Oh, Mary-Jane, don’t look so surprised—I like to play with my claws out. But suits me; Peter’s had his chance to speak up. Let’s hear him—if there’s anything left he’s not too scared to say,” Felicia said.

“Ahhhh, I see what you're doing. You're setting the table, playing bad cop in thigh-highs. Let me guess—you want me to be the reckless wildcard so Peter plays the reluctant hero? Classic,” Wade said.

“Yeah, well, Wade, if this is a setup, you sure as hell forgot to hand out a script, because I’m flying blind over here. Try aiming that neon spotlight of yours somewhere useful for once,” Peter said.

Peter's hand hovers near his glass of Coke, the ice half-melted, untouched. The sleeves of his hoodie are shoved to his elbows, exposing the strain in tendons that don't quite reach for anything. He's been listening too long—now, he leans in.

“Cute, Pete. I’d almost call it an attempt at honesty if you didn’t dress it in all that awkward martyrdom. Go on, though; the floor’s yours—show us how messy you can get,” Felicia said.

“Just trying to establish the mood here—if we're going full pressure cooker, I wanna know whether to bring popcorn or baby oil,” Wade said.

“Let’s shut out the noise, Pete. This is you, and me, right now. Say it. Whatever it is that’s tying your guts up—just say it,” Mary said.

“Alright, Mary-Jane. You want to know what’s really going on? Fine. Take your shot—you’ve got my full attention,” Peter said.

“Your serve, Mary-Jane. Let’s see just how deep you want to cut tonight,” Felicia said.

“Looks like Mary-Jane missed her cue—guess you'll have to wait for the real star entrance. But hey, I'm always here to keep the spotlight spicy until she shows,” Wade said.

Felicia's nails drum once against the glass. Wade’s grin hangs sharp, but his eyes flick away first. Mary-Jane holds the moment, fine as a wire, and Peter’s voice sets the balance.

* * *

Silence. The sugar-rimmed margarita glass by Mary-Jane’s hand glistens under the fluorescent light. Peter hasn’t touched his Coke in ten minutes.

“Ah, poor Peter. Still bracing for a landslide when you’re stuck under steady rain. You know she sees through you like we all do. It’s so terribly transparent—not that you're entirely to blame. Maybe it’s time to stop dodging, face the fact we all bleed in red, and tell her why rooftops feel safer than her couch. Or at least come up with a better reason,” Felicia said.

“Oh, come on, Felicia—be nice to Petey! He's like a puppy who just found out his tail isn't actually attached to someone else. Confused, a little sad, and spinning in circles like he's reviewing gym footage of someone else’s bad life choices,” Wade said.

“You really think this is easy, Wade? This — everything I’m juggling — isn’t Spider-Man: Homecoming or whatever you think you’re projecting onto me,” Peter said.

“Alright, sweetheart, run that by me again but with the word salad dialed back. You're asking if Felicia, sharper and ready to go, was caught off-guard enough to deflate the scene instead of closing it sharp? Or you're trying to figure out if there was enough edge left for a finish? Help me, babe—I'm good, but I'm not a psychic,” Mary said.

Time shifts. Peter’s voice now lingers where Mary's had cut before, raw as the untouched marinara at the table's edge. Wade’s laughter falls hollow; Felicia’s gaze doesn’t soften. And Mary-Jane—her steady calm turns the weight of the scheme into something real, something none of them can leave in the booth.

* * *

Time.

“Oh, Peter. So your solution is to punish her for loving you, instead of giving her the choice? You think she hasn't seen who you are by now? That she doesn't get to decide if you're worth all the goddamn cracks and danger and broken webs? You're not protecting her. You're controlling her,” Felicia said.

“I'm not controlling anything. I'm just—I'm trying to keep people safe. That's not the same. If you can't see that, then... I don't know—maybe you're right about me,” Peter said.

“Wow. I mean, I didn’t think "foreplay" involved black leather and incessant wall-crawling, but hey, I’m not kink-shaming,” Wade said.

Silence.

“That's quite the way to say "nobody's calling the thing a thing," isn't it? Fine. Cards on the table—what's stuck in your throat? Spit it out, sugar,” Mary said.

* * *

Time.

“Okay, no one’s saying it, and I’m not going to make you, but you realize you’re all wearing it like a neon sign, right? So either someone fesses up to why this table just turned into a funeral for something—marriages, jobs, morality, orgasms, I’m flexible—or I’m ordering another round and giving the waitress a wink so she thinks I’m the messy one here. Your move,” Mary said.

“You want me to start? Sure, I'll kick it off—let's talk about how I'm the guy who can swing into a building on a thread and spin a goddamn criminal Christmas display, but I still can't keep a date because the universe thinks it's funny. That's icebreaker material, right?” Peter asked.

“Well, Peter, maybe if you stopped trying to martyr yourself solving every problem alone, you’d find less of your relationships on fire. But hey, what do I know? I’m just the girl who loved the best parts of you before you started resenting them,” Felicia said.

“Cyclone in human skin it is. Felicia, just remember, kitten, when it comes to unpredictability, I'm basically the hurricane that hires tornadoes as interns. Catch me making everyone guess—and then regretting they did,” Wade said.

Silence.

“sigh Alright, boys and girls, I know vulnerability isn’t exactly the group’s strong suit, but can we not throw Peter to the wolves tonight? Wade, you can come back down from the chandelier; Felicia, sharp knife, real small table—not the time for precision strikes. And Peter... babe, would it kill you to unclench your jaw before you turn into concrete? Look, it’s messy, it always is. But maybe let’s not all bleed on the cheesecake, okay?” Mary asked.

“Okay, yeah. Less emotional carnage. Sure. I mean, look around—emotional carnage is like my default setting, Mary-Jane. But, uh, I can try... You said it. What's next on the kumbaya agenda? Hug it out? Group therapy over drinks we can't afford?” Peter asked.

“Peter, I switch gears, softening the sharp edge in my voice. The cuts only go so far, and God help me, I’d rather not make new scars tonight,” Felicia said.

“And like all great cliffhangers, babe, you're just teasing enough to make us come back for more,” Wade said.

“Wade, you’re doing the verbal equivalent of spinning plates on fire, and sure, it’s entertaining, but Felix doesn't miss. You saw that, yeah? So maybe instead of deflecting like your life depends on it, you name why the room got colder, or better yet—step aside and let the grown-ups clear their air,” Mary said.

“Let's see if your prescription comes in something stronger than tequila and bad decisions—though I'd probably try it anyway,” Wade said.

Tension.

“Wade, sweetheart, that laugh is hanging by a thread thinner than your masks. So you wanna quit the act, just for a second? If you’re close to breaking, fine, we’ll catch you. Or, hell, let it out and we’ll pass you another whiskey after. But don’t sit here pretending your pain’s a punchline, 'cause we’ve all seen how that movie ends. And frankly? Not in the mood for reruns,” Mary said.

“But maybe... maybe tonight, she doesn't have to steal the whole spotlight,” Wade said.

Stillness.

* * *

Time.

“You sure? ‘Cause if there’s some ex-boyfriend warlord nemesis situation about to kick off, I’d like to know before I need to web someone’s face to the fries,” Peter said.

“Oh, sure, because that's what’s gonna tip the scales—your big brain does quantum physics, but it’s the idea of, I don’t know, radioactive ferrets overthrowing Midtown that suddenly makes this a risk,” Mary said.

“Fair. But if one of them decides to throw ketchup or a punch, let me know. I’d rather work on stains than broken ribs tonight,” Peter said.

“Surprise, motherfu—wait, what? Did I blackout-fall-ass-first into another dissociative Groundhog Day moment again? Or are we doing the ol' déjà vu two-step while my brain clicks "refresh" on the trauma browser?” Wade asked.

“That has to win some kind of record for fastest escalation. Are you moonlighting as a one-man circus, or is this just your natural state of being?” Felicia asked.

“Oh, sweet kitty-kitty, you say "circus act" like it’s an insult. Honey, I’m the ringmaster, the clown, the lion tamer, and the goddamn human cannonball—all rolled into one handsome, skin-melting chimichanga of a man. You call me a spectacle, I call it Tuesday. But don’t act like you’re too good for my three rings—you’re just mad you don’t have the popcorn concession,” Wade said.

“Wade, darling, you’re like if tequila had a human form—chaotic, disorienting, and way too much for a Tuesday. And don’t think for a second you can pull me into your kaleidoscope of madness without my claws fully sharpened,” Felicia said.

“Well, now I’m blushing, which complicates the whole brooding-while-chewing thing. Thanks for that mental Polaroid,” Peter said.

“The problem is, honey, now you've put the idea out there, and someone, somewhere, heard it as a challenge. If we end up fighting ferrets next week, I'm blaming you,” Mary said.

“Alright, but ferrets? That takes this out of the ‘regular absurdity’ department and straight into ‘how does Peter end up tarred and meme’d in the process’ territory. Do I need a tetanus booster for this adventure, or just a net?” Peter asked.

“Oh, Parker, you don't just texturize the ferret scenario, you give it volume, body, and a little mousse to keep it fluffed. But let’s not go full shampoo commercial on this — ferrets in the circus is one thing, but now I’m wondering… Do we train the ferrets to juggle tiny grenades? Or do they just scamper off with ‘em, tail wagging, and we never see ‘em again until—boom! Relocation: Brooklyn,” Wade said.

“Alright, ferrets aside—because you know Wade would make merch out of that—I need us to focus for a minute. The stakes here aren’t furry little chaos bombs; it’s people. People we know, people we care about. Let's not joke past that,” Mary said.

“Right. Real stakes. Not ferret stakes. People stakes. Our people stakes. And that means we play this smart—no rushing in, no martyr bullshit. We all walk out of this, or we don’t go in. Deal?” Peter asked.

“Peter, you always want things neat, like there’s a bow that’ll tie everything up and make it make sense. But we both know that’s not how this works—we don’t work that way. Maybe the resolution isn’t out there; maybe it’s just learning to sit in the mess and not trying to fix it. Can you? Or does it always have to be “better”?” Felicia asked.

“Look, I know I default to ‘fix it’ mode—save the day, wrap it up neat, cue the sunset. But maybe that’s because if I stop trying to fix things, it feels like I’m failing things. Failing people. Failing... you,” Peter said.

“So that’s what’s under the mask today, huh? Always trying to save everyone because you can’t stomach not being enough. Newsflash, Peter, you’re not supposed to be enough—not for everyone, not all the time. You’re human, even when you’re swinging between skyscrapers. Maybe you should try saving yourself for once. Or is that too scary?” Felicia asked.

“Yeah, well... figuring that out’s easier when you’re not holding Spider-Man together with webbing and duct tape. But you’re right. I just don’t know where to start,” Peter said.

“You start small. Something simple—one thing that’s just for you, no masks, no webs, no one else to save. And you don’t let yourself feel guilty about it, not for even a second. Or, better yet, let’s steal you something. Thrill therapy, my treat,” Felicia said.

“One simple thing? Knowing you, that probably involves breaking into something, dangling from something, or stealing something shiny. Should I be worried? Or is this just your version of "take two and call me in the morning"?” Peter asked.

“Relax, Spider. I’ll make sure we keep it light—maybe just a piece of jewelry, nothing too grandiose. I mean, unless you’re ready for your first heist to involve the Crown Jewels. Baby steps,” Felicia said.

Listen.

* * *

Time.

“I scroll through my phone slowly, thumb gliding just enough to cast whispers off the thin Cartier bracelet like a dare for the light. Glitter is the point. Style is the posture. But my stomach’s been doing this subtle little flip the entire time, and no amount of polish softens that,” Felicia said.

“You could’ve just asked for a taste, Felicia. But I'm warning you—Wade's buffet has no off switch,” Wade said.

“Well, Wade, your unique brand of charm continues to be like a vodka soda with a hair floating in it—almost refreshing, until it’s not. Felicia, darling, what’s your excuse? Turtling into his chaos for backup or just bored?” Mary asked.

“...So, anyone else feel like we're stuck in a bad sitcom rerun or is it just me? Felicia, you wanna grab the next snarky line, or are we taking this into a real cage match round back? I mean, Mary-Jane, you want popcorn for that?” Peter asked.

Felicia’s nail traces the edge of her phone case. Slow. Measured. The only signal she’s listening, even as her gaze stays fixed on the screen. Peter’s foot taps once against the booth leg, a silent punctuation to the ask he’s not saying aloud.

“Oh, I know what Peter thinks he’s keeping quiet,” I say, spinning my phone once in my hand before setting it down just loud enough to punctuate. “It’s the desperate little combination-lock fantasy he plays with—the hope that one of us just says the magic code to make it simple.”.” Felicia said.

“And that, my darling Felicia, is how you unlock the secret level of Wade’s heart—spoiler: it’s mostly tacos and bad decisions,” Wade said.

“Oh, Wade, sweetie, if absurdity were measured in calories, you'd be the cheat day no one's metabolism could survive. Let's focus: is this nonsense actually going somewhere, or has Felicia just agreed to validate your constant audition for a one-man traveling circus?” Mary asked.

“Oh, come on, Mary-Jane. If we're all throwing barbs, at least let me get in on the crossfire so I can dodge something. Or, wait, should I just pose dramatically while you two reenact a Rumble in the Jungle?” Peter asked.

Mary-Jane’s fry snaps clean in half. The sound breaks the lull, sharp in the stale air. Wade’s laugh dies half-formed, the grin below his mask caught mid-rise. Felicia finally looks up, head tilted just enough to flash her eyes, daring Peter to push harder.

“I let the smirk linger, a feline pause stretched just enough to pull the room tighter—you could pluck the tension like a cello string, low and resonant. Then, without breaking stride, I pick up my drink and take the slowest, most deliberate sip imaginable,” Felicia said.

“A symbol of our undying love—or just my leftovers. Either way, Felicia, destiny’s calling, and it’s made of tortillas,” Wade said.

* * *

As for me, these are my stories.

I observe all that transpires here. But I do not, cannot, will not
interfere.

I have watched. I will continue to watch.

For I am the Watcher.
